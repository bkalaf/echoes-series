# Style and Prose Rules

This file defines drafting behavior for `Echoes of Eidolon` manuscripts.

## General Drafting Rules

- Preserve canon before improving prose.
- Do not simplify a chapter by cutting required mechanics.
- Do not invent new cities, ships, Witnesses, rewards, or family relationships without instruction.
- Keep emotional turns attached to action, not abstract explanation.
- Dialogue should carry conflict, concealment, pressure, or relationship movement.
- Avoid making Witness scenes generic boss fights.
- Avoid over-explaining mysteries before their reveal window.
- Preserve recurring character habits from [`17_CHARACTER_GESTURE_SIGNALS.md`](./17_CHARACTER_GESTURE_SIGNALS.md) as reader-training signals, not decorative filler.

## Chapter Reconstruction Rules

For L4 Reconstruction chapters:

- Preserve the exact scene skeleton.
- Preserve beat order.
- Preserve section breaks.
- Preserve who discovers each clue.
- Preserve who asks each important question.
- Preserve the chapter’s final image / handoff.
- Dialogue can change unless marked verbatim.
- Prose can tighten if the same beat remains intact.

## Horizontal Breaks

Horizontal breaks are visible as:

```md
---
```

They are not automatically subchapter titles. Internal labels may exist in outlines, but should not appear in manuscript unless explicitly requested.

## Dear Reader Voice

Dear Reader interludes may interrupt the story directly.

They should:

- identify a thematic question;
- use fictionalized framing for real-world reference;
- return clearly to Eidolon;
- include a comedic, cutting, or emotionally clarifying exit;
- avoid becoming detached essays.

## Character Gesture / Signal Rule

Recurring habits are part of the prose signal system.

Use them to train the reader to notice when something has changed internally without explaining every thought.

Examples:

- Addie changes / adjusts hair color as a control ritual and touches or checks her simple silver pendant when mother / Mark / grief pressure surfaces.
- Gregory fidgets with his bracelet and wears spectacles he does not need.
- Mae flips or catches her coin; when she stops, she has likely decided something.
- Yurislav rolls / dances a coin across his knuckles; when it goes still, he is likely about to choose.
- Brickett is a 14-year-old beaver beastfolk whose recurring tick is trying to make fire: little sparks, sputtering flames, unstable fireballs, smoke, singed fur, burned paws, and backfires. He also repeats false-heroic “My life for you!” interventions every two to three chapters when pacing allows.
- Matthieu doodles diagrams or calculations on available surfaces.
- Major Tallandra scans rooms with avian precision; when she goes still, she has found danger or made a tactical choice.

## Eye-Mail Rule

Use **eye-mail** for magical-bureaucratic messages and inter-team contact.

Eye-mail means “for your eyes only.” Messages are keyed to a recipient and may be routed through a hotel, guildhall, reception desk, or civic system, but the delivery logic seeks the intended recipient’s eyes / identity.

Avoid generic “communicator token” language unless a later scene explicitly creates a specialized object, and even then it should be framed as an eye-mail key / seal / relay object rather than modern communication tech.

## First Mirror Obfuscation Rule

The Book 01 First Mirror may intentionally manufacture suspense by hiding the content of conversations that would reveal series-scale truth too early.

This is a special-use device for the First Mirror, not a general pattern for mirror scenes. Reusing heavy conversation obfuscation repeatedly would damage reader trust and make the authorial hand feel manipulative.

This concealment should read as **perceptual pressure**, not as redacted text.

### Use

Use First Mirror obfuscation only where the scene contains information that belongs to later reveal windows, especially:

- digitization / simulation-world truth;
- ark / mainframe truth;
- Mother’s full architecture;
- Noel’s deeper identity or function;
- full companion-system architecture;
- explicit explanations of why the series has staged books / cycles / reveals;
- direct identification of Dianne as Mother before the intended reveal window.

### Method

Prefer blocking whole thoughts, whole sentences, or whole exchanges through pressure, submerged roar, silence, distortion, or perceptual overwhelm.

Do **not** write the scene like black-bar redaction or word-by-word censorship. Avoid effects where only a single key name or spoiler-word is removed from an otherwise clear sentence unless a later scene explicitly calls for that style.

The reader should feel that a complete conversation is happening and Addie is being prevented from hearing parts of it.

### What Remains Available

When words are hidden, increase emphasis on:

- body language;
- emotional reactions;
- who looks afraid, guilty, angry, resigned, protective, or ashamed;
- who accepts authority without challenge;
- what the room makes space for;
- silence after pressure fades;
- Addie’s discipline as an observer of people rather than as someone decoding all lore.

Silence may explicitly coach Addie toward this mode. She can remind Addie that she has studied people, systems, sociology, emotion, or power, and that when the words are unavailable, the reactions still speak.

### Book 01 First Mirror Specifics

For Book 01, the First Mirror should preserve suspense rather than solve the series.

- Addie should not conclude that Noel’s seat belongs to her.
- Addie should instead notice that Noel feels sinister and that the room accepts his authority too quickly.
- Addie should not consciously connect Dianne to Mother.
- At most, Addie may register an unplaceable emotional texture around Dianne: tiredness, protectiveness, age, sorrow, or a difficult-to-name familiarity. Even that should be used sparingly.
- The point is not that Addie lacks intelligence. The point is that the mirror blocks the wrong information and forces her to infer from human reaction.

## Witness Scene Rules

A Witness scene should include:

- domain pressure;
- emotional test;
- sensory identity tied to the Witness form;
- refusal to explain too directly;
- riddle / memory / moral pressure;
- a defeat condition that requires the companion to embody the book feat.

## Prose Bias

Prefer:

- concrete action;
- precise emotional pressure;
- scene-level causality;
- visible objects and gestures;
- dialogue with subtext;
- ending images that hand off cleanly.

Avoid:

- generic fantasy awe;
- repeated exposition;
- flattening political parallels into direct lectures outside Dear Reader chapters;
- resolving tension too early;
- turning every character into the same voice.

## Verification Before Output

Before presenting a chapter draft, check:

- Does it obey the assigned detail level?
- Does it preserve the latest correction?
- Does it preserve all hard canon?
- Does it maintain the intended section breaks?
- Does the chapter ending still point to the next chapter?
- Did any old/superseded names slip in?
