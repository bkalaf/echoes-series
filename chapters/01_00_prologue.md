# Prologue — The Mother Who Started the War

Let me tell you a story.

I want to be honest with you before I do, because honesty is going to be in short supply for a while and I'd like you to remember that at least the frame was fair. So: this story is true. Every word of it. It is also, depending on who you ask and where they were standing when it happened, completely false. Both of those things will remain the case no matter how carefully I tell it. That isn't a flaw in the telling. It's the subject.

I could begin at the end. Endings make excellent beginnings — they have all the weight and none of the suspense, and there's a particular kind of reader who only relaxes once they know how much it's going to hurt. I could begin at the city gates, which is where most people think it began, because that is where it became loud enough to notice. Or I could begin earlier, in a quiet room, with eighteen people who agreed to something they should not have agreed to, and who are all, in one sense, already in this room with us now.

I'm going to warn you once and then never again: the clocks in this story do not agree. Things that come later will have already happened. Things that have already happened will turn out not to have, yet. If you feel, at any point, that you have lost the thread — good. Hold onto that feeling. It's more accurate than certainty.

I'll also make you a promise, and it's the only one I intend to keep cleanly. The solution to the entire thing — all of it, every book, every death, the whole aching machine of it — is on these first few pages. It's here. I'm not hiding it. I'm just counting on you not to believe me.

Now. I owe you an apology, and it's the same apology I'll be making, in various costumes, for a very long time. There is going to be science in this. There is going to be mathematics. I know. I know how you feel about being handed a number when you were promised a story. But you'll have to forgive it, because in this world science and magic are not enemies — they are cousins, raised in the same house, competing for the same inheritance, each convinced the other is the reason things broke. Keep an eye on both of them. Neither one is telling you the whole truth. That's what makes them family.

So we begin with a woman. You already know her, of course — you've spent so long with her by now that you'll recognize the tiredness in her before you recognize her face, and —

No. Forgive me. Not *you.* I keep doing that. I keep speaking to someone who isn't reading this yet. Let me start that sentence again, and this time I'll say it to *you,* Dear Reader, the one actually holding the page.

We begin with a woman who is very, very old, and very, very tired, in the specific way of someone who has stayed awake so that others could sleep. She is old enough that the word *ancient* undersells it and *grandmotherly* oversells it and the truth lies in some cruel place between the two. And she is running out of time. Not in the way we all are — in a precise way, a countable way. She has sixteen months. She knows the exact number. That is the worst part of her: she always knows the exact number.

I had planned to introduce her on a warm beach at the golden edge of the world, because you deserve one nice thing before all of this, and so, apparently, does she. But she wouldn't have gone. She was already somewhere else, doing the only work she had left, and it would be a lie to put her anywhere prettier.

So we go to Skyrend Peak.

---

Skyrend Peak sits so high and so cold that the season forgets to reach it. Two days before the solstice — the year's warmest hour, in kinder places — the slums along its lower terraces were freezing. Meltwater had frozen again in the gutters. Breath hung in doorways. Down in the good districts, up in the towers with the brass and the charts, the astronomers were not sleeping either, though for a different reason: they had counted the moons, and counted them again, and gotten an answer that had not been correct for a thousand years. Three of them, drifting toward the same seam in the sky. The astronomers were beginning, very quietly and very professionally, to panic.

The Mother was not looking at the sky. She had already read the sky the way you read a letter you wrote yourself. She was looking, instead, for one particular child — a small, forgotten, entirely unimportant child, the kind of child a world keeps precisely by never noticing it has him.

She found him in a rotting warehouse at the bottom of everything, asleep in a nest of garbage beside a fire that had almost finished dying.

He was a beaver, this child. Fourteen years old and built wrong for the cold — damp brown fur matted flat, a broad flat tail tucked so badly beneath him it had gone half-numb, big blunt front teeth showing where his mouth had fallen open in sleep. His paws were caked to the wrist in dried mud. And across his arms and one singed cheek were the small burns and soot-marks of a beaver child who, against everything his body was made for, everything water and wood and instinct told him, kept trying to make fire — and kept, so far, only barely surviving the attempt.

The Mother knelt by the campfire first. That was not sentiment; that was arithmetic. A dead fire meant a dead child by morning, and she needed him alive for reasons she did not enjoy. She breathed on the coals, or did whatever it is she does that looks like breathing, and the flame came back — small, orange, honest — and pushed a little circle of warmth out into the damp wood and the cold mud, and even in his sleep the boy turned his soot-streaked face toward it the way a plant turns toward a window. Fire loved him back a little. It always would. That was going to be a problem.

She set down what she had come to leave. A backpack, sturdy, packed with care. A scroll, sealed, waiting. These were the plan. She had carried the plan for longer than the walls of that warehouse had stood, and every item in it had a reason, and none of the reasons were kind, and all of them were necessary.

Then she noticed he was shivering.

I want you to pay attention here, because everything that follows — the whole war, all of it — turns on the next few seconds, and it will never be this quiet again.

The blanket was not part of the plan.

There was no blanket in the design. There was no line in any ledger, no allowance in any calculation, that accounted for a boy being cold in a way that a fire could not fix. She could have left him shivering. The plan survived him shivering. The plan, in fact, had never once asked whether he was cold, because the plan was not built to ask that kind of question, and she had spent a very long time being the kind of thing that does not ask it either.

She made the blanket anyway. Wool, thick, warm, appearing out of nothing but the small unbudgeted tenderness of someone who had decided — just this once, just here, where no rule could reach — to be a grandmother instead of a mechanism. She tucked it around him. He didn't wake.

"I believe in you," she told the sleeping child, very softly. "I believe in you, and in the archivist who hasn't found her name for any of this yet, and in the others — all of them, even the ones who are going to disappoint me." She smoothed the wool at his shoulder. "You won't understand your part for a long time. That's all right. You only have to do one thing. When the clock runs out — and it will run out — you hold the line. You hold it until someone stronger arrives. Promise me that in your sleep, where it's safe to promise things."

He shifted. He did not promise. He did not hear her at all.

"I love you," the Mother said.

He did not hear that either.

But you did.

Now — the seal on that scroll. I've been waiting to show you. Pressed into the wax was a mark, and inside the mark, if you knew to look, a sequence: three, then nine, then eighteen, then twenty-four, then seventy-two. It repeats. You’ll see it again — in the three kinds of soul the world sorts its people into, and the three cities that make a state, and the three factions that are about to try to kill each other over all of it; in sixes and nines and twelves; in eighteens and twenty-fours and thirty-sixes; all the way up to the seventy-two cities themselves. On doors. In death counts. In the number of chairs at a table, and the shape of the sky itself — long after you’ve stopped expecting it, and every time you’ll feel that you almost understand it.

You won't. Not yet. I could tell you what it means right now, in one sentence, and it would ruin absolutely everything, and so I am going to be cruel and keep it. Forgive me. I did warn you the honesty would run out.

Here is what I will tell you.

With a backpack, a sealed scroll, a wool blanket she was never supposed to make, and one small loophole worn patiently into a rule that had held for a thousand years, that tired, ancient, grandmotherly woman had just started a war.

She started it, you understand, with an act of compassion. That will matter later more than anything else in this book. Remember that the first shot fired in all of it was a blanket tucked around a cold child by someone who had every reason not to bother.

She was gone before the coals burned low again. The boy slept on in his borrowed warmth, mud-pawed and singed and entirely unaware, and beside his head the sealed scroll lay waiting — patient as the wax, patient as the woman, patient as the three moons leaning quietly toward each other in the dark — waiting for morning, and for the astronomers, and for a mathematician named Miriam who was, at that very moment, checking her figures one last desperate time, hoping with all her heart that she had made a mistake.

She hadn't.
