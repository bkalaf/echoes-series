## Rough Exit

The problem with being removed from a room by something that does not use doors is that your body keeps insisting, the entire way, that this is not allowed.

Addie felt the Conclave chamber fold. Not go dark — *fold,* the way a page folds, the seventy-two and the tiered stone and Mae's black-flipped coin all creasing toward a line that had no business existing, and then she was being pulled along the crease, through an angle that was the wrong number of degrees, her stomach announcing with total sincerity that it would like to stay behind. There was pressure. There was the specific nausea of a direction that wasn't one. There was a long, silent, airless fall that lasted either a heartbeat or a small geological era.

Then there was floor.

There had not been floor a moment before — she was fairly sure of that, in the part of her that was still filing things — and then there was, and she met it with her hands and one hip and the side of her staff, which cracked against the not-quite-stone and skittered away into a dark that swallowed the sound. She stayed down a moment. Breathing seemed like a reasonable full-time occupation.

"That," said a dry voice above her, "was a rough ride. I'm sorry. I should have warned you."

The Overseer stood a polite distance off, upright, unruffled, his robes hanging exactly as robes were meant to hang, as though he had strolled here down a corridor she'd somehow missed. He looked down at her with something that was not quite concern and not quite amusement.

"A fast exit was best," he went on. "The alternative was twenty minutes of standing up, being looked at, and finding the door. This was kinder. We'll have you back momentarily." A small incline of his head. "This part is just for you."

Addie got a knee under herself. Her ears were ringing in a color. "I have," she said, "so many questions."

"Yes." The Overseer considered her. "So do I. How did you go all *day* without fixing your robes?"

She looked down. The blue-and-white moderator's robe was still on wrong — pulled where it shouldn't pull, the seam that belonged at her shoulder sitting somewhere near her collarbone, exactly as it had been since a frantic dawn a thousand years ago. Heat climbed her neck. "I've had," she said, with enormous restraint, "a day. I got shot at. I got dragged across a city. Someone handed me a *beaver.* I got threatened by what I'm now fairly sure was cosmic middle management. And then I got folded through a wall into a room that appears to have no —" she looked around, and the looking found nothing to land on "— that appears to have *nothing.* Fixing my collar did not make the list."

Her hand went, on its own, toward the tin at her belt. One more H&H would take the ring out of her ears. One more H&H would make her hands stop doing that.

"There are finer details," the Overseer said, "that will be left to you to explain to your companions —"

"My *what.*" She wasn't sure which word she was objecting to. Possibly all of them. "You cannot keep calling them that like I picked them out of a catalogue while I am this —"

"For this part," he said, "I'll take my leave. You're in capable hands."

The words arrived a full second before their meaning did. Addie's head came up.

"Wait — are you just going to *fucking leave me in this empty room with no door?*"

He didn't answer. He looked at her a moment longer, with that expression she would spend a while failing to name, and then he stepped backward into a piece of the dark that had been, until that instant, the same as all the other dark — and the dark took him, cleanly, without a sound, and did not give him back.

She was alone.

She was alone in a nowhere with no walls and no ceiling and no door, and her staff was somewhere she couldn't see, and the pressure of the place sat on her like deep water.

So she took the pellet.

It went down bitter. She had one clean breath of relief — the ringing softened, her hands quieted, the floor felt marginally more like a promise the universe intended to keep — and then, all around her, the dark began, very slowly, to glow.

## The Eighteen Lights

*Oh, no,* she thought, with the specific clarity of the recently medicated. *Oh, no, I took the wrong one. That was the wrong tin. I'm hallucinating in a hole.*

The light did not come on the way light comes on. It *rose,* the way a lamp rises under a hand on a dimmer, dark bleeding up into not-quite-dark and then into shape, and the shapes were standing in a ring, and the ring was around her, and she was in the middle of it.

They came up one at a time, or nearly — a slow illumination passing around the circle like dawn deciding to happen out of order — and she counted, because counting was the thing her back room did when the rest of her wanted to lie down.

The first to brighten stood tall and wrong-jointed and black, all patient angles, a great arachnae shape with too many folded limbs and a stillness that was somehow the loudest thing in the room. A widow. The spider.

The second came up white and branching, a treant with a long slow grin worked into the grain of its face, roots for feet, a look of enormous private amusement. A laughing tree.

The third, she was *sure,* would be a crown, or a dragon, or a crowned dragon — the sky over Erzengel had trained her for it, third house, the ember one — and instead the light found something white and terrible and serene, an angelic thing with a judge's stillness and an inquisitor's patience, and her whole tidy expectation fell over.

*Right,* she thought, catching herself with a small lurch. *Not pictures. It's not going to be pictures. Stop matching the pictures.*

She let the rest come without arguing with it. A green giant of a thing, stone-shouldered, law in the set of it. A black surgeon-shape, goblin-fine, a crucible-emptiness about the hands. A red knight with a serpent's grace and a storm banked behind its visor. A two-headed one, red and blue at once, ogre-broad, two faces sharing one weather. A green wanderer, walrus-heavy, calm as a tide that has seen your whole coastline erode and will see the next one too. A black moth-shape with a lantern for a heart, and she noted, filing, that its dark was a different dark than the spider's — *not the same family, don't confuse them.* A yellow stag, white-pelted, a harvester's blade and a steward's eyes. A gold-yellow raven, archival, its feathers hung with the idea of names. A red boar, broad and hosting, the shape of an empty chair kept warm. A green scholar the size of a small building, tortoise-slow, elephant-patient. A blue owl, round-eyed, an oracle's terrible attentiveness. A red-and-blue minotaur, sage-browed, weighing power like a butcher weighing meat. A blue one — *human,* she registered, the only human-shaped thing in the circle — robed like a priest, still and steadying. A white rhinoceros, guardian-solid, the kind of large that makes fear feel reasonable rather than shameful.

And the eighteenth came up last, small, low to the floor, a slight yellow child in a plain robe — a fox girl, she saw, a little vulpine kit — wearing, over her actual fox face, a white mask carved in the shape of a fox.

Addie stared at that one for a moment longer than the others.

"You're a fox," she said, before she could stop herself, "wearing a fox mask."

The mask tilted, birdlike, delighted.

Addie's back room, by now, had run out of shelves. She was stacking the new things on the floor of it, against the walls, in the doorways, colors on names on forms on the growing, cold, unwelcome certainty that not one item in this inventory was decorative — that every color and every animal and every position in the ring was load-bearing, was *meant,* the way the residual at the top of Skyrend Peak had been meant.

"We've been waiting for you," the little fox said, in a voice like a child reciting something lovely, "for a very long time, Adeshka."

"Call me Addie," she said, on reflex, the way she'd have corrected anyone.

The fox giggled — an actual giggle, small and sideways and entirely unbothered by the eighteen ancient dreadful things sharing her room. "Addie," she agreed, trying it out, pleased.

"Silence." The blue priest — the human one — spoke without moving. "Stay focused, sister."

"I *am* focused." A beat. "I'm the *most* focused." The fox spread her small hands at the circle. "Introductions, though. It's rude otherwise. Start with the first one."

And they went round, in the order they'd risen — Horror, Revel, Crown, Stone, Mercy, Tempest, the twinned pair who called themselves Dominion and Veils in one two-throated breath, Tides, Lanterns, Harvest, Echoes, Hearth, Sand, Omen, Meridian, Ash, Bastion — each naming itself once, plainly, and Addie stood in the center and took the names and set each one beside its color and its shape and its place in the ring, and the pattern she had been refusing to finish finishing itself anyway, all at once, the way the sky had rearranged itself over Skyrend the night everything started.

"—and Silence," the fox finished, with a little bow that put her mask briefly to the floor.

Addie looked around the circle. The whole of it. The order of it.

"You're the constellations," she said.

Not one of them said no.

## The Rules We Bleed Into

The spider unfolded a degree — not a step, just an intention toward her — and spoke, and its voice came out soft and sibilant and slightly, permanently misshapen around the mouth.

"Welcome," it said, "to the theam between thtorieth."

Addie waited for one of the eighteen ancient beings to acknowledge that the Witness of Horror had a lisp. None of them did. It appeared to be, cosmically, none of anyone's business.

"You've been *told* the shape of this," said Silence, wandering a slow sideways orbit at the edge of the ring. "About the Conjunctions. The cycle."

"I've been *yelled* at about it," Addie said. "I've been handed it like a summons and a rebuke and a piece of very aggressive weather. Nobody's ever sat me down and — none of it landed like a *briefing.* It landed like paperwork with teeth."

"Then a briefing," said the raven — Echoes — in a voice of dry pages. "There will be eighteen Conjunctions. One turned, as this one is, toward each of us."

"You've met some of the ones who'll stand with you," said the boar, Hearth, warm and broad. "Others have only been named yet. Named, they're set apart — the ordinary rules of a Conjunction, the way power moves and is taken, don't touch a named companion the way they'd touch anyone else."

"On Eidolon," Addie said, "or in Erzengel?" Because it mattered — because it was exactly the kind of thing everyone said fast and meant loosely, and she had learned today that loose words cost cities.

"That," said the minotaur, Meridian, weighing it, "depends on the power invoked. World. Nation. Office. The rule bends toward whichever authority you reach for."

"We're one nation," Addie said. "On one world. So it's the same thing."

"You do not *have* to be one nation." Meridian said it the way you'd correct a child who had mistaken a wall for the edge of the world. "Cities have seceded before. Nations have been made and unmade. What is fixed — the one thing that has *never* bent — is that power passes only during a Conjunction. Never between. That is the constraint. The single nation is a habit. You have simply held the habit so long you mistook it for a law of the sky."

And there it was, going cold down her spine: two thousand years of one flag, one Conclave, one continuity, and the whole time it hadn't been *nature.* It had been a *choice,* worn smooth by repetition until nobody could see the seam. Somebody had disguised a decision as a fact.

She filed it, on the floor, against the wall, and made herself keep listening.

"Twenty-five days," said the owl, Omen, unblinking. "That is an ordinary cycle. Twenty-four of them are three weeks of your eight — clean, countable — and then one day at the end that belongs to no week. Then the beacon rests. Then the next begins."

"The last one is different," said the elephant, Sand, slow as sediment. "The final Conjunction runs twenty-seven days. Precisely."

"And when the — when the *process ends,*" Addie said, hunting for the phrase they kept not quite saying, "what does that mean. Ends how."

A pause moved around the ring like weather.

"Reunification," said Sand. "Or not."

"Reunification with *who?*"

"That," said the moth, Lanterns, gently, and its lantern-heart dimmed a shade, "is one of the ones you have to find yourself. We won't take it from you by saying it too soon."

She let that go, because pushing on a locked door only tells the door you're weak. "Each Conjunction points at one of you," she said instead. "Fine. What did you *witness?* You're all *Witness of* something. Witness of what?"

"Of our Conjunctions," said the raven. "The whole of them. The beginning. The middle. The end. We were there for all three, every time. That is the office."

"And each one," said the boar, "turns on one of your companions. The one best suited to stand against the Witness it's aimed at."

Addie almost laughed. "So — hang on. *You* pick the person who's best equipped to *beat* you? You choose your own worst matchup? That's insane. That's the worst possible way to run a —"

"We do not choose," said Meridian, quiet.

The laugh died in her throat. "...Then who does."

The little fox stopped her sideways orbit. Under the mask, Addie could feel her looking.

"Mother," said Silence.

And somewhere in Addie's overfull back room, a soot-streaked fourteen-year-old with a bag full of geometry said, again, in his small certain voice, *Mother said. Mother said the order was very specific.*

She looked around the ring — really looked, this time, not cataloguing but *seeing* — at eighteen ancient terrible things arranged in a circle they had not drawn, running a process they did not author, aimed at each other by a hand none of them controlled.

"You're not running this," she said slowly. "Any of you. You're on the board too. You're —" the word arrived and she let it "— you're *pawns.*"

Nothing in the circle denied it. Something in one or two of them — the stag, the boar, the two-throated one — went very still, the specific stillness of a nerve touched. Not a flinch, exactly. The shape a flinch would make if it were allowed.

"The beacon tells you where you are in it," said the tempest-knight, moving them past it, and she was grateful and knew she was being *managed* and let herself be. "Green opens the first city and starts the active days. Yellow, and the second city is ready for you. Orange, the third. Red —" a low sound, like distant thunder agreeing with itself "— red opens the last city in the sequence. That is where the Witness is faced."

"Four cities to an ordinary Conjunction," said Sand. "Every one of the seventy-three appears once across the whole of it. Never twice. No city is walked to for this purpose more than a single time in the entire tale."

Addie did the arithmetic before she'd decided to. "Eighteen Conjunctions, four cities each — that's seventy-two. There are seventy-three." She looked up. "The last one has a fifth."

"She counts," said the owl, with something almost like approval.

"The final Conjunction has a fifth city," Sand confirmed. "To account for the last of them."

"And what happens in the fifth city."

"The final battle."

"—with *who,*" Addie said, and heard her own voice go thin.

"Yes," said the fox, sweetly, and offered nothing else at all.

## The Price Of Failure

"There is a thing you have to find," said the goblin-surgeon, Mercy, whose voice was precise and quiet as a scalpel set down on cloth. "In each Conjunction. A legendary object. It may be real. It may be a story. It may be a proverb, or a symbol, or a thing everyone agrees is imaginary right up until the moment it is sitting in your hand."

"So," said Addie. "Let me. Let me just say it back. I have to find a legendary object — which might be *real,* or might be a *metaphor* — on the strength of a riddle vague enough to mean a stick, a feeling, or the concept of Tuesday. That's the assignment."

"You'll be given three that could answer the riddle," Mercy allowed. "You are not sent in blind. You are sent in *narrowed.*"

"Oh, well. Three. That's —" she pressed the heels of her hands to her eyes "— that's not better, that's just three ways to be wrong."

"And the party takes up its burden," said the stag, Harvest.

"My — my *what.* You people and your *words.* What's a burden."

"Knowledge," said Harvest, evenly, "that, when accumulated, weighs on the soul. That is what the word means here. Not a load you carry in your arms. One you carry in the place that decides who you are." A pause. "Your burden."

She had no answer for the shape of that, so she said nothing, and Harvest went on.

"The first burden shows you why the second city matters — how it bears on the Conjunction you're inside. When you and yours understand *that,* fully, the beacon turns yellow. And we add to what you carry."

"Because of course you do."

"The riddle cannot be answered without the second city. When you grasp how the thing connects to —" the stag chose the words with visible care "— to *your history,* and how the object stands inside that history, the beacon turns orange."

Addie caught it. She caught it the way she caught a dropped digit in a column — a small wrongness that rang. *Your history.* Not *ours.* They kept saying *our story* about the whole enormous machine and then, at the seams, *your history,* like a border they were careful not to cross. She set it on the floor of the back room where she could find it later and did not say a word about it aloud.

"Orange opens the third city," Harvest said. "Which is where you come to understand why the object matters to something larger than the Conjunction at all. And there we add to your burden a final time. And when the true object is in hand — the beacon turns red. The last city opens. And there, you, or the one chosen for that story, faces the Witness."

"And the object kills it," Addie said.

"No."

She looked up.

"The object is not used against us," said Mercy. "It is never used against us. It is *kept.* For the final battle, and for nothing before it. What faces us down in the red city is not a weapon at all. It is the connection you've bled to make — the tissue between what was and what is and what will be. It is your understanding of the story, and of the thing, and of why that history was ever allowed to matter. *That* is what a Witness cannot stand against. Not a sword. A *comprehension.*"

Addie was quiet for a moment. Then: "This is all really fucking vague."

"Mother has worked it out," said the blue priest, Ash, and his was the steadiest voice in the ring, the human one, the one that did not echo or hiss or rumble. "Each of us knows our part. We have watched a long time. We have signed our names to it. What she asks of you is inside your power to give. We would not stand here otherwise."

"Then tell me the other end of it." She made herself say it. "If I don't. If we lose. What's the price."

The circle cooled. She felt it, an actual drop, and it was Sand who answered — the great patient elephant-scholar, slow and level and without any cruelty in it at all, which was worse.

"Complete glassing," Sand said, "of the home and seat of power of every race, every species, every people and culture that walks this world. And the seventy-third seat, the one where you govern them all. Turned to glass. All of it."

Addie's mouth had gone dry. "You just — you just said you'd destroy all seventy-three cities. On Eidolon. Every city there is."

"Oh, look," said the fox, small and pleased and utterly without warmth. "She's smart. She *does* get it."

"We would not *do* it," Ash said, sharp, a correction thrown across the ring at Silence as much as offered to Addie. "Understand the logic and stop hearing a threat. If you fail, the glassing is not something we *choose.* It is what is already coming. The thing we are here to prevent. We are not the knife at your throat, Adeshka. We are the ones trying to make sure the knife never falls." He let that settle. "Each city you walk to before the last will give visions — to whichever of your companions is the heart of that story."

"Visions of what."

"Visions," said Sand, and would not soften it. "Of what was. Of what is. Of what will be. Of things forgotten on purpose. Of things that were never permitted to happen at all. You cannot understand where the future goes until you understand, completely, where the past has been."

"We are guardians," Ash said. "Of your future. Of the future of everything that breathes down there. That is the whole of our office, under all the masks."

"And you've done it before."

"We have failed," said Ash, "many times before."

She heard the shape of it open behind the words — the *we,* the *many,* the *before* — a corridor going back and back into a dark she wasn't allowed down yet, other Addies, other rooms, other worlds glassed while eighteen ancient things watched and signed and could not stop it. She did not ask. She could feel that the question would be answered with silence, and she had had enough of silence for one lifetime, and also Silence was standing four feet away wearing a fox's face.

"And if I have questions," Addie said. "Which I will. Because half of what you've told me is a door with the handle painted on."

"You will *always* have questions," said Lanterns, kindly. "That is not a flaw in the telling. That is *why* it is told this way. The whole shape of it is questions."

"Baby steps," offered the fox.

"I am not," Addie said, with feeling, "being *baby-stepped* through the *apocalypse.* I'm sorry. I want that on the record. Whatever the record is. I am *not comfortable* with any of this."

Ash looked at her — the priest, the human face, the blue.

"Do you think *we* are?" he said.

And that stopped her, because there was no performance in it. Eighteen things older than her nation, older maybe than the sky's current arrangement, and they were standing in a circle putting the survival of every living thing on a twenty-nine-year-old with her collar on wrong and a pocket full of anxiety pellets, and they were doing it, she understood suddenly, *scared.*

"Mother built this," Ash said, "from the beginning. Knowing it came here. Knowing *you* came here — you, in this room, and eight of your companions out there in the world being gathered even now, waiting for you to come back and explain a thing you barely understand. She has been arranging toward this hour for two thousand years."

*Two thousand,* said the back room, snapping to. *Two. Not one.* Everything for the last three days had come stamped with a *thousand* — a thousand years the beacon dark, a thousand years the residual resolving to one, a thousand cities, a thousand this, a thousand that. And here, at the bottom of everything, quietly, in the mouth of the steadiest thing in the room: *two.* She did not know yet which number was the lie. She only knew, with the cold certainty of a woman who counts for a living, that one of them was, and that the gap between them was a place where something enormous was being hidden.

She said nothing about it. She had gotten very good, tonight, at saying nothing about the things that mattered most.

"You are standing here," Ash said, "which is proof enough for us that she was right where it counted. She has been *wrong* — we will not pretend otherwise; she has misjudged, and misjudged badly, and paid for it in worlds. But the convergences that had to land, landed. And here you are, standing on one of them."

"That's not faith," Addie said. "What you're describing. That's not — you keep saying faith like it's the same as closing your eyes. That's just a very long ledger with good columns."

"Yes," said Ash, and for the first time something almost like warmth moved through the blue of him. "That is *exactly* what faith is, for a thing like us. Not blindness. *Convergence.* Evidence that keeps arriving. We watched you today, Adeshka. On the steps. In front of the seventy-two. Empirical creatures can be moved by empirical things — and you were, in front of everyone, *evidence.*"

Addie looked at the floor of the room that had no floor until she'd needed one.

"Fine," she said, quietly. "Fine. I can — I can have *some* faith. A little. Provisionally. Pending further data."

Somewhere in the circle, the boar chuckled, low and pleased, like a host who has finally gotten a guest to eat.

## The First Mirror

The whole ring turned toward her at once. Not a movement — an *attention,* eighteen weights settling onto her at the same instant, and it should have been the most frightening thing yet, and somehow, after everything, it only felt like being seen.

The little fox stepped out of the circle.

She came across the not-floor in her small yellow robe, unhurried, sideways as ever, and stopped in front of Addie, and tipped the white mask up so that the carved fox seemed to consider her from a few feet away.

"I have faith in you," Silence said, and there was no giggle in it now, none at all. "More than faith. Faith is for the others, the careful ones with their ledgers. Me — I *know* you can do this."

"You don't know anything about —"

"We all saw your speech." The mask held steady. "Every one of us. From here, we could see it. And if there was one of us in this circle who still had a doubt in them when you were carried in tonight —" she tilted, considering "— you'd already said the one line that answered it. Out loud. In front of everyone who could hurt you for it."

"...Which line."

"'I honestly don't know,'" said Silence.

Addie's breath caught. She remembered saying it — on the steps, the whole city and the whole Conclave listening, when it would have been so easy, so *safe,* to pretend she had a plan.

"I said that," she said, "because it was true. I didn't have a — I *don't* have a plan, I just wasn't going to stand up there and —"

"I know." The mask tilted the other way, and under it, Addie was almost sure, the small fox was smiling. "That's *exactly* why it mattered. Anyone can promise. You told the truth to a room that wanted a lie. Do you have any idea how *rare* that is, from where we sit?"

And then Silence lifted one small hand, palm up, and held it out.

She did not reach for Addie. She did not grab. The hand simply stayed there, open, in the air between them — offered, and left offered, a question that would go on being a question until Addie answered it herself.

*Choose,* said the whole shape of it. *That's the difference. He dragged you here. They buried you in the truth. But this part — this part is yours.*

Addie thought of the people on the other side of this impossible room. Mae, somewhere back in the Conclave with her coin turned black-side up, asking who Brickett was and why a beastfolk child had no last name. Gregory, his name written into the second slot of a roster he never asked for. Major Tallandra with her feathers and her fury. Yurislav. Carrio. Rhün. The soot-streaked fourteen-year-old with the geometry and the borrowed word *Mother* and no surname of his own. Eight of them out there, and more not yet gathered, and every one of them waiting on the far side of a wall that had told her, at the very start of all this, that she was compelled to do a thing and also free to refuse it — a contradiction she still hadn't solved and was beginning to suspect was the whole point.

She looked at the small open hand.

"Are you ready," said Silence, "for the first mirror?"

"Absolutely not," Addie said.

And took her hand.

Silence's small fingers closed around hers — gentle, and final, and warm in a way nothing in this room had any right to be — and the dark at the edge of everything began to lean in, and the ring of eighteen ancient lights began to fold toward a single bright seam, the way a page folds, the way a story folds when it is about to become the next one.
