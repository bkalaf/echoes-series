## Pull Up Your Green Sleeves

No one had really spoken since they got back.

They were arranged across the wide back terrace of Mae's estate in the loose, broken shapes of people who had sat down wherever their legs gave out, and they were all, without quite meaning to be, facing the same direction: out past the garden wall to where the great beacon stood over Aquila Matara and burned its impossible yellow into the dark. The color got into everything. It lay along the porch railing and pooled in the wine no one was drinking and stained the white china and the leaves of Mae's cold-season roses and the undersides of ten stunned faces, so that they all looked faintly jaundiced, faintly unwell, lit by a thing that should not exist.

The silence had started as a choice. Now it had become a trap none of them knew how to climb out of.

The staff had been sent away — Addie had heard Mae give the order at the door, low and final, *leave us,* and even Max had gone. So it was just the ten of them, and one other: a small, fastidious kobold in a neat dark coat, perched at a side table with an open ledger, a fresh nib, and the expression of a professional who was beginning to suspect his hourly rate had not been negotiated for this.

Brynn was humming. Barely — under her breath, almost below hearing, the same handful of bars over and over, a low working-song Addie would later learn was called *Pull Up Your Green Sleeves.* It was the sound of a woman soothing herself with her hands still, and no one asked her to stop, because it was the only sound there was.

Matthieu was drawing. Whatever surface had been nearest — the back of a note, the margin of Tikket's spare paper — was filling with small furious diagrams: waves, and triangles, and three-city clusters, and long low ship-shapes, over and over, as though if he drew the impossible things enough times they might resolve into sense.

Gregory sat very still and very grey, a man who had emptied himself behind a monument and not yet been refilled. Rhün had placed himself near, not touching, just *there,* a bank of quiet warmth, ready.

And Mae stared at the beacon like she wanted to fight it. Like if she looked at it hard enough it might explain itself, or apologize.

Everyone was processing it differently, Addie thought. And every single one of them was drowning.

---

It was the kobold who broke it.

"Forgive me." He set down his pen with the small precise click of a man restoring order to a world that badly needed it. "I am Tikket. I am Lady Van'kareth's counsel, and everything said on this porch tonight sits under privilege, which is why I am here and the staff are not. But I must point something out." He folded his small clawed hands. "I am here to take notes, but I cannot read your thoughts. You are going to have to say these things out loud."

No one said anything.

"The Lady pays me by the hour," Tikket added, "so I am entirely content to sit here until dawn watching you all not-speak. But you are, every one of you, clearly in some species of distress, and whatever it is that happened to you today, you need to talk about it. And—" he offered them a small, professional, reassuring smile "—do let me reassure you. I have practiced law for a very long time. Nothing you say is going to surprise me."

Ten faces turned to look at him.

It was not hostile. It was almost gentle. But every one of them wore some version of the same expression, and the expression said, as clearly as words, *oh, you sweet man. You have no idea.*

Tikket's smile faltered, very slightly, at the unanimity of it.

Mae exhaled, and put her wine down untouched, and finally spoke.

"All right." Her voice was rough. "Let's start with the only question that matters." She looked around at all of them. "What did we just witness?"

And Addie understood, looking at the circle of them, that it had landed unevenly. Some of them had been pulled all the way down into the yellow — she had, and Mae, and Gregory who'd been sick with it — and had seen the thing whole. Others had caught only pieces, been held nearer the surface, come out with fragments and a horror they couldn't yet name. Those ones were watching the rest, waiting, dreading the download.

---

"We should tell everyone what we saw," Addie said. "All of it, start to finish, and — the rest of you stop me. Fill in what I miss. I won't have caught everything." She rubbed her eyes. "There were two visions. The first was — a shore. A dying one. There was a man giving orders. José." She paused. "And another one. Frank."

"Wait." Gregory's head came up, and for all his greyness the scholar's instinct was intact. "Do not forget, Addie. You are our continuity piece." He said it with weight, because it was true, and because it was a great deal to hang on one exhausted woman. "You're the only one of us who's been inside more than one of these. So you have to tell us — were any of those people *familiar* to you? Did you recognize anyone?"

"José," Addie said at once. "Yes. He was — he was in the first one. The council. The room with the wrong sky and the burning names. He sat at that table." She could still hear him. *People are never cargo.* "I'd know him anywhere."

"And Frank?"

"No." She was certain of it. "Frank was new. And I'd have *remembered* Frank — the voice, that accent, the Irish lilt of it, he had a whole *presence.* He wasn't at the table. He wasn't anywhere I've been." She frowned. "José carries through. He's the thread that runs between the visions. But Frank — Frank's something else. Something added on. A separate line."

"That," Yurislav said quietly, from the shadows, "seems worth remembering."

---

"There was talk first," Carrio said, picking up the thread, his eyes distant. "Before the worst of it. A conversation none of us could follow — and then something..." He gestured, helplessly. "Something *broke loose.* Somewhere. Something came apart that shouldn't have, and after that there was no more talking, only orders. Because a wave was coming to kill the city."

Tallandra's head turned. Brynn's humming went on, low.

"A *wave?*" It was one of the ones who'd surfaced with fragments — Addie thought it was Yurislav again, or Brynn. "You keep saying wave. You mean a flood? A tide?"

Brickett snorted, the reflexive laugh of a fourteen-year-old at the word. "A big scary *puddle*—"

"*No,* Brickett." Mae's voice came down like a blade, and the boy's grin died. "You do not understand. We *saw* the wave. It was enormous. You saw the Three Hundred Steps, up to the Conclave?" She held his eyes. "Imagine that. Times ten."

The humming stopped.

It stopped so completely that everyone felt it — Brynn's hands going still in her lap, the small working-song gone out of the yellow dark — and the absence of it was louder than the tune had ever been. Brickett gulped, audibly, and slid himself around behind Rhün's bulk, and Rhün, who could easily have stopped him, did not.

"Keep going," Matthieu said, not looking up from his drawing, his pen scratching fast. "Please. Before we lose the details — memory decays, and we cannot afford to lose a single piece of this. Keep going."

So they told it. The screaming on the piers. The great grey ships out on the water. And the terrible fact at the center of all of it: that the ships had *pulled away.* Cast off their moorings and driven for open water and left the thousands on the shore behind them.

---

"Then they made the right decision," Tallandra said.

The whole porch turned on her.

She did not flinch, and she did not take it back. "I understand exactly how this group feels about command, and authority, and who gets to decide," she said, evenly. "I have felt the sharp end of that from you all myself. But I am a soldier, and I am going to ask the soldier's question, because someone has to. What *else* was there to do, in that moment?"

No one answered.

"Open the ships to a panicked crowd of thousands," she went on, "and you may well lose everyone already aboard — swamped, crushed, capsized at the pier. And the ships may have been *full.* Even if they were half empty, they could not have taken everyone. So who chooses? Who stands at the rail and points — you, and you, not you? Which is the greater monstrousness: leaving them all, or picking a handful and making the rest *watch themselves be refused?*" She let that sit. "Their failure — the real one — was earlier. They should have started loading people long before it came to that. By the time they understood what was coming, it was almost certainly already too late to do anything but run." She looked around at them. "Sometimes you have to pick some. You have to pick the lesser of two evils."

Addie hated it. She hated it precisely because it was *fair* — because it took the clean, simple horror of a man abandoning a city and made it complicated, and reasonable, and human, and she could no longer stand cleanly on either side of it. Tallandra hadn't absolved José. She'd done something worse. She'd made him *understandable.*

---

They walked it forward to the end. Frank climbing into his own flying machine and lifting away. José into his — a contraption none of them had words for, a thing that rose off the drowning shore into the failing light. The ships driving at the wall of water and, impossibly, seeming to *climb* it, right at the edge of the vision, before the whole thing cut away to nothing.

And then the second vision. The throne room. And here they were on firmer ground, because this one they had understood.

They pieced it together from what each had caught — the banners, the crests, the cut of the clothes, the species and the sigils, the cities named aloud. Dovren on his throne, congratulating a founding, celebrating the *separation* of peoples. The Human cities he had named and damned for their disobedience: *Khembal. Qinyue. Skjarnheim.* And the ones he had rewarded, sourly, for kneeling: *Luminthalas. Grendhollow. Nox Vaelor.*

Tikket was writing faster now, and worse — his neat clerk's hand degrading into a scrawl as the words outpaced any professional's ability to stay calm about them. The triad shape of it hung in the yellow air, visible to all of them and understood by none: one Human city, one Mythos, one Beastfolk, bound in threes, the power run through the Human seat — and then wrenched apart, on purpose, for profit.

---

Brickett made a small sound. "Hm."

Every head turned.

"No," he said quickly, ducking. "No, go on, it's — go on."

"Brickett." Addie leaned toward him. "I have not known you long. But when you make a sound like that, you have something. *Spill it.*"

Brickett wrestled with it a moment, then let it out in a rush. "You all did notice the human cities he called out were Chinese, Norse, and West African, right?"

Silence.

"*Qinyue,*" Brickett said, ticking them off on his claws with the confidence of a boy naming a thing that was simply, obviously *there.* "That's the Chinese one. And what did *your* team find in a tavern? The Red Thread of Fate. That's the Chinese story." He moved to the next claw. "*Skjarnheim.* That's the Norse one. And what did *your* lot find? The Thread of the Norns. Norse story." He looked around at their slowly-changing faces. "Two of the three cities he punished match two of the threads exactly. And those two threads were the *wrong* ones." He got to the last claw. "But *Khembal* — Khembal's West African. It doesn't match any thread we chased. And Khembal's the city that Luminthalas *replaced.* And Luminthalas is where we were standing when we finally got it right." He shrugged, uncomfortable with the weight of the stares. "So the Red Thread and the Norns were the fakes. The decoys. And the odd one out — Khembal, and the city that took its place — that's the one that led us to the real one. Ariadne. That's all. It was just — sitting there."

Addie felt it land in her chest with a click that was half vindication and half nausea — because he was *right,* she was sure of it the instant he said it, and being right meant the false trails had been *laid,* deliberately, by something that had wanted them to fail two ways before they succeeded once.

---

"There's one more thing." Mae's voice had gone flat and careful. "About the throne room. Since we're saying things out loud."

She was still looking at the beacon.

"There was a Van'kareth in it. In the Nox Vaelor delegation. Mine. An early one — I knew the shoulders, the crest, the *way* of them, the way you know your own family across a room even a thousand years off." Her jaw worked. "And they were not *surviving,* which is the story we tell about the old ones, isn't it — that they did what they had to, that everyone did. They weren't surviving. They were *ahead* of it. Complying before they were asked. Negotiating trade routes and advantage and position while that man stood up in front of the world and called people *mongrels.*" She finally looked away from the beacon, at the group, and there was no shame in her face at all — only a cold, clean fury. "It felt like watching my family learn how to *profit* from someone else's punishment. Learn it early. Learn it well." A pause. "That's where Nox Vaelor's power comes from. That's where *mine* comes from. I built my whole life on top of it and never once looked at the foundation."

Carrio, who came from an old house himself, understood it first — Addie saw it in the tightness around his eyes, the aristocrat's private recognition of a familiar rot. Yurislav said nothing at all, and his silence had a shape to it, because a man who had been *lifted* by Dovren-shaped approval knew exactly how the poison went down sweet.

And Addie understood, watching her, that Mae was not asking to be comforted. She was not confessing. She was *reporting.* She was making the room look, with her, at the thing she had just been forced to see: that the rot was older, and deeper, and more *hers,* than anyone at this table had wanted it to be.

## Rapid Acceptance

"I feel," Gregory said, into the quiet that followed, "even more lost than I was before we saw any of it."

It was, from him, a confession of some magnitude.

"I do not know where to *go* with this. We were given so *much* — the wave, the ships, José, Frank, Dovren, the cities, the punishments, the false threads. An avalanche of it. And none of it is *pointing* anywhere. It's just — weight. Weight without a direction."

"There's got to be something in it, though," Brynn said, quiet but sure. "There always is. You just have to find the load-bearing bit."

So they went around it again — José, and the wave, and the ships, and Frank, and Dovren, and the three drowned cities and the three fattened ones, and the decoy threads. Circling. And slowly, out of the circling, one thing kept surfacing, the way the same stone keeps showing above a falling tide.

The dam.

"It's still the dam," Addie said, and felt the rightness of it settle. "Everything touches it. The river Dovren diverted. The city he drowned. The atrocity dressed up as a conjunction. The arch, the coins. It's the physical *anchor* — the one solid place where the catastrophe and the river and the city and the crime all meet in one spot of ground. We don't need to understand all of this tonight. We need to find *where the dam actually was.*"

"Which won't be where the records say," Matthieu said, finally setting his pen down. "If they misfiled the dam the way they unfiled everything else — renamed it, moved it, drowned the name along with the town — then the official location is exactly the one place it *isn't.*"

The plan that formed was small, and practical, and felt woefully inadequate against the size of what they'd seen. Split into pairs — one group of three to make the numbers work. Cycle through the cities strung along the Jadefall River, one after another. Narrow it down on the ground. Set the official records against the local stories and the shape of the land itself, and find the seam where they stopped agreeing.

It was not an answer. It was a direction, which was less, and — tonight — was all they were going to get.

"We're going in circles," Mae said at last. "And circles won't sharpen. We stop. We sleep. We start on the river in the morning."

---

Tikket had stopped writing some time ago.

He sat very small at his side table, ledger open to a page of degenerating scrawl, staring at the middle distance of a life he no longer recognized. Addie went and crouched by him.

"Are you all right?" she asked, gently.

"No," Tikket said, with the honesty of the truly overwhelmed. "No, I don't — I don't understand how you're *doing* this. Any of you. Normally. Like it's a — a *Tuesday.*" His small hands were trembling around his pen. "This is insanity. Everything we thought was true seems to be *not* true, and the — the *constellations* are talking to you, and you are all sitting here on a *porch* acting as though this is simply *normal.*"

Across the terrace, Yurislav was tucking his silver coin back into his coat. He'd been running it, Addie realized, the entire night. Now he put it away.

"Rapid acceptance," Yurislav said.

Everyone looked at him, because Yurislav did not, as a rule, offer things.

"That's how," he said, to Tikket, to all of them. "There's no other way. We either accept what's happening fast enough to *act* on it — or we fight it, and stall, and disbelieve, and we all die when they destroy the seventy-three cities." He said it without any drama at all, which was what made it land. "So we accept it. Quickly. Because the alternative isn't survivable."

"Destroy the—" Tikket's voice cracked. "Destroy the *cities?* What does he mean, destroy the—"

"You can all go to bed," Mae said, rising, and there was something almost kind in it under the exhaustion. "I will fill him in on the horrifying little detail we've left out."

One by one they went — pushing up out of their broken shapes, filing back through the tall glass doors into the warm dark of the house, leaving the yellow behind them. Addie went last of the companions, and paused at the threshold, and looked back once.

Mae had drawn a chair up beside the little kobold lawyer, and was leaning in to tell him the thing none of them had said out loud all night, and behind the two of them the great beacon burned on over the sleeping capital, staining the garden and the glass and the whole impossible world its steady, patient, waiting yellow.
