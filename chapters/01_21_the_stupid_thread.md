## The Ribbit Hole Morning

Addie woke on a floor cushion in a puddle of borrowed blankets, with no memory of choosing to sleep there, and it took her a long, foggy moment to understand where she was.

The Ribbit Hole's common room had been made, at some point in the small hours, into a nest. Couches, blankets, a drift of pillows, floor cushions scattered anywhere a body could fold — and every one of them occupied, because although the manager had offered proper rooms to all of them the night before, almost the entire company had simply gone down where they sat and not gotten up. It was not luxurious sleep. It was the other kind. The kind you fall into after a shelter night that shredded you, and a swamp that nearly finished the job, and then one strange gentle evening of talking to people who wanted nothing from you but the conversation itself — the first hours in longer than Addie could count where nobody had been braced for the next blow.

She had slept, she realized, like something that had finally been allowed to put its guard down. She hadn't known how tired she was until the tiredness let go.

She remembered pieces of the night. Talking, low and easy, with two of the young workers — Mae on one side of her, Gregory improbably thawed on the other — about nothing that mattered. About *ordinary* things. Not which city was next. Not which constellation was coming for them. Not how, exactly, one was supposed to prevent the destruction of all seventy-three cities in the world. Said plainly, inside her own head, that last one was so enormous and so absurd that she laughed silently into her blanket, and it came out closer to a wheeze.

The Ribbit Hole staff were up already, moving with a careful, anxious attentiveness that was not quite hospitality. It was the walk of people waiting for the other shoe to drop — for someone powerful to wake in a bad mood and discover they'd accidentally agreed to something they'd regret. Addie watched a young man refill a water jug he'd already filled, and felt the old familiar guilt of it: how much weight the ten of them carried into a room without ever meaning to set it down on anyone.

She started waking the others, one at a time.

Carrio was gone. That she clocked immediately. And Brickett was — also gone, she thought, with a small spike of worry, until Yurislav lifted his head off a couch arm and said, thickly, "No. He's here. I can hear him," and Brynn, from under a blanket, mumbled, "Snorin'. Somewhere. Little bastard's snorin'."

They found him by digging. He had, at some point, burrowed *under* the entire pile of cushions nearest where Addie had slept — down into them the way a beaver goes under a heap of debris, out of sight, out of the weather, thoroughly gone. When Yurislav peeled the top layer off, Brickett surfaced rumpled and squinting and deeply, personally offended to have his roof removed. Addie couldn't even laugh at him properly, because underneath the comedy was a small ache: the boy had slept so hard, buried so deep, that for one whole night he had stopped guarding every single one of them. He'd earned that. She almost put the cushions back.

Carrio still had not appeared.

---

The staff had begun, cautiously, to put The Ribbit Hole back in order for the day when one of the young men crossed the room to Addie and cleared his throat.

She recognized him — one of the workers who'd sat up late with Brickett and Carrio before she'd gone under. He looked at her directly, then not directly, then directly again.

"Do you need something from me?" Addie asked.

"No, I—" He shifted. "Everyone who needs to be present appears to be present. I think."

"I'm sorry?"

"Should — that is — may someone outside come in?" His ears were going a distinct color. "Or would you rather go to the door yourself? You should probably go to the door, Head Moderator."

Mae was already up, and had a glaive in her hand from somewhere Addie hadn't seen her keep a glaive, and announced she was coming too. They went to the door together and opened it, and found no one there — no one, at least, at eye level.

They looked down.

Standing on the step, beaming up at them with the incandescent pride of the very small, was a frogfolk child of perhaps five or six. She was tiny. She was also wearing what had that morning very clearly been a bright pink dress, and was now a bright pink dress in the way a battlefield is technically still a field: the pink survived only in the parts the mud had not yet reached, which were few. Strawberry and chocolate, Addie thought helplessly, like a layered ice with all the cream scooped out of the middle. Someone had dressed this child with love and an eye for the occasion. The child had then located the nearest mud and made her own decision about the occasion.

"How did you know we were here?" Addie asked.

The girl's grin widened.

"Papa has eyes everywhere," she said.

It was, Addie decided, a sentence that should not be that unsettling coming out of a mud-caked six-year-old, and yet.

---

The girl's gaze slid past Addie and fixed, with terrible purpose, on Mae.

"First of all," she announced, "Daddy says you get the hug."

Mae had approximately no time to process this before the child launched off the step, crossed the intervening distance at speed, and wrapped both arms fully around the Speaker of the Conclave's knees.

A truly heroic quantity of mud transferred, instantly, from dress to Mae.

Addie had seen Mae'vyri Van'kareth stare down a garrison. She had watched the woman hold a blade to a noble's throat over a towel joke and not change her breathing. She had never, not once, seen the expression that crossed Mae's face now — pure, uncut, wide-eyed *horror,* the specific paralysis of a lethal person who has absolutely no protocol for the thing currently happening to her legs.

Mae's mouth shaped words at Addie, silently. *Help me, damn it.*

Addie was no help at all. Addie had a hand pressed over her own mouth and was shaking.

"Hug her back," she managed, in a whisper.

But that was its own problem, because the child came up only to Mae's knees and was, by virtue of her grip, actively preventing Mae from bending down to reach her — so Mae stood there, arms half-raised, trapped in the geometry of affection, until she gave up and settled for patting the small muddy head with the fingertips of one hand, as one might pat something that could still, conceivably, explode.

The girl tipped her face up, immensely satisfied, and finished her sentence.

"Get the hug," she confirmed.

When at last she released Mae's knees — a span of time that Addie estimated at four seconds and Mae would later describe as a lifetime — Mae looked down at the ruin of her clothes and said, with quiet feeling, "I'm going to kill that frog."

"You," Addie whispered, "are threatening her father. In front of her."

The child did not appear to notice, or to care.

## The Stupid Thread

The girl pivoted to Addie next, chin high with the importance of a courier discharging a sacred duty.

"And *you* get the..."

She reached up under the hem of her dress, rummaged, and produced — with a flourish — an object.

Addie stared at it.

It had, perhaps, once been a sphere. It was hard to be sure. Whatever it had begun as, it was now a compacted, mangled, thoroughly loved lump of swamp: mud, and bits of reed, and, tangled all through the mess in a hopeless snarl, a great deal of red thread.

"You get the stupid thread," the girl said, holding it out with the flat honesty of the very young. "It's not a very fun toy. So you know."

Addie looked at Mae. Mae looked at Addie.

"Do you think," Addie said slowly, "that's—"

"I think," Mae said, "I'm going to go get everyone else. Right now." She was already backing toward the common room. "Because I think the moment you *touch* that thing, we're all going to find out. Aren't we."

And Addie realized, with a small cold drop in her stomach, that Mae was right. If it was the true thread — the actual Ariadne's Thread, come to them at last not through archives or brothels or tavern theology but through a six-year-old's toy chest — then touching it should do exactly one thing.

It should turn the beacon.

---

The companions came out of The Ribbit Hole in a straggling, blinking line, and each of them, on seeing what the child was holding, arrived at a fresh and visible new level of disbelief.

"Is that—"

"Do you think that's actually—"

"What in the *world*—"

And then Carrio appeared.

He came out last, and he looked, frankly, like death that had been left out in the rain. Hungover to his back teeth, hood down, moving with the tender caution of a man whose skull had betrayed him. He took in the scene — the beaming muddy child, the snarl of thread — and simply nodded, as though a small tragedy had been confirmed.

"Figured Tallowmire had it," he rasped. "There was something about him. That whole night, in the swamp — he was *guarding* something. Something precious. I couldn't tell what." He winced at the daylight. "Couldn't tell if it was the string or the kid, honestly." He gestured limply at the mess in the girl's hands. "Apparently. The string."

"*Thread,*" said Gregory.

"String," said Carrio. "Same thing."

Gregory drew breath to explain, at length, that it was categorically not the same thing, then sighed the sigh of a man conserving his strength for larger battles and let it go.

Addie smiled at the little girl, who was glowing under all the attention. Above them, along the front of The Ribbit Hole, staff had begun leaning out of windows to watch. On the street, a few passersby had stopped, and were pointing, and murmuring, and Addie caught the edge of one voice asking another, quite reasonably, *why is there a child at the brothel—*

"*Fuck* my life," Addie said, with feeling.

Then, immediately, crouching to the girl's level: "I'm — I'm so sorry. Don't tell your papa I said that. Please."

---

The girl, undeterred, pushed the muddy lump toward Addie again. She wanted it *taken.* She had a job to do and she intended to complete it.

Addie took a breath, and reached out, and closed her hand around the stupid thread.

The ground rumbled.

It was slight — a shiver up through the stilts and the boardwalk, felt in the soles more than heard — and then, above them, out over the wetland, the great beacon *pulsed,* and its color deepened, and shifted, and set.

Orange, into red.

Nobody moved for a second. There it was. Confirmation, delivered by a six-year-old in a wrecked party dress: this filthy tangle was Ariadne's Thread, and the final checkpoint had just turned over, and the last light before the end of the road was now burning red across the whole city.

The child, meanwhile, took the turning of a cosmic beacon as her cue to relatch, joyfully, onto Mae's knees.

Mae's face did something complicated.

Everyone, helplessly, began to laugh.

"*Moderator,*" Mae said, with enormous dignity and mud to the shin, "please remove the small child from my legs so we can return back to my manor and discuss our next steps." She swept the whole snickering company with a flat stare. "And if any one of you ever says a single word about this again, there will be consequences."

The threat landed with somewhat less than its usual weight, on account of the child still being attached to her.

Mae looked down. She patted the small head, one more time, and something in her cracked toward the human.

"Okay," she said, strained. "Okay. Thank you so much. You're very sweet. God damn it, Addie."

## Mirefang Delay

They did not get out of Pollyr Fen quickly.

There was some snarl at the Portalport — a guard matter, something down by Mirefang, a name that meant nothing to Addie and drifted through the transit hall as so much overheard noise, delays passed mouth to mouth in the queue. So they waited, and while they waited, they read.

The news was everywhere: on the criers' boards, in the broadsheets, murmured along the benches. The cities were declaring. More than half had done it already, and the count was climbing toward two-thirds even as they stood there — most of Concord in, most of Ruin in, the allegiances hardening as the beacons marched toward whatever they marched toward.

Schism, of course, was the holdout.

*Of course they are,* Addie thought, with a tired fondness. She'd wager half the Schism cities wouldn't send word at all — not out of defiance, necessarily, but because declaring meant *filling out the form,* and no one in Schism had ever once, in the history of the faction, wanted to fill out the form.

Two Concord cities remained undeclared. Only two. Pollyr Fen — this one, the drowned and grieving one they were standing in, which had rather a lot on its mind. And Vyrath'Kai. The dragon home city. VKI.

Addie folded the paper and felt the size of it settle on her properly for the first time. It was Day Seventeen. The beacon was red. Eight days remained to work out how, precisely, one survived a thing called Horror — and the red light meant it had stopped being a problem for later. It was the problem for *now.*

## Sandwiches In The Rain

Aquila Matara was raining.

They came through the terminal and out into a grey, steady downpour, and by the time they reached Mae's estate any thought of the back porch had drowned, so they took lunch inside, in the dining room, with the rain running down the tall windows.

Max served it looking mildly affronted, not at them, but at the circumstances. He had not planned for the household to be home, and so there had been no time for anything proper, and lunch was, therefore, sandwiches. Good sandwiches, served with impeccable disappointment. A house of this name did not serve sandwiches; in all his tenure it never had; and that it was doing so now, with the world’s great clock running red in the windows, plainly struck Max as the truer catastrophe — not the monster at the end of the maze but the collapse of a standard, the day the household finally could not rise to the occasion because the occasion had become the end of everything. He set the platter down the way a man lays out battlefield casualties. “I had not planned for apocalypse lunch,” he said. “This is not our standard.” It was a strange, flat, domestic hour — an anticlimax so complete it almost felt like a joke: the beacon had turned *red,* the whole world was choosing sides, a monster waited at the end of the maze, and here they sat, damp and quiet, eating sandwiches while the rain came down.

Mae was not among them. Mae was somewhere in the depths of the house, waging private war on the mud that had, she was certain, worked itself into every crack and crevice she possessed.

But the room, for all its anticlimax, had a new quality to it. For the first time since the scrolls had lit, the companions could actually stop, and sit, and turn their minds to the thing at the end of the road. To Horror.

---

"We should just get it over with," Rhün said, around a sandwich. "Go and face the thing. Now, or close to it." He shrugged his great shoulders. "And if we lose — well. Maybe the whole arrangement gives us more than one try at it. These things sometimes do."

"I don't think we'll be that lucky," Brickett said.

He said it quietly, and without his usual bounce, and it landed the way his quiet things always did. He was turning a crust over in his paws, not eating it.

"I think," he went on, "Addie should get ready to go in alone. I can — I can kind of *see* it. The way the last few have worked. I think when it comes to Horror, the thing's going to shut the rest of us out and it'll just be her." He didn't look up. "No do-overs. No spares. Her."

Nobody rushed to disagree, which was its own answer.

"Then I'll train you," Yurislav said to Addie. "Before it comes to that. What I can teach in eight days, I'll teach."

"It will be difficult," Matthieu observed, gently, "to dodge four short swords with a single staff."

"I'll train her anyway." Yurislav met Addie's eye, and there was no comfort in it, only honesty. "And I'll tell you now — the lessons are going to hurt. If they don't, they're useless."

"Which is precisely why the better use of the time," Gregory cut in, "is *research.* We have the thread now — the actual object. We still don't fully understand the city-state connections, the founding structure, why any of it was tied to Ariadne in the first place. There could be more in it. Instructions. A method. Something we're meant to *do* with it, not just carry—"

"Agreed," said Matthieu, "the thread may not be inert—"

And then everyone was talking at once. Combat training over research. Research over combat training. What the city allegiances meant. Whether Horror would let anyone stand beside Addie at all. Whether Addie could possibly survive it alone if it wouldn't. Whether the filthy tangle in her pocket had secret instructions knotted into it. The voices climbed over one another, faster and louder, eight days' worth of dread converting itself into noise across a plate of Max's sandwiches.

---

From somewhere down the hall, the arguing reached Mae.

Addie heard her before she saw her — heard the approach of a woman who had spent the last hour digging swamp out of her person and had returned to find her dining room dissolved into ten people shouting strategy at each other over the fate of the world.

Mae appeared in the doorway. She took it in for exactly as long as it took to take in.

"Nope," she said. "Nope nope."

And she turned around and walked back out.

Addie put her head in her hands, there at the table, the rain running down the windows and the red light of the beacon somewhere beyond it, and the whole impossible circus roaring on around her.

*This,* she thought, *is going to be a long eight days.*
