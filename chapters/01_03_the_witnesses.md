# The Witnesses

## The Scene Between Stories

Witness Space (Metaphysical)
Dravenkar, 1 Hearthwake
Subjective time — immediately after the nomination is signed

There was a darkness that gathered rather than fell.

It thickened in a place that had no floor, and yet a floor was implied by the split that ran down the center of it — a vertical seam, black on blacker, that breathed. Not air. Something older than air. Every so often the seam drew in and let out a wet, scraping sigh, patient as tide, and each sigh was measured: one hundred and forty-four seconds, exactly, between one and the next. A clock kept this place, though no clock hung here. Time was the only architecture the dark permitted.

The Witness of Horror knew what to call it. She had named it long ago, in the soft sibilant voice that unspooled from somewhere inside her pitch-black shape — a great Arachne bulk of folded limbs and too many joints, huge and mournful and gleaming like wet obsidian, frozen at the northern edge of the seam where the logic of the room had pinned her.

"The thcene," she whispered, the lisp catching on the word the way it always did, "between stories." She could not move. None would move until the chain was complete.

The clockwork clicked.

One hundred and forty-four seconds, and a second split tore open in the dark, and a second Witness was drawn through it and locked into place along an unseen track. Then another. Then another. Revel came first of the second wave — a white treant, branches lifted as if mid-laugh, bark pale as bone; then the Crown, an angelic silhouette all edges and judgment, a shape built to sit at the head of a tribunal; then Stone, a green giant of fused monoliths, grinding softly as it settled; then Mercy, a small black goblin surgeon, alchemist's satchel at its hip, hands too clever and too red.

Click. Another one hundred and forty-four seconds. Tempest arrived in a rumble — a red draconic knight wreathed in the memory of storms; and Dominion and Veils, one being under two heads, red bleeding into blue where the two argued over a single throat; and Tides, a green walrus wanderer heavy with the pull of far water; and Echoes, a golden raven whose feathers were pages, an archivist of everything anyone had ever failed to forget.

Click, and click, and click. The last wave filled the ring. Harvest, a pale gold stag, antlers hung with the season. Lanterns, a moth-keeper of pitch-black wings, small cold lights swinging from its hands. Hearth, a great red boar, host of an empty table. Sand, a green elephant scholar, its skin written over in the names of the dead. Omen, a blue owl, both eyes open on futures that had not chosen yet. Meridian, a minotaur-sage, half red and half blue, a mind like a lattice. Ash, a human priest lit from within in brilliant blue. Bastion, a white rhino, a wall that had learned to grieve.

Seventeen. Seventeen links fixed along the seam like statues carved from light and old decisions, each one a shape and a color and a weight of everything it had once chosen. The room held its breath through the final revolution.

Then the eighteenth shadow split — not at the edge, but at the very heart of the seam, the epicenter — and something small stepped out.

A fox-kit. Tiny, yellow as a lit window, no larger than a real child, wearing a smooth porcelain mask with two dark slots for eyes. Silence walked to the center of the assembled circle, sat down cross-legged on the floor that was not there, and set her small palms flat on her knees.

Stasis snapped.

It broke over all of them at once, the way an ice shelf breaks — a crack you feel in the teeth before you hear it — and eighteen ancient things came unfrozen and remembered, all in the same instant, that they had feared this.

Ash was first to find his voice, and the blue light of him wavered with it. "Sister." He said it to the fox-kit, gentle and horrified. "Silence. Why are we *here?* We were to dream. We agreed to dream, all of us, until Reunification — to sleep and wake at the shore and not before. There are children depending on us. There are children who will *die* if we are not where we swore we'd be." His voice cracked upward. "What did you *do?*"

Silence tilted her masked head. When she answered, her voice was a child's and was not a child's, light dancing on water a mile deep. "You're standing in the room, Ash," she said. "If you're standing in the room, then you already have the answer. You just don't like it yet."

The circle erupted.

Tempest's storm-voice rolled over Hearth's bellow, and the boar's over the treant's, all of them speaking the same sentence in eighteen registers — *this was not the timeline, this was not the plan, we were promised the shore* — and the not-floor shook with the weight of very old, very frightened power arguing with itself.

The command that cut through it came from *outside* the eighteen tracks.

A ridge of shadow rose at the eastern edge of the circle — a place nothing was supposed to be able to enter — and hardened, and became a shape: an elderly figure, rat-kin, stooped, wrapped in robes of black and purple and tired gold. He stepped over a boundary that a moment ago had been absolute, and set foot in their secure and sacred and sealed room as though he had every right.

Eighteen Witnesses went stone-still. Not from stasis. From the older animal fear of a stranger standing inside the one place that was supposed to be safe.

Only Silence did not flinch. She looked up at the intruder through the slots of her mask, and something almost like fondness moved in her small body.

"It's all right," she said to the circle, though nothing about it was. "This one, I know. This one, once, we would all have trusted with our lives." She inclined her head to him. "Say hello. They've forgotten your face."

The old rat spread his hands, and there was apology in the gesture, and exhaustion far beneath it. "I'm new to this," he admitted. "The role. The whole — apparatus. They brought me in moments ago, blinking, and read me my duties off a page with the ink still wet." A dry, humorless sound. "But I've been brought current. And I'm here to explain the addendums."

## The Addendums

"There are no addendums." Meridian's minotaur voice was pure reason, red and blue braided into one flat refusal. "The script does not have addendums. The script is *finished.* Who altered it? Who reached into a closed agreement and *wrote* in it?"

"The Master," the Overseer said, and let the title sit, and did not decorate it. "The compression is this. Eighteen Conjunctions. Not spread across an age as you were told — one after another after another, rapid, the last of them falling just before Reunification. That is the new shape of the road."

"*Eighteen consecutive,*" Revel said, and the treant's branches shuddered. "With no recovery between? No fallow season to knit ourselves back together? Overseer, that doesn't place us for success. That tears us apart and scatters what's left."

"Quiet, Revel." Silence did not raise her voice, and did not need to. "Let him finish."

The Overseer finished. "The brutality is the point. It is the only arrangement — the only one, out of every path that was tried — that sets every piece, yourselves included, into the exact place it must occupy for this to work." He looked around the ring, and for a moment the tiredness in him was almost tenderness. "You were always going to be spent. This spends you in the correct order."

And the terrible thing was that the circle softened. Not because the words comforted them. Because they were still, after everything, loyal to a plan they had loved — and to the one who had made it.

Ash asked the question the rest of them were circling.

"Where is Mother?"

The room swallowed it whole. The seam breathed once, wet and slow, into the silence that followed.

"Mother made this place," Silence said. "Every seam of it. She—"

"That is not what I asked you." Ash's blue light had gone very steady, which was worse than shaking. "I asked where she *is.*"

The Overseer answered, because Silence would not. "She cannot be here," he said. "Not the way you expected her. Not — here."

Mercy's clever red hands went still at its sides. "Then whom do you speak for, old man? If not her — for whom?"

"No one," the Overseer said. "Not exactly."

"That is not an acceptable answer," the Crown said, and its edges brightened toward inquisition. "That is not an answer at all—"

"Give me the floor." Silence had risen to her feet, small and yellow in the center of them. She said it to the Overseer, not the circle.

He gave it to her at once, and stepped back into his ridge of shadow, and looked almost relieved to be rid of it.

The playfulness left her. It did not vanish — it never fully vanished — but it drew back like a tide, and underneath it was something so old the room grew colder to hold it. "Mother sat with me," Silence said, "for what I *thought* was a very long time. She walked me through the parts of the story she made deliberately impossible to read. The obtuse parts. The cruel parts. The parts none of you were ever supposed to have to understand, so that you could sleep."

Sand, who was the memory of them all, lifted its written head. "*Thought,*" the elephant said quietly. "You said *what you thought was.* You don't know how long it was."

"No," Silence agreed. "None of us do. That's rather the point of a dream, isn't it. You can't trust the clock inside it." She let that land. "The road she chose is bold. It is insane. And it is the only one that ends anywhere but the dark. I need you to hold both halves of that at once."

"What happened to us?" Ash's voice had gone to a whisper. "Silence. Look at us. Look at what we *are* now. What did she do to us?"

"Nothing." Silence turned slowly in the center of the ring, taking in each monstrous, luminous shape. "This room doesn't show you as Mother sees you. It doesn't show you as the children will see you, either — that comes later, and it will be its own cruelty. It shows you," she said, "as *you* see yourselves." She spread her small arms to the whole assembly. "That's what a Conjunction is, under everything. A mirror held up to the soul. And this is only the first, and you are already flinching from the glass."

They looked. They could not help but look — at the spider huge with old dread, at the two-headed thing at war with its own throat, at the surgeon whose hands would not come clean, at the boar presiding over a table with no guests. Each of them saw the shape their own judgment had cut for them, and not one of them could say it was wrong.

"No." Ash refused it the way the drowning refuse water. "We agreed to *preservation.* To be kept. To wake at the shore, whole, and begin again. That was the covenant."

"We agreed to be *useful,*" Silence said, and let the word draw blood. "That's the truer wording, and you know it. Preservation was the promise. Usefulness was the price." A beat, softer. "I agreed to it too, Ash. I agreed to all of it, so hate me for saying it if you have to, but hate me knowing I signed the same page."

Omen's blue owl-eyes, which saw forward, narrowed on the fox-kit. "This isn't the only change," the oracle said slowly. "You wouldn't wear that face for a scheduling addendum. What else. What are you *not* saying."

Silence turned toward the frozen spider at the northern edge of the ring.

"The Deceiver is alive," she said.

The seam breathed. Nothing else did.

"That's impossible." Omen's wings flared. "We *saw* him die. All of us. We watched it happen."

"If he's back—" Revel's branches had gone rigid, all the laughter gone out of the treant "—if the Deceiver is truly back, then we don't stand a chance. None of us. Not the children, not the companions, not—"

"If Mother knew," Dominion and Veils said, both heads at once for once in agreement, red and blue braided into a single grim certainty, "if she knew he had returned and built this road *anyway* — then the road accounts for him. Then we trust her. That is what trusting her *means.*"

"She knew." Silence's voice did not rise. "And to walk this road, she has to break every one of us. Herself first, and hardest, and last of all." She began to move around the circle again, small and inexorable. "You'll each take the center in your turn. One Conjunction, one of you, like players walking on stage when the light finds them. And the star of it — the one Mother has set every hope she has left upon — is a woman below who does not yet know she is anyone at all." She stopped. "For it to work, you must go to her as monsters. Not because Mother thinks you are. Because, judged by what you did, and what you allowed, and what you preserved, and what you *still* make possible by existing — that is the honest word for you. Monster. Wear it."

"Then we must accept being *hated,*" Hearth said, and the boar's voice was ash and grief.

"No." Silence shook her masked head. "You must accept being *defeated.* That's the rule of the kind of story this has to be. Monsters are defeated. Every one of you will lose, on purpose, in your turn — so that the ones who beat you walk away stronger for it. The companions are being sharpened. All eight of them, one blade at a time, against the day they face the Deceiver and there is no losing on purpose left to do."

"Nine," said the Overseer, from his shadow.

The word dropped into the ring like a stone into a well.

"Eight," Silence said, without turning.

"The count I was given is nine," the Overseer said, mild and immovable. "Nine companions. And one of the nine is a replacement — a seat that must be filled by someone not yet standing in it."

"There are eight." Silence's small shoulders had gone very still. "We'll take the argument up another time, old friend, when it matters and not before."

The Overseer looked at the back of her porcelain mask for a long moment. Then he inclined his head, and chose — visibly, deliberately — not to press it.

## The Ballot

Witness Space (Metaphysical)
Dravenkar, 1 Hearthwake
Subjective time — immediately after the nomination is signed

Silence lifted one small hand, and the void filled with paper.

They came out of the dark like a blizzard — thousands upon thousands of white ballots, each one stamped with the sigil of a guild, wheeling and drifting through the ring of Witnesses in a slow storm of institutional intent. "These," Silence said, walking through the falling paper untouched, "are the nominations. One from every guild in Erzengel that put forward a name for the seat. Every frightened, ambitious, desperate choice made tonight, in a thousand rooms, under a noon deadline." She held out a palm and let a ballot settle on it. "Their answer to the question Mother asked without asking it."

"Are they real?" Meridian's lattice-mind turned on the storm. "These choices. Are they *actual* — freely made — or are we watching theater?"

"Some of them," Silence said.

"That is not an answer. Are the ballots real."

"Some were written by hand," Silence said, letting the one on her palm lift away again. "Some by habit. Some by fear, which writes a great many things people later call their own idea. And some—" she watched a single ballot turn in the air "—some were written by mathematics, a long time ago, before the hands that signed them were ever born. You asked."

"*Silence.*" Ash said only her name, but he said it like a warning.

"You wanted honesty from me tonight." The fox-kit's masked face tilted, and the playfulness flashed back, too bright, a child poking a wound to see. "Now you flinch from that glass too? Make up your minds what you can bear."

She swept her hand, and the storm collapsed. Ten thousand ballots folded back into the dark all at once — and where a blizzard had been, nine white slips remained, hanging in the air over the center of the ring.

Silence pointed to one of the nine. There was no play in it now. Her small arm came up and leveled at a single ballot with an intent so total the other eight seemed to dim around it.

"That one," she said. "Above all the others. Whatever else falls, whatever else fails — that one matters."

And then, unexpectedly, she softened, and turned to face the circle of monsters she had assembled, and for a moment she was only very small and very old and very tired.

"I haven't seen you," she said, "in such a long time. All of you. I'd forgotten." Her masked face moved from shape to shape, spider to treant to giant to owl. "It will be better to see you again at the *end* of this — when it's finally done, and the glass shows something kinder. I would like that. I would like to see your true faces once more before the dark."

"And until then?" Sand asked, low and patient, the elephant's written skin catching the light. "What are we to do until then?"

Silence looked up.

Above them, the ceiling of nothing softened, and something like stars bled faintly through — the ghost of a sky, or the promise of one.

"We keep our places," she said.

"In the room?" Revel asked.

"Not only in the room."

Omen's owl-eyes closed, then opened, understanding arriving like grief. "We *watch,*" the oracle said.

"We watch." Silence's voice was almost gentle. "Everything we built, and everything the children make of it. Every kindness they manage. Every cruelty they excuse. Every story that rots, and every story that flowers out of the rot. We hold our places, and we bear witness, and we do not look away — because looking away is the one thing we no longer have the right to do."

At the northern edge, the frozen spider stirred, and Horror's soft, ruined whisper crossed the ring.

"Monsters," she said.

"Witnesses," Silence answered.

"There is a difference." She said it to Horror, and to all of them, and perhaps most of all to herself. "There *has* to be a difference. It is the only thing standing between what we are and what he is. Say it with me. Answer the roll."

And the roll call, which had begun a thousand years ago as a formality, became a vow — each name spoken into the dark, each shape answering with the last free choice it would make for a long age.

Horror answered. Revel answered. The Crown. Stone. Mercy. Tempest. Dominion and Veils, both throats at once. Tides. Echoes. Harvest. Lanterns. Hearth. Sand. Omen. Meridian. Ash — last of the great ones, and slowest, and surest. And Silence, in the center, closing the ring: *witness.*

The light began to contract.

It drew backward into the seams, drawn thin, and the eighteen luminous shapes went with it — dissolving into the black edges of the room that was not a room, or upward into the false and fading stars, or sideways into some elsewhere the eye refused to hold. One by one the mirrors emptied. The circle unmade itself.

Until only the old rat remained.

The frame of the moment dropped low, and close, past the empty tracks, down to the single white ballot still hanging in the dark where Silence's finger had left it — the one that mattered above all others. It turned, slow, until its face showed.

**ADESHKA H. SONNTAG.**

The Overseer crossed the empty floor and took it out of the air. He held it a moment in his old gray hands. Then he folded it, once and carefully, along a crease he took some trouble to make clean, and slid it inside his coat, over the place a heart would be.

"We're counting on you, girl," he muttered, to no one, to the paper. "Saints help us. We're counting on you."

## The First Shot

Dravenkar, 1 Hearthwake
Subjective time — immediately after the nomination is signed

The Overseer did not get up.

He stayed where he had knelt to take the ballot, folded down in the cold center of nothingness, and turned his old hands over and stared at them as though they belonged to someone he had known a long time ago and mostly disliked.

"You dragged me up out of a comfortable death," he told the dark — told the one he knew was still there, listening, though she had let no one see her all this while. "Warm, it was. Quiet. Finished. And you reached in and hauled me back into these tired bones and stood me in front of eighteen people who trusted me once, so that *I* could be the one to tell them what you'd done to them. And now you'll make me go down there and explain the rest of it to *her.*" He shook his gray head. "You always did save the worst errand for whoever couldn't say no to you. Old girl."

From the silence, unseen, Mother watched him.

And Mother smiled.

It was not a cruel smile, though it was not a soft one either. It was the smile of a woman who has run the same impossible sum ten thousand times and finally, tonight, likes the answer.

She had waited a long age to move a single tangible piece. Now she moved one.

Far off — impossibly far, in a direction the room did not contain — something silent broke, all at once, into a great many pieces. It made no sound, because it had never been the kind of thing that could. It simply stopped being whole.

And Mother missed.

She had aimed, and she had fired, and she had *missed* — and anyone watching, had anyone been permitted to watch, might have thought the old girl's hand had finally failed her after so long asleep.

They would have been wrong. Some first shots are not thrown to strike the thing they point at. Some are thrown to be *seen* — to measure the wind, to mark the range, to nudge a trajectory a hair's width so that everything downstream of it arrives, in the fullness of time, exactly where it was always meant to land. A warning. A ranging shot. A correction written into the future in a hand too small to read yet.

What she had aimed at, and what she had struck instead, and what would come of the striking — none of that surfaced in the dark of the scene between stories. It would keep. It would wait for a young woman with ruined hair and a signed ballot she never asked for to walk out into a changed morning and feel, without understanding, that the ground beneath her had been quietly moved an inch to the left.

For that was the whole of the calculation. Everything Mother had spent — the war she had started with a blanket, the peer she had raised from the dead, the eighteen she was about to break — all of it bent toward a single narrow window: a woman on a set of steps, in front of a frightened nation, opening her mouth to say a true thing out loud. The speech that would shake Erzengel to its foundations and start the last clock running down toward Reunification.

Mother settled back into her silence, and her absence, and her sixteen dwindling months.

She had chosen the one who would give that speech. She had turned it over every way there was to turn it.

She trusted, now, that she had chosen right.
