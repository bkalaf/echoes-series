## Terminal Split

The crowd did not so much disperse as *evaporate.*

Brimscale came down off the gate at a heavy, unbothered amble, and the front rank of reporters discovered urgent business several streets away; Ashwing dropped off the parapet and drew one long, lazy circle over the avenue, wings trailing banked fire, and the rest of them remembered that they were made of paper and skin. Within a minute the great press that had besieged Mae's estate all morning had thinned to nothing, and the nine companions walked out into open street like the last people left after a fire drill.

Addie watched Rhün the whole way. He didn't look at the fleeing crowd, didn't so much as glance at the recording-lenses being hastily packed away — he had eyes only for the two enormous creatures, a hand flat against Brimscale's shoulder, a low word for Ashwing that Addie couldn't hear and the drake plainly could. With the beasts he was easy, fluent, unhurried. It was only people he went stone around. She filed it, and did not pry at it, because she had a feeling the shape of that particular wound was not hers to open.

---

The Portalport was all noise and motion — travelers, hawkers, the whir and clank of the courier system overhead, brass tubes rattling along their rails and dropping eye-mail canisters into sorting bins with a *thunk.* The teams gathered at the departure arch to split.

One of the courier tubes jumped its rail.

It was nothing — a canister that overshot its bin and swung out over the concourse on its brass arm, wobbling toward the group at roughly knee height, entirely harmless, already slowing.

Brickett did not see a canister. Brickett saw *death.*

"MY LIFE FOR YOU!" he bellowed, and flung himself bodily between Addie and the wayward tube, small arms thrown wide, and a reflexive spark leapt off his paws — which, instead of producing the defensive fireball he had clearly envisioned, produced a puff of grey smoke, a smell of scorched fur, and a fresh singe on his own sleeve. The canister bonked gently off his shoulder and swung back to its rail, unharmed and uninterested.

Brickett, carried by his own momentum, hit Addie squarely in the ribs.

"*Oof*—" She folded around him, wheezing, and set him back on his feet with both hands. "I appreciate the sentiment," she managed, once she had air again. "I do. But I would very much like to survive your devotion, Brickett, that's — that's twice now."

"It looked *very murdery,*" Brickett insisted, patting his smoking sleeve.

"It was the post," said Gregory, who had already put out an arm a half-second too late — as had Rhün, and Tallandra, all three of them beginning, Addie noticed, to reflexively brace whenever anything moved near her. The pattern was training itself into the whole group.

"Teams," Addie said, still rubbing her ribs. "Before he defends me from a bread cart. Go."

And they split — Matthieu, Yurislav, and Tallandra for Aes Sidhal; Gregory, Brickett, and Rhün for the White Tower; and Addie, Mae, and Carrio down toward Luminthalas and a dwarf who did not want to be found.

## The Bountiful Harvest

Luminthalas was a city built in tiers, and the wealth all lived at the top.

Team Three came out of the local port and went *down* — down stairs and switchback ramps and soot-black lanes, and as they descended, the light went with the altitude. Far overhead, the high-elf quarter had strung its skybridges and hanging gardens right across the sky, so that here, at the bottom, at the flat noon of the day, it was permanently the grey-blue of late dusk. Addie pulled her tunic tighter against a cold that had nothing to do with the season and everything to do with never getting the sun.

"When we find her," she said. "How do you want to handle it?"

"Watch first," Mae said. "Find her baseline — how she stands, who she trusts, what she's afraid of losing. Then offer her the thing she values instead of the thing we want. Everyone has a lever. Most people hand it to you in the first two minutes if you're paying attention."

Carrio said nothing. He was scanning the lane-mouths and storefronts with the flat, continuous attention of a man who'd been raised to expect knives, working a piece of gum, slow and even.

The Bountiful Harvest was, of course, almost impossible to find — the lower quarter was a maze of unmarked courtyards and hand-lettered signs gone illegible with soot, narrow enough in places to touch both walls. They found it by the line: a long, patient, packed queue of working people, wrapped twice around a little courtyard, waiting for a table.

And over the door, where a proper sign should have been, someone had nailed up a fresh board bearing a large, crude, unmistakable drawing of a hand with a single finger raised, and beneath it, in furious black letters, two words:

**FLYING SCROLLS.**

Addie read it, read the gesture that supplied the words the sign left off, and felt the first flicker of something like fondness for a woman she hadn't met.

"That's not the restaurant's name," Mae observed.

"No," Addie said. "That's a woman who has received one too many magical letters from people she's done with, and has decided to say so. Loudly. In pictures." She looked at the line, and the sign, and the soot. "She thinks the Moderator scrolls are from her family. Whatever they did to her, she'd rather flip off the sky than open one." A beat. "We're going to have to be very careful, and very kind, and probably very fast."

---

Mae started for the front of the line.

Addie brought the ironwood staff across in front of her — not hard, just *there,* a bar at waist height. "No."

"We are on a clock that ends in the destruction of cities," Mae said, with genuine bewilderment, "and you want me to queue for *soup.*"

"I want you to understand where you are." Addie kept her voice low, for Mae alone. "Up top, a place in line is an inconvenience. Down here, it might be the only thing a person owns today that nobody can take. It's the one bit of the world that's fair — you came, you waited, you're closer than the person behind you, and *no one* gets to change that with a title. You cut this line and you don't just insult them. You confirm the thing they already believe about people who look like us." She let the staff drop. "And practically? They'll close it up like a wound. Every one of them will defend their spot to the death, just to prove they can, and we'll be slower than if we'd waited. Trust me. I have stood in a great many of these lines."

Carrio's jaw stopped for a moment. "Patience," he admitted, "is not a virtue my family cultivated. But I take your point." The gum resumed. "We wait."

They waited.

The cover lasted about four minutes.

It was not, as Addie had assumed, the jewelry. She'd caught people staring and murmured to Mae that they'd clocked the noble earrings, and Mae had actually *laughed* — a short delighted bark — and said, "Oh, Scholar. They are not looking at Carrio's ears."

"You gave a speech to a hundred thousand people yesterday," Carrio said, "on a broadcast that has been playing in a loop across seventy-two cities ever since, most recently accompanied by a towel. You don't have a private face anymore. That's gone. I'm sorry — it doesn't come back."

And that was when the nobles found her.

Three of them, highborn elves, drunk at midday and slumming in the lower tiers for the thrill of it, and one caught sight of the cyan hair and lit up with the specific cruelty of a bored man handed a target. "It's the *towel Moderator!*" he announced to the courtyard. "Tell us, sweetheart, is the cyan all *over,* or just up t—"

Two things happened at once, and both of them were too much.

Mae's coin vanished and a blade she'd had somewhere near her wrist was suddenly *not* near her wrist, and Carrio's hood had flared and his hand had come up with the fluid, practiced economy of a man reaching for a vial, and Addie realized, with a lurch of pure dread, that her two most dangerous companions were about to open a highborn noble's throat in a soup line, in public, over a *towel joke,* within fifteen minutes of arriving.

"*Stop,*" she hissed, and got a hand on each of them, and the moment held on a knife's edge — but a scene had already been made, and the whole courtyard had turned to look.

## The White Tower / Aes Sidhal Intercuts

Three tiers up and a world away, Gregory H. Frydrake had read forty-one volumes and found forty-one kinds of nothing.

He'd worked the ancestral logbooks, the old legal registers, the indexed myth-concordances, every thread-lore reference the White Tower held — the Red Thread, the Norn-strand, the Ariadne cycle — and every trail ran into the same wall: citations to sources that no longer existed, cross-references to volumes never copied, a scholarly apparatus pointing confidently at empty shelves. He adjusted the spectacles he did not need and turned his bracelet round his wrist, twice, which was the closest Gregory Frydrake came to swearing.

Behind him, Brickett had constructed a small fort out of what appeared to be irreplaceable etymological dictionaries and was eating a hand-pie inside it. Rhün stood at the window with his back to the stacks, radiating the particular boredom of a being who found dead paper less interesting than almost anything else in existence.

"This is a *reference room,*" Gregory said, with the strained patience of a man near the end of it, "not a kitchen, not a paddock, and not a *kennel* for fire-drakes. Brickett, those dictionaries are older than the city—"

Brickett, anxious, had begun to worry a small spark between his paws.

"*No.*" Gregory was across the room faster than Addie would have believed him capable of moving, real horror in his voice. "No open flame. I mean it. There is not a librarian in this tower who would not have all three of us *executed,* lawfully and with paperwork, for a single ember in this room."

The spark died. Brickett folded his paws. "It's not in the books," he said, after a moment. Not sulking — thinking. "You keep looking *in the books.* But there's no starting spot, is there? No — coordinate. You can't find the middle of a story if nobody wrote down where it starts. The books are the wrong *box* for the question."

"Records exclude things on purpose," Rhün said, from the window, not turning around. "The polished ones especially. But a thing that got left out of the register doesn't stop being *true.* Beast-handlers know things no naturalist ever wrote down. Soldiers keep songs that are more accurate than the battle reports. Old ugly folktales carry the parts the official history was too embarrassed to keep." He shrugged, a slow grind of banked stone. "Sometimes the truth survives in the places nobody thought were worth guarding."

"We were assigned to *research,*" Gregory said. "Not to go drinking in taverns collecting ballads."

"There's got to be another way to look at it, though," Brickett pressed.

"There is," Rhün agreed, and did not say what it was, because he didn't know yet either.

Gregory stood there for a long moment, jaw working, a scholar being asked to distrust the only method he had ever trusted.

"Folklore matters too," he said at last, each word pulled through his teeth. "That is fair."

Brickett brightened.

Gregory raised one finger. "But that is not what we are researching now." And he turned back to the stacks, and pulled the forty-second volume, and Brickett and Rhün exchanged a look over his bent back that said, plainly, *he'll come around* — and neither of them said it, because he wasn't there yet.

---

In the ancient Satyr city, Matthieu H. Cardinal was asking the same question six different ways and drawing the whole time.

He had a scrap of Academy ledger-paper and he was covering its margin with a small, precise diagram — the Jadefall River as a line, the dam as a notch, arrows branching off it into consequence — while he questioned the chief archivist, a nervous fae-kin clerk with ink on his fingers.

"So there is *nothing,*" Matthieu said, pleasantly. "On the Bountiful Dam. In the records of the one Academy positioned to hold them."

"There's *no reason* for there to be anything," the clerk said, and it did not sound like a lie. "It was a failure, High Theorist. First years of the Dovren line. The masonry cracked before it was finished, the monsoon cycle that year came in wrong, the reservoir drained, the works were abandoned, the sector went wild — elementals moved into the ruins, and no one's been able to clear them since. It never became a *town.* Nothing was ever *accomplished* there. Archives keep records of things that *worked.* Why would anyone copy the ledgers of a hole in the ground that killed a construction crew and produced nothing?"

Yurislav had been rolling his worn coin across his knuckles the whole time, slow and even — until the clerk said *produced nothing,* said it the way you'd note a rained-out festival, and the coin stopped, and Yurislav's broad white-furred hand came down flat on the counter hard enough to make the inkwell jump.

"Then explain the *symbol,*" he said, low. "If nothing came of it. If the project meant nothing and the people meant less. Why does the *symbol* it made — the arch, the three coins — why is that stamped on every coin in the nation two thousand years later, if the thing it was born from was a *hole in the ground?*" His jaw was tight. "Things that mean nothing don't leave marks that outlive the people. Somebody *kept* that on purpose. So somebody thought it was worth keeping."

Tallandra had not been idle. She'd read the exits, read the clerk's hands, read the way his eyes went to a particular closed door when the dam came up — and now she leaned in with the flat practicality of a soldier. "Forget the story of the failure," she said. "Answer four things. Who *paid* for the works. Who *profited* when the land emptied. Who gave the order to *abandon* the sector. And who, exactly, was the last person to *stop writing it down.*"

"You're — you're chasing *ghosts,*" the clerk said, and there was the smallest crack in it now.

Matthieu looked at his own diagram, at the notch and the branching arrows, and drew one more line: a box around the empty space where the records should have been.

"He's not lying," he said quietly, to Yurislav and Tallandra, as they stepped away from the counter. "There genuinely are no books. I believe him completely." He tapped the boxed emptiness. "But a hole shaped exactly like a cover-up is not the same thing as nothing. The *absence* is the data. Somebody didn't fail to keep these records. Somebody *unkept* them." He pocketed the scrap. "We keep digging. Just not here."

## The Lunar Flare

Three hours the line had cost them, and Addie had spent the last of it doing the arithmetic she couldn't stop doing: the green beacon was due tonight, and by her count they had less than two hours before the sky changed and the twenty-five-day clock finally, actually began. The line inched. It was slow, and cold, and — she had to admit — entirely orderly, a small fair society running on nothing but the agreement not to cheat.

They'd solved the recognition problem crudely: after the noble scene, Carrio had relieved one of the drunk elves of his good travel cloak, and Addie now wore it hood-up, cyan braids hidden, invisible to the long lenses that swept the lower tiers from the skybridges above.

Carrio had drifted to a stone support pillar with a clean sightline through the restaurant's front window, chewing his gum, watching the kitchen. Watching the small, broad, furious-looking figure moving between the tables inside — a dwarf woman, soot to the elbows, barking orders, and on one thick finger a heavy signet ring that caught the lamplight as she gestured.

Carrio went absolutely rigid.

The gum stopped.

"Carrio," Addie said.

He was already moving — pushing off the pillar, starting for the window, cutting the line without a thought — and it took both Addie and Mae to physically stop him, a hand each, hauling him back before he could shove through three hours of somebody else's patience.

"The *ring,*" was all he said, staring.

High above, on the Citadel tier, a great bell began to toll the Hour of the Sphinx — and under its last note, a different sound came up the lane: the flat, ugly, coordinated tramp of a peacekeeper column, and horns, and the barked cadence of a district being shut down.

---

"The ring," Carrio said again, low and urgent, letting them pull him back but not looking away from the window. "The dwarf's signet. The design on it — it's the same. It is the *exact* same as the earrings my family has worn for nine generations." He finally turned to Addie. "The ones the old rat complimented. You know the ones."

The cold went through Addie that had nothing to do with the sunless tier.

Because she'd been carrying the Mark of the Orbs as a small, private, two-person mystery — her pendant and a beaver kid's backpack clasp, a strange coincidence between an orphan and an orphan-adjacent historian. But a dwarf woman she'd never met, in a soot-black kitchen at the bottom of a city, wore it too. On a *family* signet. And so did Carrio's ancient house.

She drew the pendant out from under the borrowed cloak — the plain silver disc, her mother's, her mother's mother's — and held it up. Carrio, without a word, unclasped one of his earrings and set it beside the pendant in her palm.

They were the same. Not similar. *The same* — the same interlocking orbs, the same proportions, struck from the same design as surely as two coins from one die.

"Brickett's clasp," Addie said. "It's this. Exactly this." Four of them, now. A historian, an orphan, a serpent-prince's house, an exiled dwarf. She turned to Mae, half a wild hope in it. "Mae. Where's yours? Your house — the Van'kareths — you must have—"

"I've never seen it," Mae said.

She was looking at the pendant, and the earring, and there was something in her face Addie had not seen there once — not in the Conclave, not over the soup, not with a blade at a noble's throat. Mae'vyri Van'kareth, Crown Princess of Nox Vaelor, the most dangerous mind in seventy-two cities, looked, for one unguarded moment, *left out.*

"I have never seen that symbol in my life," she said again, quietly. "It's on nothing of mine. Nothing of my family's. Two thousand years of Van'kareth records and there is not one mark like it anywhere in them." Her hand had drifted to her own bare collar, where no pendant hung. "Why does everyone at this table have it but me?"

"I don't know." Addie closed her fist around the two matching pieces. "There are too many threads, Mae, and I don't — I don't know which one to *pull.*"

"*Threads,*" Mae repeated, catching the word, her eyes cutting up to Addie's. "You said *threads.*"

But there was no time to follow it, because the horns were louder now, and the tramp of boots had reached the courtyard.

---

The peacekeepers came to close the street, and they were going to close it *now* — before the line was served, before anyone at the back got fed, a whole quarter's evening meal shut down on a garrison's schedule.

Mae made the call the way she made all of them: instantly.

"Delay costs us the day," she said, and reached over and *tore the cloak off Addie's head.*

Cyan braids and field armor, out in the open, under the grey-blue light. Mae raised her voice to carry over the horns, and she named all three of them — herself, Speaker of the Conclave; Carrio Vessalor of the high serpent houses; and *the Head Moderator, Adeshka Sonntag* — to the whole packed, cold, exhausted courtyard.

Addie braced for the worst. She knew this crowd. She was *of* this crowd. She waited for the resentment, the thrown thing, the jeer at the pampered official in her expensive armor come to gawk at the poor.

It didn't come.

Instead a ripple went through the line — recognition, dawning, spreading face to face — *that's her, that's the one from the beacon, the one who gave the speech, the one who said none of us are cargo* — and the whole courtyard, three hours cold and hungry and forgotten, went quiet, and then began, hand over hand, to lift their arms and turn them at the wrists.

The silent applause. The same gesture the plaza had given her in Aquila Matara, come to find her again at the bottom of a different city.

"We didn't know," a woman near the front said, stricken, half-apologizing. "The Chief Moderator, in *our* line — we'd have — we didn't *know* it was you."

And the restaurant door banged open.

A tall, harried human woman came out first, an order-pad still in her hand, and took one look at Addie's field armor and the official bearing and the unmistakable shape of a *scroll* in Mae's grip, and her face collapsed into pure dread — *Rosalind,* Addie would learn, Brynn's wife, who had drawn a middle finger on a board over her own door and meant it.

And behind her, shouldering out of the scullery, soot to the eyebrows, grease on her apron, a cleaver still in one soot-black hand and the signet ring bright on the other, came the dwarf herself, small and square and absolutely done with the entire universe.

"Right," said Brynn H. Stonevein. "Which one of you is here about the bloody flying scrolls, and what in the deep is it going to cost me to make you *leave.*"

---

Addie opened her mouth to answer her — and the peacekeepers reached the courtyard, and everything went wrong at once.

"District's closed," the watch commander barked, his phalanx locking shields across the lane-mouth. "Everyone clears. Now."

Mae stepped into their path, and her voice went to the register that had silenced the Conclave. "I am the Speaker of the Conclave, and you will hold your line while these people are fed."

"The Conclave," the commander said flatly, "has no authority during a Conjunction cycle. We've had the notice. You're nobody right now, Speaker." He lowered his visor. "Clear the street."

And Addie felt it all begin to slide.

Mae's hand had gone still and certain in the way that meant a Venom-Blade was about to earn her name; Carrio's hood flared, and the poison-reach began, fluid and lethal; the phalanx braced; and in the middle of it, packed into the narrow courtyard with nowhere to go, were a hundred cold civilians and a soot-covered dwarf and her terrified wife — every one of them about to be caught between a princess, a serpent, and a garrison, in a fight that would paint the tallow-tier red and end the companions' first day in a massacre over a *dinner line.*

*A fight here ends everything,* Addie understood, with total clarity. *Before the green beacon even lights. This is how it all dies — not to Horror, not to the enemy, but to a bad afternoon and everyone's worst reflex.*

She had spent two days holding the thing behind her eyes shut. The cold blue pressure. The power in the pendant, in the Mark against her skin, the thing that had frozen Tallandra in a Skyrend street and lit her eyes on the imperial steps.

She stopped holding it.

She let go, and it came *up* — not blue this time but white, a clean and total white, pouring out of the pendant and out of her skin and out of the Mark itself, a light like a full moon dropped into the courtyard from a hand's breadth away. The whole tallow-tier went blinding, shadowless white. The phalanx staggered back, shields up over their faces; the civilians threw their arms across their eyes; Mae's blade and Carrio's poison both hung, forgotten, in a world that had suddenly lost every edge to the glare.

Into that white silence, Addie spoke, and she did not raise her voice, and she did not make it kind.

"Everyone is going to be still," she said, "and calm, and no one is going to raise a hand to anyone else in this courtyard."

She let the light swell until it hurt.

"Or I will take the light back, and I will make quite certain that not one of you can see at all."
