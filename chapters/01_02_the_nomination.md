## The Gale Exposition

The brochures called it *instantaneous transdimensional transit.* Addie, stepping off the pad with her stomach three seconds behind the rest of her, would have called it being squeezed through a damp leather boot — sideways, by someone who resented you personally, and then asked to smile about it at the far end.

She made it two steps onto the lower platform before the nausea crested, held, and grudgingly receded. Portal travel did that to her every single time, and every single time some cheerful part of the system insisted this was a solved problem. It was not a solved problem. It was a problem they had learned to stop apologizing for.

Portalport Station received her with all the warmth of an institution that had been over-engineered by people who would never have to use it. Locals called it The PP, which was crude, and accurate, and the only honest piece of signage in the building. Brass arrivals boards clacked through destinations no one wanted. The air smelled of ozone and cold metal and the particular despair of travelers who had been promised speed and delivered indignity. She was late. Of course she was late. The transit folds had backed up somewhere over the eastern spans, and the hour had slid past comfortable into the long thin stretch of the night when only the desperate and the professional were still moving.

She could live with late. The Meridian Accord was paying, and traveling on the guild's coin was tolerable for one reason only: if she timed it right, she could steal a single night of somewhere new before the archives swallowed her whole. She was a long way from home tonight — a long way from Qasr Siroth, from the dry gold light and the caravan noise of the only city that had ever been hers — but Skyrend Peak was new, and cold enough to be interesting, and she intended to see exactly one beautiful thing before duty resumed.

She moved through the crowd the way she always did: head down, shoulders set, a straight line at a constant velocity, trying to project the specific social forcefield of *an immovable equation you would be foolish to interrupt.* It mostly worked. She was tall, and built like someone who walked everywhere and meant it, with the warm brown skin and the strong straight features that favored her mother's side — the Egyptian side, the side with a name and a city and a grave. The other half of her, the father's half, was a blank space in a registry she had long ago stopped visiting. She wore the blank the way she wore the cold: without complaint, because complaint changed nothing.

The forcefield failed at a crate of alchemical reagents.

She clipped it with her hip, the vials inside chiming an alarming chord, and an elegant traveler in traveling silks rounded on her with a hiss of some high-lineage vowel-work. Elf. Some sub-lineage of elf — the back room of her mind offered up three candidate courts and a dialect map before she overruled it. She had an eidetic archive back there, a room that never closed, cataloguing everything whether she asked it to or not; tonight she declined to let it work. She gave the traveler a flat apology with no interest attached and kept walking. Not every insult required a full transcription.

The child, however, arrived faster than her defenses.

It struck her at thigh height, a small sticky comet, and dragged one jam-glazed hand the entire length of her satchel before ricocheting away shrieking with joy. Addie looked down at the crimson smear now curing into the good leather and reflected, with the serenity of the truly exhausted, that every culture in the known world had independently invented the same creature: a small human who smelled of spoiled fruit and existed to ruin things that had cost money.

"*Adeshka!*" a woman's voice called after the comet. "Adeshka, we do not run!"

Addie's step hitched, just slightly, at the sound of her own name in someone else's mouth, worn by a stranger's child. She watched the sticky little Adeshka careen back to a harried mother, watched the whole warm machinery of it — the scoop, the scold, the forgiveness — and felt the old dry thought surface unbidden and unresisted: *the world already has enough people making more people. I decline to add to the supply.* It wasn't cruelty. She had checked, more than once, and it wasn't cruelty. It was just a door she'd looked at and quietly not walked through. She stepped around the reunion and escaped the station.

The Gale Exposition hotel was per-diem architecture: clean, gray, and punitive, designed to remind a traveler that comfort was a luxury the guild had specifically declined to fund. Room 314 smelled of industrial lavender and other people's departures. She let her luggage drop where gravity suggested and made directly for the minibar, because after The PP she had earned exactly one uncivilized indulgence.

The little cabinet held three spirits, a row of overpriced nuts, and — she saw it as her hand closed on the good whiskey — a discreet brass plate reading *biometric weight sensors active; itemized to registered account.* Which meant every gram she lifted would land, itemized, on an expense card that ran straight back through the Meridian Accord's internal affairs, straight to some auditor, straight — eventually — to Kwame's disappointed face.

She let go of the whiskey.

"No," she told the minibar. "You would love that. You would *love* to bill me forty crowns for a thumb of scotch and three nuts and make it a whole conversation. Absolutely not." She shut the cabinet on her own dignity and went to run water on her face instead.

The bathroom mirror was unkind and honest, which was the same thing. It showed her a woman who had been awake too long: braids working loose, shadows under the eyes deep enough to file paperwork in. She reached up and lifted the pendant off over her head to set it safe before what came next.

She held it under the light for a moment, the way she always did. It was a simple thing, or it pretended to be — a small disc of old silver alloy on a plain chain, worn smooth by more hands than hers. But the mark pressed into its face had never been simple. Nested rings, orbs inside orbs, a geometry that sat wrong for jewelry — too exact, too *functional,* less like ornament and more like a map, or a diagram, or the working face of some small lunar machine built to a purpose no one had bothered to write down. She had spent years on it. She had gone through every codex, every heraldry index, every symbol registry the archives held, in three cities and four dead scripts, and found precisely nothing. The mark belonged to no house, no guild, no faith, no era anyone had catalogued.

And then, as always, the mystery lost to the grief, because that part was simpler and hurt more. The pendant was her mother's. It had come to her out of the cold machinery of an estate settlement — one line item that had survived the lawyers and the debts and the slow bureaucratic dismantling of a life — and when Addie held it she did not think about nested orbs. She thought about river silt and dried jasmine, the exact smell of her mother's hands, a smell that had no codex and needed none. She set the pendant carefully on the shelf, where the mystery could wait, as it had waited her whole life.

Then she looked back at her hair, and found the silver-blonde again.

Two streaks of it, running back from her temple, stubborn as scar tissue. They had resisted every pigment she'd ever thrown at them and every catalyst too; dye slid off them, magic guttered around them, as if that specific silver had opinions about being changed. She hated what they meant — time, and her mother, and the parts of herself she didn't get a vote on. So she did the one thing she could still choose.

She laid out the red pigment and the blue, and the little vial of life-magic catalyst that would set them, and she began to work the color through with her fingers. This was hers. Whatever the sky did, whatever the guild billed her, whatever the mirror insisted — the color of her own head was a small visible piece of the world she still got to decide, and she intended tonight's decision to be deliberate, and controlled, and armored: a clean, chosen thing to wear back out into a night that had already tried to smear jam on her.

The catalyst warmed under her hands. The color began to take.

And her mind — her stupid, tireless, never-closing back room — drifted, the way it always did the moment her hands were busy, sliding sideways into the day's unfinished work: the moon-position data she'd been asked to review, the node-alignment tables that hadn't reconciled, a geological anomaly in the eastern readings that kept not making sense, the numbers turning and turning, and the catalyst reacting to her divided attention the way life-magic always punished a distracted caster—

The spell shorted out.

It went off in her scalp like a struck nerve, a bright hot crack of feedback, and she yanked her hands back with a curse that would not have passed the Exposition's guest-conduct policy. When the water in her eyes cleared, the mirror delivered the verdict: her hair was a catastrophe. Half of it had seized a furious red, the other half a bruised uneven blue, the two colors meeting in no pattern at all — and running back through the wreckage of both, untouched, unbothered, gleaming, the silver-blonde streaks she had been trying to bury.

She stared at it. She had time to think one entire clear thought — *of course* — before three hard knocks hit the door.

Not polite knocks. Urgent ones. Fist-and-authority knocks.

"If that is the minibar auditor," she called, wet-handed and dye-streaked and past all patience, "I want you to know I touched *nothing,* and I will contest the nuts in front of a tribunal."

The door, when she wrenched it open, framed a hotel attendant who took one look at her and went pale for two entirely separate reasons. The first was the object in his own shaking hands: a slim dispatch bound in crimson ribbon, sealed with a mark that made his knuckles white — imperial priority, the ribbon color they only used when someone very high up had decided that ordinary channels were too slow. The second reason was her hair, at which his eyes snagged and then fled, as though he'd witnessed something he'd need to unsee.

"Sorcerer-Historian Sonntag," he managed. "Eye-mail. Priority. It — it came routed through the house because it's keyed to *you,* to your eyes, it wouldn't open for reception, it wouldn't open for anyone—" He swallowed. "They said if I let you delay reading it the house could lose its license. Please. Please just take it."

She took it. The ribbon dissolved at her touch — reading her, confirming her, the delivery-magic finding the specific person it had been thrown across the world to find — and the dispatch fell open, and the message inside was not written in ink. It burned up off the parchment in cold violet light, and it was short.

*Presence required, immediately. The beacon burns.*

Addie crossed the room and tore open the curtains.

Across the dark of Skyrend Peak, above the rooftops, Stormspire Aerie was bleeding violet into the sky — a tower she had walked past many times — this was not her first trip to Skyrend Peak — always dead and ornamental, its beacon silent and unburning, now pouring light up into the clouds in a color she had only ever met in the oldest texts. She did not need the back room for this one. She did not need a single equation. She had spent her whole underfunded, overlooked career learning to tell the real thing from the fakes, and every instinct she owned stood up at once and said the same word.

It wasn't a malfunction. It wasn't a festival stunt or a noble's vanity or a trick of the weather.

The ancient network was awake. And it had sent for her by name.

## With Kwame

She reached the Meridian Accord Guildhall on foot, coat thrown over half-dry clothes, hair still a warring mess of red and blue and defiant silver, looking less like a scholar of the guild and more like something the transit folds had failed to fully reassemble. She did not care. Caring was a luxury the beacon had just repossessed.

The Guildhall was, on ordinary nights, a monument to comfortable self-importance — marble, brass, the slow choreography of clerks who believed the world's paperwork was the world. Tonight it looked like an anthill under a boot. People ran who had never in their lives run indoors. Somewhere a bell would not stop. The air had the high thin whine of an institution discovering, all at once, that it was not in charge of anything.

"By the *saints,*" said Jaren, planting himself in her path with a scribe's clipboard and an apprentice's fatal confidence. He looked her up and down — the coat, the clothes, the disaster on her head — and his mouth curled. "They summon the great Conjunction expert and *this* falls out of the portal. Did you lose a fight with a paint cart, Sonntag, or is this the new academic look? Very bold. Very — festive."

Addie stopped. She was tired, and frightened underneath the tired, and Jaren had just volunteered to be the place she put it.

"Jaren," she said, pleasantly, "I have exactly enough patience left to give you one piece of professional advice, so treasure it. The stylus behind your ear is the most authority you will ever hold. If you keep standing in my doorway, I'm going to demonstrate the full range of administrative intake valves it was designed for, and neither of us will enjoy the filing." She leaned in half an inch. "*Move.*"

Jaren moved. "Upper tier," he said, to her back, with markedly less music in it. "Guildmaster's waiting. Section Four transit."

The Section Four car was one of the horizontal kind, a glass box that slid sideways across the building's face, and as it carried her toward the upper tier it gave her a long unbroken view she would have paid money to avoid: window after lit window across Skyrend Peak, and in every one of them a person standing perfectly still, faces turned up, violet light on their upturned faces, watching Stormspire burn. A whole city, holding its breath. She had never seen anything so quiet contain so much fear.

Kwame was already pacing when the car released her.

That was the first wrong thing. Guildmaster Kwame Odion Mensah did not pace; he *presided,* vast and unhurried, a man whose ornate robes and rolling patriarchal warmth had made him feel, for the eleven years she'd known him, like the one stable object in the guild's whole spinning economy. The man in front of her now had that man's face and none of his composure. His robes were slept-in and wrong. His skin had gone ash-gray. His hands, when he crossed the room and seized her by both elbows, were shaking.

"You're here. You came through the folds." He gripped her like a man confirming she was solid. "They were locking transit as you moved. I didn't know if— you're *here.* Good. Good."

"Kwame." She had never said his name gently to him before; it felt strange in her mouth. "What is happening? That's an initialization loop out there. That's the network coming up — the beacon's not just lit, it's *cycling,* it's talking to the others. I need to know how far it's gone."

"Further than that." He let go of her elbows only to gesture at the whole shaking building. "The Conclave's called an emergency session. Both armed factions — Concord and Ruin — are moving militia into the streets under 'guard' authority. The gates are locking city by city. It is not a warning, Addie, it's already *happening.*"

She reached for the one handhold she had — the history, the precedent, the thing she actually knew. "Then it follows the old protocol. Beacon activation appoints a Facilitator. Passive role. You log the state data, you record what the Conjunction does, you manage the boundaries between the parties and you *don't touch the outcome.* That's what the records say. That's the whole point of a Facilitator — a neutral clerk with no teeth—"

"The records are dead." Kwame said it quietly, and the quiet was worse than shouting. "Whatever's happening tonight, the old protocol doesn't answer to it. Come and look at what actually arrived."

His office was worse than the corridor, because it was still. On his desk, on black parchment that drank the lamplight, lay a document, and pressed into it was a seal — or the ruin of one: a wax impression cracked and broken up, so that she could tell a seal had been there and not, for the life of her, make out what it had been. That unreadable smear made the back of her neck cold before she’d read a single word.

"The Master Seal,” Kwame said. “You won’t have heard of it — almost no one has. A mark the High Sovereign and a handful of bureaucrats are sworn to watch for and otherwise never name; I only learned of it myself tonight. They swear this one arrived intact and was broken by the royal astronomer’s own hand, up in her tower, three hours ago. What you’re holding is only a copy — the same seal, run off and pushed out to every guild in the city, for reasons I’m told will become obvious forthwith. I’ve read it four times and I need you to read it once, because you’ll see the thing I can’t stop seeing."

She read it.

She found the thing on the first pass. It was one word, and it was everywhere, and it was wrong.

"It doesn't say Facilitator," she said slowly. "Anywhere. It says *Moderator.*" She looked up, and the historian in her had gone very cold and very awake. "That's not a synonym, Kwame. A Facilitator records and steps back. A Moderator *acts.* This — this reads as an active seat. Absolute arbiter. Situational exemption from the standing rules. Authority that overrides the ordinary voting patterns entirely." Her finger hovered over the clause without touching it. "Someone took the toothless old office and gave it teeth, and then made it the most powerful chair in the world."

"And every recognized guild," Kwame said, "has until noon tomorrow to put one name in that chair. One binding nominee, each. Or the guild loses its voice for the whole cycle."

Addie laughed. She couldn't help it; it came out sharp and disbelieving. "Then may the saints help the ministers, because that's a knife fight for people who *want* the knife. That's a political nightmare — that's a thing for the Conclave's sharpest sharks, the ones who dream in leverage. It is emphatically not a job for an archivist who disproves other people's ghost stories for a living." She shook her head. "Whoever our guild picks, I’ll happily brief them. I hope they're paid well and sleep with the lights on."

Kwame did not laugh.

He reached behind him and lifted a wooden scroll container off the desk, and held it, and the silence stretched until she felt it change shape.

"The political departments were asked first," he said. "All of them. Every minister, every operator, every ambitious name that's spent a decade angling for a bigger chair." He turned the container slowly in his shaking hands. "Every one of them refused, Addie. Because they understood exactly what you just said out loud — that whoever sits in that seat is the most powerful person alive, which means they're also the largest target that has existed in a thousand years. They all wanted the power. Not one of them wanted the mark on their back."

"Then I don't understand why I'm—" She stopped. She did understand. She watched him arrive at it in his frightened, gentle face a half-second before he said it, and she felt the floor of the night tilt.

"You're the only Conjunction specialist we have who's worth the word," Kwame said. "You've disproved twenty-five events the histories swore were conjunctions — twenty-five, Addie, and you're not yet thirty, when the whole field is dead men and cranks. You have no political identity, which every shark at the top treated as your disqualification, and which the council treated as the entire *point* — you owe no faction, you're bought by no bloc, there's no lever behind you for anyone to pull. And you, more than anyone breathing, understand the literal mathematics of what it costs a civilization to survive one of these." He stepped close and pressed the wooden container into her hands, folding her fingers around it with his own shaking ones. "You understand survival as *arithmetic.* That's what they need in that chair. That's what I need to have found for them."

The wood was heavier than wood.

"Kwame," she said. "What did you do."

"The Meridian Accord has already submitted its nominee." He would not look away from her, and there was terror in it, and there was love in it, and the two were not separable. "The papers are signed. The seals are set — the guild's, the council's, all of them, properly, tonight, before you walked in that door. It's done. I'm sorry. I couldn't ask you first. If I'd asked, you'd have said no, and then it would have been someone else's name, someone worse, someone owned—"

She opened the container.

Her own name looked back at her from the scroll, inked in a formal hand beneath a column of institutional seals stacked heavy as a verdict. *Adeshka H. Sonntag.* Nominee. Moderator.

Behind the glass of Kwame's window, Stormspire flared, and violet light swept across the room and across the scroll and across her ruined red-and-blue hair and the two silver streaks that had refused, all night, to be anything but what they were.

And in the back of Addie's mind — the room that never closed, the archive that never stopped sliding one number against the next — everything went, for the first time in her adult life, completely silent. The equations stopped. The cataloguing stopped. There was nothing back there now but the weight in her palms and the light on the wall.

She had spent her whole career on the other side of the glass. Behind the library desk. In the margins. The one who studied what happened *to* people, safely, afterward, with citations.

The old threads had started to unravel, all of them, all at once. And they had just pulled her through the desk and into the center of it, with her name already signed, and no version of the night in which she got to say no.
