## Empty Windows

Skyrend Peak did not cheer for them.

Addie noticed it the way you notice a held breath — before you can name what's wrong, your own lungs go shallow to match. In Aquila Matara the streets had been a wall of bodies and noise, the whole capital pressing in to watch the Head Moderator go and either save them or die trying. Here, in the city that had made her, the streets were empty.

Not abandoned. Empty in a watched way. They ran the long climbing avenues toward the Aerie and the shops were shuttered and the fountains were still, and up above them, in window after window after window, the people of Skyrend Peak stood behind their glass and looked down and did not come out.

They understood. That was the thing that put a cold hand on the back of Addie's neck even at a dead run. Aquila Matara had gathered because Aquila Matara had wanted a show. Skyrend knew better. Skyrend had watched the sky go purple over this very peak first of all the cities in the world, twenty-four days ago, and it had done the arithmetic every night since, and it had decided that whatever was about to happen at the top of the hill was not a thing you crowded around. It was a thing you witnessed from a safe distance, with your children behind you and your door shut.

She should have felt something about that. Some of it was home — the crooked slum roofs down the eastern slope where she'd grown up, the observatory dome catching the last red light, the very stones she'd been carried up the first time she'd climbed to the Conclave. There should have been room in her chest for all of it.

There wasn't. They were too tired and too fast and too late, and the only thing any of them had breath for was the climb. Behind her Gregory and Matthieu had stopped arguing entirely, which was its own kind of alarm. Brynn's humming had gone. Carrio's jaw worked on nothing; his gum had disappeared somewhere on the road. They ran through the silence of their own home city and no one said a word, because there was nothing to say to windows.

Stormspire Aerie stood at the crown of the peak where the wind never stopped — a spire of pale weatherworn stone with the beacon platform ringing its base, the place the purple light had first bloomed. The place, Addie thought, where all of it had actually started, if Brickett was right. And Brickett was always, infuriatingly, right about this.

She didn't slow down. She hit the base of the platform, took the three worn steps two at a time, walked to the center, planted her staff, and waited.

Nothing happened.

The wind pulled at her hair. The red beacon burned overhead, huge and indifferent. The stone under her boots was just stone.

"Oh, for *fuck's* sake," Addie said, to the whole silent city. "This is not okay."

She heard the others come up behind her, boots on stone, and she could feel the panic starting to take the shape it always took now — Tallandra going rigid, someone about to say *are you sure, did you remember it right* for the second time in one day, the whole company teetering on the edge of coming apart in front of a thousand watching windows —

The shadows opened.

They didn't fall or gather; they simply *decided* to be a door, the way they always did, a seam of black peeling itself out of the ordinary evening dark at the platform's edge. And out of it, unhurried, hands folded, ancient and dry and entirely too pleased with the timing, stepped the Overseer.

The company did not sag with relief. That was the thing Addie would remember. Nobody's shoulders came down. They'd learned better. The appearance of the Overseer meant the appearance of new rules, and new rules at this hour meant new ways to lose.

"Oh, wonderful," said Carrio, flat. "Are you having fun?"

"Apparently your mother never taught you any manners." The Overseer's voice was mild as milk and twice as old. "A prince of the Embercoil, and he greets the oldest thing in the room like a fishwife short a coin. Really. If your mother could see you now she'd —"

He stopped.

It was a small thing, a hitch, a half-beat where something that had been rolling downhill snagged. The dry old face did a thing Addie had never seen it do, which was flinch.

"Oh," said the Overseer. "Sorry. Your mother situation. Didn't mean to."

The silence that followed was the worst kind — the kind with a laugh trapped inside it that no one would dare let out. Carrio's expression didn't move at all, which was how Addie knew it had landed somewhere real. Beside her Brynn made a sound like a cough that was not a cough. Somewhere behind them, appallingly, Rhün started to say something and Yurislav's hand closed on his arm.

"Right," Addie said, loud, before the moment could do any more damage. "You're not just here to be horrible to us, then. What is this."

The Overseer collected himself with the ease of something that had had ten thousand years of practice collecting itself. "No," he said. "Not merely to mock, Moderator, though I'll grant it's a perk. I'm here to join you. At the same time as you, in fact. That's rather the point of the hour."

## Missing Someone

"You're missing someone, guys."

He said it gently, which was worse than if he'd sneered it. Addie's stomach dropped before her mind had even finished the count.

"The threshold is not a door you may open," the Overseer went on. "It is a door that opens when the shape is complete. And the shape is not complete." He looked, unhurried, along the ragged line of them — Mae, Carrio, Brynn, Gregory, Rhün, Matthieu, Yurislav, Tallandra, Addie — and then at the empty place where a small loud person ought to have been. "Where's Brickett?"

Everyone panicked at once.

It was not dignified. Nine people who had faced down a false endpoint and a screaming press corps and the actual arithmetic of the apocalypse all began talking over one another about a fourteen-year-old beaver they had left concussed in a physician's care two cities and one frantic afternoon ago. Tallandra was already moving — a Major's calm laid over a Major's speed — dragging a folded sheet of eye-mail parchment out of her breastplate and a stub of ink from somewhere and beginning to write to Barry and Boss before anyone had finished a sentence.

"He's with the doctors," Addie said, "he's fine, he's — we can get him, Tallandra's already —"

"You forgot the thread," said the Overseer.

They stared at him.

"You did," he said, patient as stone. "You may check. I'll wait."

"You told me —" Addie's voice came out higher than she wanted. "You *told* me I didn't need the thread to defeat the Witness. You said it to my face."

"I did say that."

And there it was, the little curl at the corner of his answer, the shape of the trap she'd walked into with her eyes open, and she heard herself say it out loud as she understood it: "You said I didn't need it to *defeat* him. You said — there was a *present*. For the Witness." Her mouth had gone dry. "The thread isn't a weapon. It's the *present*."

"Now she's thinking," the Overseer said, to no one, approvingly.

"It's in Mae's safe," Addie said. "The thread is in a *safe*. In *Aquila Matara.*"

Mae did not curse, or panic, or explain. Mae put two fingers to her lips and whistled — one short brutal note that cut through the wind — and out of the empty air above the eastern parapet, where there had very plainly been nothing at all a heartbeat before, a man on a magic carpet dropped into view and banked hard toward them.

Verrick B. Knackle set down at the platform's edge, soft-footed even in the air, and said nothing, which was the most Verrick thing he could possibly have done. Addie filed away, distantly, the fact that he had been up there the whole time — hidden, hovering, folded into whatever contingency web the Speaker kept strung invisibly around herself at all hours. Of course he had.

"You," Mae told him. "Go to the house. Get the string."

"*Thread,*" said the Overseer.

"Not now, rat. Seriously. I will cut your head off today." She said it to Verrick without heat, the way you'd tell a cart-horse to hold still, and Verrick took it with the blank patience of a man who had been threatened by better and by her specifically many times. Mae stepped in close and dropped her voice to a thread of a whisper, mouth almost at his ear, and Addie caught only the shape of numbers — the safe's combination, passed too quiet to steal. Then, louder: "And get Max. Max knows where the teeth are. Do not open that safe without Max or you'll come back a widower rat, understand me."

Verrick understood him. Verrick was already lifting off, the carpet swinging out over the dark drop of the eastern slope, gone into the evening the way he'd come.

And then there was nothing to do but wait.

That was the misery of it. They were *right*. They had climbed to the true place, the Overseer himself had all but said so, and they stood on the correct stone in the correct city under the correct terrible light — and they could not move. Publicly, gloriously right, and pinned in place by a safe combination and a concussed child and the slow arithmetic of a man on a carpet crossing two cities in the dark. Every window in Skyrend Peak watched them stand there and do nothing.

Tallandra's parchment flared faintly and settled — an answer coming back. She read it and her face did something complicated.

"Barry and Boss have him," she reported. "He'll mend, they say. But the physicians won't release him. He's concussed. They're refusing to let him off the ward."

Gregory made a noise Addie had never heard a scholar make.

He took the eye-mail from Tallandra's hands without asking, and the stub of ink, and he wrote fast and hard, the nib nearly tearing the parchment, his lips moving. Addie caught fragments over his shoulder and understood immediately that whatever Gregory was sending was not, by any measure, a nice letter.

"Gregory —"

"I am a *surgeon,*" Gregory said, to the parchment, to the physicians two cities away, to the universe. "I am a healer of thirty years' standing and I am telling those overcautious — get the raccoon on a carpet *now* —"

"Beaver," said four people at once.

"— get the *beaver* on a carpet now, I will assume full care and full responsibility for his skull, and if any one of them would like to explain to me why they held a patient on a ward while the literal *end of the world* was being decided on a hilltop, they may put it in writing and I will read it at all of our funerals." He signed it with a stab. "There. Send it."

Tallandra sent it.

## Restless Souls

The wind climbed as the evening died. Addie stopped tracking the hour by the light — there was no light, only the red beacon and the black between the stars — and started tracking it by the cold, which deepened, and by the watching windows, which one by one lit their own small lamps against the dark and kept watching anyway.

And then, from the east, low over the slope, came carpets.

Not one. Verrick's, and behind it a second riding heavy, and a third — he'd collected help somewhere, extra hands and extra room, the way a good rat gathers everything he might need on the way out the door. They came in fast against the last of the wind, and as the lead carpet cleared the parapet Addie saw the small bandaged shape sitting bolt upright in the middle of it, head wrapped in white linen, ears sticking out at defiant angles, wholly and unmistakably awake.

Barry and Boss came down first and steadied the carpet. And Brickett — pale, wrapped, wobbling, grinning like an idiot — was helped down onto the stone, with Ariadne's Thread held up in Verrick's silent hand behind him like a spool of quiet fire.

They landed as the bells of Skyrend Peak, far below in the sleeping city, began to strike.

Addie counted them without meaning to. She felt the number arrive in her chest before she heard the last of it, the way you feel a stair that isn't there — the deep hour, the turning of the day, the Hour of the Restless Souls, midnight laid across the peak like a hand. It felt *arranged*. It felt like something had been waiting all evening for exactly this count of bells and exactly this small stubborn creature to be standing exactly here. She made herself not believe that, because believing it was a way to stop thinking, and she could not afford to stop thinking tonight. But it *felt* it.

"Come on, then," she said, and held out her hand.

Brickett took a step. His bandaged foot came down on the platform.

Verrick put the thread into Addie's palm — light, warm, absurd, a spool of string that was somehow the most important object in the world — and closed her fingers around it with the care of a man who had carried it across two cities in the dark and would not fumble it now.

The platform rumbled.

It went all the way down through the stone and up through her boots and into her teeth, the deep answering shudder they had waited a full miserable day to feel, and the red beacon overhead *pulsed* with it, and every single person on that hilltop knew, all at once and without a word, that they were finally, completely, correctly *here*.

The Overseer looked at her. There was nothing kind in it and nothing cruel; there was only the long, dry, ancient attention of the thing that had been at the start of everything and would be there at the end.

"So," he said. "Are you ready, Moderator, to defeat your first Witness?"

Addie looked at the seam of black waiting at the platform's edge. She looked at the thread in her hand. She looked, once, at the nine impossible people who had climbed all the way up here into the dark with her, and at the bandaged child grinning through his linen, and at her home city holding its breath behind a thousand lit windows.

"Not even a little bit, old rat," she said. "But let's get it done anyway."

And she reached into her tin for an H&H.

The objection came from all sides at once — *Addie, don't, not now, you don't need that going in, Addie* — Mae's voice and Gregory's and Brynn's braided together into one worried protective wall, hands half-lifting toward her.

She turned around. She looked at every one of them, this family she had not asked for and would not trade, and she gave them the only blessing she had left in her.

"Don't you dare," she said. "Fuck y'all."

She swallowed the pellet in full view of all of them, the way she'd learned to do it, defiant and unashamed, and before anyone could love her out of it she turned back to the dark, and stepped off the edge of the story she knew.

The shadows took her in without a sound, and closed.
