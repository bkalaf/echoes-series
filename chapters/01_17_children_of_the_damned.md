## Fifteen Minutes Late

Addie came down fifteen minutes late and braced, out of habit, to be the one apologizing.

She wasn't. Four seats were still empty — Matthieu, Mae, Yurislav, and, worst of all, Brickett. She stopped in the doorway and sighed at the beaver-shaped gap at the table, because in twelve days she had learned to read Brickett like a barometer. Brickett was the one who came down first, loud and rested and irrationally excited, dragging the rest of them into the day whether they wanted it or not. If Brickett was late, morale had finally gone under.

The room agreed with the diagnosis. Everyone present had the grey, over-early, under-slept look of people who had been getting up before dawn for too many days to chase a thing that kept not being where it was supposed to be. Nearly two weeks into the Conjunction, and the dam was still a rumor with a river attached.

---

Twelve cities. That was the tally, and Addie had it memorized the way you memorize a debt.

Orsakar. Volgrad. Khembal. Aes Sidhal. Glimmergrove. Thalewood. Elarion Thicket. Grendhollow. Qinyue. Xol Atarah. Suryavaad. Itzamal-Ka. They had followed the Jadefall and its logic halfway down the continent, toward the low country and the swamps, and turned up nothing that held — no ruins, no reliable signpost, no oral story that pointed anywhere clean enough to stand on.

And the assumption underneath it all was starting to feel thin. *The threat is at the ruins of the dam.* Addie couldn't rationalize it cleanly anymore; she only knew they'd been aimed at the Bountiful Dam for a reason, and reasons, in this business, were not the same as directions.

She was in robes today, not the field kit — she'd torn the tunic on something in Grendhollow, the troll city that currently held the Jadefall capital's seat, and Barnaby had it, waiting on more silk. Mae, with the driest possible dark-elf irony, had declared that this was fitting. "If you're going to go and fight an *arachne,*" she'd said, "you ought to be dressed in proper Gloamwood silk. Web to fight the web. It's only good manners."

---

The four of them came in together, having collided on the stairs.

"Why," said Mae, dropping into her chair, "does everybody look as bad as I feel?"

Brickett made a valiant attempt. "At least we're getting to see the sights," he offered. "I haven't seen so many cities. The beauty of the world and all that."

"At least *you're* having fun, little guy," Yurislav said, dry as a riverbed. "Because this feels like trying to find a needle in a haystack."

They went round it again, because there was nothing else to do. They were missing a clue. It had to live in the stories, or the visions, or the plain mechanics of the dam. They'd been over all three a hundred times, and nothing in any of it was dam-adjacent except the dam, and the dam was nowhere. The talk had the weary, worn-smooth quality of a coin handled too long.

---

"Let's go all the way back to the bottom," Addie said. "Brickett. What does a dam *do?*"

Brickett shrugged, practical. "Backs up water. Controls the flow. But it doesn't stop a river *forever* — water finds a way. Downstream might get a bit less waterlogged, sure, but drying land out takes ages. A dam that only stood a few years?" He shook his head. "It shouldn't be able to erase a whole *civilization,* not the way we keep assuming. Even the upstream flooding wouldn't fit the history right. That's why we bothered the satyrs at Aes Sidhal, and that was about the least useful day we've had."

"There's still something missing." Addie pressed her eyes. "It's the *first* vision. It keeps snagging me. Something about the first vision."

Brynn had been humming, low and on-and-off, over her tea. She said a word into it.

"Sorry?" said Matthieu.

"*Cataclysm,*" Brynn repeated, slower for the room. "We keep saying it. All of us, for days. *Cataclysm.* But it's a *chosen* word, in't it. Somebody picked it on purpose. It means more than 'a bad thing happened.'"

It landed. It didn't quite click. But it landed.

---

Barnaby bounced in with the tunic, mended and glowing, half a day early.

"Miss Sonntag! Good as new, better in fact, the new silk's stronger than the old—"

"You didn't have to drop everything," Addie said, surprised, gesturing at herself, already in robes. "I wasn't expecting it till tonight."

"My pleasure, always. I'll pop it up in your room." He beamed. "And then I'm away for the day — Lady Mae's given me the whole of it."

Addie looked a question at Mae.

"Say hello," said Mae, "to a new grandfather."

The room erupted into congratulations — and Addie did not. Addie stared at the little froglok tailor with a sudden flat, total intensity, as something enormous slid into place behind her eyes.

"—congratulations," she said, a beat too late and far too fervent. "That's — Barnaby, that's wonderful, you should *go,* go and see the little one, right now, thank you, you're brilliant, *go*—" and she nearly shooed him out the door.

Everyone stared at her the way they now stared whenever they suspected the head injury was acting up.

Addie turned back to Brynn, eyes bright.

"Brynn. You're absolutely right. *Cataclysm.*"

---

"We've had it backwards the whole time," she said, and it came out fast now. "We kept thinking dam, farmland, river control — *dry-city* thinking. But Pollyr Fen is a *frogfolk* city. And frogfolk have a *life cycle.* Nursery pools. Hatcheries. Their young spend the first *year* of life in the *water.*" She looked around at them. "It's in the *name.* *Pollyr.* Their young. Their water-stage." Her voice dropped. "You don't have to dry the whole swamp to end a people like that. You just have to control the water at the wrong moment. Foul the nursery pools, choke the spawn beds, hold the source hostage for one turn of the cycle, and you've killed a *generation* without spilling a drop of blood." She let it sit. "Wouldn't you consider it cataclysmic if someone took away your water source and controlled it without your consent? Especially after blaming your entire species for making him look fat?"

The room went very quiet, and then very alert. It was the strongest thread they'd held in days, and they all felt it.

They ran to the window to check the beacon.

It stayed yellow.

Brickett skidded back in. "It's not enough. Knowing the *city* isn't the step. We have to know *why* — why Pollyr Fen's tied to *this* piece of the story."

"It's *why they're connected* to this part of the story," Addie corrected, already moving. "And that could be the item, the atrocity, the dam, the story — anything. We've got a kind of *how.* We still don't have *why.* Which means we go."

From the kitchen, Max called after them, "Do try to come back with the correct number of limbs!" — and something quiet and pleased in Tikket's answering murmur made Addie suspect the two of them were entirely content to be left behind.

## Pollyr Fen Arrival

The portal spat them out into another world, and it took about two heartbeats to feel like fools for every dry-city assumption they'd made.

There were no streets. The *water* was the street. Pollyr Fen unrolled around them in canals and boardwalks and shallow glinting pools, stilt-halls on reed-thatched legs, floating markets, temple docks, nursery basins thick with lily and lantern-light, and everywhere the frogfolk, moving between plank and pool and open water with an amphibian ease that made the outsiders look like exactly what they were. The air was warm and green and wet, and it smelled of reeds and clean rot and living things.

Two frogfolk guards flanked the port threshold. They wore Concord guard harness — the cut of it, the structure — but over it, unmistakable, the colors and the armbands of Schism.

Tallandra went rigid, a wingbeat from fury.

"*Non-faction-supporting,*" Carrio murmured, at her elbow. "We are, publicly, no one's."

She bit it back. It cost her.

---

"We're looking to learn about the hatcheries," Addie began, careful. "The young. And what the Bountiful Dam would have done to Pollyr Fen, two thousand years ago—"

"Why," said the first guard, flat and instant, "are you asking about our young?"

The second guard's eyes had gone wide with recognition — *the companions, that's them* — but the first guard plainly did not care who they were.

"We're being pointed this way," Addie said honestly. "It matters. We don't fully know why yet. That's the truth."

The guards exchanged a look. "Paper doesn't keep, here," the first one said. "Damp. If it's *history* you're after, you want the priests. They hold that. And they decide who's fit to hear it."

It was, everyone privately agreed, a very strange answer.

Mae's jaw set — she did not, as a rule, enjoy being told she had to be found *worthy* — and Addie put a quiet hand on her arm. *These are your people, Mae. Schism. Down here at the bottom, exactly like Qasr Siroth.* Mae subsided. Tallandra muttered something clipped about the armbands, and the group folded around her and steered her, gently, toward the temple wetland quarter.

---

The walk was the argument. Pollyr Fen made its case with every step.

The outsiders waded and balanced and waited for ferries and transferred awkwardly from plank to raft while the frogfolk simply *moved,* and the more Addie looked the more she understood that this was not a poor city or a decayed one. It was *sophisticated* — sophisticated precisely *because* of the swamp, grown into and out of the wetland so completely that she could no longer tell where the city ended and the fen began. Directions here were given in canals and lily-basins and the colors of lanterns, not street-names. Gregory drifted along murmuring about the water-and-lotus symbolism, the birth-cycle geometry, the deliberate labyrinth of the canals. Brickett clocked every loose plank and hidden tie-off and shallow crossing, reading where the locals *actually* went. Tallandra looked at the whole beautiful impossible place and visibly hated how you'd ever secure it. And Mae — Mae read the toll-points, the ferry chokes, the market moorings, and exactly who took a cut of every crossing.

---

A frogfolk woman near a temple dock was collecting coin, and she was not, Addie saw at once, begging for herself. She was raising money for new baptism baskets — *tadpole baskets,* someone said — because the old ones had gone soft and unsafe with age.

Addie stopped the whole company dead, dug out every coin she had on her, and pressed the lot into the woman's hands.

The fundraiser blinked, then recognized her. "That's very nice of you, Head Moderator," she said, with a slow amphibian grin. "It's nice to see you without a towel on."

The companions howled.

A tiny frogfolk girl detached from the woman's skirts and wrapped both arms around Addie's leg. Addie, going pink, patted her damp head. "It's — my pleasure," she managed. "When you see someone who needs a hand, you try to leave things a bit better than you found them. That's all." She coughed, thoroughly embarrassed. "We have — we have places to be. Come on, all of you."

## Brother Tallowmire

The temple was old stone and wet wood and the smell of green water, and the person in charge did not stop for them.

Brother Tallowmire G. Reedfen was mid-baptism, lowering tadpoles into the very baskets the whole city was fundraising to replace, and he took one look at the party of well-dressed outsiders, made them sit down, and went right on with his rite — shooting them, between blessings, looks of frank and unhurried loathing.

Addie ate an H&H.

"*Superb* opening move," Mae said. "Antagonize the priest by scoffing your sweets at him."

"I do not know how the rest of you are managing this," Addie said tightly, "because my stress is through the *roof,* Mae. We are running out of *days.*"

Mae wanted to push it. Addie refused. And it was Brynn, of all people, who backed her: "Waiting's what worked in Luminthalas, when this lot came for *me,*" she pointed out. "Maybe give the woman her way."

They waited. It took until dusk.

When Tallowmire finally came over, wiping his hands, he was wary and slow. "We don't tell people these things," he said. "There's a reason for that."

But he hadn't sent them away — and everyone in the group knew what *that* meant.

Then the fundraiser and the little girl came in with the day's collection, and the woman said, plainly, that all of it save a single silver had come from the woman in the robes. Tallowmire's head came round. "Did you know," he said slowly, "that was my wife?"

"No." Addie met his eyes. "I only saw they needed baskets and thought it'd be a nice thing to do." She held his gaze. "I'm from Qasr Siroth, Brother. Schism, same as you. I know exactly what it is to have people read your *politics* off you before they ever bother to read *you.*"

Tallowmire studied her for a long, weighing moment.

"All right," he said at last. "Come with me. I will tell you a story."

---

"Hope you brought good shoes," was all the warning he gave.

He led them out past the edge of the living city and into the deep swamp, and the way was *punishing* — mud to the shin and then the thigh, downed logs, root-tangle, reed-walls, black water. Brickett, who could swim perfectly well, ended up on Yurislav's shoulders where it got too deep, and the big bear-kin carried him without a word, and something in the ease of it said the two of them had stopped keeping score of who owed whom.

Tallowmire told the story as he went, moving fast, throwing it back over his shoulder, vanishing ahead and waiting to see if they could keep up. It was, Addie understood, a test woven into a telling — you did not receive this thing sitting comfortably; you earned it through the mud.

Two thousand years ago, he said, the frogfolk had lived in peace, and then found they couldn't — they'd left Suryavaad, the dry plains north of the desert, near the river but never near *enough,* never right for a water-people. So they'd packed up and gone looking, and found these swamps, unclaimed, uncontested, far from anyone, even far from Mirefang Hold. And they'd built a life. They took up tailoring, because it was the thing they did best in all the world, and they earned, and the city grew.

The swamp fought them the whole way. A slab of packed rock and root blocked the path until Gregory set his hand to it and holy light cracked it clean. Yurislav and Rhün shifted a fallen tree that should have needed ten men. And at a flooded gap, an old extension bridge sat folded across the water, its release a small ring-trigger on the far post, and the only way to reach it was a rope and a bad angle. Brynn worked the problem; Addie set an arrow to it and *pushed* — reached with the cold thing behind her eyes and curved the shaft in flight, bending its path to loop the rope around the trigger. It took two tries. The first went wide and she felt the strain of it behind her teeth. The second caught, the trigger turned, and the bridge unfolded across the black water with a groan.

Tallowmire watched them do all of it, and helped with none of it, and Addie understood that too. He wasn't cruel. He was waiting to see if they'd quit.

They didn't.

## The Swamp-Church

"We're here," Tallowmire said.

They looked around, and for a moment didn't understand. It was only beautiful — old worked stone standing in shallow silver water, reeds, the last light and the first lanterns doubling in the wet, and scattered everywhere across the site a strange and lovely geometry: perfectly round stone spheres, and little pyramid-shaped forms, hundreds of them, thousands, set into the water and the moss like some patient ancient art.

"The swamp-church," Tallowmire said. "The first one. For the first city, before we moved and built the Pollyr Fen you walked through today."

"Why is there no record of it?" Matthieu asked.

"Because it didn't stand long." Tallowmire's voice had gone flat and old. "Dovren grew angry with us. So Dovren turned off the water. That dam of his had no true outlets — it wasn't built to *manage* a river. It was built to *stop* one. And once it was up, he blackmailed us. Extorted us. We paid." A pause. "He never turned the water back on. The land we'd worked went waterlogged and dead, useless for farming. The dam itself collapsed, eventually — under its own weight, or because it had already done its job and nobody troubled to keep it. But by then—" he gestured at the beautiful, terrible field of stone "—by then Pollyr Fen had lost three generations of eggs. And more young than anyone will ever count. There was a time," he said, very quietly, "when the youngest person in this whole city was thirty years old. Because for thirty years, we could not bring a single child safely through the water."

---

Brickett had wandered off among the little stone shapes, delighted, crouching to peer at one and then the next, the way he peered at everything.

"These are *so cool,*" he started to say. "Look at how perfect they—"

And then he stopped.

He went very still, one small paw hovering over a round pale stone, and Addie watched the understanding arrive in him like cold water.

"Oh my God," Brickett whispered. "These are your *eggs.*"

The whole company felt the ground of the place shift under them. Not art. Not geometry. A *graveyard.* The spheres and the little pyramids were calcified eggs, calcified young — the ones the receding water had left to the sun, the generations that had dried in the mud and turned to stone where they lay.

"We built the new city somewhere else," Tallowmire said, "because we could not raise a future on top of this *horror.*"

*Horror.* Addie caught the word where it fell, and by the faces around her, so did everyone else.

The last of the sun went down as he said it, and the moon came up over the reeds — and the stones began, softly, to *glow.* A cool pale light woke inside every calcified form, and in the glow you could see them: the faint curled shapes of the young that had never come, tadpole-small, held forever in the moment the water left them. And across the swamp, quietly, frogfolk began to arrive — wading in ones and twos out of the dark to kneel among the glowing dead and pray, ignoring the outsiders entirely, because this was not a place outsiders came. It was grief. It was memory. It was worship.

"Brother," Addie said softly. "Might we sit with you? An hour. Quietly. To honor them. We won't intrude."

Tallowmire looked at her for a long moment, and something in his weathered face gentled at last.

"That," he said, "would be lovely. You would be the first outsiders here in two thousand years. Stay as long as you like."

And the companions sat down in the shallow water among the glowing eggs, and were silent, and let the silence be as long and as heavy as it needed to be.

---

It was Mae who broke it — quiet, for once uncertain. "How did the other cities let this happen? Where were their neighbors, their *allies?* How did no one *help* them?"

Tallowmire had been cold to Mae from the first word she'd spoken. Now he turned and looked at her fully, and there was something almost pitying in it.

"You truly don't know," he said. "Do you."

Mae went still.

"The Van'kareths broke us," Tallowmire said. "Your family. They helped make that dam *affordable* in the first place — and then they ran the trade lines that fed our allies, Suryavaad and Mirefang Hold and the Crucible and the rest, and one by one they *collapsed* them. Captured them. Waited for the ruin to come, and bought the bankrupt companies for coppers at the debtors' auction the moment it did." His voice never rose. "A good part of your family's fortune, Lady Speaker, was built out of the wreckage of this city. And they moved so *well,* and so *early,* that some of us have always wondered how they knew what was coming before it came."

Everyone looked at Mae.

Mae looked like she'd been struck. "I can't—" she said, and it cracked. "I can't listen to any more of this." And she stood up out of the water and *walked,* fast, away into the dark — Mae'vyri Van'kareth, who did not break, breaking — and only Brickett scrambled up and went after her.

The rest of them stayed with the priest, stunned, and he went on, because the story wasn't finished. The city bankrupted. The years of extortion. The truth carried mouth to mouth, generation to generation, never written, never advertised — because Dovren had punished them once already for existing, and no one wished to invite a second visit. "And the *next* cities he turned on," Tallowmire said grimly, "suffered worse than we did. Though what, exactly, we've lost the whole of. On purpose, some of it." He shook his head. "There is *so much* from the birth of this nation that has been shoved down into the dark. You have found one grain of it. There is a *beach.*"

---

There was a wet sound, and Brickett hauled himself up onto the stone beside the priest, dripping.

"Sorry. Sorry to interrupt." He put a small earnest paw on Tallowmire's shoulder. "Brother — I need your full name. All of it."

"Why?" said Tallowmire.

"You will *not believe* what she's about to do," Brickett said. "But I need your full name. Trust me."

Tallowmire looked at Addie. Addie looked back and thought, with great precision, *what in the actual hell are the two of them up to.*

Brickett just grinned, wet and mischievous.

"Brother Tallowmire G. Reedfen," the priest said, slowly.

"Perfect." And Brickett slid back into the black water and was gone, explaining nothing.

The rest of them sat with the worshippers, and the glowing eggs, for the better part of an hour.

---

Mae came back with Brickett out of the dark, and she was cold, and clipped, and plainly carrying something too large to set down.

She looked at the priest. "You. With me. Now. Show us the way back."

She did not explain. Brickett, beside her, smiled and held his tongue.

And the way back was *nothing.* Tallowmire walked them perhaps twenty-five feet to the side of the brutal route they'd fought through, and there was a clear, easy, sensible path that had been there the whole time.

"So," Mae said, understanding. "You took us the hard way just to be an asshole."

"Well, yes," said Tallowmire. "You deserved it."

"Yes. I know I did." Mae's voice was strange, level, stripped. "But *they* did not."

It struck the whole group as odd — because Mae was not, this time, shoving the blame off herself and onto her ancestors. She was picking something *up.* Accepting a weight that wasn't personally hers to carry, and carrying it anyway.

They were still out in the fen when she said it. The beacon did not change out there, among the dead. It waited.

It was only when they came back up into the living city — into the temple wetland quarter, the lantern-canals, the warm green heart of Pollyr Fen — that the whole company stopped dead at once.

Tallowmire turned. "Why are you stopping?"

They were looking at the water. At the reflection running along every canal and basin and wet stone in the quarter.

The beacon had turned **orange.**

---

Tikket was waiting for them in the temple quarter — fully dressed, briefcase in hand, lawyered to the teeth and visibly hauled out of a comfortable evening at speed.

Mae pointed at him. "Go talk to him, frog."

Tallowmire, baffled, went.

Tikket produced a sheaf of documents with a small professional flourish. "You, sir," he said, "are now the owner of Van'kareth South Aurreth Shipping Co." He caught the priest's blank look. "A Van'kareth-controlled southern shipping concern. The Lady has transferred the entire company — every share, every hull, every route — to you, and to an entity you'll control, in restitution to Pollyr Fen."

Tallowmire stared at him. Then at Mae. Everyone stared at Mae.

"It's blood money," Mae said, flat and fierce. "I am *not* keeping fucking blood money." Her jaw worked. "I can't go back. I can't un-drown your children or un-ruin your city or un-make my ancestors into the vultures they were. But I can look at a fortune that was built on your bones and I can *give back the part of it that was yours.*" She turned to Addie, and there was something raw in it. "Your *fucking* mother's stories. Leave the world a better place than you found it. This—" she gestured at the priest, the documents, the whole grieving city "—this is the best I can do."

She rounded back on Tallowmire.

"And I don't want *one* monument. I want a hundred. I want a *thousand.* Build churches. Tell every soul in the seventy-three cities exactly what was done here." Her voice shook. "This is *not* going to be forgotten."

"We — we can't," Tallowmire said, overwhelmed. "We can't risk angering Dovren again—"

---

Carrio spoke.

He had been quiet for a long time — through Luminthalas, through the yellow, through the swamp — and when he finally uncoiled it stopped the room.

"Brother. Do you know who I am?"

"You're the prince of the Embercoil," Tallowmire said.

"I am. I'm also, as it happens, the de facto leader of Ruin at the moment." He let the group's heads snap around, and waved it off before anyone could speak. "Yes. Whatever. We all have our secrets." He looked back at the priest. "The point is this. If Schism won't shield you from Dovren — and it seems it hasn't in two thousand years — then Pollyr Fen has the protection of Ruin. Starting tonight."

"And my forces out of Nox Vaelor," Mae added. "Not the whole city — I'll be honest with you — but enough. They'll do the same."

Everyone looked at her again.

"*What.*" Mae glared around at all of them. "My family are all assholes and they do not *deserve* to run that city." She turned back to the dumbfounded priest. "And yes, before anyone asks — the *whole* company. I'm keeping six others; it won't so much as dent us. But it will make one clearly wrong thing a little less wrong. It's one of the few I control outright." A thin, vicious little smile. "And it will *infuriate* my family. Which, I'll admit, only makes me happier."

Restitution, protection, and a declaration of war on her own house, all in one breath.

## Children Of The Damned

Mae fixed the priest with a look that brooked nothing.

"And look here, frog. When I say a *thousand* monuments—" she leaned in "—I mean a thousand monuments."

Tikket cleared his throat, ever the professional. "One small matter. The company will need a new name. It is, as of tonight, no longer Van'kareth property, and it would be — legally and morally — improper to keep the name of the family that built it on this city's ruin."

Tallowmire had no words at all.

But Brickett did. He looked out at the orange light lying across the water, and back at the glowing memory of the swamp behind them, and at the little frogfolk girl watching them from a temple dock, and he said it quietly, like it had always been the name.

"Children of the Damned."
