**Chapter 12: The Unwound Core**

Van'kareth Estate, City of Aquila Matara

Aegalor, 3rd of Hearthwake, Conjunction Day 1, Hour of the Star-Siren [Noon +6 hours]

<span class="beacon beacon--green" role="img" aria-label="Green Beacon"></span> Green Beacon - Day 1 of Conjunction #1

</div>

The great obsidian doors swung shut behind them and cut off the green.

Outside, the whole capital had gone wrong-colored — the beacon had turned at last, and Aquila Matara pulsed with a sick, beautiful, underwater light that got into everything. Inside Mae's house, the black stone held it off, and the foyer was warm and lamplit and ordinary, and Max was standing in the middle of it with his hands folded and his face set in the particular stillness of a butler bracing to deliver bad news gently.

"Welcome back," he said. "You'll be pleased, and I use the word loosely, to hear that exactly one group was successful today."

Addie leaned on the ironwood staff. She was too tired to do the arithmetic on his tone. "Which one?"

"Yours." He said it without any pleasure at all. "Which is the problem. Go in — the others are not in good spirits, and I'd rather they took it out on you than on my crockery." His gaze slid past her to Brynn, taking in the soot, the satchel, the square set of her, the cleaver she had absolutely not left behind, and something flickered behind his professional calm. "And this would be—"

"Brynn Stonevein," said Brynn. "And before you ask, no, I'll not be softening how I talk for anyone's comfort, so you'd best get your ear in."

Max held her eye for a moment, then inclined his head with genuine respect. "We'll manage. And if we don't, madam, we shall do charades." He turned for the corridor. “This way. Mind the mood.” Set into the wall along it, at a servant’s easy reach, was another of the dead bells — this one bound in a neat sleeve of the same black cloth as the drapes, so that you would only find it if you already knew to look. Addie was beginning to know to look.

---

Mae stopped at the tall family-room window, and for a moment none of them said anything.

The city lay below them drowned in green — the towers, the skybridges, the river, all of it pulsing slow and luminous like something seen from the bottom of a pond, lovely and profoundly unwell at once. Addie stood beside her and felt the historian in her go very quiet, because she was looking at a thing no living person had ever seen: the capital of Erzengel in the green phase, the world's oldest machinery lit and running after a thousand years of dark.

"No one alive has watched this," she said. "Not one person. We're the first in forty generations."

"Lucky us," said Brynn.

The rest of the room was already full and already unhappy. The other six were scattered across Mae's high-ceilinged family room in the postures of people who had spent a long day failing at something — Gregory turning his bracelet, Matthieu with a half-covered sheet of paper, Tallandra by the far wall with her back to stone and her eyes on the exits, Yurislav a shape in the shadows, Rhün radiating heat near the cold hearth, Brickett sprawled and grumpy on a rug.

The introductions were brief and lumpy. Brynn's blunt Khaz Vorem cadence collided with a room full of scholars and nobles, and there were two or three moments where a sentence had to be run past twice before it landed — but she said each thing exactly once at exactly the same speed, and let them catch up, and did not apologize, and Addie watched the room recalibrate around a person who would not be made smaller for their ease.

Then Rhün's head came up, and he crossed to the hearth in two strides and did something to the projection-relay set into the wall above it.

"You need to see what they're saying," he said. "Now."

---

The wall lit, and there was Addie's own face on it, badly.

A hyena-kin reporter Addie recognized from the imperial steps — Tamsin R. Hale, the hostile one — was mid-sentence, grave and delighted at once: "—the so-called Head Moderator, unelected and unaccountable, who used *unsanctioned arcane force* against a crowd of unarmed Schism citizens in the Luminthalas lower quarters this very afternoon, hours before the sacred green phase—"

The feed cut to a drawling, laconic correspondent standing before a painted skyline. "—and you have to ask, mate, what kind of a *guardian* blinds a breadline. Blinds *working folk.* Sources down in the tallow-tier are calling it an unprovoked assault—"

And then, smooth as oil poured on still water, the High Sovereign himself: Dovren Judax Thorne, framed in gold, wearing an expression of exquisite, fatherly disappointment. "I confess I had *hoped,*" he said, "for better from the office. To see such power turned against the very people it was meant to serve, on the eve of our most solemn hour — it grieves me. It should grieve us all."

Mae killed the feed with a slash of her hand.

"That," she said, and the profanity that followed was fluent and heartfelt, "is a disinformation campaign, dressed for a funeral. Coordinated. Placed. They had the hyena, the border correspondent, and the *Sovereign himself* lined up and ready within *hours.*" She rounded on Addie. "He's not attacking what you did today, Scholar. He's attacking *you.* He is building the story of who Adeshka Sonntag is before you've had the chance to be anyone at all — so that every true thing you ever do afterward gets read through *rogue,* and *unaccountable,* and *blinded a breadline.*"

Addie sat down, because her legs had stopped consulting her, and felt the cost of the white light land at last — not in the courtyard, where it had saved everyone, but here, on a wall, turned inside out into a weapon aimed at her own throat. Her first act of real power, and it already belonged to Dovren.

---

In the shadows, a silver coin began to travel across a set of broad white-furred knuckles, over and back, over and back.

"He's not wrong to be disappointed," Yurislav said.

The room turned as one.

"I don't mean—" Yurislav's jaw worked. He was not a man who spoke often, and he was plainly regretting starting. "I mean that I know him. Better than any of you. The Sovereign lifted me out of *nothing.* I was a garrison nobody from a border post that isn't on most maps, and he saw something in me no one else ever had, and he raised me to stand at his own back. He *chose* me." The coin kept moving. "A man who did that is not only the thing on that wall. That's all I'm saying."

"A man who did that," Gregory said, cold and precise, "does it to *everyone* worth using, Yurislav, and calls it love, and it costs him nothing, and it buys him a lifetime of exactly this — a good man in the shadows defending him out of gratitude while he blinds breadlines on the wall." He didn't raise his voice. That was what made it land. "He didn't see something in you. He saw a *use* for you. It's what he does. It's the only thing he does."

Yurislav looked genuinely wounded, and genuinely confused, caught between the room's certainty and the memory of the one man who had ever told him he was more than a border post — and the coin went over his knuckles, and over, and over, because he had no idea what else to do with his hands.

Addie filed it, carefully, under a heading she didn't like: *a door in him that Dovren already knows how to open.*

---

"Wait," said Brickett, sitting up. "*He* gets to play with a coin?"

The tension broke on it, a little, the way he was good at breaking things. "I got *shouted* at," he said, wounded, pointing at Mae, "for touching yours. And *he's* just — flipping it about right there in the open—"

"You didn't touch mine," Mae said. "You *pickpocketed* mine. There's a distinction, and you're old enough to learn it."

Brickett drew himself up with enormous dignity. "May I *please,*" he said, "play with your coin."

"No," said Mae. "Not right now." But she was already reaching into her coat, and she came out with the coin — the blackened coin Addie had watched her flip through a hundred moments of the last two days — and she did not flip it. She set it down on the table, deliberately, faceup, and slid it across the wood until it lay beside Yurislav's.

The room went quiet in a different register.

They were the same coin. Not similar — *the same,* the same size, the same weight, struck from the same die, two halves of an obvious set. And each was blackened, old soot worked deep into the metal, and the black lay on *opposite faces:* Mae's darkened across the tail, Yurislav's darkened across the head.

"Where did yours come from?" Tallandra asked, from the wall, her eyes sharp now. "Both of you. Why do they match?"

"Family," Mae said. "Always been ours. I never thought a thing of it."

Yurislav was staring at his own coin as though it had changed shape in his hand. "There's a mark on mine," he said slowly. "Under the black, on the clean face. A little symbol. I've had it my whole life and never once worked out what it was."

And at the table, four people — Addie, Carrio, Brynn, and Mae — went still at exactly the same instant, because all four of them already knew what he was about to find.

---

Yurislav turned the coin over, and there it was.

The Mark of the Orbs, small and silver-clean against the soot: the same interlocking circles Addie wore against her heart, the same die that had struck Carrio's earrings and a dwarf's signet ring and a beaver's backpack clasp.

Tallandra was already moving. She unpinned the clasp that held her cloak at the shoulder — a soldier's clasp, plain, functional — and set it down beside the coins, and it bore the Mark too.

"Why," Addie began, "didn't you show this ear—"

"Because I have spent the last two days," Tallandra said, and there was a blade in it, "recovering from having my own body *taken away from me* by a word out of your mouth on a Skyrend street. Forgive me, Moderator, if comparing *jewelry* was not at the front of my mind while I relearned how to trust my own hands." She held Addie's eye until Addie looked down. "I never saw the exact symbol until just now. There was rather a lot going on."

"Fair," Addie said quietly. It was.

Then Matthieu drew a pocket watch from his waistcoat, popped the case, and turned it so the back caught the green light — the Mark, engraved, worn soft with generations of thumbs. And Gregory, wordless, slid a thin silver bracelet off his wrist and set it down, and it was there on that too, and the table became a small constellation of it: pendant, earrings, clasp, twin coins, cloak-clasp, watch, bracelet, all of them faintly, unmistakably resonant now that they lay together, all the same sign, all older than any of them.

Only Rhün had nothing to add, and no one made him reach for one.

"It's the fathers," Matthieu said softly, pen already moving, mapping it. "Every one of us — trace it back and it comes down the *father's* side. The H." He tapped the middle of an imagined name. "It's not a first name and it's not a family name. It sits in the middle, on the father's line, and it carries *this.*"

It was a discovery, and it explained nothing, and every person at the table felt both halves of that at once.

Only Mae, quietly, had turned her own coin over to its clean face — and where Yurislav's bore the Mark, hers was smooth. Blank. The matched twin of a marked coin, carrying nothing at all. She looked at it for a long moment and said nothing, and Addie, watching, understood that the cruelest version of a mystery is the one that pairs you to everyone else and then leaves you out.

---

"How is any of this *possible,*" Addie said. It came out smaller than she meant.

"Set that beside the other impossible thing," Mae said, and there was no mockery in it. "What you did in that courtyard, Scholar. I want you to understand it, because I don't think you do. You didn't throw light. You reached up and pulled the strength of *all three moons* down into a tallow-district courtyard at once and let it out through your own body." She let that sit. "That is not a thing that is *supposed to be able to happen.* Not by a Moderator. Not by anyone. There are old stories about people who could touch one moon. Three at once is not in the stories at all."

"I saw it from six feet away," Carrio said, unusually flat. "She's not exaggerating. The air went white to the bone. I've watched a great deal of power in my life. I have never seen *that.*"

Addie put her hand over the pendant, felt it lying quiet and heavy and secret against her, and did not pretend to anything.

"I don't know how I did it," she said. "I want to be completely honest with this room, because someone made me promise to be." A glance at Mae. "I didn't summon anything. I *stopped holding a door shut,* and something came through, and I pointed it. That's the whole of my understanding. I am so far out of my depth I can't see the shore." She looked at the glowing constellation on the table. "And I think that—" she gestured at the Marks "—and this—" she touched the pendant "—are the same question. I just don't know how to *ask* it yet."

---

"On the road down," she said, "I told you all I didn't know which thread to pull. The Red Thread of Fate, Ariadne's Thread, the Thread of the Norns. Three of them, one riddle, and I couldn't tell which was the right one."

"Then perhaps," Mae said, "that's the wrong question too. Perhaps you don't pull the *right* one." She tapped the table. "Perhaps you pull all three."

Something changed in Gregory's face.

He'd been turning his bracelet, staring at the Mark on it, and now his head came up slowly, and he had the specific expression of a man arriving at a conclusion he found personally offensive. "The archive was empty," he said. "The whole day. Forty-one volumes and every trail ran into a citation with no source behind it. And the *entire time,* the beaver and the elemental kept telling me I was looking in the wrong container." He said *the beaver and the elemental* the way another man might say *a pair of children,* but there was no dismissal left in it, only a kind of grim surrender. "Because the answer isn't in the records. The records are exactly where a thing like this *wouldn't* be. Threads. Fate, and mazes, and the women who spin the world." He pressed the bridge of his nose. "It's *folklore.* The thread we need is not a metaphor and it is not a citation. It's a *story* — a real one, load-bearing, hiding in the ugly old tales nobody thought worth guarding." He looked, frankly, appalled. "It is the single most academically undignified conclusion I have reached in twenty years, and I believe it is correct."

Across the room, Brickett silently, gloriously, mouthed *told you.*

---

The commotion and the faint glow of the artifacts had begun to draw the household — staff drifting into the doorway, half-curious, and among them Barnaby the froglok, still in his tailor's apron, entirely unbothered by whatever was electrifying the room, carrying a spool of dark thread and muttering about a hem.

"—and I *said* to him, you can't take in a Gloamwood seam after it's set, it's like trying to un-ring a bell, and Ariadne's thread only works going *out,* not—" He caught a word out of the air, the room's word, and brightened. "Oh! Are we talking about Ariadne? Lovely old story. The maze, you know. The great labyrinth, and the monster in the middle of it — the bull-headed thing, terrible business — and the clever girl who loved the hero and gave him a single spool of thread to tie at the door, so that after he'd gone all the way into the dark and killed the thing, he could follow the one true line *back out* and not be lost in there forever." He beamed at them all. "That's where the saying comes from. The thread that gets you back out of the maze." He turned to go. "Anyway. The hem."

The room had gone absolutely silent.

Addie was on her feet without deciding to be. Gregory's mouth was open. Matthieu's pen had stopped dead.

Mae came out of her chair like a shot.

"*Barnaby!*" she roared down the corridor. "Get your ass back in here!"
