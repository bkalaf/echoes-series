## The Shelter Test

"You'll not get out tonight," Tallowmire said, once the lawyer had packed his documents and the orange light had stopped feeling like a hallucination. "Portalport's shut or as good as. You're welcome to stay. It isn't much."

They could have forced a transit. A word from the Head Moderator, or the Speaker, and some tired port-warden would have been made to open a grid at midnight for ten exhausted strangers.

"No," Mae said, before anyone could weigh it. "We save that. Emergency authority is for emergencies — and if *everything* is an emergency, then nothing is, and the one time we truly need to pull rank, no one will jump because we've spent the word on a bad night's sleep." She looked, briefly, surprised at herself for the restraint. "We stay."

Tallowmire's eyes moved over her, reassessing something, and Addie understood the offer for what it also was. Not only hospitality. A second test. The priest wanted to see how the prince and the Speaker and the Moderator behaved when they were put *among* the people instead of above them.

He led them to a shelter.

---

It was full to the reeds.

Displaced families packed every good cot and every screened-off private corner — frogfolk and others, whole households camped on the raised dry mats with their lives in bundles around them. The shelter-keeper, flustered, tried to clear the best spaces for such important guests, and every one of the companions refused, and kept refusing until the families stopped protesting and kept their beds.

They took what was left: a carved-out patch of open floor near the water-edge, and made do.

It was a wetland shelter to its bones — reed screens for walls, raised platforms of woven matting to keep bodies off the wet, damp wood and low green lanterns and the constant soft nearness of dark water, everyone sleeping close in the humid dark. The frogfolk were entirely at ease in the cramped, moist, communal press of it. The outsiders were not, and no one slept well, and Addie thought that was probably part of the lesson too.

---

She woke — or never quite slept — into the deep middle of the night, and found Gregory and Mae awake too, and the three of them ended up in a low circle of murmured talk with the families whose children had finally gone down.

There were frogfolk, and a broad quiet crocodilian woman with a clutch of half-grown children and, cradled in damp cloth, eggs she never once stopped watching. And two male showbirds — peacock-kin, their plumage gone dull and travel-ragged — who had been robbed on the road and had, they said, had no luck since.

Addie asked them the only question that mattered to her anymore. What did they hope for. What did they want the Conjunctions to *change.*

"Nothing grand," one of the showbirds said, smoothing a broken feather. "We're not asking for the world remade. We'd just like people to treat one another *better.* That's the whole of it."

"The government talks," the crocodilian woman said, flat, rocking her eggs. "It's very good at talking. Talking doesn't feed a child. Talking doesn't watch a clutch through a cold night."

"We've been let down before," an old frogfolk man said. "Many times. Maybe this is the once it's different. But every time this city's reached for better—" he stopped. Another frogfolk touched his arm, and a woman said his name low, and three of them hushed him gently, and no one finished the sentence. They didn't have to. Addie knew exactly which old punishment they were declining to name aloud, out here in the dark, two thousand years on and still careful.

Across the little circle, Tallandra and Carrio had gone quiet in a particular way, and Addie caught the look that passed between them. *No one here is performing. No one is angling.* It was, Carrio would say later, one of the most honest conversations any of them had had since the scrolls lit.

---

Brickett, meanwhile, had drawn a small worshipful crowd of toddlers.

He was doing tricks — little sparks off his paws, small doomed attempts at fire that fizzled and popped — and the children shrieked with delight, and they shrieked *loudest,* every time, when a spark backfired and singed his own fur and he yelped and shook his paw. He'd caught on to that quickly, and started backfiring on purpose, and was, Addie could see plainly, having the time of his life.

She watched him and felt something turn over in her chest.

He was *fourteen.* She kept forgetting it, because he'd walked into her life mid-catastrophe and never once stopped for permission — but he was a child, and he was good with these children because he understood, from the inside, what it was to want a warm distraction and someone's attention on a frightening night. He gave the exhausted parents an hour off their feet and never once complained about it. Somewhere behind the beaver-clown was a boy who'd never really gotten to be one, and was borrowing a little of it back now, from strangers' toddlers, in a swamp.

She would remember that she'd felt this, later. She'd make herself remember it.

## Fifteen Minutes

As the last of the children dropped off and the parents with them, the companions drew back into their corner, and Carrio produced, from somewhere in his coat, a small enchanted hourglass.

He turned it over. A soft pocket of hush closed around the ten of them, sealing their voices in.

"All right," he said. "We have fifteen minutes before the enchantment breaks. What?" He caught the looks. "I like my private time. Don't get judgy."

Gregory murmured something that dimmed the bubble from the inside, so it wouldn't shed a glow across the sleeping shelter, and in that small pocket of quiet dark the group could finally, at last, talk.

---

"Tomorrow it's the beacon again," Matthieu said. "The seam. We should decide how we go in."

"Same as before?" Carrio said. "Let the Overseer pick one of us who knows the story to go in as witness — and Horror picks one too."

"It makes sense," Brickett said, ticking it on his claws. "Somebody who's working *for* us chooses one person who knows the story. Somebody working *against* us chooses one who knows the story. Balanced. Fair, even, which is suspicious on its own."

"Why did you insist," Tallandra asked, watching him, "that the Overseer choose last time? You pushed for it. Specifically."

Brickett wriggled. "Because the Overseer gave us the hint. And because I think there's something else going on with him." He shrugged, small and evasive. "But I don't have enough to say what yet. So I'm not saying it."

Which set the whole corner turning the same uneasy question over: whether the old rat was, in any sense they could trust, actually on their side.

---

"He's deceptive," Yurislav said slowly, out of his shadow. "The Overseer. But not — it doesn't feel like he lies to *hurt* us. It feels like the opposite. Like he's lying to *protect* us."

"From what?" Mae asked.

No one had an answer.

"He's the hardest read I have ever had in my life," Tallandra said. "And I read people for a living. Clearly he's being deceptive. He's not telling us everything. But it *switches.* There are points where he's completely truthful, and then completely deceptive. It's the hardest thing to read."

"There's real deception in him," Carrio agreed. "I feel it too. I just can't make it add up to *malice.* That's what won't sit."

"Can anyone read the Witnesses?" Mae asked. "Because I'll tell you the strange part. I didn't get an *aggressive* read off them. The strange part is that I got a read at all."

"Self-protective," Yurislav said. "That's what came off them. They were hiding — guarding something."

"Horror read as almost nothing *but* deception," Tallandra said quietly. "Near total. Barely a true thing in it."

Brickett looked around the circle. "You all just said four different things," he pointed out. "Didn't you notice? You each read something *different.*"

Gregory sat up a little. "That may be worth formalizing," he said. "If we each perceive a different layer, and we pool them deliberately—"

"Matthieu reads patterns," Brickett supplied, warming to it. "Gregory reads symbols. Tallandra reads lies. Yurislav reads what people are *feeling* underneath. Carrio reads *people* — what they want, where the seams in the act are, what you could push on." He beamed, pleased with his map.

"Some of these gifts," Brynn observed dryly, "are a good deal more use than others."

Rhün rumbled agreement from the dark, and did not offer what *his* was — because, Addie suspected, he genuinely didn't yet know.

---

"And I," said Brickett, "can read Mother."

The corner stopped.

"Brickett," Matthieu said carefully, "Mother left you one gift and one note. That's — that's the whole of what you've told us."

"No." Brickett shook his head, matter-of-fact. "She leaves me notes every *day.*" He caught their blank faces and tried again. "When *you* say note, you mean a piece of paper with words on it. She left me one of *those* because it had instructions. The rest are little things. Things you all miss." His paws sketched the shapes of them in the dark. "Extra rubbish piled up just so, pointing me toward a safe doorway. A candle somebody left burning that wasn't there before. A light down one alley that means *don't take this one.* Ordinary stuff, nudging me. Away from the bad thing. Toward the warm one. She's been doing it my whole life. That's how I know it's her."

"And the written one?" Addie asked, very still.

"That one was different. That one had *proper* instructions." Brickett's voice dropped. "Specific ones. About saving *you.*"

Addie's breath caught, because in all these weeks it was the first time the boy had come out and *said* it — that Mother, whoever or whatever she was, had told him in *writing* to keep Adeshka Sonntag alive.

Brickett realized what he'd let out about half a second too late, and both small paws flew up to clamp over his own mouth.

"*Dammit,*" he said, through his fingers.

---

They pressed him, because he'd already spilled too much to gather it back up cleanly.

"I can't tell you all of it," Brickett said, hands still half over his mouth, muffled and miserable. "I *can't.* But the note was — it was clear. I was supposed to get attached to Miss Sun-Tag. I was supposed to be — *pivotal.* In something coming up. That without me, you lot don't make it." He swallowed. "And the very first thing I had to do—" he looked at Addie "—the first thing I had to do was stop my namesake."

"Your—" Addie frowned. "Your *name?*"

"My whole life," Brickett said, "I thought my parents named me after a charcoal briquette and just couldn't spell. *Brickett.* I thought it was a *joke.*" He gave a small, cracked laugh. "It wasn't. I was named after the *brick.* The one that was going to come down on your head. The one I was put in the world to *stop.*"

And it landed on Addie like the brick itself. That ludicrous first moment — the flailing beaver kid hurling himself between her and a falling stone, *my life for you* — the single most absurd entrance of her entire life. It hadn't been chance. He'd been *sent.* He'd been named, before he was even born, for the exact piece of masonry he was going to catch, so that she would be alive to be standing in this swamp tonight.

She didn't know whether to laugh or weep. She did a little of both.

## Backstabbers

Mae leaned in.

"Brickett," she said, and her voice had gone warm, and smooth, and gentle, which was precisely when Addie's nerves prickled. "You've already told us so much. You told us about the pack. About where you found her. About Skyrend Peak, before the scrolls even lit—" she said it as though he had, as though these were settled things "—so really, what's the harm in the *rest?* You're among friends. We're all on the same—"

"He didn't say all that," Yurislav said.

"—same side," Mae went on, and Tallandra was nodding along now, and Matthieu was murmuring a soft *that's right, you did mention* — the three of them closing a gentle, coordinated net around a fourteen-year-old, walking him toward doors he hadn't opened —

"*Enough.*" Yurislav's voice wasn't loud. It stopped the corner dead all the same. He unfolded to his full white-furred height. "Mother told him not to say anything. You don't know the consequences. He'll tell us when he's ready." He looked at Mae, and only Mae. "We need what he knows. I understand that better than any of you — I've *been* the one everyone needs a thing out of. But we are not going to *manipulate a fourteen-year-old* into betraying the one instruction that has kept him alive his whole life. We're not. I don't care how much we need it."

"We might *all die,* Yurislav," Mae said, cold now, the warmth gone in an instant, "if we're squeamish about—"

"Then we die having not done *that.*" He was already reaching down for Brickett, one huge hand gentle on the boy's shoulder. "Come on, little guy. We'll sleep on the far side. Somewhere we don't have to keep one eye open for backstabbers."

And there it was, out in the open at last — the first real line drawn down the middle of them. Yurislav, who put his body between the powerful and the ones who couldn't defend themselves. And Mae, who would bribe, and press, and lie, and cross any line at all, if the thing on the other side of it was worth enough.

---

Yurislav and Brickett went, and the corner they left behind felt poisoned.

Gregory looked ill with himself — he'd leaned into the coaxing, or nearly, and knew it. Brynn had started to hum, low, the way she did when the emotional weather in a room had gone badly wrong. And Addie sat in the dark feeling frankly *gross* — because she had wanted it too, God help her, she had wanted to know what Brickett knew badly enough that she hadn't said a word to stop it.

She made herself a quiet promise, there on the damp matting. However much she needed what was in that boy's head — and she needed it — he had been put in the world to save her *life,* and he was very possibly the purest soul among the whole scheming lot of them. If it came to a line, she was on Yurislav's side of it. She decided that plainly, so she couldn't pretend later that she hadn't.

She tried to sleep.

She couldn't, because a few feet away in the dark she could hear Mae breathing — hard, even, furious, and wide awake — the sound of a woman who had been told *no* and had not remotely accepted it.

*Well,* Addie thought, staring up at the reed roof, trying to make it a comfort, *at least she isn't sharpening a knife.*

And her tired, treacherous mind, unhelpful to the very last, offered up the worse thought right behind it — the one that kept her awake a long while after:

*What does poison sound like, when someone works it into a blade?*
