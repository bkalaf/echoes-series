## To The Ten

They walked toward the beacon on no sleep and less talk.

Yurislav and Mae had not exchanged a word since the corner of the shelter had gone poisonous, and neither of them looked likely to start. The rest of the group had arrived, without discussing it, at the same unspoken treaty: get through the beacon first. Whatever had cracked open between the two of them last night would keep until they were out the other side of whatever the day held.

"I hate this," Addie admitted, low, as they went. "The seams. The visions. I hate walking into them."

"You're the Head Moderator," Gregory said, not unkindly. "And the continuity piece. You're the only one who's carried memory across all of them. We can't do it without you, and I'm sorry for that, because I can see it's costing you."

She didn't argue. It was true, and it was still awful.

Pollyr Fen was quiet around them. That was the strange thing. After the broadcast and the towel and the whole shrieking circus of the last two weeks, she'd expected to be gawked at — and instead the city gave them room. No crowd pressed in. Guards inclined their heads and stepped aside. It was deference, not fear, and it had the feel of a place that had already been told something the companions themselves hadn't been let in on. The whole wetland made way for them, gently, and said nothing.

---

They started noticing the offerings a few hundred steps along.

Food, set out on clean reed mats at the edge of the boardwalk. Bowls of fresh water. Small carved things. A frogfolk elder pressing her webbed hands together in a silent blessing as they passed. Little folded notes weighted down with river stones.

At one crossing, chalked onto a dry board above a nursery pool, someone had written: *To the Ten, good luck. — CotD.*

Mae saw it, and laughed — a small, surprised, unguarded sound.

The whole group turned to look at her.

"Children of the Damned," she said.

It wasn't triumphant. It was something softer and more startled than that — a woman watching a thing she'd flung out in fury eight hours ago come back to her already worn smooth by other hands, already a *word* the city used, a sign for the ten strangers who had knelt in their graveyard and given something back. *To the Ten.* Addie watched her file it away somewhere careful, and they walked on.

---

The beacon platform rose out of the temple wetland on pale stilted stone, and the moment all ten of them stood on it, the ground gave a low confirming rumble that ran up through their boots — *right city.*

"How much," Yurislav said, to no one, "do you want to bet it's that damn—"

"That damn *rat?*" The Overseer peeled out of a shadow that had no business holding a shape, cane already tapping. "Well. That damn rat is here."

He swept them a small, mocking bow. "Congratulations on your waypoint, companions. You have correctly discovered that the lovely city around us is your third waypoint. One more to go, and then you, Miss Sonntag—" he pointed the cane at Addie "—get to face the Witness of Horror." And then, apropos of nothing, he pulled a face — hunched, clawed his fingers, made his eyes enormous, did the whole campfire-ghost-story bit, entirely and deliberately absurd.

"He certainly finds himself funny, doesn't he?" Brynn observed, thick and dry.

"Sweetheart," the Overseer said, dropping the face at once, "when you've seen what I've seen, you take your laughs where you can. Even if you're the only one laughing." He struck the cane against the stone. "Now. Shall we?"

The world folded away around them into the grey between-place.

## Same As Before

The Scene Between Stories was as it had been — the vast grey nowhere, and the ring of the Witnesses around its edge. But this time only one of them burned fully present. Horror loomed active and enormous in the near dark, and the other seventeen hung dim behind her, distant shapes, watching without watching.

"*We were taking betsss,*" Horror said, the words dragging through pincers, thick and wet and wrong, "on whether you'd make it thisss far." Her many eyes moved over them. "You've found the city at your backsss. Very clever. But to face *me,* little Moderator, you need the *legendary object.*" A sound that might have been a laugh. "Do you have the right one? Or are you chasssing a wild goose?" Her head tilted. "Same little game as before, to see? Each sside picksss one who knows the story?"

Addie looked around at the nine faces beside her. "Any objections?"

There were none.

"Same as before," Addie said.

---

"Overseer choosesss first," Horror said.

The Overseer did not even pause. "Major Tallandra."

Tallandra's head came up, genuinely startled — she had spent every prior seam expecting to be left in the support layer, the one who watched the exits, not the one pulled into the dark. She stepped forward anyway, because she was Tallandra, and being startled had never once stopped her from doing the next thing.

"My choice," Horror purred, "was made before you walked in." Her gaze swung, and settled, and gleamed. "This selfish little playboy. Step forward, Prince of the Embercoil."

Carrio chuckled — light, easy, unbothered — and stepped up beside Tallandra. But Addie had spent weeks learning to read him now, and she caught what lived under the chuckle: nerves, real ones, pulled tight. Tallandra stood beside him rigid with the specific stillness of a soldier holding herself together over an injury and a fear at once.

Addie took a hand of each of them, and squeezed, hard, and made them both look at her.

"We're ready," she said. "Do your worst."

---

"My *worst?*" Horror's amusement was awful. "I've already done that. You cannot change your past." The pincers clicked. "But I can show you the two visions I've been *assigned* to."

Addie caught the word and carried it straight into the back room of her mind, where she kept the things that didn't fit. *Assigned.* Not *chosen.* Not *my* visions. Visions she'd been *given,* handed, made to show — by someone, or something, above even a Witness. Horror was not, whatever else she was, entirely free.

There was no time to turn it over. The grey was already dissolving into somewhere else.

## José And Noel

They stood in a room at the top of the world.

It was walls of glass on every side, and beyond the glass a city — if it was a city — that Addie's mind refused to accept. Towers past counting, needle-thin and impossibly tall, marching to every horizon in a grid too vast and too regular to be anything grown; and everywhere lights, rivers of them, a cold electric brilliance with no torch or moon behind it, burning indifferent all the way to the edge of sight. It was beautiful and it was wrong, and the two witnesses beside her flinched from the sheer *scale* of it, from a place built by hands that had known things the world had since unlearned.

Inside, behind a great pale desk, sat a man. Immaculate, wealthy, cold as clean glass, entirely at his ease above the endless city he was clearly in the business of deciding to abandon. And across from him, small in a chair too large for the moment, sat José — the man from the council table, the man from the drowning pier, and Addie's stomach dropped, because she was about to learn where the drowning pier had come *from.*

He was already frightened.

"You're firing me now?" José said.

"Yes." The cold man didn't blink. "You've already set this up. I no longer need an assistant." He steepled his fingers. "I have people of actual value to bring with me. And I'll be taking your seats."

*Seats.* Addie understood it instantly, and hated that she did — passage on the great ships. Places on the arks that had pulled away from the screaming pier. This immaculate man was stripping José's family of their place in the lifeboats, over a desk, above a glittering doomed city.

"My family," José said. "At least — put us in some lottery, some system, I *helped build this*—"

"No. You bring no value to the new group." A small, patient pause. "What value can you offer me?"

"You—" José's voice cracked. "You *asshole.* I did *unspeakable* things for you. I handled every filthy piece of it you didn't want your own hands on—"

"You were the intermediary." The cold man conceded it the way one concedes the weather. "And I no longer need an intermediary. You and your family would be *feeders.*" He said it gently, which was the worst of it. "I allowed you to come because you were useful. This is just efficiency at work."

José began to cry. He stood, to leave.

"See," the man said, "this is why you're pathetic. You do not even try to negotiate. You simply want to cry and walk away." He let José get to the glass. "There is one thing you could do. Do it, and your family's seats are yours again."

José stopped. His shoulders dropped. "Of course there is one thing," he said, bitter, wet. "What would that be?"

"I need two people to develop marriage problems." The man's voice never changed. "Hans, and Frank. Frank is unhappy — Hans is *consumed* by the project, and a neglected man is a vulnerable one. You'll find someone. A companion for hire, to seduce Frank. It must happen on a particular date, at a particular hour, which I will give you." He tilted his head at José's confusion. "I need him out of the way. He is going to create problems for future plans I have."

And Addie, watching, felt the cold reach of it — a scheme aimed at *later,* at plans this man would not name, laid down here in a glass tower over a dying world.

"And consider the larger point," the man went on, expanding, obscene. "We are rescuing the *species.* So — the non-breeders. If we are rescuing the species, what value would they really offer?"

Beside Addie, Tallandra had gone stone-still, and Addie knew what she was reading, because Addie could read it too without the gift: the flat, reasonable, patient voice of a predator who had decided cruelty was simply arithmetic. They exchanged one look. *We have both seen this exact man before, in smaller rooms.*

José didn't refuse. That was the thing Addie would not be able to forget. He was disturbed — she saw it move across his face — and he did not refuse.

"There's one more thing," the man said. "No extra civilians. I need more space. Delay transports, misroute shipments, create inefficiencies. Whatever language helps you sleep. Tell *no one.* Do all of it, and your seats are guaranteed."

José looked at the floor for a long moment.

Then he nodded.

And through all of it — the firing, the tears, the seduction, the sabotage, the quiet murder of thousands by *inefficiency* — Carrio had not once looked at the vision. Addie noticed it and filed it. He stood with his back half to the whole terrible scene, staring instead, fixed and unblinking, at the towering shape of Horror at the edge of the grey. Reading her. Reading her the entire time.

*Good,* Addie thought. *That might be worth more than the vision.*

## The Well of Souls

The glass tower dissolved, and a throne room built itself out of the grey, and Addie's first thought was *Aquila Matara again* — the same young-capital feel, the ceremony rising before the city was finished.

Then she looked out the windows, and it wasn't.

There was no sea. No coastline, from any window, on any wall — only land, dry land running to a dry horizon, and half-raised scaffolding, and the bones of a city still going up. Landlocked. *Wrong.* And Addie felt the floor of her own certainty tilt, because she had walked away from two prior visions *sure* she'd been standing in Aquila Matara, and now she wasn't sure of anything — two thousand years could move a coastline, rename a city, reshape what a tired frightened witness assumed she was seeing. She'd been reading her expectations onto the dark.

There were only two figures in the room. Young Dovren — unmistakable, unbent, at ease on a throne that wasn't finished yet. And, across from him, a small ratlike shape leaning on nothing, that Addie knew and did not know: the Overseer. The same crooked silhouette. But *younger,* somehow, softer at the edges, and — this was the part that unsettled her most — *emotional.* This Overseer had feelings in its voice. The one she knew had bled all of that out a long time ago.

Watching this one, she understood she was seeing where the flatness had come *from.*

---

Dovren rose and crossed to the windows, hands clasped behind him.

"I'll say this," he said. "You surprised me. You actually honored our deal. I didn't think you had it in you." He turned. "But we have a *problem,* because I did not agree to the way you *executed* it, and you did not tell me your whole plan before you set it running. And now there's unrest in the streets, and it is getting dangerous. Too much has changed too fast. We don't stand a chance unless you and I come to an understanding."

The Overseer's small shoulders sagged. "I fear you are right," it said, and there was real weariness in it. "I miscalculated the balancing of populations."

"*You think?*" Dovren stopped looking out the window and looked at the rat.

"Nearly every one of the twenty-four cities is tearing itself apart over who rules it," he went on. "Because you divided them into neat *thirds* — a third of one kind, a third of another, a third of the third — and people are not stupid, they found the one thing they had in common and they *sorted* themselves by it overnight. You made the division *easy.* You practically drew them the lines." He shook his head. "I could have told you this would happen. I could have *helped.*"

"You're probably right," the Overseer admitted. Then, flatly: "Truth is, I don't trust you, DJT."

"And you probably shouldn't." Dovren's smile had no warmth in it. "But we need to fix this."

He spread the rest of it out — the borders that had never been drawn, the all-out land-grab that had erupted between every city, each one seizing what it could. All but one. "There's a single city not grabbing," he noted. "Not out of virtue. It's too busy *expelling* one of its own peoples to reach for anything. Which will leave it soft, later, for whoever wants it." He looked at the rat with something like accusation. "And your *well.* Your well must be full to the brim by now."

The Overseer went still in a new way. "I will struggle," it said quietly, "to empty it."

Addie didn't understand the *well.* But she understood the way the small creature said it — the way you'd speak of a dam with the water rising behind it — and she understood *pressure,* something to do with the dead, or the waiting, or lives that had nowhere yet to go. She filed it whole and unexplained, next to *assigned,* next to *seats,* next to a woman in a bright vest saying *let them live in my keeping.*

---

"So we build more cities," Dovren said. "We give them room. Let the beasts split off, and the other lot — what are you *calling* the other lot?"

"Mythos," said the Overseer.

"Half-breeds."

"I don't *like* that," the rat snapped, with more heat than the current Overseer would ever have shown. "They are not half-breeds."

"They are whatever is *easiest to weaponize,*" Dovren said, "and *half-breeds* does the job. That's your trouble, you know." He tapped his temple. "You do not understand people or politics enough to know how you weaponize against and how you weaponize for. It's the same lever. The whole *point* is to split them, and hand each half a reason."

Beside Addie, Carrio inhaled slowly through his nose. She saw the line go into him like a hook — the prince who read people, who lived by exactly this arithmetic of leverage and division, watching the man who had *invented* the modern version of it lay the mechanism bare. He would carry that line out with him. She was sure of it.

"New cities buy us everything," Dovren pressed. "Relief. Growth. They drain the pressure off your *well.* They cool the fighting. They let the peoples come apart cleanly instead of at each other's throats."

"Cities are not arbitrary," the Overseer said. "The first ones were *placed* — set at exact points, to focus the Conjunctions and hold the beacon network together. I can't simply found a city wherever the map has a gap. They need the right ground, the right sky, the seed of power, the linkage. Hidden things. Things that have to be *there* before anyone arrives."

"Then *determine* it." Dovren waved a hand. "Locations, logistics, all of it. And tell me — how much of the real work can you do *quietly?* Without making the truth obvious to the people who think they're building it themselves?"

The Overseer understood him then. Addie watched the understanding land on the small creature — the enormity of what he was proposing. Cities that would *look* founded. Peoples who would *believe* they had raised their own walls, when in truth the bones of the place had been set waiting for them, hidden, ahead of time.

"Like ordering a thing from the old world," Dovren said, glib and grinning, "and having it turn up at your door in two days."

It should not have been possible to make a horror sound so *small.* He managed it.

"I'll gather the twenty-four leaders," he went on. "Encourage the restless ones to leave — go and *start construction* on the new cities. They'll think it's opportunity." He was already somewhere else, already counting. And Addie could see the two of them wanting the identical plan for opposite reasons — the rat reaching for it to *stabilize,* to correct a wound it had made; the man reaching for it because a scattering people was a people you could carve into factions and clients and collaborators.

---

"Have you checked the quantum side of things?" Dovren asked.

"As much as I feel comfortable checking." The Overseer sounded reluctant. "It isn't *predictive,* DJT. I keep telling you that. It's a possibility-check. It tells me whether we're moving in a *promising* direction. It does not tell me how to *correct* course when we aren't."

"Then I don't see the *use* of it." Dovren's lip curled. "I'd rather have a thing that says yes or no."

"You want a magic eight ball," the Overseer said. "It is not that simple."

Addie didn't know the words. *Quantum. Magic eight ball.* They meant nothing to her, the way *Ancient Greece* had meant nothing, the way the glass tower had meant nothing — unplaceable fragments of a world that had somehow spoken before hers. She saw Tallandra frown at them too. Only the shape of the argument came through: the man wanted certainty, and the rat was trying to explain that what it had wasn't certainty, only a hint at whether they were headed somewhere survivable.

"I don't understand why you waste resources on it," Dovren said.

"Because we get *one attempt,*" the Overseer said, sharp now. "One. If we fail, there is no going back to a saved point and trying the passage again. There is no *restore.* So I would like to know, as best I can, whether we are walking toward the cliff."

"I already told you," Dovren said, "I don't care."

And Addie watched something go out of the small creature. A light dimming. The warmth in its voice thinning toward the flat, affectless thing she knew — as if arguing with this man, and losing, was the exact process by which the Overseer she'd met had been *made.*

---

"Quelling the fighting will be the hard part," the Overseer said, tired.

"Then do what I did in the old days," Dovren said. "Bribe them not to. Pay them to hold. It buys time — and time thins the cities, splits the peoples, lets the factions set like plaster. That's all we need."

The Overseer was quiet a moment. "And if none of it holds," it said, "then worst come to worst — the world is still new. We could start over."

"*Start over.*" Dovren rounded on it, and for once his objection had real weight under the poison. "And how many times would we start over again until it's perfect? Or just until it's *okay?* You'd have us in a loop forever, wiping the board and setting it again, and we'd never get a single thing to *stand.* No. We go *forward.*" He was right, narrowly, and he clearly relished being right. He dropped back onto the unfinished throne with an ugly, satisfied smile. "You know, I never thought I could work with you. Not after what you did."

The Overseer's voice went cold — the first breath of the flatness Addie knew. "I did what was necessary. I've told you the story. He would have killed us all."

"No," said Dovren. "He would have killed *you.*"

And the Overseer turned.

It turned away from Dovren, all the way around, and looked *directly at the three of them* — at Addie, at Tallandra, at Carrio, standing where no one in a memory should have been able to see. Its small dark eyes found Addie's and held them, and the wrongness of it crawled up her spine, and then, slowly, deliberately, it raised one small paw and *waved.*

Then, still holding Addie's eyes, it spoke back over its shoulder to the young king.

"That is the exact same thing. If he had killed me, we would have all died." A pause, small eyes glittering straight into hers. "In fact, we still might."

And it stepped sideways into a shadow and was gone.

Behind it, alone on his half-built throne, staring at the empty space where the rat had been, young Dovren said, as the whole vision began to come apart:

"What the hell does that fucking bitch *mean* by that?"

---

They came back into the grey nowhere, the three of them, and it was silent, and it stayed silent.

Horror loomed and said nothing. The Overseer was not there. Addie waited as long as she could stand and then said, to the dark, "Okay. Hello? Are you going to start?"

Nothing answered.

And then the Overseer stepped in from the edge, late, brushing something off his sleeve. "I apologize for my tardiness," he said. "There was one thing I had to take care of." He glanced at the ten of them, and something passed behind his eyes. "I've a thing to say to you — privately. We'll talk, after."

"*Now,*" said Horror, slow, the pincers clicking, her many eyes sweeping the whole gathered group, "that all the companions are here—"

Addie saw Brickett's ears go straight up.

She saw it, and she didn't understand it, and she filed *that* too — the small beaver going suddenly, sharply alert at four ordinary words, the way he'd gone alert at something similar once before. *All the companions.* As if the count were a joke only he was close to getting. As if there were one more of them in the room than anyone had bothered to name.

Then the grey came apart, and dropped them back onto the pale stilted stone of the Pollyr Fen beacon platform, into the wet green morning, at the Hour of the Waning Crescent with the sun climbing on toward the Iron Foundry — ten exhausted people carrying more answers than they'd left with and far more questions.

"The thread's here," Carrio said, with a certainty Addie didn't yet know to question. "In Pollyr Fen. Come on — this way. I know where we're going."

And he set off, ahead of them, leading — though Addie would only understand later that he was leading them somewhere other than the shelter, and had not the slightest intention of telling them why.
