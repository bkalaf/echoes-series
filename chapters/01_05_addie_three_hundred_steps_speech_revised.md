# The Three Hundred Steps

*Book 1 · Chapter 01_05 — Aquila Matara · Hearthwake 2, Stonewake*
*Protected fragment B01.PROTECT.SPEECH.01 — long-form public speech, anchored by "I honestly don't know."*

---

The crowd was quiet. That was the thing that struck her first — not the size of it, not the press of faces filling the plaza and climbing the lower stairs and spilling back toward the harbor, not the signs, not the portal still cooling the air at her back. The quality of the quiet. She'd read enough accounts to know what a frightened crowd sounded like before it became something worse, and this was it: the breath a city holds between the end of the last thing it understood and the start of whatever comes next.

Above her, the white stone of the Citadel took the last of the light. Below her, the Beacon burned its color into the dusk, and thousands of people kept glancing at it before they let themselves look afraid.

She looked at the red-haired woman standing at the front of it all. Still there. Still holding her ground. Addie registered the fear in her eyes and noted, with something close to respect, that it hadn't moved her feet.

Beside Addie, a soot-smudged beaver kid gripped a fold of her sleeve like it was the only fixed object in the world. A spark jumped off his knuckles — nerves, not intent — and he smothered it against his own cloak before it caught. She let him hold on. She wasn't going to be the one to tell him to stop.

She looked over her shoulder.

Major Tallandra H. Highwatch stood on the steps above her, wings drawn close, a brace strapping the left one flat, her expression a blade with nothing behind it. Eagle-headed, blue-and-silver, a career's worth of threat-reading in the tilt of her skull. She stood for the Concord Guard, which meant she stood for the Crown, which meant she stood for the idea that order, maintained at sufficient cost, is indistinguishable from peace.

Verrick stood beside her. Soft-footed and precise, with the specific stillness of people who have spent long careers saying nothing while making certain they were present when everything happened. The Speaker's man. Addie considered that a meaningful distinction — the Speaker was one person; the Conclave was seventy-two cities — and suspected almost no one else in this plaza currently had the bandwidth to parse it. The people a person chooses to send to stand on steps at critical moments say a great deal about what that person wants to happen next.

And Addie stood in the gap between them and the people they governed.

*Schism,* Tallandra had called her, earlier, in transit — deployed as shorthand, a category, a tidy container for the ones no one had fully managed yet. The forgotten and the overlooked and the ones who'd learned to move sideways through structures that were never built to include them. Her guild. Her people. And, yes: the ones who'd learned to manipulate from the shadows, because the shadows were the only architecture they'd ever been handed.

She had a few things to say about all of it.

She turned back to the crowd, and she didn't project. She let her voice carry on its own, the way it did when she wasn't performing — when she was simply telling the truth at a volume that matched the size of the room.

"My name is Adeshka H. Sonntag," she said. "I know you know that. It's been moving through this crowd for two hours now, attached to about fifteen different theories, and I'd love to hear every one of them, because I suspect some are more accurate than what I've been told officially."

A sound moved through the front of the crowd. Not laughter yet. The thing that comes just before it — something held, beginning to loosen.

"Yesterday morning I woke up in a transit dormitory, because I'd traveled overnight for a research appointment. The hair color I'd been trying to fix for three weeks was still wrong. I had seven hours of archival work ahead of me, and I was trying to decide whether the bread in the canteen was worth the line." She paused. "It wasn't. For the record. It never is."

The laughter came now. Quiet, half-surprised at itself.

"And then the Beacon lit."

It stopped.

"I'm going to be precise about what I am, because you deserve precision right now more than you deserve comfort. I'm a Conjunction Historian. Three degrees — astrophysics, sociology, anthropology — from the Volgrad Institute, which is not a prestigious institution, because Volgrad is not a prestigious city." No apology in it. No self-deprecation. A plain statement of geography. "It's a working city. It has a working university. People study things there because the things interest them, not because the credential opens a particular door."

She glanced back, once, at Verrick.

"The Conclave considers conjunction studies a dying field. I was told that explicitly, by a senior funding committee, four years ago, when my grant was denied for the third cycle running. The phrase they used was *academically non-productive.* The phrase I used in reply is not appropriate for this crowd." Another ripple. "The point being: whatever is currently running this process disagrees with the Conclave about the value of my work. I find that deeply satisfying, and I'd like the record to reflect it."

Then her voice changed. Not louder. Heavier.

"I don't yet know what this title means in practice. I don't know its scope. I don't know what I'm permitted to do or forbidden from doing, because the scroll was written in a dialect that hasn't been primary in four hundred years, and the three translators sent this morning each produced a different reading and then argued for two hours instead of resolving it." She looked at Verrick again. "Which I'm told is consistent with the institution's general methodology. So I suppose I shouldn't have been surprised."

Verrick's expression didn't move. His eyes did.

She looked down, then. At the beaver kid still fisting her sleeve.

Her free hand found the pendant at her throat — the small silver disc her mother had worn, and her mother's mother, a thing that had been in her family so long no one had ever thought to explain it. She had worn it her whole life without a name for the mark pressed into its face.

She had a name for it now. She'd had it for exactly a day. *The Mark of the Orbs.* The kid had told her — this morning, in the back of a pawn shop, soot on his paws — had said it plainly, the way you name a thing everyone already knows, the same mark stamped into the clasp of the pack some stranger had left him. And she had stood there realizing she had never once heard it spoken aloud in twenty-nine years of wearing it against her skin.

A homeless child. Abandoned by every single person who was ever supposed to keep him. Who had walked out of nowhere into the worst day of her life and put his own small body between her and a death she never saw coming — and who knew the name of a symbol her family had carried for generations and never named.

She didn't understand it. That was the part that climbed her spine and closed a cold hand around something. She didn't understand *any* of it — not the mark, not the kid, not why the two of them were standing on the same stone under the same convergence with the same sign between them. She only understood, with the clean certainty she usually saved for a finding she could not disprove, that *something was moving here that she could not explain.* Something larger than a title. Larger than her career, her rejections, her opinion about bread. Larger than this plaza.

And if that was true — if something she couldn't measure had reached down and set all of this in motion — then the careful version was not going to be enough. Not the managed version. Not the one with the edges filed smooth, the one she'd half-decided to give them because it was safer.

The gloves, she decided, were coming off.

"So I'm going to tell you everything I know," she said. "All of it. Eleven years of it, right here, in front of the reporters and the recording-glass and the guards and the people on those steps — because you deserve to hear it before it gets managed into something more palatable by people who find the original inconvenient."

She let one full breath pass.

"We have been lied to about the conjunctions."

The quiet that followed was a different quiet. Deeper. More careful.

"Not in small ways. Not in the ordinary way history smooths complexity into a cleaner story. I mean we have been systematically, deliberately, and in several cases *legally* prevented from understanding what these cycles are — because the correct understanding of them is profoundly threatening to the people who benefit from the wrong one."

She tilted her face up, toward the horizon where the three moons were leaning into convergence, their silhouettes unmistakable against the failing light.

"Over my career I have disproven twenty-five events our official histories file as conjunctions. Twenty-five. Events the textbooks cite as cosmic intervention — forces beyond human agency, the machinery of fate grinding along to its own indifferent logic. Events we've mourned, built monuments to, written elegies for — and, above all, never assigned responsibility for." She paused. "Because you cannot hold a moon accountable."

She brought her gaze back down.

"What those twenty-five events actually were: localized magical catastrophes, caused by infrastructure no one maintained because maintenance was expensive. Famines caused by policies that valued export revenue over the people who grew the grain. Wars begun by single families whose third generation of power had started confusing the state with private property. Plagues that spread because the districts they started in weren't judged worth a quarantine. Collapses. Purges. Clearances of land and people our language has no honest word for — so we borrowed *conjunction* instead."

She wasn't shouting. She was reciting eleven years of findings in the voice of someone finally handed a room large enough to hold them.

"Here is what makes that lie so durable." Level. Nearly academic. More damning for it. "Conjunctions were *real.* They happened on a schedule — every few years, reliably, as far back as our records reach. And they *did* coincide with upheaval, with the kind of seismic shift that terrifies anyone who profits from the current arrangement. Power could change hands. Structures that had calcified for decades could crack open. For a brief window, the rules were different."

She let it sit.

"And then, a thousand years ago — they stopped."

Absolute stillness.

"No one knows why. I won't stand here and pretend I do. The moons kept moving. The celestial conditions stayed present. But the event itself went silent, and it has stayed silent for a thousand years." She looked at the sky, then back. "Until yesterday."

A murmur moved through the mass of faces. Not panic. Something older than panic. Recognition.

"Here's what interests me as a sociologist, not only as an astrophysicist: in the thousand years since the conjunctions stopped, we did not stop having catastrophes. We kept having famines, wars, collapses, purges. We kept doing to each other exactly the things history used to file under cosmic intervention. We simply lost the filing system." Dry, precise. "And in losing it, we had to face — briefly — the possibility that we were the cause. That the machinery producing our suffering was never celestial. It was administrative."

She turned. She looked at the steps.

"That was an uncomfortable possibility. So I'd like you to look at the institutions standing in front of you, and ask yourselves how long they've been comfortable."

She faced the Concord first. Tallandra held her gaze without flinching. Addie noted it and did not mistake it for agreement.

"The Guard exists to keep order. I'll say that plainly, because most of the drama around this subject is a distraction from the plain version. Order isn't inherently wrong. Keeping it isn't inherently violence. But I have a precise question, and I want it answered precisely: *whose* order? Weighted toward whom? Kept at whose cost?" A beat. "Because the order I've watched distribute itself across this nation for eleven years is not neutral. It has a direction. It flows upward. And the people at the bottom, looking up at it, do not experience it as peace. They experience it as weight."

She said it the way you read a number aloud in a room that has to hear it.

"That isn't any one guard's fault. But the Guard enforces those systems. And the Guard is the reason those systems have never once had to answer for themselves — because you cannot interrogate an institution that can arrest you for asking."

Above her, Tallandra went very still. Addie had learned, in a single day, what that stillness meant. She kept talking anyway.

She turned again.

"The Ruin —" and here her voice shifted, out of the clinical and into something closer, the register of a thing moved from the record into the room "— I know you. Not each of you. But I know what you're selling, because I've read every version of it across eleven cycles, and it has the same shape every time: the current structure is so corrupt that its total demolition isn't just justified but *necessary,* the suffering required to reach the future you've theorized is an acceptable price, and you've done that math on behalf of people who were never invited to the calculation."

A sharp, attentive quiet in the part of the crowd she knew was listening hardest.

"I understand why it's compelling. I understand it the way you understand a thing that is emotionally coherent and historically wrong. Radical transformation is real. It happens. But it does not arrive from the outside in. It does not come from burning the structure down and trusting that whatever rises from the ash is better than what stood there, because you *willed* it to be." She shook her head. "I've read what comes after. Every time. What comes after is whoever was best organized in the rubble. And I'd like you to think — carefully — about whether that has ever, once, been the people you claimed to be freeing."

She stopped. She breathed.

"And then there's us." Quieter. Different. "Schism. My people. The ones told there was no seat at the table, who found their way in through the window and the service door and the places where the walls didn't quite meet. The ones who learned the rules of the powerful don't reach people the powerful forgot to include — and discovered, over time, that this was a kind of freedom. That living outside the official accounting meant living outside official accountability."

She looked at the faces that were hers — wearing the expression of people hearing themselves described by someone who isn't making it an accusation, but a reckoning. The beaver kid's grip tightened on her sleeve.

"We have done real harm. From the shadows, through the gaps, in the quiet language of people who convinced themselves that operating without transparency was fair, because no one was ever transparent with us. We've moved information like currency and called it survival. We've protected our own at the cost of the principles we claim. We've looked at the corruption of the world and decided that working around it, unaccountably, was *clean* — because at least we weren't the ones who built it." A pause. "It isn't clean. We know it isn't. And I am not going to stand in front of you and pretend otherwise just because the others are worse."

The plaza held its breath.

"None of you are innocent," she said. "Including me. Including everyone on these steps. Including every faction, guild, and institution that has spent a thousand years building its own version of the truth and defending it at the expense of the real one."

She let that land.

Then something in her expression opened — like a window in a room that's been shut too long.

"I want to tell you something I think about more than almost anything in my field, that no one has ever funded me to chase, because it's the kind of question that makes powerful people nervous." She looked out over them. "Our nation's name. Erzengel. Do you know where it comes from?"

The silence turned curious now. Waiting.

"It's ancient. Older than the Accord, older than the schism, older than nearly everything we treat as foundational. The tongue it comes from hasn't been anyone's first language in so long the linguists file it as dead." A beat. "It means *archangel.* Across the seventeen languages spoken today within Erzengel's borders, that's the closest we get — archangel, the highest order of divine messenger. The one you send when the message is too important to trust to anything lesser."

She looked at the moons.

"I've always found it interesting that we named ourselves after a messenger. Not a ruler. Not a conqueror. Not an empire, or a dynasty, or a golden age. A *messenger.* Something in between. Something whose whole purpose is transmission — carrying a thing from where it began to where it needs to arrive, intact, uncorrupted, without the messenger substituting their own interpretation for the message."

Her gaze came back down.

"I don't know who named us that, or why, or whether they knew something we've spent a thousand years forgetting. I honestly don't know. But I find it notable that on the very day the conjunctions return — after a silence no one has ever explained — the title I've been handed is *Moderator.* Another intermediary. Another messenger. Something whose job, as best I can tell, is not to rule. It's to *witness.*"

Her voice dropped, and carried anyway.

"So consider the possibility that a civilization named for a messenger, governed for a thousand years in the absence of the one mechanism that ever forced it to account for itself, has spent that thousand years delivering a version of itself that was heavily edited in transit. That what we've been told we are, and what we actually are, have drifted apart. And that whatever begins tonight — whatever the moons are doing, whatever the Beacon is telling us — is the moment the original message and the edited one are finally set side by side for comparison."

Her hand was still at her throat — still resting on the mark she couldn't read, that a child had somehow been able to name. She let it go. She stepped forward, off the plinth, onto the open stone.

"Whatever is running this process looked at everything available and chose someone who has spent her whole career being told she studied something that didn't matter. Three degrees that impress no one at a formal dinner. Eleven years of rejections. A very strong opinion about canteen bread." The corner of her mouth moved. "I don't think that's an accident. I don't think it wants the Erzengel that exists on the official documents. I think it wants the real one."

She looked up at the moons.

"This is real. I want to be unambiguous. I spent eleven years building the methodology to rule conjunctions *out,* precisely so I could never be fooled by the appearance of one. What is happening in that sky satisfies every condition I have. The moons are moving. The mechanics are present. The Beacon burned the right color in the right sequence." A pause. "This is not a drill. This is not a local anomaly. This is not a political event wearing a cosmic mask. I would tell you if it were. I've built a career telling people exactly that, and I'm not going to stop tonight."

Her gaze came down.

"And I can tell you, with the full weight of what I know, that the mechanics of a conjunction do not care who you are. They don't care what you've built or destroyed, or what you told yourselves about why. They don't distinguish the Sovereign's bloodline from the person sleeping in the shelter district. They don't read our classifications or honor our exemptions." She let it land. "The conjunction arrives. And then we do what we do. We make our choices. And when the cycle turns, we do what we have always done."

Hard now, and quiet.

"We find something cosmic to blame."

She turned. She faced all three of them — Tallandra, Verrick, and the empty air where a Schism representative might have stood, if Schism had ever trusted an official seat enough to fill one.

"Not this time," she said. "I don't know what my role requires. I don't know the full shape of what's coming. But I know this: whatever happens in this cycle, we are going to *account* for it. With names attached. With decisions and decision-makers on the record. We are not going to build the next thousand years on top of a story we told ourselves to avoid the weight of what we actually did."

She faced the crowd again. Just the crowd. The frightened, grieving, volatile mass of people who'd woken in an ordinary world and watched the sky change color and did not yet know what it meant for their children, their homes, the structure of everything they'd been promised was stable.

"I don't know if we survive this *well,*" Addie said. "I won't tell you we do. Our history suggests we are not, on the whole, a people who survive well. We *survive* — we are remarkably, stubbornly, almost insultingly good at simply surviving. But *well.* With dignity. With each other intact. With something worth handing forward." A small shake of her head. "That has always been the harder thing."

She found the red-haired woman again. Held her eyes.

"What we build after this cycle sits directly on top of how we got there. On every choice we make between now and the turn. On whether we chose our fear or our dignity, when both were standing in front of us and only one of them was comfortable."

A long beat.

"The conjunction is not the enemy," she said. "The conjunction is the mirror. It shows us, at scale, what we already are. What we've always been. And then the cycle turns, and we decide what to do with what we saw."

She took a breath.

"So. That's what I have. I'm twenty-nine. My hair is still the wrong color. I skipped breakfast and I'm going to regret it. I've been handed a title I don't fully understand, by a process I can't yet explain, and I intend to understand both before I do anything in the name of either." Something shifted — not softer, exactly. More direct. "And I promise you this: the moment I know something that touches you — something real, something true, with the diplomatic smoothing left off — you will hear it from me before you hear it from anyone standing on those steps."

She looked at the steps.

She looked at the crowd.

"That's all I've got. I wish it were more. But I'd rather hand you the honest small thing than the eloquent large lie."

She stopped.

Nobody clapped. That was the thing that would follow her up the stairs — not the roar she'd braced for, but the opposite: thousands of people holding perfectly still, the way you go still when someone has finally said the thing out loud. The Beacon burned above the plaza. The beaver kid was still holding her sleeve.

Behind her, Verrick inclined his head toward the great bronze doors at the top of the Three Hundred Steps, the way you gesture a guest toward a room they will not be allowed to leave easily.

Tallandra's stillness broke. She'd made her assessment.

"Moderator," she said. "They're ready for you inside."
