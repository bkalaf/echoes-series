## Across Luminthalas

Two days had taught Addie a thing her three degrees never had: that the truth does not live in libraries. It lives in the mouths of people who have to read power correctly to survive it, and it is told, always, in the rooms official history is not allowed into.

So they had left the archives and gone where the stories actually were. Sepraday and Maskwa dissolved into a blur of other people's lives — taverns and immigrant kitchens, service corridors and market counters, back rooms and pleasure houses and the long patient queues where working people traded rumor to pass the time. And the stories had come.

Her own team had found theirs in a private house on the upper terraces, from a big northern-blooded man with fjord-cold vowels and a courtesan's easy company, who told them, unprompted and unsmiling, about the **Thread of the Norns** — three women at the roots of the world who measured out a life and cut it, fate and debt and doom, no romance in it anywhere. Carrio had understood at once why such a thing survived in a place like that: people tell the truth where the official rooms cannot overhear them. Mae had gone quiet in a way Addie was learning to read — not contempt for the workers, but the deep discomfort of a Speaker discovering how much real knowledge of the world ran through channels her Conclave had never mapped.

Gregory's team had come back from a workers' drinking-room with the **Red Thread of Fate** — told by a tired, funny woman of one of the old eastern lines, wiping down a bar, who framed it as an invisible cord tied between two people destined to find each other, however far it stretched or tangled. Gregory had been quietly humbled that the cleanest account of it he'd heard in his life came from a bartender and not a single one of his forty-one volumes. Brickett had loved it instantly, and loudly misunderstood half of it. Rhün had noticed the practical shape underneath: a thread that *joins* people pulls you *toward* someone — not necessarily toward a way out.

And Matthieu's team had come back with almost nothing, which was itself the finding — fragments of old oath-language, founding disorder, a standardization that arrived suspiciously fast, and everywhere the small cold draft of a subject people had learned not to raise. Yurislav had gone tight-jawed around all of it, because it smelled of the kind of tidy history a man like Dovren builds. Tallandra had treated the whole two days as reconnaissance: noting who lied quickly, who recited doctrine, who flinched at an old question.

Three threads. All three good. But it was Ariadne's that kept winning the argument, and Addie knew exactly why: it was the only one of the three that was not about the fight at all. It was about the way *back out.*

And she could not stop turning over a small thing from the throne-room vision — a thing she'd barely marked at the time. The way the young Dovren had rounded on a tailor over the cut of a garment, incandescent, wildly out of proportion, furious at being made to look like the soft and heavy thing he in fact was. She hadn't weighed it then. She weighed it now. *A king that petty about a tailor. And a tailor's people who guard the one story about a thread that brings you home.* It refused to feel like a coincidence.

She couldn't prove any of it. That was the trouble. But when Gregory made his case — that they should go back to the White Tower, not to repeat the failed library grind but to ask an entirely *different* question — she was the first to agree. And Mae spent the whole of Eldrane morning grumbling that she did not like building a mission on early city-state trivia she was fairly certain she already knew.

---

They came out of the Portalport together into Section 5, the arcane quarter, and Luminthalas took Addie's breath the way it always did.

It was a city built in pale marble and moonstone, terraced up a mountainside in tiers of polished white stone, and everywhere the blue — blue crystal lamps, blue glass catching the high cold mountain light, the deep sapphire glint of alchemical glassware in the supply courts around them. Above the civic roofs the domes of the great observatories stood open to the sky, and armillary spheres turned slow on their plinths, and star-maps were worked into the very paving under their boots. It was quiet in the way only a scholar's city is quiet: fountains, turning pages, wind crossing the terraces, the murmur of a lecture drifting from an open stair.

The White Tower was in Section 1, clear across the city, and Gregory had chosen the long walk on purpose. So they walked, and they talked, and the talking was the point.

"I keep running all three," Addie admitted, as they crossed a blue-lit garden bridge. "The Norns — that's fate, doom, the measured length of a thing, the debt you can't escape. The Red Thread — that's people, destined to find each other. And Ariadne — that's the way out of the maze after the monster's dead." She shook her head. "And the Norns feel too *final* to me. The Red Thread too — relational. Ariadne is the only one that's about *surviving your own victory.*"

"Why," Gregory said. Not hostile. He just wanted it clean. "Give me the *why,* Adeshka, not the feeling."

"That's the problem." She hated saying it. "I don't have a clean one. Not yet. The closest I can get is that Barnaby, and tailors, and the way Dovren lost his mind over a *garment* in that vision, and the Minotaur's horns, and Brynn's comma, and the words *back out* — they all keep pulling the same direction. Every thread of it. And I know that sounds like instinct dressed up as evidence."

"Sometimes a pattern isn't proof," Carrio offered. "But it's still the road to the next question. That's not nothing."

"It's not strategy either," Mae muttered. "I don't like betting a mission on a hunch that happens to point at political history. Especially political history."

That was when Addie noticed the hands.

A knot of citizens on the promenade had stopped, and lifted their arms, and turned their wrists — the silent gesture, the one from the imperial steps, the one from the tallow-tier. And then another group did it. And another, further on, as word of who they were rippled up the terraces ahead of them. It was not hostile. It was reverent, and unified, and it did not stop, and it made the whole company deeply uneasy.

Brynn drew the most looks, being new and being *stared at,* and she liked it least. "This," she said, in her thick Stonevein burr, "is deeply uncomfortable. That's *reverence,* that is. On their faces. And I've done nothing to earn a single soul looking at me like that."

"Agreed," Gregory said, too quickly. "We have given these people no actual reason to trust us."

"It's prophet-making language," Matthieu said, watching the wrists turn. "In gesture form. That's what worries me about it."

Addie's jaw tightened, because she of all people knew what a symbol could be turned into once enough people believed in it. "I don't like it," she said. "I've watched what happens to people who let a crowd decide they're chosen."

"Public morale," Mae tried, and even she didn't believe it, and said so a breath later. "—no. That's too clean to be applause. Nobody organized that and it's still *identical* across the whole tier. That's not morale. I don't know what that is."

Tallandra said nothing at all. She was reading the balconies, the bridge exposures, the sightlines down from the higher terraces — because a crowd that unified was also, to a soldier, a crowd that could turn.

Brickett enjoyed being adored for approximately seven seconds. Then a cluster of small children copied the gesture at him, solemn and wide-eyed, and he went pink under his fur and hid behind Rhün.

## The White Tower

The White Tower rose out of Section 1 like a held breath — white stone gone the soft grey of great age, tall windows, blue lamps burning low, and inside it the particular hush of paper and ink and cool still air that Addie's whole soul answered to.

At the reference desk, running it like a command post, sat an elderly High Elven woman, small and grandmotherly and radiating the specific authority of someone who has guarded a thing for longer than most nations last.

Her grandmotherly face took one look at Gregory, and Brickett, and Rhün, and hardened into granite.

"Not *you* assholes again."

Gregory went absolutely still.

Brickett started to beam — began, clearly, to interpret this as fame — and then watched it curdle in real time as he understood it was not, in fact, admiration.

"It took me *three days,*" the old woman said, with terrible precision, "to get the smell of soot out of the rooms you were using. Three. And we are not — " she folded her hands " — doing that today."

Rhün examined the middle distance with the air of a being electing not to volunteer whose fault the soot had been. And Brickett looked genuinely, personally wounded, as though the soot were not a mess but *evidence* — proof of how very hard he had been trying — and its being held against him was a fresh injustice in a lifetime of them.

---

Matthieu stepped smoothly in front of Gregory before Gregory could apologize them into permanent exile.

"Madam." He gave her a formal little bow. "You have my word — we are not here to occupy your rooms, or repeat any part of the mess my colleagues made. We're here to ask a question." He drew breath, and then, to the low collective horror of the entire company, proceeded to tell a total stranger far too much. "We think we were pointed to Luminthalas through what amounts to a *shortcut.* A cheat. There are beacons — you'll have seen them. Purple, then blue, then the green that's on the city now. We believe a fourth, yellow, may only come if we've found the right path. And we were sent *here,* to this city, before we understood why. We think we skipped a step. And we've come to believe the thing we're hunting is tied to the *original* founding of the country. The first twenty-four city-states."

The companions were all making very slightly different faces of *stop talking.*

The old scholar, however, had stopped looking annoyed. She was listening now, closely, her sharp old eyes moving over Matthieu's face.

"It sounds absurd," Addie said, because out loud it did. "I'm aware it sounds absurd."

"Oh," said the old woman, almost idly, turning back to her ledger. "Well. You probably don't want *Luminthalas,* then."

The whole group went quiet.

They turned, one after another, and looked at her.

"What," said Mae, from inside her hood, "do you mean by that?"

---

"I mean," the scholar said, unbothered by the sudden weight of ten stares, "that if you're after the *actual* founding — the oldest layer, the true origin of the thing — then you don't want this city at all. You want **Khembal.**"

Mae pushed forward to the desk. "Luminthalas is the regional capital. It has been for as long as anyone's kept a record. Why in the world would we need Khembal?"

"Well, that's — " the old woman began, carefully, choosing her ground, "that's rather the — "

Mae, impatient, pushed her hood back just enough.

The scholar saw the face beneath it, and Addie watched the exact moment recognition landed — watched the color leave the old cheeks, watched ninety years of institutional survival instinct snap into place. "Madam *Speaker.*" She was half out of her chair. "Forgive me — I did not — of course. Of course the White Tower can requisition the proper founding documents for the Speaker of the Conclave, any records you require, from any city, I'll have the call-slips drawn this very — "

"Stop." Mae's voice was quiet.

She reached across the desk and laid her hand, lightly, over the old woman's — and the scholar flinched as though the touch might burn.

"I didn't show you my face to frighten you," Mae said, and there was something in it Addie had not heard from her before — not the Speaker, not the blade. "I'm not here for the official answer. I have *had* the official answer my whole life. I'm here for the *true* one. And I think you're about to tell me they aren't the same thing."

---

The old scholar looked at Mae's hand on hers for a long moment.

"Are you," she said, very low, "giving me permission to say it? You, specifically. The Speaker."

Mae glanced at the companions. They looked back at her as baffled as she was — *permission for what?* — and she turned back to the desk. "Yes," she said. "Of course. Say it."

And still the woman hesitated, and Addie understood, watching her, that this was not the coyness of someone hoarding a piece of trivia. This was a person deciding whether to break an inherited prohibition older than her own long life. The pause cost her something.

"Luminthalas," the scholar said at last, "and Nox Vaelor, and the rest of the great non-human capitals — they were not capitals in the first years. Not at the founding."

"I know later cities were raised," Mae said. "After the first consolidation. Everyone — "

"That is not the part I mean." The old woman's voice steadied as she went, the way a person's does once they've stepped off the ledge. "In the first *decades,* the non-human capitals did not exist as seats of power *at all.* The original twenty-four city-states — every founding capital — was *human.* The triad was there, yes, in seed: for each one, a human people, a beastfolk people, a mythos people. But the power ran through the human city. Only the human city. The others were — attached." She let that sit. "The split into the cities you know came *after* — over the next thirty, forty years. Land-grab. Migration. Fracture. Consolidation. By around the fiftieth year the map had mostly set into the shape you were born into. And it has scarcely moved since. Not, in truth, since the Conjunctions themselves stopped, a thousand years ago."

The scale of it landed on the company one face at a time.

"That," said Carrio softly, "seems like an enormous thing to bury."

And Mae said nothing at all, which was itself the loudest thing in the room — because Mae'vyri Van'kareth knew the machinery of the present world better than anyone alive, every gear of Conclave and faction and succession, and she had just discovered, in front of witnesses, that she did not know the first thing about the machine that had *built* it.

---

"There was a pact," the scholar went on. "A law. Dovren — the first of the name — called the founding years too chaotic, too divisive, to be spoken of openly. And so the story got *smoothed.* The version you were all raised on: early disorder, a necessary consolidation, honest expansion of land and people, and at the end of it the stable, balanced seventy-two cities we enjoy today." Her mouth thinned. "What the smoothing *removed* was the plain fact that non-human power was held back — delayed, made small, made to depend on the human capitals — through the whole of the founding age. The law didn't burn every record. It did something cleverer. It made the *subject* improper. Dangerous. A thing that, raised in the wrong room, sits very close to treason."

"But your order kept it," Addie said.

"We kept it." Something fierce came up in the old face. "Elder scholar to elder scholar, down the whole long line of us. Passed hand to hand. And I will tell you the truth — half of us never knew *why* it mattered. We only knew that it *did.*" She looked directly at Addie. "Because if there is one thing I have learned in ninety years, child, it is that the things you are forbidden to talk about are almost always the exact things you *have* to talk about. A thing that harmless would not need a *law.*"

Gregory had gone quiet, and his face was doing something complicated — shame and hunger at once — as a man who had spent his life *cataloguing* history came face to face with someone who had spent hers *defying* its suppression.

"She's scarier than Mae," Brickett whispered, to no one.

Mae did not deny it.

---

"By the fiftieth year," the scholar was saying, "it was set, and it has hardly changed since the Conjunctions stopped — "

And Addie stopped hearing the rest, because the shape of it had just closed in her chest like a hand.

"Thank you," she said quickly, and drew the group a few steps back from the desk — far enough for something like privacy, close enough that the old woman could still watch them, and catch the odd word, and know she was being trusted with the sight of it.

"It's Ariadne's Thread," Addie said.

Half of them hadn't got there. She could see it.

"Listen. You don't get out of the maze until *something guides you back.* And this — " she gestured at the desk, the old woman, the whole white weight of the Tower " — this *is* the string. A story someone tried to make the whole world forget, kept alive on purpose by an order built to remember it. *That's* why we were sent to Luminthalas and nowhere else. Not for the archive. For the *tradition* — for the one place that had a structure strong enough to keep a forbidden story breathing for a thousand years." She was talking faster now. "Silence told me stories are connective tissue. This is the *proof* of it. The story is the thread."

She looked around at them.

"And does it not strike a single one of you as *impossible?* Twenty-four city-states. Appearing in clean triads. All at once. With humans as every original seat of power." She shook her head. "I have three degrees in how societies actually grow, and they do *not* grow like that. Not clean, not sudden, not symmetrical. That is not how anything real develops. That's a thing that was *drawn.* On purpose. By someone. And then, within fifty years, it fractured — and then a *law* arrived to make sure no one ever asked *why.*" She pressed the pendant flat against her chest without noticing she did it. "We can go and beat Horror. We might even manage it. But it won't *matter* — none of it will matter — if we can't find our way back out of the maze once the monster's dead. We need the thread. This is the thread."

---

As the word *out* left her mouth, the city answered.

It began underneath them — a deep thrum rising up through the mountain and the marble and the soles of their boots, less a sound than a *pressure,* the feeling of something enormous drawing breath. It built, and built, and Addie's teeth ached with it.

They couldn't see the beacon from the reference desk. But the White Tower had windows everywhere, tall glass and high skylights that had held the sick green of the phase for days — and now, all at once, the green on the clouds outside guttered and *turned.* Yellow flooded the archive. It poured through every pane at once and washed the whole vaulted room gold — the pages, the desks, the blue lamps, the polished white stone, every stunned upturned face — a warm, terrible, confirming gold.

Nobody moved.

"*Sweet hell,*" Carrio breathed.

"Well," said Matthieu, faintly, "if anyone was still wanting confirmation."

Addie stood in the yellow light and felt the checkpoint close behind them like a door. The research was over. Whatever came next would not be found in a book.

"We have to go to the beacon," she said. "All of us."

"Why all of us?" Yurislav asked.

"Because we're one group now." She said it simply, because it had stopped being a hope and become a fact somewhere in the last four days. "And I think — I think all of us have to go and stand in front of the Witnesses. Together."

Gregory had gone the specific grey of a man who had been so busy solving the riddle that he had never once let himself think about what solving it *summoned.*

"You look," Brickett observed, studying him, "like somebody folded up your soul and put it in a cupboard."

---

They were turning for the doors when the old scholar's voice caught them.

"Did I—" She was standing now, one hand at her throat, the yellow light lying across her ninety years. "Did I just help you do *that?*"

Mae crossed back to the desk. She pressed a heavy handful of gold into the old woman's hands, and closed the thin fingers over it, and did not let go.

"You don't understand what you just did," Mae said. "You have no idea. That old story your order refused to let die — the one half of you never even knew the *use* of." She held the woman's eyes. "Your order might have just saved this world."

And then the Speaker of the Conclave leaned across the reference desk and kissed the old scholar on the cheek, and the woman came entirely undone.

Brickett got there next. He barreled around the desk and threw his arms around one thin leg. "Sorry about the soot smell," he said, muffled and fierce. "It was probably me."

"Or me," said Rhün, gravely, from behind him. "I'm sorry, too."

And then they all came, each in their own voice — Gregory's reverent, precise formality; Matthieu's earnest, slightly-too-detailed gratitude; Tallandra's clipped soldier's respect; Carrio's warm courtly grace; Brynn's plain, uncomfortable, wholly sincere thanks; Yurislav's quiet trouble at everything he'd just learned about the country he'd served. And Addie last, who took the old scholar's hand and thanked her, simply, for the sheer courage of having kept a forbidden thing alive.

"I half thought it was madness," the scholar admitted, wet-eyed. "All these years. An old woman guarding a story about — about *nothing,* I sometimes feared. I never once knew it was *important.*"

"Turns out," Addie said, "stories are a great deal more important than any of us gave them credit for."

"You've made my ninety years *mean* something, child." She gripped Addie's hand hard. "Do you understand that? You have."

Addie, who had no idea how to stand under a thing that large, received it the only way she could — badly, and gently, and with her eyes stinging — and said thank you, and meant it, and let it be.

They walked for the doors, the yellow light lying long across the white floor ahead of them.

"So," Brickett announced to the group, "we're off to go meet the *Widow Spider* — with hallucination lady. Just so I've said it out loud. Anyone else the *littlest* bit uncomfortable with that?"

"Yeah, Brickett," Addie said. "I am. And I've *met* her. You all have absolutely no idea."

Brickett considered this.

"Well," he said, brightening, "I guess we're finally going to find out if you're crazy, or if you've still just got the head injury."

The great doors of the White Tower opened, and yellow-lit Luminthalas lay waiting beyond them.
