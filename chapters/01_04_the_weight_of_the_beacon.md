## The Locked Door

Addie sat in Kwame's office and stared down the lunch as though it were a casualty she was expected to identify.

She had lost the thread of time somewhere in the small hours. Kwame had left her here the night before — *stay put,* he'd said, *stay put and let the panic outside invent whatever story it needs, because it will invent one either way, and it's safer if you're not in front of it when it does* — and then, from the far side of the door, she had heard the lock turn. Not slam. Turn. Quiet and deliberate and from the outside, which was a very particular sound to fall asleep to. She had spent whatever hours followed turning one question over like a stone: was she being protected, or contained, or imprisoned, and was there in the end any real difference between the three when you weren't the one holding the key.

The light through the high window had gone thin and white and nearly vertical. Close to high noon, then. She'd been in this chair a long time.

The lunch was the worst part, because it was *thoughtful.* Fresh fruit, good bread, something bright and green, and — she'd checked twice, unable to help herself — pointedly, deliberately meatless. Someone had asked how she ate. Someone had bothered. And it had been carried in an hour ago by a guard in Concord white-and-blue, which was the actual alarm, because the Meridian Accord was a Schism house to the marrow, and a Concord guard walking its halls like he owned the floor meant the ordinary rules had already stopped applying.

"I don't think I have the constitution for a hunger strike," she informed the sandwich. "You should know that about me. It's not you."

The door did not open.

It stopped, in the space of half a breath, being a door at all. It came off its hinges in one piece with a sound like a struck bell and was slammed flat against the floor, and the flat gray daylight of the corridor poured in over the wreck of it.

Her staff was across the room, propped in the corner where she'd left it, a full and useless six feet away. Her hands threw up a ward instead — reflex, a shimmer of defensive light between her and the doorway, cast before her mind had finished deciding to be afraid.

Two armored mountains came through first. The boar-kin was broad as the doorframe, tusks capped in dull steel; the bear-kin was somehow larger, a wall with a pulse. Behind them, unhurried, came a third — eagle-kin, in armor that caught the light brighter and colder than the others', blue and silver where theirs was white and blue, a single-edged sword held one-handed and low. Her left wing was strapped flat in a medical brace. Her head turned as she entered, and kept turning — a small, constant, avian scanning, cataloguing the corners, the window, the staff, the ward, Addie's hands, in a rhythm that never quite stopped.

The room locked into silence.

It broke when Kwame arrived at a dead run, key still uselessly in his fist. "I had the *key,*" he said, staring at the door on the floor with genuine grief. "It was a good door. I *liked* that door. I hate the Concord guard, I want that on a record somewhere—"

"Feeling's mutual," the boar rumbled, without heat.

The eagle-kin ignored all of it. Her scanning eyes settled on Addie and stopped, which was somehow worse than the movement. "Adeshka H. Sonntag," she said. Not quite a question.

"Technically." Addie let the ward fade. "It's Addie, though. Adeshka is — that's a mother's name. And Kwame's. Only the two of them ever—" And it hit her there, absurdly, in the wreck of a doorway with three killers in the room: that she was down to *one.* That the only mouth left in the world that said *Adeshka* the way it was meant to be said was the trembling old man by the door, and when he was gone the name would go with him. The grief arrived without permission and she folded it flat, the way she'd folded the fear, and set it aside for later.

The eagle sheathed her sword. "Major Tallandra H. Highwatch," she said, formal now. "Concord Guard. Your detail." A short gesture. "Lieutenant Barry C. Bristleback. Lieutenant Boss R. Grizzlepelt. You'll get used to them."

"My *detail.*" Addie looked from one to the next. "Why in the world would a grant-rejected archivist need a protective detail?"

Tallandra didn't answer. She looked, instead, at Kwame.

And Addie saw the old man's face clearly for the first time since they'd broken in — saw past the outrage about the door to the thing underneath it, which was terror, undisguised and complete. Her hand moved on its own toward the little tin of emergency H&H at her hip.

"I'm sorry," Kwame said, and his voice shook. "Addie. I'm so sorry." He drew a rolled vellum scroll from his robes, its wax seal already shattered, and held it out to her like a thing that had burned him.

She looked at it and did not take it. "Kwame," she said. "I'm a nobody. You know I'm a nobody. Whatever that is, it's got the wrong—"

But she was already reaching for it.

---

The elevator carried them down through the guildhall's spine, Addie boxed in the center of the detail — Tallandra ahead, Barry and Boss to either side, Kwame small and silent in the corner. They were taking her to the Conclave, to the Seat of the 72, which meant, Addie translated privately, that she was being escorted to a marble room full of elected old men to watch them argue about the grammar of a document none of them could read.

"It arrived four minutes after the deadline," she said, reading the scroll again because reading it gave her hands something honest to do. Her eye kept catching on one line. *The Overseer will collect the Moderator shortly following the Blue Beacon.* "*Collect.*" She said it like a bad taste. "I don't like *collect.* You collect a parcel. You collect a debt. You collect a body."

Boss, the bear, gave the smallest sympathetic dip of his head.

"It's the only window the whole thing has," Addie went on, half to herself. "A Conjunction. It's the one time everybody in the world is actually equal — provided you know which rules to pick up and swing." She frowned. "Which is why the timing is wrong. The thousand-year mark isn't until next year. So *why now.*"

"I really did like that door," Kwame said, wretchedly, from his corner.

"Magic'll fix the door," Boss offered.

"Everything's fixable if you've got enough finger-wavers on the payroll," Barry said. "That's the trouble with a Schism house. All theory, no—"

"Bristleback." Tallandra didn't turn around. Barry shut his mouth.

Addie tipped her head back against the wall. "What hour is it? I've genuinely lost the plot."

"Hour of the Bishop," said Boss.

"Two hours past high noon." She stared at him. "It took three Concord guards two entire hours to locate one archivist who was *locked in a tower.*"

Boss laughed, a low pleasant rumble. "Ma'am," he said, "I dearly wish it were only three of us."

The elevator opened, and she understood the joke.

The lobby had been forcibly emptied — fifty guards in white-and-blue holding a shield wall across the great doors, and behind the wall a crowd of ordinary people pressed and terrified, and beyond the doors, out in the daylight, a sea of that same armor filling the street from wall to wall. Her name went through the crowd like a current the instant she stepped out. *Sonntag. That's her. Sonntag.* They didn't know what she was. They only knew the beacon had turned in the night, and the guard had shut the city, and the guard was hunting someone, and here she came out of the tower with a blade on every side of her.

Barry set a heavy hand on her shoulder. "We've got your back," he said, and to her surprise he meant it.

"Say your goodbyes, Guildmaster," Tallandra told Kwame. "He doesn't come with us."

Kwame took Addie's hands in both of his shaking ones. "I'm sorry," he said again. "And — saints forgive me — I'm glad. Selfishly. I'm glad it's you and not one of them." His eyes were wet. "If your mother were standing here, Addie. You know exactly what she'd tell you."

And Addie, without deciding to, said it with him, the two of them together, the old refrain worn smooth by a lifetime of use:

"*Leave the world a better place than you found it.*"

Then the detail closed around her into a tight wedge, and Tallandra gave the order, and they moved.

The crowd broke over them in whispers — *witch, traitor, it's her fault, she did this, why her* — and Boss leaned down and muttered, "You're a nobody, remember. Pay 'em no mind." Addie thumbed a calming pellet from her tin and swallowed it as they crossed the threshold, and even so the noise of the street hit her like a flat hand across the whole body.

Ahead, Tallandra's head snapped once and went still, and Addie was already learning to dread that stillness. The crowd-control line was buckling. "Blades away," Tallandra called, sharp. "Shields out. Now." She caught the arm of a lean, swift jackrabbit-kin officer running relay along the flank — no name, just speed — and the order went out through him at a sprint, threading the whole chaotic street faster than any shout could. All down the line, Concord swords went back into their sheaths and the guards put their weight behind blunt shields instead.

"I have never," Addie said, honestly amazed, "seen a Concord guard *sheathe a weapon.*"

"Concord doesn't try to kill the people it's protecting from themselves," Tallandra said, eyes already moving again. "Save the pity party, Moderator. We're not through the worst of it."

## Miss Sun-Tag

The wedge ground forward through the press of bodies. Signs bobbed above the crowd — *THE END IS NEAR* in shaky paint, and, a little further on, held aloft with great sincerity by someone who could not spell, *THE END IS NEIGH.* Addie almost laughed. It was that or the other thing.

Something small and filthy darted between the armored legs ahead.

A beaver — a *child,* a beaver-kin boy, soot-blackened and damp, a broad flat tail dragging behind him and a battered leather pack bouncing on his back. He popped up at the wedge's edge, wide-eyed, and shouted with total confidence:

"Miss Sun-Tag! Miss Sun-Tag, our mother sent me!"

"Ignore it," Barry advised.

He vanished. He reappeared atop a rain barrel a dozen feet on, clutching the pack to his chest, still sooty, still beaming. "I've got a message! From our mother!"

"I'm an only child," Addie called back, despite herself.

The escort cut hard down an alley and back out into thicker crowd, and there he was again, impossibly, balanced on a window ledge, squinting at a crumpled scrap of paper. "Miss Sun-Tag, are you — hang on—" he peered at the note "—'are you a beacon, because—'" A guard lunged and grabbed for his ankle. The boy yelped, flung out one paw, and produced a burst of fire — flashy, sputtering, more smoke than heat, and it singed a good deal more of his own whiskers than it did the guard's gauntlet. He scrambled free in the confusion, coughing, one sleeve smoldering.

"That one assaulted a Concord officer," Tallandra said flatly. "Take him."

"He didn't *hurt* anyone," Addie snapped. "He was trying to get *away* from a man three times his—"

"That's how it starts, ma'am," Boss said, not unkindly.

The boy leapt — actually leapt, off a stall awning and across two guards' shoulders like stepping-stones — and landed crouched ahead of the wedge. "I'm supposed to be attached to you!" he insisted. "I can *prove* it, I just—"

Addie saw it before any of them did.

High and to the right, out of the white glare of the low sun, a brick — a full construction brick, gray and heavy — came down at her head. Not thrown. *Aimed.* From an angle nothing on any rooftop could have thrown it, out of a place there was nothing to throw it *from.* The guards were all turned toward the boy. They would see it far too late.

The beaver child saw it too.

"**My life for you!**" he screamed, joyful and terrified, and launched himself off the cobbles straight into its path. Both small paws came up. Two fireballs — tight, overcharged, unstable — punched into the brick, and it burst, not into shards but into a soft harmless cloud of gray dust that sifted down over everyone like flour. The boy's paws smoked. His face went from triumph to horror as he registered, mid-air, that he had aimed himself directly at Addie.

He hit her in the chest like a sack of wet mortar and drove every bit of air out of her.

The whole street went silent.

Addie folded to her knees, no breath, spots swarming. The boy scrambled up, patting frantically at her face. "Don't be dead, don't be dead — I know medicine—" and attempted something that was not medicine, that was in fact closer to assault, some invented desperate pantomime of what he imagined healing looked like. To fifty watching guards it read as one thing only: a feral asset had knocked the Moderator down and was now mauling her.

Tallandra had him by the scruff of his coat before Addie could wave them off, hauling him up and back, her braced wing straining, her blade coming around. "*No*—" the boy shrieked, kicking air. "You can't — she's not allowed to die, Mother said, Mother *said*—"

Addie's lungs caught. Air rushed back in, cold and enormous.

"I'm sorry," the boy babbled, hanging from Tallandra's fist. "I saw the brick, I *panicked,* I always — I can prove I'm supposed to be here, I know the symbol! On your necklace! I know the mark on your pendant—"

Tallandra's blade found his throat.

Something happened behind Addie's eyes.

They flared — she felt it, a cold pressure, a color she couldn't see but knew was blue — and a word left her that was not shaped like her ordinary voice, that landed in the street with a weight that bent the air.

"**Stop.**"

Tallandra froze. Mid-motion, blade a hair off the boy's skin, every scanning instinct in her locked rigid — stopped, not by choice, held in place by something reaching straight past her will. The whole detail stared. Addie stared too, at her own hand, at what she'd just done and had no memory of deciding to do.

She got her breath, and her voice, back down to its own size.

"Say that again," she told the boy, very quietly. "The symbol. What did you just call it."

He swallowed, small and filthy and hanging from a killer's fist, and said it:

"The Mark of the Orbs."

## Geometry and Mathematics

After that, the detail went to maximum alert and got everyone off the street.

They took refuge in a cramped pawn shop three doors down, whose owner had — as Boss put it — "graciously donated the premises," a graciousness considerably eased by the two crossbows aimed politely at his counter. Inside, among the shelves of pledged and forfeited lives, Barry and Boss physically pinned Tallandra between them long enough for Addie to work out how to *let go* of whatever she'd grabbed. The invisible hold released. Tallandra staggered a half-step, straightened, and without a word drew her knife and a whetstone and began, slowly, to sharpen it while looking at Addie. It was not a task. It was a sentence.

Medics fussed at Addie's ribs. "When you're recovered, Moderator," Tallandra said pleasantly, over the rasp of the stone, "you and I are going to have a very detailed conversation about what you just did to me."

"Any chance," Boss murmured, leaning in, "you could do the mind thing to *me* sometime? On purpose? No offense, ma'am, but from where I stood that looked like an absolute blast."

The boy sat in the exact center of the little shop, ringed by an empty radius no one would voluntarily enter, because he smelled of wet fur and woodsmoke and river mud and freshly burned hair, all at once and powerfully. Every time he tried to stand, the lean jackrabbit-kin officer — the fast one from the street — pressed him gently back down with the flat of a polearm.

Addie crossed the radius and crouched in front of him, thinking, without quite meaning to, of her mother, who had spent every free hour she had on children exactly this forgotten.

"All right, little guy," she said. "Let's start over."

"I'm not *little,*" he objected, with enormous dignity.

"What's your name?"

He drew himself up. "You're Adeshka H. Sonntag," he announced, "Chief Moderator." Then, to her: "I'm Brickett. Brickett H." He gestured, with a strange practiced sadness, at the empty air where the rest of a name should go. "That's — there isn't a last part. You get the family part from your mother, and I never got mine. Met her, I mean. So." A shrug that had clearly been rehearsed to hurt less and did not work. "Just Brickett H."

Something went hot and tight in Addie's chest that had nothing to do with the bruise.

"I turned fourteen," Brickett added, brightening. "Yesterday."

The whole shop heard it and understood it at once: an orphaned, homeless, half-starved child, fourteen years old as of a day ago, whom the world had produced and then simply set down and walked away from.

Addie made herself keep her voice level. "Brickett. How did you know that title? *Chief Moderator.* That word doesn't exist outside a handful of sealed rooms. How did you—"

"Clear the shop," Tallandra cut in. "Core detail only. He's naming classified material in front of a pawnbroker."

"Oh, *wonderful,*" Addie said, rounding on her. "Yes. Let's manage the information flow. Heaven forbid the frightened people find out anything true when we could bottle it and ration it out through 'proper channels'—" she hooked her fingers in the air "—'information flow control,' as if a *symbol* leaked to a fourteen-year-old is the crisis today—"

"'Information flow control,'" Brickett echoed solemnly, and his small paws hooked the exact same air quotes, with the exact same weary contempt, a flawless little mirror.

The bell over the door rang. A stag-kin in Concord grays ducked under the lintel, antlers scraping, a slate of measurements under one arm.

"Sergeant Oren V. Antlerfell," Tallandra said. "Forensics. Well?"

"The brick is the interesting problem." The stag set down his slate. "It did not come from a thrower. It did not come from any rooftop on this street, or the next. I ran the scorch pattern, the dust scatter, the impact cone, and the descent angle three times, because I disliked the answer, and the answer did not change: the point of origin sits high — *impossibly* high — back into the glare of the sun, at an angle no arm and no engine on this street could produce. Nothing was standing where that brick was thrown from. There is nothing there to stand on."

"It's a brick," Addie said. "How complicated can the geometry of a falling brick—"

Oren looked, briefly and genuinely, wounded. "It is *trigonometry,* Moderator," he said. "Straightforward, honest trigonometry — scorch, scatter, angle, arc. The math is not the difficult part. The difficult part is that the math describes something that cannot have happened." He inclined his antlered head. "I would stake my sergeancy on it."

Addie shut her mouth. She had spent eleven years being the smartest person in every room she entered, and it was, she realized, going to be a genuinely humbling week. "Understood," she said. "Thank you, Sergeant. I'll take the math."

She turned back to Brickett, and the anger went out of her, and she offered him her hand.

"You saved my life," she said. "Thank you. Shake on it."

Brickett looked at the hand as though she'd offered him a live coal. "What do I do with it?"

"You take it," Addie said gently. "It means we're square. It means I see you."

Carefully, as if it might change its mind, he put his small burned paw in hers. And then his face crumpled, and his eyes filled, and he held on — because, Addie understood, no one in the whole of his fourteen years had ever once shaken his hand as though he were a person. Behind her, Boss made a wet, undignified noise and pretended he hadn't.

"So I'm hired," Brickett said thickly, recovering. "I'm attached to you now. Mother said. I'm supposed to—"

"No," Addie said. "Absolutely not. I am not adopting, hiring, or in any sense *attaching* a chaotic, incendiary, self-taught fourteen-year-old fire hazard to my—"

"I did it out of order," Brickett interrupted, stricken with a sudden professional guilt. "The Mark. I wasn't supposed to say that yet, I skipped ahead, I always skip — look. Look at the clasp. On my bag." He shoved the battered pack forward.

"He does know we can all hear him," Barry observed to Boss.

Addie turned the pack's clasp to the light — and stopped. Slowly, she reached up and drew out, from under her collar, the simple silver pendant she had worn every day of her adult life. Tallandra's head tilted; the lieutenants leaned in; and it occurred to all of them at once that not one of them had ever actually *seen* the thing clearly before, as though it had spent years declining to be looked at.

She laid the pendant down beside the clasp.

The marks were the same. Not similar. The *same* — twelve nested, interlocking geometric configurations, orb within ring within orb, matched line for impossible line. Addie had taken this symbol to the finest mathematicians and symbologists in three cities across eleven years, and every one of them had turned it over, and frowned, and handed it back, and admitted they had never seen its like and could not place it.

Here it was. Stamped into a street child's luggage clasp.

"Brickett," she said, and her voice had gone very careful. "Where did you get this."

"Mother gave it to me," he said, simply, proudly. "For my birthday. Yesterday. It's the first birthday present I ever got."

The little shop absorbed the whole weight of that sentence, and no one could think of a single thing to say into it.

Then Brickett's face filled with concern. He peered at Addie. "Wait. Did you hit your head when I — you think I mean *your* mother? The one that—" He winced with real sympathy. "No. No, I'm sorry, I didn't mean her."

"My mother," Addie said quietly, "died. Months ago."

"I know. That's not—" Brickett shook his sooty head, patient with her the way you're patient with the injured. "I mean *our* mother, Miss Sun-Tag. The one that made you Chief Moderator." He said it as though it were the most obvious thing in the world. "The one who sent me."

The pawn shop went cold.

It went cold all at once, guards and medics and geometrician alike, because every person in that cramped room of forfeited things understood, in the same breath, that the filthy, singed, forgotten child in the middle of the floor — the one they had nearly executed twice in an hour — was not a nuisance, and was not a coincidence, and was not lying. He carried, stamped into his one possession and stitched into his every certain word, the direct and personal favor of whatever vast and patient thing had reached down into the machinery of the world and set this Conjunction turning.

And Addie's private, unsolvable, lifelong little mystery had just become one small visible tooth of a very large wheel.
