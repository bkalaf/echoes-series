## Family Stuff

The dress was fine. The boots were fine. The staff was leaning against the wardrobe exactly where she'd left it. Addie had checked all three twice, which meant she was stalling, and knowing that she was stalling did nothing at all to make her stop.

She crossed to the window instead and did the thing she had sworn, on the first morning, that she would never do. She hooked one finger into the edge of the curtain and peeled it back an inch and looked.

They were still there.

Six days now. The reporters had worn a shape into the street outside Mae's manor the way water wears a channel into stone — a permanent camp of easels and folding stools and long-lensed scrying-glass on tripods, tarps strung between the ornamental trees against the morning damp. On the first day she had walked out onto the steps and answered them with her chin up, because that was what a Head Moderator did. On the second day she had answered fewer. Somewhere around the fourth she had stopped going out at all, and now here she was, twenty-nine years old and the appointed shield of the nation, hiding behind a rich woman's drapery like a child avoiding chores.

At the front of the pack, as always, were the two she'd learned to pick out by their outlines. Tamsin R. Hale, the hyena-kin — Ruin's own correspondent, the one who asked the ugliest questions in the mildest possible voice — sat on her stool with her long muzzle propped on one paw, patient as a trap. Beside her, notebook already open, sat Erin P. Callahan: polished, human, faintly drawling, dressed far too well for a street camp, the kind of correspondent who could make a slander sound like a courtesy. One a blade and one a smile, and Addie had long since given up deciding which she trusted less. Neither of them looked cruel. That was the part Addie kept snagging on. They looked bored, and hungry, and entirely willing.

Word had come down two nights ago from the Office of the High Sovereign. He would provide an escort to the beacon. It had been phrased as an honor and it was not an honor; the same message, read plainly, said that the Sovereign did not intend to let his seventy-three cities be pulled down around his ears over one archivist, and if she was going to do this she would do it under his guard where he could watch. Addie found she couldn't even resent it. If she were him, she'd want to watch too.

She let the curtain fall.

The manor behind her was quiet in the specific way a house gets quiet when the people in it have been trying very hard, for days, not to kill each other.

It had started with Carrio.

He had admitted it himself, which she supposed was something — stood in the middle of Mae's beautiful dining room with his hands open and said that yes, all right, the story of where they were going next had gotten out through one of his Red Thread contacts, and yes, he'd been the leak, and no, he hadn't thought it would spread the way it had. Mae had gone very still. And then Mae had said, in a voice Addie had never heard her use, that of course he'd thought it would spread, that he'd never in his life done a careless thing, that carelessness was the mask he wore over the choices he made on purpose.

What came after that was not an argument between colleagues. It was older than that, and worse. Addie had understood, listening to it happen, that Mae and Carrio had known each other for a very long time before any of the rest of them had existed to either of them — that whatever this was, it was the fury of a woman who had loved someone stubbornly and been repaid in exactly the currency she'd been warned about. Not romance. Nothing so simple. More like a dangerous aunt and the nephew who had learned every one of her tricks and used them back on her.

Mae had slapped him. The sound of it had stopped every conversation in the house.

And then — this was the part Addie still couldn't make sit right — it had been Mae who fled. Not Carrio. Mae had turned on her heel and swept out and up into her own wing, the study-and-bedroom-and-little-library suite where she went when the world had disappointed her, and the door had shut, and the lock had turned, and that had been the end of Mae for the better part of a week.

Max had appeared in the hall within the minute, wiping his hands on a cloth, and delivered the household's standing warning with the calm of a man who had delivered it before: no one was to go up those stairs unless they were personally prepared to come back down with fewer fingers, fewer eyes, or fewer heads than they'd started with. He'd said it to the room and then he'd said it again, slower, looking directly at Brickett.

Mae had come down yesterday. She had appeared at breakfast dressed and composed and radiant, greeted them all as though the last five days had simply not occurred, and every single person at that table had understood, without being told, that the correct and survivable thing to do was to greet her back and say nothing. Max had confirmed it in an undertone by the sideboard. *Not unless she brings it up first. She will not bring it up first.*

Brickett had taken all of it badly.

Addie had sat him down two days into Mae's silence, with Matthieu on one side and Yurislav on the other, and tried to explain gently that some people, when they were hurt, needed to be left completely alone, and that this was not a punishment and not a sign that anyone had done wrong — it was just a thing that people needed sometimes. Brickett had listened with his whole face and drawn precisely the wrong conclusion from it, which was that *he* had done something, that this careful three-person conversation was the soft front edge of being let go.

She had spent an actual hour after that convincing a fourteen-year-old beaver that she was not firing him. When logic failed she'd reminded him, a little desperately, that she couldn't fire him because she wasn't even paying him — and he'd looked up at her, wounded and earnest, and said that she was paying him in experience, and that experience was worth more than coin. Which had not helped. Which had, in fact, broken something small in her chest that had not un-broken since.

Because the worst of it was watching him now. Brickett, who could not be still, who narrated his own footsteps, who had once knocked a candelabra into a soup tureen out of sheer enthusiasm — Brickett sat these days with his tail tucked flat and controlled against the floor and his paws folded in his lap and his whole small body arranged into the shape of a person trying very hard not to be a problem. He'd gotten quieter every day. He'd gotten *good*. And every time she looked at him being good, Addie felt her heart come apart a little further, because she would have given a great deal to hear him break something.

And it wasn't only those two fault lines. Rhün and Brynn had been circling each other for days — Brynn had opinions about pets, about people who kept animals and called them companions, and had shared those opinions loudly and repeatedly in the vicinity of a dwarf who kept a great smoldering lava-beast as a mount, and Rhün had returned fire with a running commentary on stuck-up dwarves who thought a beard and a grudge made a personality. Gregory and Matthieu, for their part, had spent the week driving each other quietly insane over technicalities so small Addie couldn't have repeated a single one of them, each man apparently convinced that the survival of scholarship itself depended on winning.

Everywhere she looked there was a crack. Pairs that had fought. Pairs about to. Old hurts that a week of forced proximity had scraped raw and set bleeding again.

She stood in the doorway of the dining room, staff in hand, ready to go and lead these people toward something that might kill her, and she had the thought — unwilling, unwelcome, arriving sideways like all the worst true things did — that she had no earthly idea what siblings were supposed to be like.

But she suspected it might be this.

The thought landed in her with equal parts horror and something warmer she didn't have the courage to name yet. These impossible, exhausted, furious people. This was the thing that was happening to her. They were becoming, without anyone's permission, her family.

She tested it the way she tested everything, sideways.

"What time is it," she said, to the room.

"Quarter past," said Gregory.

"It is not quarter past," said Matthieu. "It is ten past. The bell was ten minutes ago."

"The bell is slow."

"The bell is not slow, you have said the bell is slow for four days, the bell is *fine* —"

"*Stop it,*" Addie said.

They stopped it.

From the low couch by the cold hearth, Mae did not look up from her coin. She turned it once over her knuckles, caught it, and said, in a tone of exquisite forewarning, "Do not give us a speech, scholar. Not today. I will go back in my room."

Addie looked at her.

She was so tired. That was the thing. She was tired all the way down, and she was probably going to die in a couple of hours in front of a crowd, and this woman had spent five of their last six days sulking behind a locked door over a fight with a man she loved, and Addie discovered, standing there, that she had exactly none of her usual diplomacy left in the tank.

"Speaker," she said, "would you do me a favor? Pretty please? Shut the fuck up and listen to me."

The coin stopped turning.

For a moment nobody in the room breathed. Mae looked genuinely startled — startled, and then, underneath it, something that on anyone else Addie would have called embarrassed. Mae looked down at her own hands and flipped the coin over, once, silently, and did not go back to her room.

"I don't want responses," Addie said, before anyone could recover enough to give her one. "I don't want apologies and I don't want a discussion. I need you thinking, all of you, about how to keep me alive, because in about two hours I am going to stand in front of a constellation and it is very possible I do not walk off that platform. So. I'm going to say one thing and then we're going to go."

She made herself say it.

"I had a stupid thought this week," she said. "I thought you all felt like siblings. That's the thought. Congratulations. Because that's what this has been, hasn't it — everybody lashing out, everybody dying on the smallest possible hill, everybody insulting the people they'd take a knife for, all of us acting like being tired is some kind of moral failing in the person standing next to us." She swallowed. "We've been shut in together, almost every hour, for twenty-four days. And I want to say — thank you. For lasting as long as you did. I mean it. I could not have wished for a better group of nine."

The room was very still now, but it was a different stillness.

"So if I do survive this," she went on, and let the edge of a smile in, "and any of you cannot set your bullshit down long enough to do the *laundry*, or plan the next thing, or get through one dull afternoon without a war — then trust me, you do not want to find out what it's like to have Tallandra rattling around inside your heads."

And the room laughed. Really laughed, all at once, the first real laugh in days — even Tallandra, who made a sound like a door that hadn't been opened in a while.

Gregory, still smiling, turned toward Matthieu as if to add something, and Addie pointed the staff at him without heat.

"No," she said. "No. There's no time for that bullshit. Here's what we're doing. Assume every apology's been said. Assume everyone's going to try to do better. We move on." She let it settle, then said the thing that mattered, the thing she needed them to carry down those stairs. "This is one of eighteen. If I do this — if it works — there are seventeen more. Seventeen more of these, and we do not get to wait until the last possible morning for the next one. So. Get it out of your systems now."

The laughter was gone. In its place was something quieter and more useful: a room full of people, ashamed of themselves in the good way, finally sober.

"Well said, Moderator."

Max was in the doorway with his sleeves shoved up, and he'd clearly been there long enough. "Said it much nicer than me, you asshats. And I mean you, Mae." He looked around at all of them, unhurried, damning. "You either go put down a constellation or every last one of you dies. Those are the options on the board. Pick." His eyes came to rest on Mae, and gentled about a quarter of a degree. "And *you*. If she pulls this off, you and I are going to have words."

Mae inclined her head, regal even flopped on a couch. "I know," she said. "Now get out, Bellwether, before you make me feel something in front of the children. We are trying to save the world."

Matthieu, gathering his papers, had stopped with his head tilted at the wall by the door. "That’s the fourth one," he said, to no one in particular, and pointed his rolled diagrams at the little brass bell mounted there, its clapper bound in wire and cloth. "There’s one in every room I’ve set foot in. The bath. The long hall. My own quarters, over the bed. Every last one wrapped or tied or gutted so it can’t ring." He looked round at them. "Why would a house put a bell in every room and then go to all that trouble to silence every single one?"

"They’re servant bells," Carrio said, without looking up from his gloves. "Old blood-house fashion. Pull a cord in the parlor, a bell rings belowstairs, and some poor soul comes running — so you never have to raise your voice or trouble yourself to learn a name. A house like this would’ve had them wired to every room in it."

From the doorway, Max made a sound. It began as a cough, lost the argument, and became a laugh — a big, wheezing, wholly personal laugh, the laugh of a man remembering something that was not, on its face, funny at all.

"Don’t," said Mae, without heat, "get him started."

Max got himself under control with visible effort and said nothing, which was somehow worse.

"My family used them to summon staff," Mae said to the room, in the flat voice of someone reciting the dull half of a story. "You ring; a person appears; you never once have to look them in the eye. I ended the practice the day the house became mine — which was the day they sent me here to sit with the Conclave and mind the capital’s manners. Exile, if you want the honest word, though no one in Nox Vaelor had the courtesy to use it." She turned her coin over her knuckles. "The bells stay quiet. The staff and I manage like people."

Someone — Gregory, probably — started to ask why, if she loathed them so, she had never simply had them torn out of the walls. Mae’s eyes flicked up. Something crossed her face and was gone. She flipped the coin again and did not answer, and the question died in the air the way questions did around the Speaker once she had decided they would.

Addie filed it — another old scar the people who lived with it had learned to wear smooth — and left it where it lay.

Max got out.

They gathered their things after that in a silence that wasn't the brittle kind anymore. Nobody made speeches; they'd had the speech. Instead there were hugs, and shoulder-pats, and Yurislav resting a hand briefly on Gregory's neck, and Rhün and Brynn managing to occupy the same three feet of hallway without drawing blood. Addie caught Brynn near the door with her humming trailing off mid-note — Brynn hummed when she worked, always had — caught her staring out at the reporters with a strange expression.

"I was just thinking," Brynn said, when she noticed Addie noticing, "that it's a shame we didn't bring the big idiot lava beast into the city. He'd trample that whole press pack flat and I would enjoy watching it." She frowned at herself. "The thing might be growing on me." Then, quickly, defensively, jabbing a thumb back toward Rhün: "I still hate *him*, obviously. He's a dick." A beat. "You have to hate someone in your family, right?"

And she shouldered her satchel and charged out the door first, into the noise, before anyone could say a word.

Addie followed her, smiling in spite of everything, thinking: *these people.*

## The Questions Outside

The guards took the worst of it.

The High Sovereign's escort formed up around them the moment they cleared the steps, a moving wall of crown livery, and Major Tallandra fell in at the front of it and moved through the crowd like a prow through water — tall, plumed, unhurried, visibly *pleased* to be doing something with her hands at last. The reporters were shoved back and they went, but they didn't go quietly.

The questions came anyway, over the shoulders of the guard, hurled like stones.

They were not, Addie made herself understand, merely rude. She had told herself for six days that they were rude, an annoyance, weather to be endured, and standing in the middle of it she finally let go of that comforting lie. They were vicious. There were questions about the brothel in the red-lantern district and what she'd really been doing there. There were questions about the beaver — filthy ones, cruel ones, the kind that took a fourteen-year-old orphan and made him into a rumor. There were questions about who she'd shared a bed with to get her post, and whether the Sovereign knew, and whether the whole doomed nation was in the hands of a woman who'd charmed her way up. They were vicious because the people asking them were terrified, and terrified people are hungry, and what they are hungry for is someone to blame before the sky comes down.

Somewhere in the thick of it Tamsin's voice found her, mild as milk poured over broken glass — something about the little pellets again, about a Moderator who couldn't cross a room without her tin, was it true, would she care to comment. Addie thumbed an H&H from the tin in full view of the whole street, swallowed it, and kept walking. She'd learned that much on a Skyrend street a lifetime ago: you did not feed them.

The companions walked through it and said nothing, because there was nothing anyone could say.

And then, gradually, as the guarded route bent toward the wide civic avenue and the beacon platform came into view at the end of it, Addie found the noise getting easier to leave behind. Not quieter. Easier to set down. Time did a strange thing as she walked, thinning and slowing, the way it did in the last minutes before an examination she'd studied years for.

She had a plan. She thought she had a plan. She wasn't at all sure it was the right one.

She was thinking about the Overseer, and the sideways thing he'd said to her, the clue she still couldn't hold straight in her mind. And under that — surfacing the way it always did when she got close to something she couldn't explain — the memory of the night when she was young and the moons had done what moons do not do, and her eyes had gone that impossible color, and something had moved through her that she had no word for and had never told anyone. Three moons. Three fields she'd spent her life buried in: what the world was made of, how people lived together, what had happened before. Science, society, history. It sat there in her chest like a key she couldn't find the lock for, and she knew — the way you know a storm is coming from the smell of the air — that they were the same thing, somehow, the moons and the fields, and she did not understand it and today was not the day she was going to.

She let it go. She'd carried it this far. It could wait a little longer.

The crowd thickened as they neared the platform, and then, oddly, it quieted. People pressed close but stopped shouting. There was a sense — she could feel it moving through the mass of them like a change in pressure — that they might be about to see the one thing they would tell their grandchildren about. That whatever came next, they wanted to be able to say they had been there.

At the foot of the platform Gregory stopped and swept an arm toward the steps.

"After you, Addie."

She turned and stared at him. In four weeks he had called her Moderator, or scholar, or *you there* when he was being difficult, and never once her name.

"Really, Gregory? Now?"

"You may die in a few minutes," Gregory said, entirely reasonable, "and I found I wanted to have done it once."

"You," Addie told him, "are insufferable," and she stepped up onto the platform.

## The Wrong Beginning

Nothing happened.

She stood on the platform with the red beacon rising cold and enormous above her, the whole crowd holding its breath, and the ground did not move and the light did not change and the world went on being exactly the world.

She waited. Behind her, one by one, the others climbed up too — she felt the boards take their weight — and still nothing. No rumble. No answering pulse from the stone. She walked forward until she stood at the very base of the beacon, close enough to touch it, and she stood there, and the others gathered at her back, and a full minute went by. She counted it. Brickett, beside her, counted it too, on his fingers, silently, his lips moving.

At the edge of her vision Tallandra had begun, very slightly, to shake.

"How is this wrong," someone said behind her — Matthieu, low. "Addie. Are you certain you remembered it right? Is it possible you —"

"This is where it began," she said. "For all of us. It has to be. Aquila Matara is the first city, it's the first beacon, it's where the whole —"

She stopped.

Because Matthieu had stopped too, gone rigid, and she watched the answer arrive on his face a half-second before he said it.

"It can't appear twice," he said. "A city can't be on the list twice, Addie. Aquila Matara *is* the first beacon. But not for us." His voice dropped. "It was the first city for the *nation*. It was already used. It's already been counted. That's why it's not answering — this beacon was lit before any of us were born."

And he was right. She knew he was right the instant the words left him, felt the certainty of it drop through her like a stone through water, and she lost her composure completely — felt the color go out of her face, felt her careful posture come apart, because she had been so sure, she had stood these people up in front of the entire nation on the strength of being *so sure* —

The companions felt it go, and they began to come apart with her. Brynn swore. Gregory's hand found the platform rail. Brickett made a small stricken sound.

"*Stop.*" Tallandra's voice, iron, pitched to carry no further than the eight of them. "You are on the news. Every glass in this city is on your faces. You will not — panic — in front of them."

For one heartbeat nobody moved.

Then Mae looked at the guard, and at the sea of upturned faces, and at the scrying-glass drinking all of it down, and Mae understood the moment they were in more completely and more quickly than any of them. She turned to Addie and said, under her breath, two words: "Pray. Now."

And Mae knelt.

She went down at the base of the red beacon in her beautiful clothes, on the public stone, and bowed her head, and began to pray — out loud, unhurried, word after graceful word — as though this had been the plan from the beginning.

Addie caught Tallandra's eye, and Tallandra gave the smallest nod, and Addie knelt too. She bowed her head. And through the great gathered crowd a hush went out and then a movement, a slow ripple as understanding spread — and people began, in their hundreds, to kneel, and to fold their hands, and to pray alongside them at the foot of the beacon that had refused to speak.

Mae let it hold for exactly as long as it needed to, and then she rose, and she pivoted, and she took hold of the entire narrative with both hands.

"This was all part of the plan," she announced, to the crowd, to the glass, to the nation. Her voice was warm and certain and carried to the back of the square. "There is an order to these things, a way the story is meant to close, and it does not close here in the open where it began. We go now to the Seat of the Seventy-Two, to speak with the Overseer himself, and to bring this Conjunction to its proper and quiet end — by tomorrow. There is nothing to fear. There was never anything to fear. Everything is exactly where it should be."

It was a masterwork. It was also, from beginning to end, a lie, and Addie watched her tell it without a flicker.

"That's enough," Carrio muttered at her shoulder. "Mae. You're overselling it."

Mae ignored him magnificently. She swept one hand up toward the great stair, gathered the companions with a gesture, and to the crowd, to the whole watching world, she called out gaily: "*Tata,* my darlings!"

Brickett, who had not said a single word all morning, who had made himself small and good and silent for days, chose that exact instant to break.

"Did she just *tata* in public?"

Mae's hand closed on the back of his hood without her expression changing at all. "Not now," she said sweetly, through her teeth, and hauled him bodily up the first three steps.

## Go With The Eagle

They went up the Three Hundred Steps faster than Addie had ever taken them — faster, certainly, than the first time, when Boss and Barry had carried her up between them like a sack of grain while she tried to remember how breathing worked. Now she took them two at a time under her own power with the red beacon burning at their backs and the clock she couldn't see running down somewhere over the rooftops.

Boss and Barry were at the top, of course. The two of them straightened as the companions crested the stair, and both snapped a crisp salute — to Major Tallandra first, and then, gravely, to Addie.

Boss peered down at Brickett with genuine concern. "You gonna be sick this time, little man?"

"Not this time," Brickett said, with dignity, still rubbing the back of his hood where Mae had gripped it.

The Conclave doors opened for them the way ordinary doors open. No ceremony, no resistance. They swung wide onto the great chamber of the Seat of the Seventy-Two —

— and it was empty.

Completely empty. Not a soul in the tiered seats, not a voice in the vast domed air, only their own footsteps coming back at them off the far walls. Somehow the silence made the mistake worse than any crowd could have. They had climbed all this way, on the last full day they had, to stand in an enormous empty room.

"We still have a day," Addie said, into the quiet. "It's not over. We have —"

"We'd have *more* of one," Carrio said, "if certain people hadn't spent half the week sulking in a locked room over a —"

Addie thwacked him across the back of the head with her staff.

It was not a gentle thwack. The sound of ironwood on skull rang around the empty chamber, and Carrio yelped, and everyone else went rigid, because whatever else was true about Carrio B. Something-or-other, he was a prince of the Embercoil, and the appointed Head Moderator of the nation had just cracked him one across the head in the Seat of the Seventy-Two.

"Grow the fuck up, Carrio," Addie said, into the ringing silence. "You too, Mae." She lowered the staff. Made herself say the rest of it, because it was true and they needed to hear her say it. "This is *my* mistake. Not hers, not his. Mine. I should have been pushing on this days ago, and I didn't, because I was too sure I already had it. So. We fix it now. Where did it start for *us* — all of us, together? Where's the one place?"

"Not Pollyr Fen," Gregory said immediately. "That was already answered."

"Not here," said Matthieu.

Addie chewed the inside of her cheek. "I was born in Qasr Siroth. Could it —"

"There were twenty-four cities standing at once, at the start," Yurislav said, quiet, gravel-slow. "There may not *be* a single founding city. There may not be one place that made us."

"Then not where we were made," Addie said. "Where we *met.* Where the whole thing that we are started turning. Someone give me cities. Where's everyone from — go."

"Awanate," said Matthieu.

"Orsakar," said Yurislav.

"Skyrend Peak," said Tallandra.

Brickett detonated.

There was no better word for it. One instant the beaver was standing folded and quiet at Addie's elbow, and the next he had launched himself backward across a full thirty feet of Conclave floor as though something had reached into his chest and *flung* him — arms wheeling, a small strangled shout tearing out of him — and he hit a councillor's writing-table with the whole of his body and went over it in a crash of splintering wood and scattered inkpots and lay there in the wreckage, blinking at the domed ceiling, dazed.

"*Brickett!*" Addie was already moving.

He waved her off with one loose, wobbling paw, his eyes not quite pointing the same direction. "M'fine," he mumbled. "'m fine. Go on without me. My life for you, Moderator." He got the words out slow and certain, each one placed like a stone. "Go with the eagle."

Addie stopped where she stood.

*Go with the eagle.* Tallandra — eagle-headed, plumed, the Major who had spread that great braced wing over Addie's shoulders on the platform — Tallandra of Skyrend Peak.

"Skyrend Peak," Brickett said, from the floor, to the ceiling, to all of them. His voice was gaining focus even as the rest of him lost it. "That's where — that's where Kwame said your name. In his office. That's where they *chose* you." He turned his head, found her, held her gaze for one clear unwavering second before it drifted again. "And it's where the brick came down. Where I —" He hiccuped. "Where I caught it. That's the place. That's where it started. For all of us. It started the day I stopped the brick."

And she knew, the way she'd known Matthieu was right, but this time the stone that dropped through her was warm. Skyrend Peak. Of course. The cramped office where a tired administrator had spoken her name into being. The stair below it where a fourteen-year-old beaver she had never met had thrown himself in front of a falling world and shouted *my life for you* at a stranger. That was the beginning. Not the nation's. Theirs.

She looked toward the high windows. The light had climbed. It was rounding High Market out there, the day tipping over its top and starting, already, to fall away toward the deadline.

"Then we run," she said. "All of us, right now, for Skyrend Peak — and we try to end this tonight." She made herself finish the thought, cold as it was. "Tonight. So that if I stand up in front of it and I *fail* — you've still got the night to find another way. We do not spend our one extra chance sleeping."

"He can't run," Boss said gently. He'd crossed to Brickett and crouched over him, and Barry was already there, easing a hand under the beaver's head. "Look at his eyes, Moderator. This one's ringing like a bell. He needs mending, not stairs."

"We'll see to him," Barry said. "You have our word. Go do your thing. We'll put your beaver back together."

Brickett gave her a floppy, lopsided thumbs-up from the floor. "Told you," he said. "Go with the eagle."

It was the worst thing she'd had to do all day, and she'd had a full day of terrible things. Leaving him there in the wreckage of a councillor's table, concussed and grinning and proud of himself — it felt like leaving a piece of her own chest behind on the Conclave floor. But he had told them to go, twice now, and telling them to go was the truest thing he owned, and to refuse it would have been to take it away from him.

"Fix him," she told Boss. "Whatever it costs."

Then she turned, and the companions turned with her, and they ran — back through the great empty chamber, out through the doors that opened like ordinary doors, and down the Three Hundred Steps into the falling afternoon, faster than they had come up.

Somewhere around the hundredth step Rhün pulled level with Mae.

"So," he said, breathing easy, entirely too pleased. "Going to go chat with the reporters again on the way out? Give them another good one for the evening edition? I've got some ketchup back at the manor, if you want it. For the foot." He mimed a generous squeeze. "You've got it in your mouth so often, might as well season it."

Mae turned her head and gave him a look that had, historically, ended careers and possibly lives.

But Addie — half a step behind, watching, catching everything now the way she always did when it mattered least and meant most — could have sworn that underneath the murder in it, right at the corner of the Speaker's mouth, there was the beginning of a smile.

Battered, and bleeding a little, and short one beaver, her impossible family ran down out of the Seat of the Seventy-Two and out across the capital toward Skyrend Peak, toward the eagle, toward the place where all of it had started — with the red beacon burning behind them and the last of the day coming down.
