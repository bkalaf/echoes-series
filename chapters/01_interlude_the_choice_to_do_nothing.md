# Interlude: The Choice to Do Nothing

Welcome back, Dear Reader. I must apologize for getting involved once again.

I know I am supposed to be telling a story, not teaching a lesson. An elegant chronicler would simply allow the scenes to unfold before you, stepping aside to let you wander through the grand, rain-swept corridors of Adeshka Sonntag's crisis without throwing the curtain in your face. But I am an untidy narrator, and I find myself continually stopping to explain things anyway. Before we watch Addie pull her first real thread, we need to ask a question that sounds deceptively simple:

*When does doing nothing become doing something?*

To explore this, let us look at a fake story. A fabricated story. A completely made-up, mythical story about a world that is absolutely not real — but a story that, unfortunately, keeps happening anyway.

Let us pretend there was a vast world divided by a Wide River. On one side lived the Sun People, an intense and highly focused island empire caught in the grinding gears of a rapidly expanding industrial machine. They built beautiful things, but their expansion required vast amounts of a very specific resource to keep their engines moving: a thick, black, *very important ancient organic sludge,* pumped up from the deep strata of the earth. The problem, of course, was that the Sun People's island did not have any.

On the other side of the Wide River lived the Apathetic Pagans. They were a wealthy, sprawling trade power, stuffed to the chin with resources, comfort, and an absolute, unyielding desire to avoid direct complications.

For a long time, these two groups were close friends. They exchanged gifts, poetry, art, and grand philosophies. They even shared thousands of beautiful, fragile flowering trees. They would sit together beneath the falling petals to contemplate *mono no aware* — the profound philosophy of impermanence. They agreed that beauty matters precisely because it disappears. A blossom is beautiful because it falls; a life is meaningful because it ends; a friendship matters because it cannot last forever. It was a lovely, high-minded relationship, built on the mutual understanding that as long as the trade ledger balanced, nobody would ask too many questions about where the iron warships were sailing.

But over time, the Sun People's machine grew hungrier, and they began conquering neighboring territories, eventually moving their soldiers into the strategically vital waters of the Thousand Island Place.

The Apathetic Pagans watched this from their comfortable porches across the river. They faced a harrowing dilemma. They did not want war, they did not want to intervene, and they desperately wanted to avoid direct responsibility. So they chose a third option. They turned off the valves. They locked the vaults. They simply stopped exporting that ancient organic sludge. The Pagans folded their arms and insisted they were not attacking anyone. They were merely refusing to participate.

Now, Dear Reader, I ask you: Was that refusal an act of peace, or was it an execution warrant written in the negative space of a ledger?

The Pagans viewed it as peaceful restraint. The Sun People, staring at their emptying fuel gauges, viewed it as an existential threat — a quiet war written in the negative space of a ledger. They calculated that they would run out of fuel long before they ran out of ambition. Left with an equation that offered no acceptable options, they did exactly what anyone studying structural incentives could have predicted.

They struck. They launched a sudden, catastrophic attack across the water, against a peaceful, beautiful island territory the Pagans held dear.

History, of course, remembers the explosion. The world condemned the attack, and the apathetic patriots pulled their weapons from the wall to march into the fire with a clean, absolute sense of righteousness. The story became neatly divided into an aggressor and a victim. But if we audit the years of quiet calculations preceding the violence, the boundaries blur. Who truly bears the responsibility for the wreckage? Is it the person who throws the punch? The person who removes the ladder? The person who refuses to intervene? Or the person who notices the fraying thread and chooses to remain silent?

I will not provide an answer, Dear Reader. I leave that weight to you.

But you must understand that Addie's entire story begins with this exact problem. A fraying thread exists in the world, and everyone can see it. But nobody wants to pull it, because pulling it creates immediate, terrifying responsibility. And yet leaving it alone simply creates consequences — and consequences arrive whether we choose them or not. Nearly every disaster in this narrative begins the exact same way: with someone convincing themselves that by doing nothing, they have made no choice at all.

Speaking of choices, let us consider the nature of sacrifice. History books love to celebrate the people who knowingly give their lives for others. We build statues to the heroes, the martyrs, the soldiers, and the parents who step into harm's way.

And then we must look ahead, at what is to come.

Do you remember the young boy from the very first scenes of the prologue? You are about to meet him officially quite soon, and you will come to know him as Brickett: a hyperactive teenage beaver beastfolk, an orphan, a scavenger, and a fiercely good-hearted soul. He is damp, soot-singed, broad-tailed, muddy, and completely obsessed with becoming a fire mage — despite having been born into a body and an ecology that suggest water, wood, dams, and mud instead. He is completely terrible at evaluating danger and utterly desperate to prove that he matters to someone, longing more than anything to be valued, even if everyone around him currently treats him as a loud, well-meaning nuisance. Even Mother occasionally refers to him directly as *my kid,* granting Brickett a level of visible divine favoritism that, tragically, does not make him even one ounce more competent.

Because Brickett is trying so desperately to look like a guardian, you are about to hear a phrase from his mouth for the very first time. Whenever he catches even a whisper of trouble, he throws himself wildly in front of Addie and bellows: *"My life for you!"*

Remember that line, Dear Reader, because it is destined to become a grand, sweeping running joke throughout the Testaments and amongst all of our companions. The comedy of the phrase, of course, is that Addie is almost never in any actual danger. Brickett regularly dives in front of entirely harmless objects, tackles confused bystanders, attempts unnecessary daring rescues, conjures little backfiring flame-bursts, and escalates minor everyday inconveniences into full-scale civic disasters. On more than one memorable occasion, he literally lunges to shield Addie from a falling ledger or a gust of wind, only to strike her squarely in the face.

It is an absurd, recurring spectacle, and it is easy to laugh at him. But do not dismiss the boy entirely, Dear Reader. I point this out to you only because comedy has a habit of hiding uncomfortable truths, and this little routine plays an immense part in our story during the later Testaments.

The phrase is ridiculous now because Brickett keeps offering the ultimate sacrifice to a world that merely needs someone to hold the door — offering a life that society at large would be perfectly willing to spend without a second thought. But by doing so, he is *practicing.* He spends his days shouting an answer to a question that most people spend their entire lives trying to avoid. One day, the danger will not be a stray breeze or an overbalancing stack of books. One day, the crisis will be terrifyingly real, the threat will have teeth, and someone will have to actually give their life to save another. To save everyone, in fact.

And when that day comes, Brickett will not have to stand at the crossroads, weighing the arithmetic of action against inaction, paralyzed by the sudden weight of responsibility. He will not need to decide what he believes. He decided a long time ago.

Many people claim they would give their lives for someone else, Dear Reader. Brickett is simply one of the few foolish enough to mean it.

But it makes you think about the grand structure of things, doesn't it? We look at Mother — at Dianne — and we see an amazing old girl, don't we? She is, at this very moment, writing her own demise into the foundational ledger of this world. She is knowingly walking straight into the fire, fully aware that she will not live to see the end of it. She does not even know whether all, or any, of her children will survive the initialization of the supercontinent's grid. But she constructs the framework anyway, because she knows that at least this way, they have a fighting chance.

I will not ruin the future chapters for you, but it leaves an interesting question hanging in the dark space between paragraphs. Knowing how her own countdown finishes, and seeing how the little beaver practices his devotion day after day — I wonder if Brickett was her inspiration all along.

That would be something, wouldn't it?

Now. Let us leave the Wide River behind. Let us step back down into our active narrative, back to the guildmaster's office, where Adeshka Sonntag is currently debating the usefulness of canteen bread. The vellum scroll with the Master's Seal has been broken in another city, and the Concord Guard — those imposing, frightening "law-keepers" whose iron fist is so rarely lawful — are spilling into the streets and the PortalPorts saying her name, and the first loose thread hangs free. Taunting. Daring. Just waiting to be pulled.

Shall we see if she pulls it?
