## The First Crime

The Scene Between Stories was waiting for her, and it had dressed for the occasion.

The last time Addie had stood in this place it had been a quiet grey nowhere, a room made of the pauses between sentences. Now it was an arena — a great circular floor under no sky, ringed by the full Witness circle, every one of them turned inward and watching. And in the center of it stood Horror, armored for a war.

Four swords. Two of them crossed at the back, two riding the hips. A double bank of crossbows braced across the shoulders. Bandoliers of vials, amber and green and one an ugly bruised violet, slung across a breastplate that looked like it had been forged out of the inside of a bad dream. A tall, spined, female-coded silhouette, mandibles ticking softly at the jaw, more arms than a person should have folded and ready at its sides. Everything about it said: *I have prepared for you. I have prepared for you very thoroughly.*

Addie tried to look like this was fine. She had gotten good, over twenty-five days, at looking like things were fine. She did not, on the inside, come anywhere close.

"Are you ready to die, Adeshka?"

The voice came out of the mandibles like wind through a cracked door. Horror tilted its spined head. "You barely found the right city, little Moderator. Wandered the whole capital first. Prayed at the wrong beacon on the evening news." A dry, ticking laugh. "I'll be generous. Come back tomorrow, when you've slept and had a good cry, and we'll —"

"Nope." Addie planted her staff. "Not even. We're doing it today."

Behind her she felt the companions shift — felt Gregory almost say *tomorrow might be wiser,* felt the whole exhausted row of them lean, just slightly, toward the sensible thing. She did not look back at them.

Horror's confidence hitched. One of the four swords slipped a half-inch loose in its sheath and had to be caught and reseated with a small, furious motion.

"There it is," Addie said. "You dropped a sword."

"*Clever girl,*" Horror hissed, recovering.

"See, that's misogynistic," said Addie. "You've got the eight legs, you play the part, fine, but 'clever girl' is a whole thing, and I'd like it noted for the record that it's sexist."

Somewhere behind her, in a mortified whisper, one of the companions asked another whether Addie had taken the wrong pill out of the tin this morning.

From the edge of the circle came the small, distinct pop of somebody eating.

Silence sat where a Witness who called herself Silence would sit, porcelain mask catching a light that had no source, a paper twist of popcorn in one white hand. She held it out, delicately, to the ashen figure beside her. "Ash?"

Ash declined.

"I have two questions," Silence announced, "for Adeshka. Before the challenge can proceed."

The circle rippled. *That wasn't prepared,* several Witnesses objected at once. *That isn't the format. This wasn't discussed.*

"Mercy," said Silence, and popped another kernel behind the mask. "You've seen what our brother has brought to the floor. Four swords. If we let this begin as it stands, it will be like watching a grown thing beat up a child. Give her the questions. It's only sporting." She turned her blank face slowly around the ring. "Well?"

And the Witnesses voted — not with hands but with a slow going-around of nods, one after another after another, each head dipping in turn like a wave passing through reeds, until it came back around to Silence. She clapped, once, pleased, and threw the Overseer an enormous, obvious thumbs-up across the arena.

Addie caught the wording of it before Silence even asked, and something in her chest steadied. *A confidence clue,* she thought. *He's telling me I can win this. He always tells me exactly what I need, exactly when he's allowed to.*

"First question," said Silence, and folded her hands. "What was José's crime?"

"His outfit," Addie said.

Revel laughed — a single delighted bark that rang around the whole circle. Every mask and muzzle and eyeless face swung toward him. He spread his branches. "It *was* funny," he said.

Addie let the quiet come back. Then she answered it for real.

"The easy answer," she said, "is what he did to Frank. Everybody in this room wants me to say Frank. But the visions never showed me Frank — they showed me the *before.* And you didn't ask me his worst crime. You asked me his *first.*" She turned the thing over the way she'd turned over a thousand residuals in a cold tower. "The first vision. The room with the window and the command to leave. He didn't push anyone off the pier. He stood there, and he had a choice about people getting left behind, and he chose — nothing. He let it happen. And once you've done nothing while people are left behind, the next thing is easier, and the next, and the next, until it's a whole life. The crime wasn't the wave. The crime was the *inaction* that started metastasizing that day. José's first crime was standing still while people were abandoned."

The Witnesses were impressed. She could feel it move through the circle, that same reed-wave, this time of respect.

Horror was not impressed. Horror got very quiet, and very sharp, and the mandibles stopped ticking entirely.

## The Co-Conspirator

"Second question." Silence had not moved. "Who was his co-conspirator for his second crime?"

Addie turned, on instinct, toward her companions.

"She cannot consult them," Horror snapped.

"Quite right." Silence clapped again — a bright, applauding little patter, like a wind-up toy. "No help, dear."

Chairs came up out of the floor behind the companions, black and high-backed, and folded them in before any of them could step aside. Straps. Gags. Nine of her people bound and silenced in a neat row at the arena's edge, wide-eyed above the cloth.

"Brickett has a head injury," Addie said, sharp. "He's concussed. You can't just —"

"Compassion," Silence sighed, "for the little one." A gesture. Brickett's gag and straps loosened a hand's width.

Brickett immediately slumped, panting theatrically, rolling his eyes back, and then hissed, out the side of his mouth, that he'd been faking, obviously, because Addie needed backup and a distraction and he was providing it. Two of the Witnesses leaned in with what looked like genuine alarm. Ash asked, quietly, whether the small one had water, whether someone oughtn't fetch the small one water.

*They don't know,* Addie realized, cold under the ribs. *They've been out here so long they don't remember what a person is. They can't tell a bit from a wound.* It was the most dangerous thing she'd learned all night.

From the row of chairs, Gregory made a muffled, urgent, deeply sincere noise through his gag that was almost certainly meant to be *she's got this, everyone stay calm* and came out as pure muffled vowel.

Addie turned back to Silence. "His co-conspirator," she said, "was himself."

She let it sit.

"He didn't need a partner. He'd already broken the lock the first time — stood still while people were left. So the second time was easier. And the third. And the fourth. And the fifth. Every compromise made the next one cheaper, until betraying the one person who was ever kind to him was just — Tuesday. He conspired with the version of himself that had already agreed to survive at any cost." She lifted her chin and looked, for the first time, straight into the spined female mask. "Isn't that right, José?"

The row of chairs erupted in muffled confusion. *José?* Carrio's eyes went enormous over his gag; he was very plainly wondering whether Addie was the one with the head injury.

"Interesting," said Horror, and the voice had changed — lower, more careful. "That's *very* interesting. Almost like somebody cheated. Like somebody was *told.*" The spined head turned, deliberately, toward the Overseer.

The Overseer shook his head, once. *No.*

"Nobody told me," Addie said. "You told on yourself. Both vision-sets had one person in common, standing at the edge of every room where something terrible got decided, and every time the others called him the same small hiding word. *Assistant.* You've been wearing 'assistant' the way you're wearing that mask." She softened, and meant it. "I'm sorry that Noel did that to you. I'm sorry he got his hands into you and made you buy your own life with pieces of your soul, one compromise at a time. That's a horrible thing to do to a person."

"That was a very long time ago," Horror said. "It is not relevant. It does not *matter.*"

"It matters now. It's the only thing that matters now." Addie spread her hands to take in the whole circle, the arena, the watching dead. "The Conjunctions are a mirror. A mirror to us — everybody thinks that part. But also a mirror to *you.* That's the part nobody tells the little Moderators. You're not just judging us. You're standing in front of the thing you've spent an eternity refusing to look at."

The four swords came off Horror's back and hips all at once, a bright brutal fan of them, and the arena's false light ran down the edges.

"Choose your next words," Horror said, "very carefully."

## Five Addies

Addie looked back, once, at her family in their chairs. Mae, furious and elegant even bound. Carrio, worried sick under the sarcasm. Brynn. Rhün. Matthieu counting exits with his eyes. Yurislav, calm as a stone. Tallandra straining against the straps like she'd tear the arena down with her teeth. Gregory still trying, gamely, to say something reassuring through a mouthful of cloth. Brickett, loose enough now, watching her with total, terrible faith.

She thought about the moons. The three of them, the way they hung over Erzengel every night of her life. The way she'd chosen, without quite knowing why, to spend that life on three things: what the world was made of, how people lived together, what had happened before. Science. Society. History.

*Funny,* she thought. *I study the three things the three moons are.*

She felt José understand what she was doing about half a second too late.

Addie slammed the ironwood staff into the floor.

Her eyes went blue — she couldn't see it but she could feel it, that old impossible light rising up behind them like water finding a crack — and a wind came up out of nowhere in a place that had never had weather, pulling her braids sideways, pulling at the edges of every Witness in the ring. She did not reach for a weapon. She reached the other way, toward the soft underside of things, toward dreams and doubling and the far side of the monster in front of her.

One Addie became five.

They stood in a loose ring around Horror, all of them with the same blue-lit eyes, the same planted staff, the same tired defiance.

Horror was *extremely* displeased.

The companions, as one, had a reaction into their gags that needed no translation.

"This is fairer," said all five Addies, more or less together. "You've got four swords and a chemist's shop. I only ever had *me.* Now there's a few more of me." A ragged laugh went around the ring of her. "And for the record — I'm not here to beat you. I'm not here to kill you. I came here to *talk.* The rest of this is just so you'll hold still long enough to listen."

The five of her began to circle, and Horror's spined head snapped from one to the next to the next, mandibles working, unable to find the seam.

At the chairs, Mae's eyes narrowed above the gag, and Addie saw the Speaker get it — saw her understand that this wasn't an attack at all, that Addie was hiding her own bleeding self in a crowd of herself, wearing the monster down the only way Addie had ever won anything: patience, motion, and time.

"You wanted to save people," the Addies said, weaving. "That's the part nobody ever says out loud about you, José. Underneath all of it, you *wanted to save people.* And a man had a knife to everything you loved and told you the only way to save anyone was to abandon everyone. That's coercion. That's duress. That's —"

Horror struck. One arm, one blade, a blur — through the throat of an Addie who came apart into blue light — and the edge of it caught the real one across the shoulder before the illusion had finished fading.

Addie gasped. Blue light welled up out of the cut and ran down her arm like luminous blood, dripping, steaming faintly on the floor.

"I can cut your dreams faster than you can dream them, girl," Horror said.

"Maybe." Addie's four copies drew back in; she pressed a hand to the wound and kept talking through her teeth. "But listen to yourself. That *contempt* — for duress, for being forced, for people who compromise to survive — that's not a monster's voice. That's José. That's a man who's spent an eternity sneering at the exact excuse he made for himself, because it's easier to hate it in the abstract than to forgive it in the mirror."

"*I'm cheering,*" Silence announced brightly to the circle, "and I shan't tell you for whom."

And then, unexpectedly, another voice rose up cheering too — warm, rolling, unmistakably lilting. Revel, on his feet, branches waving, roaring encouragement in a broad Irish brogue.

Addie's head came up.

*He gave me something,* she thought. *He doesn't even know he did it.*

"Thank you, Revel," she said.

Revel, delighted to be thanked, and entirely without malice, bellowed across the arena at the top of his voice: "GO ON THEN, THE REAL ONE'S THE ONE YE ALREADY *HIT!*"

The four bleeding-less copies winked out.

Addie stood alone, one hand clamped to a shoulder pouring blue light, and let herself smile.

"It's all right," she said. "I can stop bleeding now."

"Are you giving up?" Horror asked.

## The Human In There

Addie threw her staff away.

It clattered across the floor and out of reach, and the row of companions came up out of their chairs against the straps in a single silent scream, because they did not understand, because it looked exactly like surrender.

She saw Brickett start to rise.

Horror lunged — the full weight of it, a downstroke meant to end things, all that armor and all four arms behind one falling blade.

"You're about to regret this," Addie said.

And Horror heard, from somewhere close and small and hurtling:

"*My life for you!*"

A small shape flung itself through the air — a beaver in mid-leap, arms out, throwing his whole body into the path of the blade the way he had thrown it in front of a falling brick on a Skyrend street a lifetime ago.

Horror screamed *NO.*

The blade was already falling; there was no calling it back; so Horror did the only thing left and cut off her own arm at the shoulder to steal the weight out of the stroke — and still the dying blade drifted down and seemed to bite into the small thrown body, into the neck, and the shape crumpled.

The companions went berserk. Nine bound people howling into cloth, Tallandra's chair tearing half out of the floor.

The Witnesses recoiled as one.

"*Tee-hee,*" said Silence.

"That is *wildly* inappropriate," said Ash.

"The rest of you are dummies," Silence replied comfortably.

Addie was already moving. She crossed the floor at a dead run — not to the fallen shape, but to Horror, to José, to the maimed thing kneeling over its own severed arm — and dropped to her knees and laid her glowing hands on the ruin of the shoulder and began, gently, to mend it.

"What," Horror rasped, "are you doing."

"Mending you," Addie said. "Because you just did exactly what I needed you to do." She looked up into the mandibled mask, and her voice cracked right down the middle of it. "I needed you to show me that there's a human in there still. And you just cut off your own arm to keep from hurting a child. So. Thank you."

"Take care of the beaver," Horror said. "Go. See to the beaver."

"There's no wounded beaver."

Across the arena, in a seam of the Overseer's shadow that had opened at exactly the right moment and hidden him there the whole time, the real Brickett stood — bandaged, blinking, entirely alive, looking faintly embarrassed to be the subject of so much screaming.

"I conjured him," Addie said softly. "I made a Brickett out of moonlight and threw him at your sword. And you cut off your own arm to save an illusion." She held the healing light steady. "It worked because you're human, José. It worked because you *regret.* That thing you're wearing — the spines, the mandibles, the extra arms, the armor — that's not a monster. That's just how you've had to look at yourself, every day, since the day you decided your survival was worth other people's. Horror is what regret looks like when a man refuses to set it down."

Horror collapsed. The great spined shape folded in on itself and began, quietly, to weep — dark tears sliding down beneath the mask.

"Noel was a monster," José said, and it was a man's voice now, exhausted and old. "He was vicious. He was violent. He put his hands around everything I loved and squeezed. And I couldn't even — the wave came too fast, I couldn't save my own family, they were just *gone,* and the only person left who was ever kind to me was Frank." A shudder. "So I set Frank up. I broke his marriage so Noel could get his hands on the fortune. I told myself every single time that it was necessary. Survival. I *survived.*" The mask tipped up toward the false sky. "And at what cost."

"That is not your story to tell, brother," Silence said, coughing lightly behind her mask.

"That's not what Noel told *us,*" said another Witness, uncertain, at the edge of the ring.

"I see somebody," Addie said, "who is finally healing. After an eternity of being made to watch what his choices cost." She rose, and turned to the porcelain mask. "So I'm asking. Is that time served? Has he witnessed enough? Can you let him go?"

"Oh, dear," said Silence, and for the first time there was something almost like pity in it. "I really did think you were clever."

"I would not," said José quietly, from the floor, "choose to be anywhere else."

## The Thread Through the Maze

Addie saw it then. One single red thread, come loose from the seam of Horror's fallen armor, trailing on the arena floor.

She reached into her pocket and drew out Ariadne's Thread — the little spool they'd nearly died to fetch, the present, the key.

José stared at it. "You *found* it. You actually — how did you —"

"I don't need this to defeat you," Addie said. "That's what you all told me, remember? I didn't need it to *win.*" And she tossed the spool underhand across the floor to Brickett, who caught it against his chest with both paws.

Then she bent, and took hold of the loose red thread in the armor, the one that had come from *him.*

Horror let her.

She pulled.

The first pull took a plate off the shoulder. The second unwound a length of the black silhouette like smoke coming unspun. Addie began to circle — around and around, the way she'd circled him with five bodies, except now with just her hands and one red thread, walking it out of the shape of the monster. Round, and pull. Round, and pull. Armor came away in curling ribbons. The mandibles unknit and fell. The extra arms unraveled to nothing. The spines went soft and came apart. The whole towering female-coded horror of it unthreaded, loop by loop, into a smaller and smaller and more ordinary shape, until what knelt on the floor of the Scene Between Stories was a tired man in a work vest with dark eyes and no armor at all.

Addie wound the last of the thread into a loose coil, crouched, and pressed it into his hand.

"I think this is yours," she said.

José looked at the thread. He looked at his own bare hands. He looked as though the floor had come out from under two thousand years at once.

And Silence took off her mask.

Nobody in that circle had ever seen it, Addie understood — the way the whole ring went rigid, the way the Overseer himself went still. Whatever was under the porcelain, it stopped every one of them cold. Silence stared at José, and then at Addie, with a face nobody was supposed to see.

The straps and chairs holding the companions dissolved. Nine of them stumbled free and did not move, because none of them could yet make the shape of what they'd watched fit inside their heads.

"Mother," Silence said at last, faintly, "does have a way for dramatics."

"She certainly does, sister," said Ash.

And the Witnesses began, one by one, to come forward — hesitant, wondering, reaching — *it's good to see you, brother. It's been so long. It's good to see your face.*

"How did you *know,*" José said to Addie.

"I didn't know." She helped him to his feet. "I don't know things. I know *people.* And somewhere back there it got clear to me that all of you were human once. That whatever you are now, you started the way we start."

"Not all of us, dear," said Silence, and smiled a smile that Addie would be seeing for a while.

"Could you put the mask back on?" Brickett asked, in a very small voice. "Please? I'm not an adult and you are frightening me quite a lot."

"It was never about beating him," Addie said — half to the circle, half to herself, feeling the whole shape of it click home. "Barnaby had it right without knowing it. Ariadne's Thread was never a sword. The trick of the maze is never killing the thing in the middle. The trick is finding your way *back out.* That's all I did. I found the way out of him, and I walked it, one loop at a time." She flexed her mending shoulder and huffed a laugh. "If I'm good at one thing in this world, it's wearing people down. And three degrees' worth of school teaches you exactly one useful skill, which is patience."

The Witnesses stepped back, quietly, into their places in the ring, and the arena felt less like a battlefield and more like a room again.

"That," said Silence, mask back in place at Brickett's urgent request, "was impressive, all of you. Truly. The Conjunction is finished."

"I'll mark it," said the Overseer, "with the Master Seal. And I'll get his signature after."

The companions, finally finding their voices, said it more or less in unison: *His signature?*

"Of course I have a boss," said the Overseer.

## The Laughing Tree

The Witnesses began to fade — flickering out of the circle one by one like lamps being carried off down a corridor — until the ring was nearly empty, and the space where the Widow Spider had always stood sat conspicuously vacant beside the one Witness who had not gone anywhere.

Revel leaned on the emptiness where José used to be and considered Addie with something that, in a tree, might have been affection.

"I'm impressed with ye," he said. "Truly I am. Though I confess I fear I may've had a *branch* in the poor lad's undoing. Cheerin' for the wrong side and roarin' out which of ye was real." He tutted at himself. "Clever of ye to catch it."

"And?" Addie said.

"Ah." Revel brightened. "Well. Ye'll have worked out by now that when one Witness comes down off the wall, another steps into the light. And after the Widow Spider —" he swept his branches wide "— comes the *Laughing Tree.*"

Nobody said anything.

"All bark," said Revel, "and no bite. Ha! Who'd have *trunk* it. Get it? Trunk? Because I'm —" He looked hopefully around the arena. "Tough crowd. Wooden you know it."

"They're not a good audience," Yurislav observed.

"No," Revel agreed sadly. "They are not." He straightened, and for a moment the humor went out of the bark and left something older underneath. "I'm the next one, Moderator. I'm the one ye'll have to come and cut down. And because ye finished half a day early — showing off — ye've earned yourselves a bit of a holiday. The beacon'll go purple again in two and a half days. I'll be waiting." He inclined his crown of leaves. "I will see you at the beacon in the city for the people who reside at the edges of maps."

"Oh, *gods,* no," said Addie. "Another riddle."

"Off ye go," said Revel cheerfully, and a dozen branches came up out of the floor and *swept* — bundling the nine companions and one indignant Overseer toward the seam in the world — "out, out, the lot of ye, I've puns to workshop —"

And they were standing on the platform at Stormspire Aerie in the pre-dawn cold, blinking, whole.

Above them the red beacon pulsed once — hard, deep, a heartbeat felt in the teeth — and went out.

For one held breath the whole peak was dark and silent. And then Skyrend Peak, the city that had refused to cheer, the city that had watched from behind its glass, came apart at the seams with joy. Every window they'd passed on the way up flew open. People spilled onto the cold stone streets in their nightclothes, shouting, weeping, ringing anything that would ring. The hidden watchers were hidden no longer.

The companions fell on each other. There was a great deal of undignified crying.

The Overseer stood apart from all of it, at the edge of the platform, staring up at the sky.

"Come celebrate," Addie called to him, wiping her face. "You helped. You get to celebrate too."

"I only ever told you exactly what I was supposed to tell you," the Overseer said, not looking down, "at exactly the moment I was permitted to tell it."

"Would you — just once — try talking like a person?"

But he wasn't listening. He was looking up, and his ancient dry face had gone slack with something she'd never seen on it. "A once-in-a-lifetime experience," he murmured. "Truly. Even for me."

"MISS SUN-TAG!" Brickett shrieked, tugging her sleeve hard enough to rip it, pointing straight up. "MISS SUN-TAG, LOOK—"

Addie looked up.

The stars had moved.

Not drifted. Not turned with the hour. *Moved* — rearranged, whole constellations gone and new ones standing in their places. She found the Hollow Elf where it had never been. The Rhino. The Silent Fox. A great spreading shape that could only be the Laughing Tree. And one more she had no name for at all, a knot of stars that made her scalp prickle.

And the Widow Spider — the eight-legged sprawl that had hung over the northern sky her entire life, that she had traced on cold nights from the observatory as a girl — the Widow Spider was simply *gone.* In its place ran a long, looping, folded line of stars, doubling back on itself, worrying its way from one edge of the sky to the other.

"Does that look like a string through a maze?" she said.

"That's exactly what it looks like," said Matthieu, very quietly.

"How." Addie couldn't stop staring. "How do you rearrange *stars?* How does killing one monster in a room made of nothing physically move the actual sky?"

"I don't know," Matthieu said. "You're the astrophysicist."

"I'm just a priest," Gregory offered.

"That's probably the point," said Yurislav. He was looking at the folded line too, and there was something careful in his face. "Did we work out who's next? Where the purple's going?"

Gregory and Yurislav exchanged a look, and Addie understood that the two of them had already gotten there.

"My people," Yurislav said. "The ones the maps never had room for. On the old charts, when the cartographers ran out of the world they knew, they'd fill the empty edges with the same three words." He almost smiled. "*Here be dragons.* We got put at the edges of the maps because nobody knew what else to do with us. The city for the people at the edges of the maps is the Dragon Isles."

"And the Laughing Tree," Gregory said slowly, the color leaving him, "is mine."

Addie laughed. She couldn't help it — it came up out of her raw and helpless and slightly hysterical, there under the changed sky. "I got the *Widow Spider,*" she said. "I got the eight-legged nightmare with four swords, and *you* get the — the tree that tells *dad jokes.*"

Mae started laughing too, high and delighted.

"If I'd gotten the Widow Spider," Gregory said, with wounded dignity, "she almost certainly would have eaten me. That's what they do. Widow spiders eat their husbands."

The women laughed harder.

"Their *husbands,* Gregory," Mae gasped. "You think you could be somebody's *husband?*"

"I think," said Gregory, "that delirium is contagious, and I am catching it from present company."

"It's the beaver's pills," Carrio decided, throwing an arm around Brickett, gazing up at the maze of new stars while the whole freed city rang and wept and cheered below them. "Whole platoon of us. Secondhand H&H. That's the only reasonable explanation for any of this."
