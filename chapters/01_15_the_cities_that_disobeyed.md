## The Yellow Witness

They walked out of the White Tower and into a city gone gold, and the gold was worse than the green had been.

The green had been sickness. The yellow was *attention* — the whole of Luminthalas lit like a held breath, the marble and the moonstone and the blue glass all washed the color of a warning, and the people in the terraced streets had stopped merely turning their wrists at the companions. They had begun to *watch* them, silent and wide-eyed, the way a crowd watches a thing it will one day tell its grandchildren it saw. And Addie, walking through it toward the great beacon-obelisk at the heart of Section 1, understood for the first time the specific terror of being *right* — of having pulled a true thread and felt the whole loom shudder in answer.

"Well," said Brickett, too brightly, jogging to keep up, "on the bright side, if we die, at least it'll be somewhere with really nice architecture. That's — that's something. That's a nicer death than most people get."

Nobody laughed. Gregory was already the grey of old parchment, because Gregory, alone among them, had let himself finish the thought the rest were avoiding: that the next step was not a book, and not a scholar, and not another clever question. The next step was standing in front of the things themselves.

Mae said nothing at all, which frightened Addie more than anything Mae could have said.

Tallandra read the crowd, the balconies, the high bridges, the sightlines down from the observatory terraces — reflexively, ceaselessly — and found nothing her training could name, because her training had no category for what they were walking toward. There was no threat here she could put a number on. There was only the obelisk, rising white and enormous into the yellow sky, and the pull of it, and the sense, growing with every step, that the ground of the world was about to open under them and show them its foundations.

They reached its base together, ten of them, one group.

And the yellow reached down and took them all at once.

---

There was a shore, and it was wrong, and it was dying.

Addie stood on it without standing on it — the whole group did, ghosts at the edge of a memory that was not theirs — and everything about the place refused to resolve into anything she had a word for. A vast waterfront of grey stone and stranger materials, hard-edged and enormous, built by hands that knew things her world had forgotten or never learned. It was dusk. The light was failing. And it was full of people, thousands of them, packed onto the long piers and *screaming* — not yet, not quite, but with the held, rising sound of a crowd that has understood it is not going to be saved.

Out on the grey water sat the ships.

She knew them. Some cold certain part of her *knew* them — the vast dark shapes she'd glimpsed through a wrong-sky window in a council chamber, ships the size of cathedrals, iron mountains riding a heaving sea. And near the foot of the pier, at the center of the panic, stood a man holding a small black box, speaking clipped, hard orders into it, and Addie's breath went out of her, because she knew *him* too.

She had watched this man, at an obsidian table, take off his glasses and say, in a voice stripped of everything but grief, *people are never cargo. We cannot count them like crates of winter grain.*

And now he stood on a drowning shore with his back to a screaming crowd, and gave the order to leave them.

"Go," he said into the box, flat and shaking. "First two, now — get to open water, *go.*" And two of the great ships broke from their moorings and began to move, ponderous and unstoppable, out toward the dark. He did not look at the pier. He was making himself not look at the pier. "The rest. All of them. Out. *Now.*" And the others began to pull away too, cables falling, the gap of black water widening between the ships and the thousands of hands reaching after them.

The companions watched a man abandon a city.

Nobody in the vision could see the whole of what was coming. The dark and the distance took the details. But José — because that was his name, José, she had read it burning over an empty chair — José turned, and climbed into a machine that Addie's mind simply *slid off,* a thing that sat waiting to lift him up and away from the shore, and as it rose he saw what was coming across the water, and his face came apart.

"Oh *Jesus Christ,*" he said — words in no tongue any of them knew, an oath out of some drowned language — "*get us in the air now.*"

And the wave came.

Addie could not have said how big it was, because it was too big for the eye to hold — a wall of the sea standing up against the last of the daylight, higher than the towers, higher than anything had a right to be, a thing that was not a flood but an *ending,* rolling toward the shore with the slow unhurried certainty of a fact. Someone in the group made a sound. She thought it might have been her. She heard Brynn breathe a word and Carrio breathe another and felt the whole company recoil as one from the sheer *scale* of it, the death of it, the way it filled the whole world.

On the pier the screaming became real.

And the ships — the great impossible ships — went to full speed toward the wall of water, and did not turn, and did not flee sideways, but drove *straight at it,* and as the wave rose over them they seemed to Addie's uncomprehending eye to *climb* — to ride up the sheer black face of the thing on some force she had no name for and no science to explain, magic or worse, the hulls lifting against all sense as the water reared to swallow the world —

— and the memory cut, mercifully, absolutely, before the wave came down.

She never saw it land. None of them did. They were only left, all ten, gasping in the yellow dark, with the after-image of a standing sea and the sound of a crowd that a good man had chosen to leave behind.

## The Cities That Disobeyed

The drowning shore folded away, and a throne room assembled itself out of the yellow — and this one Addie could see clearly, every terrible ordinary detail of it, because this was not the drowned old world. This was *theirs.*

Aquila Matara, young again. The same half-built capital she'd walked through in the second mirror, the ceremony going up before the streets were finished. And on the throne, unbent and at ease and cruel without effort, the first Dovren Judax Thorne — holding court over a room full of city delegations who had come, plainly, to learn which of them the Sovereign had decided to reward and which he had decided to ruin.

He was in a *fine* mood. That was the sickening thing. He was congratulating them.

"A great day," he said, expansive. "The founding of new cities. The *separation* of peoples who never should have been herded together in the first place." His lip curled. "You'll have heard the sentiment — that we were all to be *blended,* human and beast and mythos, into one happy porridge. That we'd be *stronger* for the stirring." He laughed. "I told her this plan of blending mongrels, half-breeds, and humans would not work. So much for all-knowing."

Addie felt the room of them flinch at the words — *mongrels, half-breeds* — and felt something else underneath it, a colder thing, at *her,* and *all-knowing.* He was mocking someone. Someone absent. Someone who had designed the blending, who was supposed to know everything, and had been proven — in his telling — a fool. She had no idea who he meant. She filed it, whole and cold, next to a woman in a bright vest in a black room saying *let them live in my keeping.*

He got to the punishing.

"Some cities," Dovren said, and the warmth went out of him entirely, "did not see fit to send me their representatives today. Khembal." A pause, letting the name hang and curdle. "Qinyue. Skjarnheim." His smile was a blade. "The old human capitals. The proud ones. Who felt they might refuse the Sovereign and keep their standing both." He leaned forward. "They will keep neither. Effective now, each of them loses its regional seat. The roads will be turned. The trade will be sent elsewhere. The patronage, the courts, the coin — *elsewhere.* Let them keep their pride and watch it starve. Let every child born in those cities for the next thousand years grow up poor and cheap and *dependent,* and know exactly which ancestor's disobedience bought them the gutter." He sat back. "That is what refusing me costs. I want it *remembered.*"

Matthieu, beside Addie, had gone very still and was silently counting — *who is here, who is not, which name answers and which is only spoken.*

"And so," Dovren went on, sour now, begrudging, "the *replacements.*" He gestured, without pleasure, at three delegations who had come and knelt. "Ascentia's new seat — Luminthalas. Jadefall's — Grendhollow. And Gloamwood's—" his eyes moved to a cluster of dark-robed figures "—Nox Vaelor."

Mae made no sound. But Addie, standing next to her, felt her go rigid — felt the cold move through the woman's whole body — because among the Nox Vaelor delegation stood a figure Mae plainly, wordlessly recognized. The set of the shoulders. A crest. A name Addie didn't catch but Mae clearly did. An ancestor. A Van'kareth, kneeling in a young king's throne room, accepting a crown that had been stolen from a punished city and handed to the obedient.

The Nox Vaelor representative bowed low.

"You have our unyielding support, High Sovereign," they said, and there was no dignity in it, only appetite, only a city that had learned that loyalty was now the only thing that paid. "Whatever you require, we stand ready."

Dovren accepted it the way a man accepts a thing he owns.

And Addie watched the machine of her whole world get built in a single afternoon — watched racism turned from a private ugliness into a public *instrument,* watched identity made into hierarchy and hierarchy made into money, watched cities rewarded for kneeling and cities starved for standing, all of it dressed up as founding and celebration and the natural order of things. She watched Yurislav, off to her side, look ill at the sight of a Sovereign not as rumor but as *architect.* She watched Gregory receive the founding structure of his nation as both a scholar and a man taking a wound. She watched Brickett and Carrio flinch at the plain cruelty of it a full beat before either understood the machinery underneath.

And somewhere in the vision a herald announced a tax, to be paid, like all things now, in the new coin of the realm.

The dovren.

His name. In every hand. In every purse. Forever.

## Our Manor

They came back into their bodies at the foot of the obelisk, in the yellow afternoon, and for a long moment not one of the ten of them could speak.

Then Mae straightened, and there was something new in the set of her, something Addie couldn't yet name.

"We're going back to our manor," she said. "Now. And—"

"*Our* manor?" It was Carrio, catching it, one brow going up despite everything.

Mae rounded on him — on all of them — fast and low and raw. "Not one *word* about any of this until we are inside that house and the doors are shut. Do you understand me? Not on the street. Not at the port. Not where anyone can hear." She held them there a beat, hard-eyed. And then, because Mae'vyri Van'kareth did not leave a thing half-finished even when it cut her: "Yes. *Our* manor. It is abundantly clear to me right now that we have no idea what we are doing, or what our history really is, if what we just saw is real. Yes, our manor. This is so much bigger than us. My misplaced pronoun is probably the least of our fucking worries."

No one argued the pronoun. Addie doubted anyone but her had even noticed the size of what it meant — that somewhere between a soup line and a throne room, Mae had stopped thinking of them as guests in her house, and started thinking of them as a household. As *hers.* As one thing.

Mae was right, though. Set beside a standing sea and a starved city and a king who put his name on the money, one small slipped word was the least of anyone's worries.

---

Gregory started to vomit.

It was sudden and total and undignified, the High Theorist folding over into the shadow of the great white obelisk and losing everything he had, and for a moment the whole shattered company just looked down at him.

Rhün moved first. He crossed and put one enormous warm hand on Gregory's back, steady, and left it there, and said nothing, because there was nothing to say. Addie dug into her tin and crouched and pressed an H&H into Gregory's shaking fingers for when he was done — the small ordinary mercy of it, in the middle of the unbearable.

It should have been funny. Some far-off part of Addie's brain registered that under other circumstances the sight of the group's most controlled, most fastidious, most bloodlessly rational member noisily emptying his stomach behind a monument would have been *hilarious.*

It was not funny. It was a measurement. Gregory Frydrake had looked at the founding of his own nation and his body had rejected it, and that told the rest of them, more honestly than any words could, exactly how bad the thing they'd all just seen had been.

Someone — Brynn, maybe — started to say something. To ask, perhaps, whether it had really been as terrible as all that, whether they were sure, whether there was some reading of it that wasn't the end of everything they'd believed about where they came from.

Mae answered before the question finished.

"Yes," she said, looking down at the vomiting scholar and the yellow city and the whole ruined afternoon. "It was that goddamn bad."

And the ten of them turned, together, and started the long walk back toward the port, and the portals, and the manor — carrying, all of them now, the same terrible new knowledge: that they did not understand the first thing about their own world, and that everyone who was supposed to had made very certain of it.
