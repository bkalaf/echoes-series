## Wrong Way

They came off the beacon platform still arguing about what *horned, dead charge* and a thread and a whole graveyard of drowned children were supposed to add up to, and Carrio walked ahead of them like a man who knew the answer, so they followed him.

For a while everyone assumed he was leading them back to the shelter. Then the turns started not matching. A canal on the wrong side. A bridge that led away from the temple quarter instead of toward it. Boardwalks getting narrower, and darker, and hung with lanterns that were not, Addie slowly realized, white.

Brynn worked it out first. "You might be a prince," she announced, thick and flat, "but apparently your direction is *shit,* boy."

"Do not," said Carrio, without turning around, "call me boy."

"We *are* going the wrong way," Matthieu said, frowning at the water.

"We're not going back to the shelter." Carrio said it easily. "We slept where Tallowmire wanted us to sleep. We saw what he wanted us to see. Tonight we sleep somewhere *he* doesn't run — somewhere I can actually work." Now he did glance back, and there was a glint in it Addie hadn't seen from him before, something loosened. "You are going to experience my world whether you like it or not."

## The Ribbit Hole

It was still Pollyr Fen — the canals, the stilt-halls, the wet thresholds, the amphibian ease of the place — but dressed for a different trade. Red lanterns doubled in the dark water. Privacy screens of woven reed glowed from within. Balconies hung low over the canals lit soft and rose, and the floating bridges braided and re-braided through the district in long looping cords, and everywhere, if you looked, there was red — red lamplight, red curtain-ties, thin red ribbons knotted round wrists as some kind of sign. Addie noticed the thread of it without letting herself say the word yet. It felt too neat.

Carrio stopped at a working-house on the water's edge — its sign, when Addie finally made it out through the lantern-glow, read *The Ribbit Hole* — and a Minotaur the size of a doorway stepped into their path.

"Do you know who I am?" Carrio asked, pleasant.

The Minotaur knew. Addie saw the recognition move through him. But he did not move *quickly.*

"Get whoever runs this place," Carrio said. "Now."

The bouncer glared down at him, unhurried, deciding.

"*Ándale,*" Carrio said — one sharp word, in a tongue Addie had never heard him use, a tongue she couldn't place at all — and whatever it meant, the Minotaur's whole posture changed, and he turned and went inside without another word.

Carrio met the companions' collective stare and shrugged. "What? I have a way with people."

---

The manager came out unhurried — a poised, elegant human woman with dark eyes that had clearly seen every kind of man walk through her door and had never once been impressed by one. She took them in, the whole odd party of them, and settled her gaze on Carrio.

"And how," she said, "can I help you." Her eyes flicked to Brickett. "Though I'll note that one looks a little young for the house."

Carrio let the jab slide off. "I need a quiet place, for the rest of the day and the night. Somewhere to think, and to talk — to *every* person you employ. I want to buy the establishment out. All of it. Effective now."

"Compensating my entire staff for a night," she said, "is not a small figure, my lord."

"I am the Prince of the Embercoil. The payment is guaranteed. The *tip* depends on how fast you move."

"Four thousand dovren."

Addie did the arithmetic before she could stop herself — that was months of rent, and the lights, and eating out every night, and a whole new wardrobe on top of it, gone in one sentence.

"That figure," Carrio said, "is unacceptable."

The manager's mouth curved; she settled in to haggle.

"Five thousand," Carrio said.

She blinked.

"And they keep their clothes *on,*" he added. "For the next twenty-four hours, every one of them, fully dressed." He let that land. "We are not renting them for *that* purpose. We are renting them to pick their brains, and to treat them like people for once."

The manager's careful, unimpressible eyes went, at last, wide.

---

Carrio looked at Addie as he said the next part, and she understood he was doing it on purpose.

"Someone reminded me recently," he said, "that you're meant to leave the world a little better than you found it." Back to the manager. "So. You take your standard cut — ten percent, not a copper more — and the rest goes into your workers' hands *directly.* Tonight. I'll be asking them."

"My lord—"

"And if I find out you broke this deal—" his hood rose, and the fangs came with it, and his voice dropped into something cold and precise "—I will personally blind you, and paralyze one side of your body, so that you swim in circles for the rest of your life every time you try to bathe."

There was a small, frozen silence.

"*Charming.*" The manager did not flinch. "Put that away. You have my word — the real kind, not the frightened kind." She waited until the hood came down, then tilted her head, studying him with new and open amusement. "You know, I have friends in Vhal'Tigris. I know your *sort,* Prince. All that venom and theatre." Her smile turned wicked. "You're a little *pussycat* on the inside, aren't you."

And Addie thought, watching Carrio's jaw work, that if the man had not been a black cobra with no blood anywhere near the surface of his scales, he would have been blushing scarlet — and filed away, for later, that there was clearly a whole history there the rest of them hadn't been told.

---

The house cleared with magnificent chaos.

There were crashes. There was a flung boot, and then a flung *corset.* A naked patron went past the window at a dead sprint. A very old woman came barreling out of a back room in a garter belt, a great deal of lace, and — inexplicably, still buckled — a ball gag, moving faster than any of them would have credited. And a translucent spectral shade of a client drifted out last, radiating offense, declaring to the entire district that in all his years, living *and* dead, he had *never* been so insulted.

"Then complain," the manager said, pressing his refund through his incorporeal hand, "to someone who gives a shit."

Inside, the workers stood about confused and wary. When the manager announced they had the day and night off — with full pay, being counted out into their hands right now — several of them began, warily, to arrange themselves for what they assumed was still expected of them.

"*No,*" the manager said, firmly. "None of that tonight. These people want to *talk.* That's the whole of it." A pause, and then, briskly: "And I'm breaking out the top-shelf liquor, because I'll not sit here feeling guilty serving you the watered-down wine."

The companions looked at her.

"What," she said. "I still have to make a profit."

Rhün and Yurislav were already on their feet, ducking through the low door to help her haul the crates — and Addie watched the workers watch *that:* the enormous fire-drake handler and the huge white bear-kin, two of the most dangerous people in the room, carrying the drinks in on their own backs rather than being waited on. Something in the shape of the evening shifted, just from that.

---

Carrio shed his outer layers down to a loose shirt and dropped into a cushioned chair like a man taking off armor.

"*God,*" he groaned, "what I wouldn't kill for a dress today. I cannot *stand* wearing male clothing. Makes me feel all stuffy and like a tight ass." He stretched, tension leaving him by the inch. "But this — this is somewhere I can be myself." He waved a lazy hand at a passing worker. "Something nice, would you? Something that actually *covers* a person, and isn't made of dental floss."

A slim male worker brought it and lingered, plainly drawn to him, and Carrio slid into the flirtation like a fish into water — and used every warm, laughing minute of it to fish, gently, for the shape of the city: who held power here now, which patrons talked, where the faction pressure sat, whether anyone had ever heard of a *thread* worth hiding.

Around the room, the others coped variously.

Gregory had constructed a small fortification of chairs around himself so that no one could sit within arm's reach, and looked like a man attending a funeral by mistake. Matthieu was attempting to flirt, out of a sincere sense of duty, and failing so gently and so miserably that one of the workers had started patting his hand in sympathy. And Mae had settled in beside a striking high-elf woman and was laughing at something, all silk and charm — and mining her, expertly, for every scrap of what the district knew.

No one, Addie noticed, was made small. Carrio had bought the room a night of being asked *questions* instead of asked for anything else, and the workers had begun, cautiously, to enjoy it.

---

The manager sat down across from Addie with two glasses of the good liquor and pushed one over.

"You," she said, "are carrying more than a bad fortnight."

"I'm fine," Addie said, too quickly.

"No. I don't mean the crisis, or the towel, or whatever the papers are calling you today." The dark eyes were steady and not unkind. "I've watched a thousand people fall apart in this room. It's under all of it. You've *lost* someone. Recently. And you haven't had a single quiet minute to sit down and grieve them."

Addie's hand went to the pendant at her throat before she'd decided to move it.

The manager saw. "There it is." She didn't press on the object. She just softened, a little, into something older and more worn than the polish of her trade. "Listen to me, because nobody tells you this properly. It will feel like hell for however many days it feels like hell. One day it won't. You cannot predict when. You cannot predict why. Everybody is different. But when you feel like you cannot take any more — that will probably be the day." She turned her glass. "Now. Tell me about this person. That usually helps."

Addie looked at the red lanterns doubled in the black canal outside.

"My mother," she said. And then, because Carrio had gone and said the thing out loud earlier, she added, "She used to say that. *Leave the world a little better than you found it.* She said it all the time. I used to think it was —" her voice caught "— I used to think it was just a thing people put on cushions."

"And now?" the manager said, gently.

And Addie, for the first time since a scroll had lit and torn her whole life in half, began — haltingly, and then not so haltingly — to actually talk about her.

## A City That Wants Change

They talked most of the night, and by the grey edge of morning the companions had assembled, out of a hundred small honest conversations, a picture of Pollyr Fen that no archive would ever have given them.

The city was fractured to the bone. The Concord guard that had held it for generations had turned iron-fisted — hard enough, by the workers' accounts, that even Tallandra sat listening with her jaw tight, because a soldier knows the difference between order and boot, and this had crossed it long ago. The loyal Concord officers had been run out, or quietly exiled, or worse. Schism was pressing in to fill the space, hungry for it — the armbands on the port guards had not been an accident. And the *people,* caught between, wanted change so badly they ached for it, and were too frightened to reach for it, because the last time Pollyr Fen had reached for anything better, a man named Dovren had turned off their water and let three generations of their children turn to stone.

That, Addie understood at last, was the wide berth. That was the silence, and the deference, and the offerings left on the boardwalk. It wasn't worship. It was a whole city holding its breath, hoping, and braced to be punished for hoping, exactly as it always had been.

---

"The visions were tamer than the last set," Addie said, low, to Carrio and Tallandra, once the room had thinned toward dawn. "But the *reads* were strange. Weren't they."

"Neither of us got deception off the Overseer," Tallandra said. "That's the strange part."

"I watched Horror the whole time," Carrio said. "Not the visions — *her.* And I got — " he frowned, unusually unsure of himself "— shame, in the first one. The tower, the man taking the seats. Genuine shame, radiating off her like heat. And in the second — the throne, the founding — disgust. And *anger.* Righteous, furious anger." He shook his head. "I can't tell you *why* a Witness of Horror would feel ashamed of one and disgusted by the other. But she did."

"And the Overseer?" Addie asked.

"That's just it." Tallandra's voice went careful. "I read deception off Noel. Off Dovren. Off everyone in both rooms. And off the Overseer—" she opened her hands "—*nothing.* Not truth, not lie. Nothing at all. And I can read *everyone,* Adeshka. It was like—" she hesitated "—it was like he wasn't really *there.* Like there was no one standing in the spot where he stood."

Addie said nothing, but she put it in the back room, right next to a small paw waving at her across two thousand years.

## The Ninth Member

The best thing of the whole long night, though, came near the end of it, and it came from Brickett.

He'd spent hours with one of the workers — a fire dancer, patient and unbothered, who'd been teaching him actual, careful, un-backfiring fire handling — and he came and flopped down beside Addie with a small wet beaver *plop,* reeking of soot and singed fur and radiating pure fourteen-year-old pride.

"Brickett," Addie said. "I owe you an apology."

He blinked at her.

"In the seam. When I was watching the vision, and you were trying to get my attention, and I —" she made herself say it "— I brushed you off. I didn't even really look at you. And that was wrong. It's *especially* wrong for someone whose whole job is supposed to be *seeing* everyone. Seeing the ones people miss. That's the entire point of me, and I missed *you.* I'm sorry."

Brickett looked genuinely bewildered. "You don't have to *apologize,*" he said. "I'm your attaché. You don't apologize to—"

"Yes," Addie said. "I do."

And before he could argue the point any further, she asked him: "What were you trying to tell me?"

---

Brickett went quiet, and serious, which on him was almost more alarming than the fire.

"Okay. But you're not gonna believe me," he said. "Because it doesn't make sense." He took a breath. "I think I know who the ninth member is."

"Brickett—"

"I think it's the Overseer."

Addie stared at him. "It has to be a *human.* A noble human. You told me that yourself — you said the Overseer *confirmed* it."

"I *know.*" Brickett wrung his paws. "I know it doesn't fit. But hear the rest. At Mae's dinner — the very first one — he sat down, ate with us, and *then* he said 'now that all the companions are here.' Like the count only worked once *he'd* sat down. And in the seam yesterday? Horror didn't say a single word until the Overseer walked in *late.* And then the second he was in the room, she said it — 'now that all the companions are here.'" He counted it off, earnest and worried. "It's happened before, too. I think after Luminthalas. The same pattern. Nobody starts until *he's* there. Like the group isn't *complete* without him." He deflated. "But it's not proof, is it. Because *he's* the one saying it. And he's obviously *helping* us — he keeps *helping* us. And the ninth's supposed to be a noble human, and he's a — a talking rat with a cane. So I don't *know.* It just — it keeps not letting me alone."

Addie sat with it, and found, to her considerable discomfort, that she could not simply dismiss it.

"We'll tell the others tomorrow," she said. "All of us watching for it is better than you carrying it alone." She hesitated. "And — the Overseer said something to me. Privately. Just to me. And I think I might know what he's *pointing* at, with it." She saw Brickett's ears prick, and shook her head gently. "Not yet. We need the thread first. And I don't want you lying awake turning it over — you've got enough in that head already."

---

"Hey," Brickett protested, "you can't just — you can't *protect* me out of the interesting part—"

A large white hand closed gently over the top of his head.

"Come on, little guy," Yurislav said, hauling him up. "Leave the Moderator be." Then, with the air of a man delivering enormous news: "I think I've got Rhün drunk enough that he might actually say yes to letting you ride Ashwing."

Brickett went off like a firework.

"*RIDE ASHWING—*" He was already halfway across the room, then whipped back around, suddenly formal, vibrating with hope. "Addie! Miss Sun-Tag. Can — may I be excused?"

Addie laughed. She couldn't help it; it came up out of her tired and clean and real.

"Of course you can," she said. "Go. Be careful. Have fun."

And she watched him barrel off toward the dawn and the drake and the wholly reckless promise of it, fourteen years old and briefly, gloriously, just a *kid* — and she thought about a poisoned corner in a shelter two nights ago, and the difference between the two moments, and decided she vastly preferred this one.

The thread was still out there, unfound, somewhere in a city too frightened to hope. But they had its shape now. They had the streets, and the workers who'd trusted them with the truth, and Carrio's whole quiet network of people the world liked to pretend it couldn't see. They had a small beaver's impossible, nagging suspicion about a rat.

And they had one thing the Overseer had said, meant only for Addie, waiting in the back room of her mind for the moment she'd be ready to look straight at it.

Not yet. But soon.
