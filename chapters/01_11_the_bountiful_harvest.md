**Chapter 11: The Bountiful Harvest**

The Bountiful Harvest, Tallow-tier, Lower Quarters, City of Luminthalas, State of Ascentia

Aegalor, 3rd of Hearthwake, Conjunction Day 1, Hour of the Singularity [Noon +5 hours]

<span class="beacon beacon--green" role="img" aria-label="Green Beacon"></span> Green Beacon - Day 1 of Conjunction #1

</div>

The white drained out of the courtyard slowly, the way afterimages fade, and left the tallow-tier looking somehow smaller than before.

The peacekeepers had come apart. Their neat phalanx had broken and drifted back toward the canal gates, and pikes hung loose or lay where they'd been dropped, and not one of them, Addie saw, had the slightest appetite for testing her a second time. The civilians in the line hadn't run. They stood pressed against the walls, hushed and blinking, watching her the way you watch weather that has just done something weather should not be able to do. High above, on the skybridge tiers, the long scrying-lenses that swept the lower quarters had gone dark — cracked, some of them, or simply blind, overloaded by a light they'd never been built to survive. Whether that was for an hour or forever, Addie couldn't have said, and didn't have the strength to wonder.

She was leaning on the ironwood staff with most of her weight, and her arms were shaking, and her cyan braids lifted and crackled faintly against her neck with a static that hadn't been there before. The pendant hung heavy against her sternum — heavy in a way silver shouldn't be, cold at the center and warm at the edges — and she left it exactly alone, because she had learned by now that reaching for a thing you didn't understand only taught it that you were afraid.

Beside her, Mae had gone still. Not calm — Addie had made the mistake of reading that stillness as calm once and would not make it again. This was the stillness of a drawn wire. And Carrio was still half-coiled inside his hood, the venom-reach not entirely put away, the gum that had ridden his jaw all day gone motionless somewhere behind his teeth.

Nobody had died. That, for the moment, was the whole of the victory, and Addie would take it.

---

Somewhere inside the restaurant, a low work-song had been going — a rough, tuneless kitchen hum, the sound of someone who sang without noticing they sang.

It stopped.

The door opened again. Rosalind came out first, a tall harried human woman with an order-pad still forgotten in one hand, and she gripped the doorframe hard enough that her knuckles stood out white, her eyes going from the broken guards to the dark scrying-lenses to the woman with the glowing braids and the staff.

Brynn came out behind her, and did not grip anything.

She was soot to the elbows and grease to the eyebrows, a cleaver still in one square hand, and she stopped on her own porch and read the whole courtyard in about two seconds flat — the scattered garrison, the frightened line, the armored dark-elf woman vibrating with restraint, the hooded serpent with murder half-drawn, the exhausted stranger leaning on a stick, and the *scroll* in the dark-elf's grip. She read it in the order of a woman who had spent a life expecting trouble, and she came, unerringly, to the worst possible conclusion.

"So," Brynn said. Her voice was low and gravel-rough, her accent a blunt dwarven thing, controlled and clear. "It's finally come to my door, has it. The great machine. And it's brought the bloody *guard* with it, and lit my whole street on fire doing it." She hefted the cleaver, not a threat exactly, more a punctuation. "Whatever you lot are selling, I want no part of it, and I want you gone before you cost me a single plate more."

---

"We're not with the guards," Addie said. Her voice came out rougher than she'd meant, sanded down by the flare. "We're the reason the guards are *leaving.* They came to shut your street down. We're trying to keep them out of your business, not add to it."

"Everyone with a scroll," Brynn said, "is somebody's business."

"Not this one." Addie made herself straighten off the staff. "This didn't come from the Conclave. Not the way you mean — not a levy, not a summons, not a debt. And it did *not* come from your family." She saw the small flinch that ran through both women at the word, and understood the middle finger nailed over the door all at once, and gentled her voice. "That's what the sign's about, isn't it. You thought these were coming from *them.* From back home. That every glowing letter that turned up on your step was your people finally tracking you down to hand you a bill."

Rosalind's head came round. "Brynn. What is she talking about. What *is* that thing."

Brynn didn't answer right away. That, more than anything, told Addie the truth — that the dwarf already suspected exactly what it was, and hated it too much to say it out loud.

"There's been talk," Brynn said finally, grudging, to her wife more than to Addie. "In the market. The green light coming tonight. Folk being — *tapped,* they're saying. Named. Some old cosmic nonsense waking up, and the great families and the city fathers making their usual almighty mess of the world—" she spat the words "—and then, as ever, expecting the likes of *us* to down tools and go clean it up for them."

---

"It's not talk," Addie said. "It's not a rumor. The scroll is the proof of it." She held it out, not pushing, just offering. "I'm not going to pretend the source is clean, or that anyone asked you nicely. But it's real."

Rosalind had gone very quiet, and she was not looking at the scroll. She was looking at her *street* — at the broken guards, at the hushed line of hungry neighbors, at the district that would still be here, and vulnerable, long after this strange armored company had gone. When she spoke, it was not to Addie at all.

"Brynn." She said it low. "If this is what they're saying it is — if the whole city's in it, if the *street's* in it—" Her jaw tightened. "Then you don't get to say no. I know you want to. I know exactly what it costs you, love, and I'm not going to stand here and tell you it's some grand calling and you're honored to be chosen, because that's a lie and we're not liars." She took a breath. "But these people can't fix it without you, and *our* people are in it whether you go or not. So you'll suck it up. And you'll go. Because the neighborhood matters more than your pride does, and you taught me that."

Brynn looked at her wife for a long moment.

Then she set the cleaver down on the porch rail, and reached out with lye-cracked, work-scarred fingers, and took the scroll, and broke the green seal.

The wax didn't crumble. It dissolved — lifted off the vellum in a drift of small green embers that rose and cooled and winked out over the courtyard — and beneath it the writing swam, and settled, and became a name.

*BRYNN H. STONEVEIN.*

"H," said Carrio, very quietly, the first thing he'd said since the flare. "Again."

And Addie felt it land in her chest, relief and exhaustion braided so tight she couldn't have pulled them apart — the last name of the first set, the opening circle closing, the pattern holding one more time exactly as it had held every time before. She didn't explain it. There wasn't the strength or the hour for a lecture. It was just true, and it settled over her like the end of a very long held breath.

---

Brynn stared at her own name burning quietly on the page, and something in the square set of her shoulders went down.

"Twenty years," she said. Not to any of them. To the scroll. "I spent the better part of twenty years getting *away* from exactly this. From Khaz Vorem. From the — the inheritance, and the title, and the marriages they'd have made for me, and the long cold shadow of a house that valued me at precisely what I could be *traded* for and not one copper more." She laughed, short and without humor. "I ran. I gave up every stone of it. I found a woman who loved me and a kitchen that was *mine* and a street full of people who don't care what my grandmother's name was, and I built a whole life out of *not being that.*" She lifted the scroll. "And here it is. The pattern. Found a crack in the wall and reached right through it to haul me back."

"It has a way of doing that," Mae said.

Addie looked at her, surprised — because there was no edge in it, none of the Speaker's usual blade. Mae was watching the dwarf with something almost like recognition.

"The spool," Mae said. "Whatever it is, whoever's turning it. It doesn't lose a thread it wants. It just waits, and it finds the way back to you. I know a little about being pulled back into a house you spent your life escaping." A pause. "For what it's worth — which is not much, tonight — I'm sorry it found you."

And Addie, standing exhausted in the middle of it, looked at the four of them and felt the shape of something she had no time to hold. A dark-elf princess in field armor. A serpent-blooded prince in a poisoner's hood. A dwarven chef with flour under her nails and a highborn name she'd buried. And herself, a broke academic from a working city, with her dead mother's pendant heavy against her ribs. Nothing about them matched. Not their stations, not their peoples, not one line of their public lives.

And every thread that actually bound them — the hidden inheritances, the broken histories, the small silver mark that three of them wore and one of them, impossibly, did not — ran somewhere underneath all of it, in the dark, where she couldn't yet follow it.

Not tonight. She let it go. It would keep.

---

She looked up past the skybridges to the Citadel clock, and did the arithmetic, and didn't like the answer.

"We have less than an hour," she said. "The green beacon comes tonight, and when it does the portal grid goes down for the phase change. If we're not back through by then, we're walking, and we don't have the days to spare for walking." She turned to Brynn. "Can you leave? Now? I won't pretend it's a fair thing to ask."

Brynn looked at Rosalind.

Everything was in that look — twenty years of a life built together, a restaurant, a street, a bed, a whole small good world — and Rosalind met it, and gave her one nod. Single. Brave. Not a smile anywhere near it.

Brynn went inside.

She was back faster than she should have been, a heavy leather satchel already over one shoulder, packed and buckled, and Addie understood without being told that this bag had been sitting ready by a door somewhere for twenty years — that some part of Brynn Stonevein had never once, in all that time she'd been away, fully believed the past would let her stay gone.

"I can leave," Brynn said. It was not eager. It was the voice of a woman going to a thing she'd sworn she never would. "Let's get it over with."

---

The crowd did not cheer.

Addie had half-braced for it — the recognition, the noise, the thing that had happened on the imperial steps. But the tallow-tier had watched a woman turn the sky white with her own body, and it had watched one of their own shoulder a satchel and step out of a life, and it understood, in the wordless way that hard places understand things, that this was not an occasion for cheering.

They stepped back instead. All along the courtyard, the hungry, patient, three-hours-cold line drew itself aside and opened a corridor — a clean path from the porch to the lane-mouth — and stood in silence to let the company through. It was not worship. Addie was certain of that, and grateful for it. It was the older, graver thing: an acknowledgment that something dangerous was passing, and a decision to give it room.

She walked it feeling the true weight of the scroll in her hand and the changed quality of the quiet, and thought, with a cold little certainty: *the old rat knew.* The Overseer had sat in Mae's dining room and slipped them Luminthalas like an accident, and had known all along exactly which soot-black kitchen in which forgotten tier they would find the last of them in.

"He held her back on purpose," Carrio murmured beside her, his hood easing down at last, though the smooth court mask hadn't fully returned to his face. "You realize that. The one he made us come and fetch ourselves, on foot, the long way. The *necessity.* He called it a practice, but it was never really about the dwarf." He glanced sidelong at Addie. "It was about seeing what the ten of us do when we're thrown at a locked door together."

Addie didn't answer. She was too tired, and there was already a cold new certainty forming under the exhaustion — that a first sequence completed was only ever the beginning of a second, that somewhere out ahead another name was waiting to be pulled through another wall, and that she would not get the time to feel finished before it came.

Behind them, the restaurant door swung shut. Somewhere inside, no one resumed the humming.

The tallow-district began, slowly, to breathe again — the line inching forward, the guards melting off toward their barracks, the ordinary evening reassembling itself out of the pieces of an extraordinary one. But the silence the company left behind them felt permanently changed, the way a room feels changed after a bell has rung in it, and the group moved through it toward the terminal, and the closing grid, and the long way home.
