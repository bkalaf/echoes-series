## Day One Architecture

Morning came in grey and heavy and fog-bound, and found Addie already awake, because she had promised Mae eight hours of sleep and had managed, generously counted, about three and a half.

She lay in the black-stone guest room and did the math on her own exhaustion the way she'd do it on anyone else's — clinically, without sympathy — and concluded that she was operating on a deficit no bath could clear, and that she was going to have a bath anyway. Her bags had come in the night; someone had carried them the whole way from a hotel in Skyrend Peak and set them, unopened, at the foot of the bed. She dug through them not for clothes but for the small zippered kit at the bottom, and when her fingers closed on it something in her chest eased for the first time in two days.

Hair dye. The dye she'd started in a per-diem hotel room and never gotten to finish, because a floating eye had appeared over the sink and summoned her into the worst forty hours of her life, and she'd walked into the Conclave with the job half-done and silver showing at the roots like a confession.

She had exactly one decision she'd made entirely for herself since the beacon lit, and it was going to be this one.

Not the red. Not the blue. She held up the vial and looked at it in the fog-light and chose, with great deliberation, the brightest, most unserious, most aggressively unprofessional electric cyan in the kit — a color that said nothing whatsoever about Conjunction historiography or cosmic administration or the weight of anyone's expectations, a color chosen for the single reason that it was hers to choose.

But first: the bath.

The bathing room off the guest quarters was, frankly, obscene. A sunken basin of black stone big enough to drown a small committee, fed by four brass nozzles, each apparently plumbed to a different pleasure — she turned one and lavender steam rolled out, turned another and the water ran a warm cinnamon crimson that smelled of cardamom and someone else's much better life. It was absurd. It was also, she noted, perfectly practical: grab bars where you'd want them, non-slip stone, a bench at exactly the right height. Rich, and *functional.* Like everything in this house. Like the woman who owned it.

There was a bell in here, too — mounted by the bench, where a soaking bather could have reached it, an old brass servant’s bell exactly like the one downstairs by the kitchen door, and exactly as dead: its clapper drawn, its throat packed with a twist of oilcloth, lacquered over so many times it had half sunk into the wall. That made two.

She sank in to her chin and let the pieces of the impossible day float around her. A dwarf who'd shot a scroll and worse. A restaurant called the Bountiful Harvest. Archives somewhere in a city called Luminthalas that might explain why the cosmos wanted her there. A dam. A river. Twenty-five days that hadn't even started counting, because the green beacon wasn't due until tonight. She turned it all over in the steam and did not solve any of it, and let herself, for a quarter of an hour, not have to.

---

The morning got away from her the way stolen mornings do, and it was well past the Hour of the Iron Foundry before she climbed out, wrapped in a towel the size of a sail, cyan freshly worked through her hair and still faintly damp at the edges.

Then she heard the roar.

Not a beast. A crowd. She crossed to the tall window, and the fog had thinned enough to show her the grounds, and the grounds were *full* — a heaving press of people crammed up against Mae's gates and spilling down the avenue. Civilians. Protesters with painted boards. Reporters, dozens of them, and the long brass recording-lenses of the broadsheets on their tripods, and a line of house guards holding the gate, and beyond them a nervous cordon of city soldiers holding the crowd.

And then, one by one, with the awful slow inevitability of sunflowers turning, the recording-lenses came up off the crowd and swung toward the manor, and found the window, and found *her* — a woman in a towel and nothing else, wet cyan hair plastered to her skull, framed in the glass like a specimen.

Addie did not dive away, because diving away would have been worse, would have been *content.* She simply stood there for one long ruined second, meeting a hundred lenses with the flat dead eyes of a person who has just understood, completely, that she no longer owns a single private moment for the rest of her natural life — and then she stepped, with as much dignity as the situation allowed, out of the frame.

"Wonderful," she told the empty room. "That's going to be everywhere by lunch."

---

Someone had been in the room while she bathed.

She noticed the way she noticed everything — the arrangement was wrong from how she'd left it. On the bed, laid out with the care of a valet or a coroner, was an outfit that had not been there an hour ago. And it was, she saw as she lifted it, extraordinary: a deep-weave tunic in a charcoal so dark it drank the light, layered over dark reinforced leather, the whole thing lined in something that turned out to be Gloamwood silk, with panels of hidden padding sewn in at the places a knife would go looking. Beautiful. Expensive past reason. And built, unmistakably, to keep the person inside it *alive.*

There was a note. Two notes, really, in the same hand but two entirely different voices.

The first was gracious: *A few things, put together for the day. They'll suit you, and they breathe. Welcome to the field. — M.*

The second, below it, was not: *The leather's reinforced over the vitals. Kindly do not test this. And should you insist on bleeding, do it in the street — the foyer stone is a nightmare and Max will never forgive either of us.*

Addie found herself smiling despite everything. This was, she understood, exactly what Mae's care looked like: intrusive, presumptuous, faintly threatening, and *materially, completely useful.* Someone had measured her without asking and dressed her for a war she hadn't agreed to and would absolutely need the padding for.

She dressed. It fit like it had been grown on her. And last, before the tunic closed over it, she lifted her mother's pendant — the small plain silver disc, the Mark of the Orbs pressed into its face, the only thing she had left that was older than her grief — and settled it against her chest where it belonged, under everything, close to the skin.

Then she went down to breakfast.

## Breakfast Before Departure

She was late, and the dining room was already a warm riot of food and people — staff and companions folded together down the long table, platters of eggs and fruit and dark bread, the whole household eating like a family that happened to contain a Conclave Speaker and several instruments of cosmic policy.

There was a bell here as well, above the sideboard — its small brass mouth stuffed with felt and tied off with a ribbon so old its color had given up. Addie had counted three or four of them now, between her room and this one, and the shape of it finally resolved the way a proof does: one in every room, and every last one deliberately, patiently silenced. Not broken. *Kept, and gagged.* She filed it with the house’s other quiet contradictions and reached for the coffee.

Rhün's chair was empty. She filed it.

Everyone else was in new field clothes — variations on her own, cut to their bodies and their needs, and the sight of the whole group kitted out matching-but-not gave her an odd lurch, half comfort and half dread.

"There she is," Carrio said, silken and delighted. "Our Moderator. Radiant. Cyan." A pause, entirely for cruelty. "Well-documented."

"Don't," said Addie.

"It's already on the loop," Gregory said, not unkindly, and tipped his head toward the sideboard, where a projection stone was throwing a soft light-image against the wall — and there she was, towel and all, wet-haired in the window, playing over and over to whatever the broadsheets were saying about it. He adjusted a pair of spectacles he absolutely did not need to adjust. "They're calling it *The Moderator Unveiled.* One of them wrote a great deal about the color."

"I'm going to walk into the sea," Addie announced, and sat down, and took bread.

"Thank you for the clothes," she said to Mae, more quietly. "They're — thank you."

"Everyone got a set," Gregory said. "Overnight."

"Presentation and survival," Mae said, from the head of the table, buttering something with the focus of a surgeon. "You're going to be looked at now whether you like it or not — so you'll be looked at as *mine,* which is to say competent and hard to kill. And the padding is padding. Both things are true."

"They are *very good* clothes," said a small, ancient, damp-skinned voice, and an elderly froglok in a tailor's apron beamed his way into the room, hands clasped, radiating the specific joy of a craftsman whose work is being worn. "Nine full field kits. In a night. The reinforced seams alone — I have not done reinforced seams like that since—"

"Barnaby," Mae said, without looking up, "if you tell the Moderator the seam story before she's had a single egg, I will throw this roll at your head."

"The seam story is a *good* story—"

Mae threw the roll. Barnaby caught it, one-handed, without breaking stride, popped it whole into his wide mouth, and departed radiating triumph, and the table laughed, and Addie sat in the middle of it and understood something she filed away as important: that this household did not run on fear. It ran on loyalty freely given, and freedom, and the deep contentment of people permitted to be excellent at the things they loved. Nobody here was afraid of Mae. That, Addie thought, was the most dangerous thing about her.

---

Then Mae set down her knife, and the temperature of the room changed.

"Scholar," she said. "Last night. When you told us what happened in the mirrors." She met Addie's eyes down the length of the table. "You filtered it."

Addie went still.

"You gave us the shape and kept the edges," Mae said. "I don't blame you — you'd had a day that would've broken most people, and old habits hold hardest when we're tired. But I'm going to say this once, and then we'll never have to have it again." She folded her hands. "Complete honesty. From here. All of it, edges and all, however ugly, however unfinished. Because I cannot send you into a room alone with a frightened informant if I'm not certain you'll bring the whole truth back out of it. We already have one Mother in this story pulling strings from behind a curtain. I would very much prefer not to add you to that list."

The word landed wrong.

*Mother.* Addie's hand had found the pendant through the tunic before she'd decided to move it, and something cold and old came up her spine, and her voice, when it came, was quieter and harder than she meant.

"Be careful with that word," she said. "My mother has been dead eight months. You do not get to reach for her to win a point about my paperwork."

The room went very quiet.

Mae did not flinch, but something in her face opened — recognition, not retreat.

"My mother," Addie said, and made herself let go of the pendant, "used to give money to people on the street. Beggars, drunks, people any sensible person could see would spend it on exactly the thing that was killing them. And I asked her once — I was small, I was a little *monster* about it — I asked her why, when she *knew* half of them would waste it." She turned the bread over in her hands without eating it. "And she said: *most of them will, and that's not the point.* She said trust fails more often than it works, and you do it anyway, because the one time it lands — the one person who takes the coin and buys bread or a bed or a way out — that one is worth all the times it didn't. And because handing someone the coin *at all* tells them you believe they're still allowed to choose. That they're still a person who gets to decide." She looked up. "So. I filtered it. That was wrong, and I'll not do it again. But I did it out of the reflex of a woman who's spent her life being handed *nothing* and made to prove she deserved it. I'm not the thing behind the curtain, Mae. I'm the person you just decided to hand the coin to. Don't make me sorry you did."

A long silence.

"Fair," Mae said at last, and meant it. "That's more than fair. Intent matters to you — noted, and I'll remember it." She picked her knife back up. "We're all learning to do this under a clock none of us set. I'll try to weaponize your grief considerably less. You'll try to hand me the edges." A beat, drier. "And we'll both fail at it occasionally, and forgive each other, because the alternative is losing."

"The alternative is losing," Addie agreed.

It was, she would think later, the first real border they'd drawn between them — and the first one either of them had agreed to respect.

---

"Right," Addie said, because forward was the only direction she trusted. "The day. First problem — there is a small nation camped on your lawn, and we need to get through it."

"Fireballs," said Brickett immediately.

"No," said the entire table, in perfect unison.

"I'm just *saying,* a couple of good—" A small flame guttered to life over his open paw, entirely unbidden, the way it did when he got excited; he looked down at it, betrayed, and smothered it against the tablecloth, leaving a fresh little scorch mark among the older ones. "—I could clear a *path.*"

"You could clear a *headline,*" said Gregory.

"A formal escort," Major Tallandra offered, her eyes never once still — she'd been reading the windows and the doorways since she sat down, out of pure habit. "A garrison column, and we go through the crowd behind shields."

"And announce to all seventy-two cities that the Moderator travels under Crown guard," Gregory said. "No. The one thing we have that no faction can buy is that we belong to none of them. The moment we look Concord-aligned, we're a piece on somebody's board."

"He's right," Matthieu said, already sketching — a quick spray of boxes and arrows on the back of a napkin, teams and lines and dependencies taking shape under his pen as he spoke. "Independence is our only real leverage. We spend it the moment we borrow anyone's soldiers." He tapped the napkin. "But here's the actual work, before we worry about the door. We can't all do the same thing, and we can't all go to the same place. So."

Addie took the napkin, and between them they built it.

"Three teams," she said. "First — the scouts. We need eyes ahead of us: someone to find the Bountiful Dam on the Jadefall before the trail's cold, and to work out what the *third* city is before it lands on us the way Luminthalas did. That's Aes Sidhal, the old Satyr city, and that's you three." She looked down the table. "Matthieu — the pattern's yours. Yurislav — you've got the instinct for what a place is hiding. Major, you keep them breathing."

Matthieu's pen was already mapping the route. Yurislav rolled his worn coin across his knuckles, once, slow, and said nothing, which from Yurislav was agreement.

"Second — the archives. Luminthalas has to be next for a *reason,* and I need to know what thread I'm supposed to bring to Horror before I'm standing in front of Horror. So: the library. Gregory, that's you — nobody reads a dead archive faster. Brickett, because you see things nobody else sees. And Rhün, because if it goes wrong I'd like someone along who can't easily be set on fire."

Gregory's hand paused on his bracelet. "You're pairing me," he said, with great care, "with a magma elemental and a fourteen-year-old who *cannot stop trying to become a fire mage,* and sending the three of us into a building made of *paper.*"

"Yes," said Addie.

"Just so it's on the record that I flagged it."

"Flagged," Addie agreed. "Third team — Brynn. The scroll. Someone's got to walk into a lower-quarter restaurant and convince an exiled dwarf who shot her last invitation that we are not her family's debt collectors. That's me, and Carrio—" *diplomacy and a serpent's read on a room* "—and Mae, because if it comes to a negotiation I want the most dangerous person in Erzengel sitting at my shoulder."

Mae's coin appeared between her fingers and flipped, caught, flipped. "Flattery," she said, "will get you exactly as far as it deserves. Which today, is far. Fine."

## The Front Doors

"That still leaves the crowd," Tallandra said. "We have three teams and one very full lawn."

"No we don't," said Rhün, from the doorway.

He'd come in unhurried, banked and steaming faintly in the cool morning, and he had the look of a man returning from a chore. "I handled the crowd."

"You *handled*—" Addie started.

"Brimscale's on the front gate," Rhün said. "Sitting on it, more precisely. He's decided it's warm and he likes it. And Ashwing's up on the parapets, and every so often she stretches her wings out over the avenue and reminds everyone what she is." A flicker of something almost like affection. "The lenses have mostly stopped pointing at the house. It's difficult to photograph a Moderator when there's a fire drake looking at you."

"A *flying* fire drake?" Brickett was out of his chair. "You have a *flying* one? Does she — can she — how big is the flame, does she *breathe* it or is it a gland thing, because I have *theories*—"

"She is not a study subject," Rhün said, "and you are not getting near her jaw."

"Ashwing," Gregory repeated, and his scholar's precision surfaced through what looked, briefly, like genuine unease. "You understand a true fire drake doesn't merely *carry* flame, it *renders* it — the internal chemistry alone, in an enclosed space, near anything that burns—" He stopped himself, and adjusted the spectacles. "We are going to be in an archive. With Brickett. And now I'm thinking about drake biology. I would like the record to show I am thinking about it very hard."

"Noted twice now," said Addie.

The teams locked. Matthieu, Yurislav, Tallandra for Aes Sidhal and the dam. Gregory, Brickett, Rhün for the archives. Addie, Carrio, Mae for Brynn. And they'd split in Luminthalas and stay stitched together the only way scattered people could be.

"Eye-mail," Matthieu confirmed, already noting the threads. "One link per team, cross-routed, so any team can reach any other. We check them on the hour."

"And you," Max said, appearing at the door with a tray and the serene menace of a good butler, "will all please try very hard not to break the world before dinner. Lady Mae is doing a roast."

---

They were gathering in the great black-stone foyer to leave when a young dark elf came through the side door at a dead run — a stable hand, courier-mud on his boots — carrying a small padded case in both hands as though it might bite.

"Your Highness," he gasped, and held it out. "The order from the apothecary. Sealed. They said to tell you the hemlock's the good batch."

The foyer went very still.

Mae took the case without hurrying, checked the seals with a glance, and made it disappear somewhere inside her coat. "Thank you, Del. Tell them I'll settle the account Karskov." The coin, Addie noticed, was no longer moving between her fingers. It had simply stopped, closed into her palm, and something in the whole set of the woman had gone from *play* to *work.* "Diplomatic preparedness," Mae said, to the room, mildly. "One keeps a Venom-Blade's hand in. It's rude to let the craft rust."

And Addie thought: *the poison is not the part.*

"You're a *princess,*" said Brickett.

Everyone looked at him.

"Crown Princess," Brickett corrected, frowning at the middle distance where he kept his impossible files. "Of the royal house of Nox Vaelor. It was in the census rolls. Van'kareth's the ruling line — I saw it when I was reading up who everybody was, it's right *there* if you look—" He caught the quality of the silence and trailed off. "...Was that not a known thing?"

"It was not," said Carrio, with enormous interest, "a *widely* known thing."

Mae regarded the fourteen-year-old for a long moment, and then, to Addie's surprise, she smiled — a small, real, slightly rueful thing. "No last name and reads census rolls for fun," she said. "You are going to be a great deal of trouble, aren't you."

"That's the plan," Addie said, before Brickett could answer, and caught Mae's eye. "And it's a conversation for after. We have a dwarf to go and not-die in front of. Crowns can wait until we've all slept and eaten and not been on fire."

"After," Mae agreed, and the coin came back out, and flipped, and the work-stillness folded away under the play again — but Addie had seen underneath it now, and Mae knew she had, and neither of them said so.

---

Max set his hands to the great obsidian doors and looked back at them all — the nine of them, kitted and armed and improbable, a historian and a princess and a serpent and a soldier and a scholar and a theorist and a bear and a magma man and a fourteen-year-old beaver with singe marks on his sleeves.

"Try to come back," he said, "with roughly the number of pieces you're leaving with."

He pulled the doors open.

Outside, Brimscale lifted his enormous head off Mae's front gate and *roared* — a sound that went through the stone and up through Addie's boots — and the crowd's noise cracked and scattered. And high above, off the parapet, Ashwing dropped into the air and swept low over the avenue on wings of banked fire, and a hundred reporters forgot entirely about towels and cyan hair and remembered instead that they were soft, and made of the kind of thing that burns.

The nine of them stepped out into the blinding, fog-burning, late-morning light, into the noise and the lenses and the first real day of the impossible thing.

Yurislav ran his coin across his knuckles, once, and looked out at the mess of the waiting world, and the crowd, and the whole machinery of cosmic administration that had picked them up and pointed them at it.

"Ditto," he said, to no one, to all of it.

And they went down the steps into the morning.
