**Chapter 13: The Thread Back Out**

Family Room, Van'kareth Estate, City of Aquila Matara

Aegalor, 3rd of Hearthwake, Conjunction Day 1, Hour of the Star-Siren [Noon +6 hours]

<span class="beacon beacon--green" role="img" aria-label="Green Beacon"></span> Green Beacon - Day 1 of Conjunction #1

</div>

The sitting room had grown strangely crowded, though no one new had come in. It was the attention that filled it — every head in the room turned toward one small froglok in a tailor's apron, who had been retrieved from the corridor mid-mutter and installed on an upholstered chair he plainly felt he had not been measured for.

Barnaby smoothed a wrinkle out of his vest that was not there. Then another one, in the same place. "Why," he said, at last, "is everyone looking at me like that? Have I got chalk on me? I've usually got chalk on me."

"You've no chalk," Mae said, and her voice had gone warm in the particular way that meant she wanted something and had decided to be gentle about getting it. "Barnaby. I'd like you to tell us the story you just told us. The one about the thread. Only tell it properly — as though not one of us had ever heard it. As though you were teaching it to us for the first time."

"It's only an old story, my lady. Everyone knows it."

"We don't," said Addie. "Not really."

Gregory leaned forward. "May I?" he asked Mae, and she waved him on without hesitation. He turned to the tailor with the careful courtesy he usually reserved for primary sources. "Barnaby. Don't only tell us what happens in it. That, I suspect, we could piece together. Tell us why it *matters* to you. Why this one, out of all the stories you know."

Barnaby blinked at him, thrown — a man asked, for the first time in a long life, not what the story was but what it *meant* to him — and after a moment something in his shoulders settled, and he set his small hands on his knees, and began.

---

"Well," he said. "It's only old mythology, you understand. Nothing — nothing *cosmic.*"

Addie watched the room, because Mae had taught her to, and she saw perhaps half of them ease at that — a loosening of shoulders, the small relief of people who had braced for another impossible thing and been handed, instead, a bedtime tale.

"I always did love the old myths," Barnaby went on, warming. "The ones from the old republics. The ones everyone learned, back — you know. Back then."

"Thalorion," Matthieu supplied helpfully, naming the oldest republic anyone at the table had a name for.

"No, no." Barnaby corrected him as easily as he'd have corrected a seam. "Older than that. Ancient Greece."

The room went quiet.

It was a small quiet, and a strange one, because it was the silence of ten educated people discovering, all at once, that not one of them had ever heard the words *Ancient Greece* in their lives — and that the little tailor had said them the way you say the name of a place, a real place, somewhere that had been. Addie felt the wrongness of it go down her spine like cold water. She said nothing. She filed it, whole and unexplained, on the shelf she was beginning to think of as *things that are older than they should be.*

Barnaby, oblivious to the temperature he'd changed, gestured vaguely at the air. "Before all this. Long before. When people were — oh, primitive things, you understand. Sticks and stones and barely a handle on fire." He chuckled. "Which is why I like them, really. You can see the whole shape of a lesson in a story from before people got clever enough to hide it."

Addie leaned in.

---

"That's the thing about the good ones," Barnaby said. "They've a *point.* A lesson at the bottom, if you dig." He brightened. "And this one's got *thread* in it, which — well. I'm a tailor. You can see why it stuck."

He frowned, gathering it. "There's a hero. Ethius, I think his name was. Etheus. Something like that — it's not important, the names always slither about on me. And there's a king. Midas? Minos. One of the two. *He's* the villain, more or less. And there's his daughter, a princess, and — now, everyone tells this part wrong," he said, with a tailor's disapproval of a badly finished hem. "They'll tell you it's a love story. It isn't, quite. *She* loved *him.* That's the truth of it. Whether he loved her back—" he waggled a webbed hand "—the story's never so sure."

Gregory's eye twitched, very slightly, at *the names always slither about on me,* but he held his tongue, and Addie loved him a little for it.

"The king had built a maze," Barnaby said. "An enormous one. And every so often he'd send young people into it — tribute, punishment, I forget the why — and in the heart of the maze there lived a monster. Half a man, half a bull. Horns on it. It killed everyone who wandered in." He paused, and his voice dropped, and the craftsman's cheer went out of it. "But here's what I always thought was the clever, frightening part. The monster wasn't the worst of it. The *maze* was. Because the maze *moved.* The walls shifted behind you as you went. The path you came in by wasn't there anymore when you turned round to leave." He looked at his own hands. "So even if you were strong enough to kill the thing — even if you *won* — you'd wander that shifting dark until you starved, because knowing your way *in* told you nothing at all about your way *out.*"

Mae’s coin stopped. Across the room, Max caught her eye and held it half a second too long, and neither of them said a word. Addie clocked the look and filed it in her back room, beside every other thing in this house that did not yet have a label — the silenced bells, the servant who never went outdoors, the way the whole place felt built to keep something in or something out. She did not understand it yet. She only marked the shape of the gap where the understanding would go.

The room had gone still, and it was a different stillness than before. Addie felt it move through all of them at once — because a maze that rearranged itself behind you, where victory bought you nothing but a lonelier death, was not, tonight, a bedtime tale. It was a description. It was the exact shape of the thing they were all standing inside.

---

"So the princess gives him two things," Barnaby said, and here, at last, he truly brightened, because this was plainly the part he had been waiting his whole telling to reach. "Two gifts, before he goes in. The first is a sword — a good one, sharp enough for the job. And the second—" he touched, almost unconsciously, the small spool of crimson thread clipped to his belt "—is a ball of red thread."

"And he does the clever thing. Before he sets one foot inside, he ties the loose end of that thread to the doorpost. And *then* he goes in, unspooling it behind him the whole way, deep into the dark, through all those shifting walls — and he finds the monster, and he fights it, and he kills it."

Barnaby stopped.

"And do you know," he said slowly, as though the thought were arriving to him fresh even now, after a lifetime of tellings, "the *sword* was enough to kill the beast. That's what strikes me, every time. He never needed the thread to *win.*" He looked around at them. "He needed the thread to come *home.* Without it, he'd have stood there, victorious, over the dead monster, in the middle of a maze that had already closed itself up behind him — and he'd have died in the dark all the same. A winner. Lost forever." He spread his small green hands. "That's the whole of it, to me. That's why it stuck. What's the *point* of going in and beating the terrible thing, if you've no way to find your way back out afterward?"

No one spoke. Addie let the silence sit, because it deserved to, because she understood without being able to say it that the little tailor had just laid the beating heart of the entire matter bare on Mae's good carpet — and had no idea he'd done it.

---

"Do all your people know this one?" Addie asked, when it felt right to speak again. "The way you tell it?"

"Oh, most of us." Barnaby positively glowed. "We're the finest tailors in the realm, my people — I'll say it, someone has to — and thread's, well, thread's *sacred* to us, isn't it. So this one gets close to the heart." He patted the crimson spool at his belt. "Parents tell it to their little ones. Every young tailor hears it, sooner or later, over a first hem. The thread that brings you home." He said it like a benediction.

"Questions?" Mae asked the room.

No one asked one. It wasn't for lack of them — Addie had a dozen — but every question anyone had felt suddenly too small, or too large, to set down next to what the man had just told them. Mae read it correctly, and rose, and thanked Barnaby with real graciousness, and sent him on his way.

Addie caught him at the door.

"Barnaby. The traveling clothes." She touched the deep-weave sleeve. "I meant to say it this morning and the world kept interrupting. They're the finest thing I've ever worn. They breathe, they *move,* they held together through a very stupid day. Thank you. Genuinely."

The tailor's cheeks flushed a deep, delighted green. He put a hand to his chest. "I'd do anything for Lady Mae," he confided, "anything at all — but I'll tell you, Miss Sonntag, quietly—" he glanced down the hall and lowered his voice "—you're coming a *very* close second." He beamed. "And thank *you.* For asking after a story that meant something to me. Not many think to." Then, already turning, already gone in his mind, he began muttering happily to himself: "—now the *dwarf,* she'll want a doubled shoulder and a heavier weave, none of your drape, and boots-cut at the ankle for the width of the calf, I'll want a proper measure before she—" and his voice trailed away down the corridor.

---

The room stayed quiet until the last of that muttering had faded.

"Right," said Carrio, breaking it. "I'll ask what everyone's thinking. That's the answer, isn't it? Ariadne's Thread. That's the one we bring to Horror."

Several heads nodded.

"Maybe," said Gregory.

Addie couldn't help herself. "*Maybe.* Of course you'd say maybe. If someone handed you a sunrise you'd want it peer-reviewed."

Gregory smiled — he did have one, rarely — but he didn't retreat. "I'd want to be *certain,*" he said. "Because think about what we're dealing with. Horror gave you three threads and one riddle, on purpose, and let you flounder. A Witness is perfectly capable of handing us something that is *almost* right — close enough to feel like the answer, wrong enough to be a grave. Those are the dangerous ones. The obvious-wrong answer, you check. The *almost*-right one, you march straight into." He folded his hands. "So before we bet the mission on a tailor's bedtime story — good as it is — I'd like to know we've read the actual riddle correctly. And I don't think we have. It still doesn't parse. It never has."

---

"I don't get it either," Brickett admitted, from his rug, with the air of a great confession. "The riddle. I've been pretending I do but I *don't.*"

"Nor I," said Carrio.

"Nor I," said Yurislav, from the shadows.

"We have looked at it," Matthieu said, rubbing his eyes, "for the better part of a day, and it still reads like a sentence assembled by someone who hated us."

Brynn, who had been sitting through all of this with the patient boredom of a woman waiting for the interesting part, spoke up. "Well, what *is* it, then. This riddle. Nobody's actually said it in front of me."

Mae took the parchment from the side table and handed it across.

Brynn read it once — lips moving, the whole thing, slow — and set it down on her knee and looked up at the room like they were all being a bit thick.

"You're reading it wrong," she said.

The room stared at her.

"Look." She was unbothered by the staring; she'd plainly been stared at before. "I talk like *this.* You know what that means? It means half of you have misheard half of what I've said since I walked in that door, because you're hanging the pauses in the wrong places. It happens to me every single day of my life. Somebody says a thing dead plain, and it comes out sounding like something else *entirely,* all because of *where they stopped.*" She tapped the parchment. "This Horror. When it gave you this. Did it say it slow? One word. Then a stop. Then another word."

"Yes," Addie said. Her pulse had picked up. "Exactly like that. Word by word."

"Aye. Because that's how it *wanted* you to hear it. It hung the pauses for you. Wrong on purpose." Brynn reached over, plucked the quill from the inkstand, laid the parchment flat on the table, and made one small, deliberate mark on it — a single stroke, no bigger than a grain of rice. Then she turned it round to face them.

"There," she said. "Not a word's changed. Read it again."

Addie read it. And the pause moved, and the whole world moved with it.

*Bring the thread that makes the horned, dead charge.*

Not *the horned dead* — not some army of antlered corpses she'd been picturing without quite picturing for a day. The comma sat after *horned,* small and absolute, and split the phrase clean in two, and what had been one meaningless lump of words became two clear and separate things, and pointed, suddenly, *outward.*

Gregory saw it a half-second before the rest — she watched it hit him, watched the scholar's face open. Mae smiled, slow and genuine. Carrio breathed a soft, sincere obscenity. And Brickett, a beat behind everyone as usual, sat bolt upright and said, "*Oh.*"

Addie looked at the mark. A single stroke of ink, smaller than her smallest fingernail. And it had turned a wall into a door.

---

"'The horned dead,'" Matthieu said, tasting the old wrong reading and discarding it. "That's what tangled us. We kept hunting a *thing* — horned undead, some monster with antlers. But that grouping's gone." He set his finger by the comma. "*Horned,* on its own. And then — *the dead charge.* The dead, charging. Rising. Moving. And *horned* sitting off to the side of it like a — like a label. A clue about *which* dead, or *whose,* or *where.*"

"Two pieces," Gregory agreed, and for once there was no *maybe* in him. "A qualifier, and an action. Ariadne's thread to bring us home from the maze — and a horned *something,* and a charge of the dead, waiting at the other end of it." He looked, frankly, a little unnerved by how much sense it suddenly made.

"We don't have the whole of it," Mae said, rising, and there was something in her stance that hadn't been there all evening — the coiled readiness of a woman who has just been handed a direction. She looked around the room, at the tailor's crimson spool of a story and the exiled cook's single stroke of ink and the green light still pulsing sick against the windows.

"But we've got the thread," she said. "And we've got the comma. And unless I'm very much mistaken—" she almost smiled "—I think we finally know where to begin."
