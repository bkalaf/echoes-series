## Mae Estate Arrival

The door opened before Addie's knuckles reached it, which meant that her knock — already committed, already traveling — very nearly landed on the face of the person now standing in the gap.

He caught her wrist a hand's breadth from his nose without appearing to hurry, a satyr in black formalwear so immaculate it made her feel like something scraped off a boot. "Miss Sonntag," he said, entirely unbothered. "Welcome. You'll forgive the ambush — there was a small wager belowstairs about whether Brimscale had eaten you, and I confess I'd bet optimistically."

"Who," said Addie, "is Brimscale."

"The magma lizard." He nodded past her into the courtyard, where Rhün was crouched beside an enormous, contentedly steaming reptile the size of a plow-ox, working a thumb behind its jaw with the tenderness of a man scratching a favorite hound. "The Master Ignis's mount. Temperamental about strangers. He decided he liked you, which is the only reason we're having this conversation."

"Honestly," Addie said, "I sort of wish he'd eaten me. It would have simplified my evening."

The satyr laughed — a real one, surprised out of him. "Oh, you and I are going to get along." He stepped back and swept her in. "Maximilian T. Bellwether. Head butler, cook’s assistant, occasional accountant, unofficial therapist to a household of dramatic personalities, and whatever else Lady Mae decides I am before breakfast." He inclined his head a precise degree. "Max, though, if we’re to be friends — and I suspect we are."

"Adeshka Sonntag. Moderator, apparently. Portfolio also, it turns out, flexible."

The foyer was black stone, vaulted and grand, and it should have been cold — the architecture of a woman feared across seventy-two cities — but it was not. Chipped mugs sat on a shelf beside carved wooden animals; a child's drawing was pinned, unironically, in a place of honor; there were pressed flowers under glass, river stones, a fan of letters tied with string. Mounted on the wall beside the kitchen doorway was an old brass service bell — the kind wired through a great house to summon staff from any room — except its clapper had been bound up in a twist of felt and waxed thread, so that it could not have rung if the manor had caught fire. Addie noted it the way you note a scar on a friendly face, filed it, and said nothing. Staff moved through it all unhurried, at an hour when most great houses would have gone dark and stiff.

"Is it not late," Addie asked, "for this much staff?"

"They live here," Max said simply. "As, for that matter, do I — I’ve not set a hoof past the front veranda in more years than I’ll admit to a stranger. The lawn and I have an understanding." He said it lightly, the way a man says a thing he has decided to find funny rather than examine. "Lady Mae is of the opinion that if your people can’t live with you, they oughtn’t be your people." He glanced at her, dry. "You'll find most of what's assumed about her is wrong. It's rather her favorite weapon."

A voice rolled through the whole house then, unbothered by walls — *Max, will you get her moving, they've all been sitting twenty minutes and the soup will not hold its dignity forever* — and on its heels came the smell: bread, garlic, herbs, something slow-cooked and enormous. Addie's stomach made an undignified noise.

"Ah — one thing before you go in," Max said, lowering his voice. "The sixth slot filled while you were... away. High Theorist Matthieu H. Cardinal. Brilliant. Bewildered. Cleans his spectacles when he's about to say something that ruins your week. And Gregory's here." A pause, delicate. "You know him?"

"Four years," Addie said, and offered nothing else.

Max, wisely, took nothing else. He pushed open the kitchen door and announced, to the warm room beyond, "The missing Moderator, recovered."

---

The kitchen went quiet the way a room does when everyone has been talking about you.

It was a working kitchen and a warm one — copper hung from the beams, herbs dried in bunches, bread cooled on racks, and staff and companions were folded together around the labor of a real meal. And at the center of it, in a plain dark tunic and a cook's apron, holding a chef's knife in one hand and a half-diced carrot in the other, stood the most dangerous political mind in the Conclave.

Addie understood, all at once, what the sound behind the door had been. Not a threat. A whetstone. Mae had been sharpening her cooking knife.

"Scholar," Mae said.

"Speaker," Addie said.

"Mae." She set the knife down. "We are past titles. Sit down."

Addie did not get the chance. A small, damp, outraged projectile launched itself across the kitchen and hit her at hip height.

"MISS SUN-TAG."

Brickett had been *bathed.* Comprehensively. He was fluffed to nearly spherical, his broad tail scrubbed to a shine it had plainly never known and deeply resented, still faintly damp, smelling of soap laid over the older honest smell of wet fur and woodsmoke — and under all the fluff the singed patches still showed, dark little maps of his own misfired magic. Somewhere, Addie thought, Barry was nursing a set of scratches he would be explaining for a week.

"They wouldn't tell me where the *nasty rat* took you," Brickett said into her sleeve, muffled and furious. "Nobody would say *exactly.* I was about ready to have *words* with Mother, and I've never even met her."

"He made that abundantly clear," Mae said, and laughed, a real one. "Several times. At escalating volume."

She wiped her hands on the apron and looked at Addie, and something in her went briefly serious. "I owe you an apology, Scholar. In that chamber I spoke to the Moderator. The office. I did not once speak to Adeshka Sonntag, who'd had, I gather, an extraordinarily bad day." She tipped her head toward the room. "Sit. Eat something. And then — when you're able — tell us what happened to you."

Gregory, without a word, patted the chair beside him.

He looked exactly as she'd left him four years ago and never once thought she'd see again: composed, exact, a dragonkin scholar with a priest's stillness and a face harder to read than most people's silences. Their eyes met. Neither of them performed anything. She sat.

## The Companion Table

She gave them the riddle first, because it was the cleanest thing she had.

"*Bring the thread that makes the horned dead charge.*" She let it sit in the warm kitchen looking as useless as it had in the dark. "Three candidates. The Red Thread of Fate. Ariadne's Thread. The Thread of the Norns. I don't yet know which is right, or how you'd even go about finding out."

"That," said Carrio, from down the table, elegant and unhurried, "is quite a lot."

"That," Addie agreed, "is the correct response."

She told them the rest as plainly as she could. That there had been two of them — two mirrors, two memories, and that *memory* was the wrong word, because there had been a floor under her feet and a smell in the air and cold on her skin; they had been real in every way that mattered except that she could not be touched by them.

The first she told carefully, and less than fully, because it would not let her tell it fully — a dark council chamber under a wrong sky, a round black table, names burning like small flames over empty chairs, a tired woman who joined the room from somewhere else entirely, somewhere loud. Eighteen people deciding, she said, what to carry and what to leave, because they had already decided — before she got there — that they had to *leave;* that some enormous thing was coming, and there were ships waiting on a grey sea, and not enough of them. And that the parts that mattered most kept sliding out of her hands as she reached for them, like she'd been allowed the shape of it but not the center. "But two things came through clean," she said. "They built the whole thing on purpose. Twenty-four founding city-states — chosen, by hand, in a room, on a bad night. Not fate. Not the moons. *Designed.* And they gave it a name, and the name was ours."

Gregory had gone very still.

The second mirror she told without any trouble at all, and hated that. Aquila Matara — this city — half-built, the throne room finished before the streets were, a young High Sovereign on the throne. Dovren Judax Thorne, the first to wear the name. A court man with a leather folio named Geoffrik Vayne, walking the king through a scheme: a river diverted at a place called Bountiful, the drowned-out farmland bought up quietly through shells, sold high to settlers, salted to failure, bought back for nothing. A wetland and its people erased for a profit taken twice — and then, she said, and here her own eleven years of work stood up in her chest, *dressed up as a conjunction.* Filed as a cosmic disaster. Blamed on the sky, so no one would ever go looking for the hands. And a symbol built to sit on top of all of it: a rainbow arch, three coins descending. The thing on every coin she'd ever held.

"And when the spouses overheard the arithmetic," she finished, "Vayne took them, the same way you'd take a stain out of a rug."

Mae did not look surprised. She looked tired in a way that had nothing to do with the hour. "That's the part that's genuinely depressing," she said. "Not that it happened. That none of it surprises me."

"So." Addie made herself say it. "Twenty-five days. Which means I don't actually have the time to be anything other than fine, so if we could—"

"*No,*" said Brickett.

He came around the table and stood in front of her and held out one soap-scrubbed paw, palm up, with the total gravity of a fourteen-year-old who has decided something.

She took it.

"Everybody in this room wants something from you," Brickett said. "And every single bit of it can wait." He counted on his free paw, small claws ticking. "Your mum died eight months ago. Somebody put a *brick* at your head yesterday. This morning a scroll made you a Moderator whether you wanted it or not. Then a whole Conclave. Then you gave a speech to a hundred thousand people. Then a *rat* pulled you out of the world for two hours." He looked around the table, flat and unimpressed with all of them. "And not *one* of you asked if she was okay. If her head hurt. If she'd eaten. If she wanted a hug." He looked back at Addie. "So. Are you okay? Does your head hurt? Have you eaten? Do you want a hug?"

The whole warm room seemed to realize, at the same moment, that it had traded care for answers without noticing.

Addie laughed — cracked and wet and entirely real, the first honest sound she'd made all day. "That," she managed, "means more than you know."

"I know," Brickett said. "That's why I said it."

She wiped her eyes with the back of her wrist. "I'm okay. As much as the situation allows. My head doesn't hurt. I have not eaten, no." She looked at the bread cooling on the rack. "And I'm going to need approximately seven pieces of that bread."

Mae, without a word, turned back to the stove and began filling a bowl, and her approval was in every line of her back.

---

Gregory cleared his throat, once, the careful way he did before saying a true thing.

"You've always done everything yourself," he told her. Not unkind. Just accurate. "Four years I watched you vanish into the archives and come back with work that didn't answer the question everyone was asking — it *rewrote* the question. Alone. Every time." He turned his cup a quarter-turn on the wood. "That worked, Adeshka. I'm not going to pretend it didn't. But it will not work anymore. The problems in front of you are going to get harder, not simpler, and the specific thing people like you and me are worst at — the exact skill — is asking for help *before* the crisis instead of after it." He held her eye. "So learn it now. While it's cheap."

"We're companions now," Rhün said.

Just that. Flat, and settled, and certain, banked heat in the shape of a sentence.

Addie's head came up, because that was the *word.* The precise word the thing in the dark had used. The word she had carried out of the Witness-space and told no one.

"You didn't say it first," Rhün observed, reading her face. "I did." He let that sit. "But you were thinking it."

And the room went quiet in the specific way of people all realizing at once that a coincidence had just become a pattern, and a pattern, in a week like this one, was starting to look like architecture.

"For the record," Brickett offered into the silence, "my thing earlier about her head hurting — that was *also* being funny. As well as caring." He considered. "It did not land. I accept that."

---

Matthieu H. Cardinal took off his spectacles and began, very deliberately, to clean them.

Everyone who had known him more than a day braced.

"Forgive me," he said, mild and precise, "but has anyone actually *looked* at the names on the announcement scroll? Not read them. *Looked* at them." He held the lenses to the light. "They share a middle initial. Every one. *H.*"

A beat.

"Adeshka *H.* Sonntag," he said. "Gregory *H.* Frydrake. Major Tallandra *H.* Highwatch. Carrio *H.* Vessalor. The young man — Brickett *H.*"

"Rhün H. Ignis," said Rhün.

"Mae'vyri *H.* Van'kareth," Gregory added quietly. "Yurislav *H.* Arslan." He grimaced. "I noticed. I told myself it was noise. H is a common initial."

"H is common," Matthieu agreed. "One name sharing it is nothing. Two is a shrug. But *every* name on an eleven-slot roster —" he settled the spectacles back on his nose "— that is where coincidence starts to become expensive to believe in."

"And if the names not yet written," said Major Tallandra, slow and even, "also carry H — then the probability stops being small and becomes *absurd.*"

Matthieu and Gregory looked at each other and, appallingly, began to do sums.

"If one remaining name carries it," Gregory said, "you're already somewhere near one in seven hundred and thirty-seven trillion."

"And if both do," Matthieu said, with the quiet delight of a man watching the universe misbehave, "roughly one in twenty-two and a half quadrillion. Which is —" he searched for the image "— approximately the odds of a specific snowflake surviving a blender. That is also on fire."

"I take mild offense to being a data point in that sentence," said Rhün. "But the math is probably right."

"So it wasn't random," Addie said.

"No," said Matthieu. "It very much was not."

"The soup," Mae announced, untying nothing, deciding everything, "has held its dignity as long as it's going to. We can talk threads and quadrillions at the table like civilized people. Everybody up."

---

The dining room was set for more than eleven, because the staff ate too — a long mahogany table down the center of it, ringed with high-backed chairs.

Eighteen of them.

Addie counted, and could not help counting, and the number landed differently than it would have a day ago, because a day ago she had not stood in a dark room and watched eighteen people at a black table decide the shape of the world. She said nothing. She sat.

Two settings at the far end sat clean and untouched. She looked a question at Mae.

"The last two on the list," Mae said. "Whenever their names get written in." A small shrug that wasn't as light as it pretended. "It seemed rude not to have a place ready."

The table settled into the ordinary miracle of people eating. The bread was, if anything, better than it had smelled. Addie had gotten through two pieces of her seven when she heard it — a slow, shuffling, unhurried gait coming down the corridor, cane and heel, cane and heel.

The Overseer appeared in the doorway, swallowed to the ankles in an oversized gray wool coat, and the room went quiet one person at a time, like lamps being turned down along a hall.

"A word," he said, "with the Head Moderator."

"You can have several," Addie said. "Sit down. There's soup."

He looked, for a moment, at the two empty settings at the end of the table. Something moved behind the old face and was gone before she could name it. Then he crossed the room and sat.

Mae sent a bowl down the length of the table without a word or a glance. The Overseer received it correctly, with both hands, the way you accept a thing that was made with care — and he tasted it, and he did not perform indifference to it, which told Addie more about him in one moment than the entire Conclave had.

"It's good," he said, "to see you all together. Getting along." He had another spoonful. "Mother chose well."

The table erupted — every companion at once, questions colliding.

He raised a single finger.

The room stopped as though a string had been cut.

"I will answer one question from each of you," he said. "In a moment. First — I've come to ask for your help." He let the strangeness of that settle. "And before anyone asks: yes. The powers presently turning this machine require the help of the people at this table. That is not politeness. Free will, here, is *structural.* It is not a courtesy we extend. It is a load-bearing wall." He set his spoon down. "The third of your number is refusing her scroll."

---

He reached into the gray coat, and his hand went in considerably further than the coat had any business allowing, and came out with evidence.

The first scroll had a heavy iron hunting arrow driven straight through it.

The second was crumpled, mud-caked, and had plainly been thrown some distance into something unpleasant.

The third he set down last, and with visible reluctance, and it was *damp.* "We are not entirely certain," he said, "what became of the third. Only that it came back... compromised."

Brickett leaned over and smelled it.

"She peed on it," he announced.

The table handled this with varying degrees of grace.

"She *definitely* peed on it," Brickett confirmed, nose working. He sniffed again, frowning. "Dwarf?"

The Overseer's eyebrows rose a fraction — genuine, small surprise. "...Yes. Well done."

"We need your help," he went on, to Addie, "because the summons will keep failing while she believes it comes from someone else. She is not, strictly, refusing to *join.* She has been involuntarily exiled from her homeland, and has therefore concluded that any glowing official document arriving out of nowhere is her family attempting to locate her and, most likely, to *invoice* her." A dry pause. "In that light, I find the arrow and the urine entirely reasonable."

"Her name?" Addie asked.

"Brynn Stonevein."

"Middle initial," said Matthieu, pen already moving.

"H," said the Overseer, "of course. We've established the rule, I believe."

The whole table exhaled at once — the hypothesis confirmed by the universe's own clerk.

"She's in Luminthalas," the Overseer continued, "which is convenient, as you'll all be heading there anyw—" He stopped. "Oops."

Mae's eyes narrowed to blades.

"You'd have deduced the city order regardless," he said, unhurried, entirely unrepentant. "I've merely saved you an argument. She and her wife keep a restaurant in the lower quarter. The Bountiful Harvest."

Down the table, Gregory's hand paused over his bowl. He said nothing. He filed it — Addie watched him file it — the two words *Bountiful Harvest* going into whatever private ledger he'd been keeping since the mirrors. She clocked the reaction and filed *that.*

"Any other post you'd like me to hand-deliver," Addie asked, "while I'm playing courier for the cosmos?"

"The ninth," the Overseer said, "has accepted the scroll. But is not yet ready to be known. That is his right."

"Was opting out of dinner an option?" Carrio asked, with feeling. "Because I'd have liked to know that was on the table."

"For the ninth, at present, it is the only functional answer," the Overseer said. "It is not available to the rest of you."

Brickett, who had been quietly counting something on his claws throughout, looked up. "The ninth's a human."

The Overseer regarded him with something that was almost fondness. "Clever little beaver. Yes."

He produced one last scroll — pristine, uncreased, unpeed-upon — and set it in Addie's hands. "For Brynn. Only she can break the seal. Should anyone else try, it will simply become gray mist and be gone, and I will be disappointed, and you will have to explain to me why." He left it there. Then, rising, he paused by Carrio's chair. "Those are excellent earrings."

Carrio — who was never, ever caught off guard — was, for exactly one second, caught off guard.

"Now," said the Overseer, lowering himself back into his seat with a grunt, and reaching again for the soup. "Your questions. One apiece."

---

"Why H?" Matthieu asked first.

"H is no more important than any other letter," the Overseer said, "except in the single respect that it is the one you all share. A hardcoded commonality. The letter is a coincidence. That it is *shared* is not." He tasted the soup again. "The better question — the one you didn't ask — is why it sits in the *middle.*"

"Then that's mine," Gregory said. "Why the middle position?"

"You already know." The old eyes settled on him, unblinking. "Consider what a middle initial has always stood for. What it carries, in a name. You know exactly what the middle position means, Gregory Frydrake, and I am not in the habit of handing a man an answer he's already holding." He turned back to his bowl. "Ask me something you don't know next time."

Matthieu wrote that down like it was scripture.

"Why are the beaver and I lettered," Rhün asked, "instead of numbered?"

"*Excellent* question," said Brickett, with enormous approval.

"Administratively, you are identical to the primary Moderators," the Overseer said. "The distinction is functional, not a ranking. Under the Law of Threes, only nine active seats may exist at the final resolution. Nine. No more." He gestured, cane-tip, at Rhün and then Brickett. "You two are — think of a trial. The alternates. The ones who must be present from the very beginning, who hear everything, who miss nothing, so that if a seat falls empty you can step in and render an informed verdict without a day's delay. Your *capacity* is not lessened by the letter. Only your slot in the count."

"That is not reassuring," Rhün said.

"His answers rarely are," said Addie.

"What if she refuses regardless?" Major Tallandra asked. "Brynn."

"We are confident she will accept, once she understands the summons did not come from her lineage." He considered. "Reasonably confident."

Tallandra didn't let it go. "Then why send us? You're standing in this room. You could carry the scroll to Luminthalas yourself between one breath and the next."

The Overseer looked, for a moment, almost respectful. "I could. That I *can't simply do it* — that the variance was built to require *you* — appears to be deliberate. Mother's hand. An early exercise." He returned to his soup. "Consider it a practice quest. Before you ever have to face the Witness of Horror, it would be well for you to learn how to travel, deliver, and persuade as a group. Better to fumble it over a dwarf's dinner menu than over something that bites."

"And our existing obligations," Carrio said. "Territories. Duties of blood and rank. What becomes of those, across a cycle of Conjunctions?"

"We would never prohibit your duties to your lineage," the Overseer said. "Though I will note that cities being *destroyed* tends to reduce one's obligations considerably."

The word dropped into the warm room and froze it.

"*Destroyed,*" Mae repeated.

"I was going to raise that," Addie said quickly, "after dessert. I covered the important par—"

"The *destruction of cities,*" Mae said, with terrible calm, "is extremely important, Scholar." She turned the full weight of herself on the Overseer. "Explain that. Now. You do not get to threaten populations over soup in my house and move on to the next agenda item."

"It is not a threat. It is a mechanism." The Overseer set his spoon down, and for once there was no dryness in it. "The final battle requires all eighteen of the legendary objects. Each is one thread of a single spool. A spool cannot draw with a thread missing — it fouls, it fails, the whole weave comes apart. If your quests fail, the objects don't gather. If the objects don't gather, the final battle is lost before it's fought. And what follows a lost final battle is not a thing any of you have the imagination to want." He looked around the table, old and level. "This architecture was not designed to be *comfortable.* It was designed to *work.* Those are not the same, and I am sorry for it."

---

"For what it's worth," the Overseer said, into the silence, "the destruction of the cities — if it comes to that — would be a *mercy.*"

No one moved.

"Compared to what he would do." A pause. "Compared to what he did to me."

The dining room went utterly still. Even the staff stopped.

"I asked for death," the Overseer said, very quietly, "for a thousand years. I welcomed it. I begged for it, in the end, which is not a thing I say to frighten you; it is only what happened." He turned the soup spoon over in his old fingers, watching the light run off it. "He would not give it. First he kept me because I was *useful.* And then, when I had stopped being useful, he kept me anyway — because he had worked out that my suffering, continued without limit, without end, was itself a kind of use." He set the spoon down. "So understand me. We do not want to harm your people. We want the precise opposite of that, with the whole weight of someone who has learned what harm is from the *inside* of it. We would very, very much rather you won." A beat. "I would rather you won than almost anything I can name."

It landed and did not lift.

Yurislav Arslan, who had not said a single word all night, spoke.

"The half-built throne room," he said. "The founding of this city, in your vision." He was looking at Addie, but the question was for the Overseer. "Was that the initialization of the seventy-third node? The one that woke yesterday?"

The whole table turned to him.

The Overseer paused, and it was a real pause — the pause of a clerk handed a far more precise question than he'd budgeted for. "I probably shouldn't answer that," he said. "It shows you more of the frame than is good for you this early." He looked at Yurislav a moment longer. "Yes."

Addie leaned in. "The figure in that throne room. The one who guided me through it, who looked at me and spoke to me. That was *you.*"

"No," said the Overseer.

"It wore your—"

"It wore a shape *like* mine." Something in him had gone careful. "That memory happened during the thousand years I was... otherwise occupied. I was not available to walk anyone through anything. *She* was. She wore a form resembling this one." He gestured vaguely at his own stooped body. "And she stopped moving through the world at the exact moment she gave me back to it — because when I returned, I took the form back from her." He stopped himself, and Addie saw him register that he'd already gone too far. "Which is considerably more than I intended to say."

The lamps seemed to draw in. The corners of the room deepened.

"Time for me to skedaddle," the Overseer said.

And he was gone — not dramatically, not in a swirl. Simply no longer in the chair, the soup bowl empty, the wool coat's warmth already fading from the seat.

## Next-Day Planning

The room stayed suspended for a long moment, everyone holding what he'd left behind.

It was Max, of all people, who broke it — stepping out of his professional invisibility with the air of a man who could not physically hold the question in any longer. "How," he said to Brickett, "did you know the ninth was human? He said nothing. There *is* no ninth here to read."

"The math was obvious," Brickett said, around a mouthful of bread. He looked around the table, mildly scandalized. "Why has everybody stopped eating? You still have *bodies.* Bodies need food whether or not a rat's been threatening you."

That, somehow, was what restarted the table — people quietly, sheepishly, serving themselves again.

"I've been turning it over," Yurislav said slowly, pressing a worn soldier's coin flat against the wood under one broad thumb. "The two figures. The one who was, and the one who is. I think they may be the same — the one who was taken, and the one left behind wearing his shape."

"Master Brickett," Matthieu said, and there was nothing but respect in it, "would you do me the courtesy of explaining your reasoning? Properly. Start to finish."

Brickett straightened. Being *properly asked* did something to his whole spine — a house-less kid who had spent fourteen years being talked over, suddenly handed the floor by a High Theorist. He picked up a carrot off his plate and held it like a pointer.

"Fancy university degrees," he said, not unkindly, "miss the forest for the trees. All that math you did for the *H* — that's true, but it's *hard.* The other thing's easy. You just have to look."

He pointed the carrot at Mae, then Gregory, then the empty setting. "Mythos. Mythos. Mythos — pending." He swung it to Yurislav, then Tallandra, then Carrio. "Beastfolk. One, two, three." He swung it last to Matthieu, then Addie, then the *other* empty setting. "Human. One, two, third one pending." He set the carrot down like a man resting his case. "Three horizons. Mythos, Beastfolk, Human. Three in each. Nine seats at the table when it's all done. *That's* your Law of Threes."

Matthieu's pen had stopped moving.

"And it goes up-and-down too," Brickett said, warming to it, picking the carrot back up. "Lowborn. Middleborn. Highborn." He walked it across the grid. "Lowborn — Miss Sun-Tag's a working archivist, Yuri's a wanderer, and the pending mythos is probably your peed-on dwarf. Middleborn — Gregory's a scholar, the Major's a Major, Matthieu's a professor. Highborn — Mae runs the whole Conclave, Carrio's a *prince* unless those earrings are lying, and the last human, the one hiding from dinner — highborn too. Bet you anything."

Mae had put down her wine and was looking at the fourteen-year-old with open, startled interest.

"That is *elegant* pattern recognition," Matthieu said, faint.

"How," Gregory asked, "did you see the shape of it before any of us? You laid out the whole table's structure from the salad."

"Dunno." Brickett shrugged, honest. "It was just *there.* Same as the peed-on scroll. Some things are just there and I don't get why nobody else sees them." He frowned, and turned the carrot over, and for the first time looked genuinely stumped. "Only bit I couldn't crack was me and Rhün. Why we're out on the edge. We don't go in the boxes anywhere."

"Because the boxes weren't built with us in mind," Rhün said quietly. He didn't sound wounded. Just certain. "A lowborn dwarf gets exiled. An elemental like me gets called *illegal* right up until six years ago. The people who drew those three neat horizons never once imagined a magma hybrid or a house-less kid who names himself. We don't fit the grid because the grid never expected us." He looked at Addie. "So we're not in it. We're attached to the person who has to hold it. We pair with you."

Brickett's face lit like a struck match. "*That's* the missing part."

And Addie looked at him — this soot-marked, freshly scrubbed, house-less child who had put his own small body between her and a falling brick, and who had just read the architecture of the entire returning cosmos off a plate of vegetables — and felt something turn over in her chest that she had no defense against.

"Brickett," she said. "I think you really were sent."

He beamed at her, whole and bright and unguarded. "Sent by Mother."

"Yeah," Addie said. "I think you were."

---

She looked down the length of the eighteen-chair table and took inventory, because inventory was how she loved things she couldn't say she loved.

The pristine scroll by her plate, waiting for a dwarf who'd sooner shoot it. Two clean, empty settings at the end, kept ready for names not yet written. Gregory, still quietly turning *Bountiful Harvest* over behind his eyes. Yurislav's coin pressed flat under his thumb. Carrio, composed again, the one crack in him already sealed over.

"Did anyone else notice," Addie said, "that the old rat just handed us our first destination and pretended it was an accident?"

"Luminthalas," Tallandra confirmed. "He said we'd be going there anyway. Then caught himself."

"Either he genuinely slipped," Mae said, "or he chose to frame a decision he'd already made as an accident, so it would feel like *ours.*" She swirled her wine. "With that one, I'd put my money on the second. Always."

"Either way," Tallandra said.

"Either way," Mae agreed. She set the glass down and looked around her table — the companions and the household folded together over the wreck of a good meal — and made the call the way she made all of them, fast and final. "Right. We know where we're going. We know who we're collecting. The rest of it is an impossible thing, and impossible things need a *plan,* and a plan needs a brain that's slept." She turned to Addie. "How long have we got, exactly?"

"Twenty-five days," Addie said. "From the green beacon."

"Twenty-five days." Mae nodded once. "So no one does one single further thing tonight until they've had eight hours of sleep. Not a scroll, not a theory, not a quadrillion. We are going," she said, with total conviction, "to fucking *need* it."

The table began, at last, to eat in earnest — the meal finding the easy rhythm it had been reaching for all night, the terror shelved for the length of a shared dinner.

Yurislav pressed his coin flat one more time and looked at the space where the Overseer had been.

"I really don't like that rat," he said.

And every voice at the eighteen-chair table — companion and staff, human and beastfolk and mythos, without a glance exchanged or a beat of coordination — answered him at once.

"Ditto."
