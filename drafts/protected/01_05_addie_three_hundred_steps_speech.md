The crowd was quiet. That was the thing that struck her first — not the size of it, not the press of faces stretching back to the far end of the plaza and beyond, not the signs or the silence of the Portalport behind her. The quality of the quiet. She'd studied enough historical accounts to know what a frightened crowd sounded like before it became something worse, and this was it: the breath held between the end of the last thing you understood and the beginning of whatever came next.

She looked at the red-haired woman standing at the front of it. Still there. Still standing her ground. Addie registered the fear in her eyes and noted, with something close to respect, that it hadn't moved her feet.

She looked over her shoulder.

Tallandra stood on the steps behind her, wings drawn close, her expression a blade with nothing behind it. The Captain of the High Sovereign's personal dispatch — white-and-gold armor, a medical brace on her left wing, eyes that had been doing threat-assessment since before Addie had sat down for her first academic examination. She represented the Crown. The Sovereign's reach. The idea that order, maintained at sufficient cost, was indistinguishable from peace.

Verrick stood beside her. Soft-footed and precise, with the specific quality of stillness that belongs to people who have spent long careers saying nothing while ensuring they were present when everything happened. He was the Speaker's man — the personal representative of the Speaker of the Conclave, which was a different thing from the Conclave itself, a distinction Addie considered important and suspected most people in this plaza did not currently have the bandwidth to parse. The Conclave was seventy-two cities. The Speaker was one person. And the people a person chose to send to stand on steps at critical moments said rather a lot about what that person wanted to happen next.

And Addie stood in the gap between them and the people they governed.

Schism, Tallandra had called her. That word, deployed as a shorthand, a category, a polite container for the ones we haven't fully managed yet. The forgotten and the overlooked and the ones who'd learned to move sideways through structures that were never built to include them. Her guild. Her people. And yes: the ones who, in extremity, had learned to manipulate from the shadows, because the shadows were the only architecture they'd been given.

She had a few things to say about all of it.

She turned back to the crowd, and she didn't project her voice. She let it carry on its own, the way it did when she wasn't performing. When she was simply telling the truth at a volume that matched the size of the space she was standing in.

"My name is Adeshka H. Sonntag," she said. "I know you know that. I know it's been moving through this crowd for the last two hours, attached to about fifteen different theories, most of which I would love to hear because I suspect some of them are more accurate than what I've been told officially."

A sound moved through the front of the crowd — not laughter yet. The precursor to it. The releasing of something that had been held.

"Yesterday morning, I woke up in a transit dormitory because I'd traveled overnight for a research appointment. The hair color I'd been trying to fix for three weeks was still wrong. I had a seven-hour day of archival work ahead of me, and I was trying to decide whether the bread in the canteen was worth the line." She paused. "It wasn't. For the record. It never is."

The laughter came now. Quiet, almost surprised at itself.

"And then the beacon lit."

The laughter stopped.

"I want to be precise about what I am," Addie continued. "Because you deserve precision right now more than you deserve comfort. I am a Conjunction Historian. I hold three degrees — in astrophysics, sociology, and anthropology — from the Volgrad Institute, which is not a prestigious institution, because Volgrad is not a prestigious city." She said this without apology, without self-deprecation, as a plain statement of geography. "It's a working city. It has a working university. The people who go there study things because the things interest them, not because the credential opens a particular door."

She glanced back at the steps. At Verrick.

"The Conclave considers conjunction studies a dying field. I was told this, explicitly, by a senior funding committee, approximately four years ago, when my research grant application was denied for the third consecutive cycle. The phrase used was academically non-productive. The phrase I used in response is not appropriate for this particular crowd." Another brief ripple of laughter. "The point is: whatever entity is currently running this process apparently disagrees with the Conclave's assessment of my work. I find that genuinely satisfying, and I want the record to reflect it."

Her voice shifted. Not louder. Heavier.

"I don't know what the title I've been given means in practical terms. I don't know the scope of it. I don't know what I'm authorized to do or prohibited from doing, because the scroll was written in a dialect that hasn't been primary in four hundred years, and the three translators dispatched this morning each produced a different reading and then spent two hours arguing about it instead of resolving the discrepancy." She looked at Verrick. "Which I understand is consistent with the institution's general operating methodology, so I suppose I should not have been surprised."

Verrick's expression did not change. His eyes did.

"What I can tell you," Addie said, turning back, "is what I've spent eleven years learning. And I'm going to tell you right now, in this square, in front of the cameras and the reporters and the guards and the people on those steps — because you deserve to hear it before it gets managed into something more palatable by people who find the original version inconvenient."

She let the silence sit for one full breath.

"We have been lied to about the conjunctions."

The quiet that followed was different from the quiet before. Deeper. More careful.

"Not in small ways. Not in the ordinary way that history smooths over complexity in favor of a cleaner story. I mean that we have been systematically, deliberately, and in several cases legally prevented from understanding what these cycles actually are — because the correct understanding of them is profoundly threatening to the people who benefit from the incorrect one."

She turned her face upward, toward the darkening horizon where the secondary moons were beginning to lean into convergence, their silhouettes unmistakable against the twilight.

"Over the course of my career, I have successfully disproven twenty-five events that our official histories classify as conjunctions. Twenty-five. Events that our textbooks cite as cosmic interventions — as forces beyond human agency, as the machinery of fate moving according to its own indifferent logic. Events for which we have mourned, built monuments, written elegies, and most importantly — never assigned responsibility." She paused. "Because you cannot assign responsibility to a moon."

She brought her gaze back down.

"What those twenty-five events actually were: localized magical catastrophes caused by infrastructure that was not maintained because maintaining it was expensive. Mass famines caused by distribution policies that prioritized export revenue over domestic survival. Wars that were begun by single families whose third generation of political power had begun to confuse the state with personal property. Epidemics that spread because the regions they started in were not considered worth the cost of a quarantine response. Collapses. Purges. Clearances of land and people that our language doesn't have good words for — so we borrowed the word conjunction instead."

She was not shouting. She was not performing. She was reciting the findings of eleven years of archival research in the voice of someone who has finally been given a room large enough for what they know.

"Here is what makes that lie so elegant, and so durable." Her voice was level, almost academic, and somehow more damning for it. "Conjunctions were real. They happened regularly — every few years, reliably, for as far back as our records extend. And they did coincide with upheaval, with transformation, with the kind of seismic political shifts that terrify anyone who benefits from the current arrangement. Power could change hands. Structures that had calcified over decades could be broken open. For a brief window, the rules were different."

She let that sit.

"And then, one thousand years ago — they stopped."

The plaza was absolutely still.

"No one knows why. I will not stand here and pretend I do. The moons were still moving. The celestial conditions were still present. But the conjunction itself — the event, the mechanism — went silent. And it has been silent for a thousand years." She looked at the moons, then back at the crowd. "Until yesterday."

A murmur moved through the thousands of gathered faces — not panic, not yet. Something more primal. Recognition.

"Here is what I find interesting, as a sociologist and an anthropologist, not just an astrophysicist: in the thousand years since the conjunctions stopped, we did not stop having catastrophes. We did not stop having famines, or wars, or collapses, or purges. We did not stop doing the things to each other that history had previously filed under cosmic intervention. We simply lost the filing system." Her voice took on a dry, precise edge. "And in losing it, we had to confront, briefly, the possibility that we were responsible for what was happening to us. That the machinery producing our suffering was not celestial. That it was administrative."

She turned. She looked at the steps.

"That was an uncomfortable possibility. And I want you to look at the institutions in front of you and ask yourself how long they've been comfortable."

She faced the Concord first. Tallandra held her gaze without flinching, which Addie noted and did not mistake for agreement.

"The High Sovereign's guard exists to maintain order," Addie said. "I want to say that plainly and without drama, because I think most of the dramatics around this subject are distractions from the plain version. Order is not inherently wrong. The maintenance of it is not inherently violence. But I want to ask a precise question, and I want it answered precisely: whose order? Ordered in favor of whom? Maintained at whose cost?" She paused. "Because the order I have observed, in eleven years of studying how power distributes itself across this nation, is not neutral. It has a direction. It flows upward. And the people standing at the bottom looking up at it do not experience it as peace. They experience it as weight."

She didn't shout it. That was the thing. She said it the way you say a number — the way you read a finding aloud in a room full of people who have to hear it.

"That is not the guard's fault, individually. I want to be precise about that too. The guard does not design the systems it enforces. But the guard does enforce them. And the guard has been, for a very long time, the reason those systems do not have to answer for themselves. You cannot interrogate an institution that can arrest you for asking."

She turned.

"The Ruin faction —" and here something shifted in her voice, a register that was less clinical and more personal, the way a voice changes when it's moved from the record to the room "— I know you. Not all of you personally. But I know what you're selling. I know the shape of it, because I've read every iteration of it across eleven historical cycles, and it has the same structure every time: the current architecture is so corrupt that its total demolition is not just justified but necessary, and the suffering required to produce the future you've theorized is an acceptable exchange, and you have done the math on behalf of people who were never asked to participate in the calculation."

A sharp quiet in the section of the crowd she knew was listening most carefully.

"I understand why that argument is compelling. I understand it the way you understand something that is emotionally coherent and historically wrong. Radical transformation is real. It happens. But it does not come from the outside in. It does not come from burning the structure down and assuming that what rises from the ash will be better than what preceded it, because you willed it to be." She shook her head. "I have read what comes after, in every case. What comes after is whoever was most organized in the rubble. And I want you to think, carefully, about whether that has ever been the people you claimed to be liberating."

She stopped.

She breathed.

"And then there is us," she said. Quieter now. Different in register. "Schism. My people. The ones who were told there was no seat at the table and found their way into the room through the window and the service entrance and the places where the walls weren't quite flush. The ones who learned that the rules of the powerful don't apply to people the powerful have forgotten to include — and found, over time, that this was a kind of freedom. That existing outside the official accounting meant existing outside official accountability."

She looked out at the faces that were hers — the ones wearing the expression of people hearing themselves described by someone who is not making it a critique, but a reckoning.

"We have done real harm," she said. "From the shadows, through the gaps, in the quiet language of people who've convinced themselves that operating without transparency is justified because no one was ever transparent with us first. We have moved information like currency and called it survival. We have protected our own at the expense of the principles we claim to hold. We have looked at the corruption of the world and decided that working around it without accountability was clean, because at least we weren't the ones building it." She paused. "It isn't clean. And we know it. And I am not going to stand here in front of all of you and pretend otherwise just because they are worse."

The square held its breath.

"None of you are innocent," Addie said. "Including me. Including everyone on these steps. Including every faction, every guild, every institution that has spent a thousand years building its own version of the truth and defending it at the expense of the actual one."

She let that land.

Then something in her expression shifted — opened slightly, like a window in a room that has been closed too long.

"I want to tell you something that I think about more than almost anything else in my field, and that nobody has ever funded me to investigate, because it is the kind of question that makes powerful people nervous." She looked out at the mass of faces. "Our nation's name. Erzengel. Do you know where it comes from?"

The silence had a different quality now. Curious. Waiting.

"It's ancient. Pre-Accord, pre-schism, pre almost everything we consider foundational. The language it comes from hasn't been spoken as a primary tongue in so long that most linguists classify it as dead." She paused. "It means archangel. The closest translation we have, across the seventeen languages currently spoken within the Accord's borders, is archangel — the highest order of divine messenger. The one sent when the message is too important to entrust to anything lesser."

She looked at the sky. At the moons.

"I have always found it interesting that we named ourselves after a messenger. Not a ruler. Not a conqueror. Not an empire or a dynasty or a golden age. A messenger. Something intermediary. Something whose purpose is transmission — carrying a thing from where it originates to where it needs to arrive, intact, without corruption, without the messenger substituting their own interpretation for the content."

She brought her gaze back down.

"I don't know who named us that, or why, or whether they knew something we've spent a thousand years forgetting. I genuinely don't know. But I find it notable that on the day the conjunctions return, after a silence no one has ever been able to explain, the title they've assigned me is Moderator. Another intermediary. Another messenger. Something whose job, as best I can understand it, is not to rule, but to witness."

Her voice went very quiet. Carried anyway.

"And I want you to consider the possibility that a civilization named for a messenger, governed for a thousand years in the absence of the only mechanism that ever forced it to genuinely account for itself, has spent that thousand years delivering a version of itself that has been heavily edited in transit. That what we have been told we are, and what we actually are, have drifted. And that whatever is beginning tonight — whatever the moons are doing, whatever the beacon is telling us — is the moment when the original message and the edited version are placed next to each other for comparison."

She stepped forward, off the plinth, onto the open stone.

"The entity that assigned me this role apparently looked at everything available and selected someone who has spent her career being told she was studying something that didn't matter. Three degrees that don't impress anyone at a formal dinner. Eleven years of grant rejections. A very strong opinion about canteen bread." The corner of her mouth moved. "I don't think that's an accident. I think whatever is running this process is not interested in the version of Erzengel that exists on the official documents. I think it's interested in the actual one."

She looked up at the moons.

"This is real," she said. "I want to be unambiguous about that. I have spent eleven years developing the methodology to rule conjunctions out, specifically so I could not be fooled by the appearance of one. What is happening in that sky tonight satisfies every condition. The moons are moving. The celestial mechanics are present. The beacon burned the right color in the right sequence." She paused. "This is not a drill. This is not a localized anomaly. This is not a political event wearing a cosmic mask. I would tell you if it were. I have made a career of telling people things like that, and I will not stop now."

She brought her gaze back down.

"And I can tell you, with the full weight of what I know, that the mechanics of a conjunction do not care who you are. They do not care what you've built or destroyed or told yourselves about why. They do not distinguish between the Sovereign's bloodline and the person sleeping in the shelter district. They do not read our political classifications or honor our exemptions." She let that land. "The conjunction arrives. And then we do what we do. We make the choices we make. And when the cycle turns, we do what we have always done."

Her voice went hard and quiet.

"We find something cosmic to blame."

She turned. She faced all three of them — Tallandra, Verrick, the empty space where a Schism representative might have stood if Schism had ever trusted official positions enough to fill one.

"Not this time," she said. "I don't know what my role requires. I don't know the full scope of what's coming. But I know this: whatever happens in this cycle, we are going to account for it. With names attached. With decisions and decision-makers on the record. We are not going to build the next thousand years on top of a story we told ourselves to avoid the weight of what we actually did."

She faced the crowd again. Just the crowd. The frightened, grieving, volatile mass of people who had woken up in an ordinary world and watched the sky change color and did not yet know what it meant for their children, their homes, the structure of everything they'd been assured was stable.

"I don't know if we survive this well," Addie said. "I am not going to tell you we do. Our history suggests we are not, on the whole, a civilization that survives well. We survive — we are remarkably, stubbornly, almost insultingly good at simply surviving. But well. With dignity. With each other intact. With something worth handing forward." She shook her head slightly. "That has always been the harder thing."

She looked at the red-haired woman. Held her gaze.

"What we build after this cycle is built directly on top of how we got there. On every choice we make between now and the turn. On whether we chose our fear or our dignity when both were standing in front of us and only one of them was comfortable."

A long beat.

"The conjunction is not the enemy," she said. "The conjunction is the mirror. It shows us, at scale, what we already are. What we have always been. And then the cycle turns, and we get to decide what to do with what we saw."

She took a breath.

"So. That's what I can tell you. I'm twenty-nine years old. My hair is still the wrong color. I did not eat breakfast and I'm going to regret that. I have been assigned a title I don't fully understand, by a process I am not yet able to explain, and I intend to understand both before I do anything in the name of either one." Something shifted in her expression — not softer, exactly. More direct. "And I promise you this: the moment I know something that affects you — something real, something true, without the diplomatic smoothing and the information management — you will hear it from me before you hear it from anyone standing on those steps."

She looked at the steps.

She looked at the crowd.

"That's all I've got. I wish I had more. But I'd rather give you the honest small thing than the eloquent large lie."

She stopped.
