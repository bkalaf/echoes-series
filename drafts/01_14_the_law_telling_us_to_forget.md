## Title Rationale

The chapter's true discovery is not merely that Ariadne's Thread is the right mythic thread.

The discovery is that an old law / pact tried to make the first founding years disappear, and that the High Elven elder-scholar tradition preserved the story precisely because someone tried to forbid it.

The title should point toward memory, suppression, and oral history without spoiling the yellow beacon turn.

# Offscreen Day Two / Day Three Research Summary

Research lane:

- private salons;

- service workers and customers who cross ethnic / civic boundaries.

- The story carries weight because it is not decorative mythology. It is fate, obligation, measure, debt, and doom.
- Carrio understands why such a story survives in intimate places: people tell the truth where official rooms cannot hear them.
- Mae is visibly uncomfortable with the usefulness of the lane, not because she despises the workers, but because it exposes how much real political knowledge lives outside noble and Conclave channels.

Research lane:

- taverns;
- workers' drinking rooms;
- immigrant kitchens;
- travelers' tables;
- songs, wedding jokes, old matchmaker sayings.

Discovery:

- Rhün notices the practical side: if the thread joins people, it can pull a person toward someone, not necessarily toward an exit.

Important tone:

- The woman is not a prop. She should feel like a person with her own life, humor, exhaustion, and reasons for preserving the story.
- Gregory should be humbled, not humiliated.

## Reconvening Decision

After two days, the companions compare all three thread legends:

1. **Red Thread of Fate** — connection between people.
2. **Ariadne's Thread** — a way back out after facing the monster.
3. **Thread of the Norns** — fate / doom / obligation.

All three still feel promising.

The collective decision is that Ariadne's Thread has the strongest poetic and structural fit:

- Horror's riddle says to bring the thread.
- The comma reframes the problem as **horned, dead charge**.
- The Minotaur is horned.
- Ariadne's Thread is not about killing the monster; it is about surviving after the monster is defeated.
- Addie recognizes that defeating Horror / Regret will not matter if they cannot find their way out of the larger historical maze.

Addie argues that Barnaby is not an accident:

- Barnaby is a tailor.
- His people carry the Ariadne story close.
- In the vision, Dovren's reaction to criticism of his clothing and to the status of tailors felt unusually charged.
- Addie cannot prove the connection yet, but she is convinced the tailor / thread / old founding layer is the right path.

Gregory argues for returning to the White Tower Archives in Luminthalas, not to repeat the same failed library method, but to ask a better question:

- Why Luminthalas?
- Why were they pointed there through what felt like a cheat?
- Why did the path skip a step?
- What happened with the original twenty-four city-states?
- Why would an archive city preserve the wrong thing or hide the right one?

Mae is grumbly and skeptical because she knows the current political structure intimately. She knows city-state succession, Conclave function, faction arithmetic, and the modern capitals.

Her hidden mistake:

- She knows how the system works **now**.
- She does not actually know how it worked **then**.

That realization should surface during the chapter.

## Subchapter 6 — Permission to Say It

1. The old scholar asks whether Mae is giving her permission to say this.
2. Mae looks at the companions.
3. They look as confused as she is.
4. Mae says yes, of course.
5. The scholar still hesitates.
6. This pause matters: she is not withholding trivia; she is risking an inherited prohibition.
7. The scholar says Luminthalas and Nox Vaelor were not original capitals in the first years of the country.
8. Mae says she knows later cities were founded after the first political consolidation.
9. The scholar says that is not the part she means.
10. The scholar explains that in the first decades, the non-human capitals did not yet exist as capitals.
11. The original twenty-four city-state capitals were human.
12. The triad structure existed in seed form: one human, one beastfolk, one mythos-linked population, but power flowed through the human capital city.
13. The split into later cities happened over the next thirty to forty years, after land-grab, migration, consolidation, and political fracture.
14. By around year fifty, the capitals were largely set.
15. They have not changed much since, and essentially not since the Conjunctions stopped a thousand years ago.
16. The companions register the scale of the cover-up.
17. Someone says that seems important to cover up.
18. Mae is hit hardest because she knows the modern machinery but not the original structure that created it.

## Subchapter 7 — A Law Telling Us To Forget

1. The scholar explains the pact / law.
2. Dovren I framed the first years as too chaotic and divisive to discuss openly.
3. The public story became:
   - early chaos;
   - necessary consolidation;
   - land and population expansion;
   - eventual stable seventy-two-city structure.
4. The forbidden layer is that non-human political power was delayed, minimized, or made dependent on human capitals during the foundational period.
5. The law did not erase everything, but it made the subject improper, dangerous, or treason-adjacent in formal institutions.
6. The High Elven elder-scholars preserved the memory anyway.
7. They passed it from elder scholar to elder scholar.
8. They did not always know why it mattered.
9. They only knew it mattered because someone had made a law telling them to forget.
10. Required line / idea: **"If there is one thing I have learned in ninety years, it is that things you are forbidden to talk about are usually the things you have to talk about."**
11. This should land as the moral opposite of official recordkeeping.
12. Gregory should look ashamed and fascinated: this is scholarship as defiance, not scholarship as cataloging.
13. Brickett may whisper that this lady is scarier than Mae.
14. Mae does not deny it.

## Subchapter 8 — The String Guiding Us Out

1. The scholar quickly adds that by year fifty the capitals were set and nothing much has changed since the Conjunctions stopped.
2. Addie hears the phrase / structure connect.
3. Addie thanks the scholar and pulls the group away from the desk just far enough for a semi-private huddle.
4. The scholar can still see them and catch pieces, but not every word.
5. Addie says: **"It's Ariadne's Thread."**
6. The others have not all made the connection yet.
7. Addie explains:
   - You cannot make your way out until something guides you.
   - This preserved story is the string guiding them out.
   - Luminthalas mattered because the High Elven scholar tradition had a structure to keep the forbidden story alive.
   - The story itself is connective tissue.
   - Silence said stories were connective tissue; this is the proof.
8. Addie asks if no one finds it odd that twenty-four city-states appeared in neat triads, all at once, with humans as the original power centers.
9. She says everything they know about science and social development says that should not happen cleanly.
10. Within fifty years, the system fractured into more cities.
11. Then a law told people not to talk about why.
12. Addie says defeating Horror / Regret will not matter if they cannot find the way back out of the maze after they beat the monster.
13. This is the moment the Ariadne answer becomes fully operational.
