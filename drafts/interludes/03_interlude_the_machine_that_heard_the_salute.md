# Interlude: The Machine That Heard the Salute

Dear Reader, I must apologize for tearing you out of the story yet again. I know you are anxious to see what Gregory does next with that strange little token, now that the spotlight is finding him and his academic posture is beginning to warp under the heat of a true social theater. But before we can appreciate why a piece of stamped metal can completely upend a city of twenty-five districts, we must step entirely out of our digital lattice. We need to leave Gregory staring at his scales, exit the simulation, and speak of an older world. We must talk about secrets, symbols, and machines.

Think about a coin for a moment, Dear Reader. Or a crown. Or an encrypted string of text sitting in a database table. What are they? A coin is just a scrap of copper or silver; a crown is merely a heavy hat encrusted with shiny pebbles; a title is nothing more than a rhythmic vibrations of the larynx. They possess absolutely no physical power to make a single human being work, fight, or die. Yet they move armies. They do so because of an unwritten, invisible contract: a collective agreement to pretend. They work because we perform recognition around them. Authority is not an objective substance you can dig out of the ground; it is a performance. And the most dangerous thing about a performance, Dear Reader, is that performance always leaves patterns.

Let us drop down onto a real timeline, long before our system operators began compressing souls into files. Let us look at a time when the world was split between a tired, sputtering island called the Crumbling Empire and a terrifyingly efficient engine of ideological slaughter known as the Determined Occultists.

The Occultists were obsessed with structure. They believed, with a ferocity that bordered on religious mania, that absolute, ritualized obedience would make them invincible. Every document had to be signed with the exact same bureaucratic punctuation; every morning report had to follow the same rigid terminology; and every single citizen, soldier, and clerk was legally compelled to raise their right hand and utter a specific, mandatory phrase to honor their leader: a verbal salute-phrase dedicated exclusively to the High Occultist. To omit it was treason; to perform it was to reinforce the state's total, unyielding grip on reality.

To coordinate this massive performance across continents, the Determined Occultists used a device that was, for its time, an absolute masterpiece of mechanical engineering: the Riddle Engine.

Imagine a heavy typewriter enclosed in an elegant wooden box. Inside its guts sat three or four brass rotors with twenty-six electrical contacts on each side. Every time a telegraphist pressed a key on the keyboard, the first rotor clicked forward by one notch, changing the entire electrical circuit. Pressing the letter *A* might light up the letter *Q* on the lampboard above. Pressing *A* a second time would click the rotor again, lighting up a *Z*. The current would pass through the rotors, bounce off a reflecting mirror at the back, and rush back through the maze in reverse. To make matters worse, a plugboard at the front allowed the operator to swap pairs of letters using cables, multiplying the potential mathematical configurations into numbers that human minds had no right to calculate.

Every single day at midnight, all operators across the Occultists' network would change their rotor selections, their ring settings, and their plugboard cables according to a secret codebook. There were billions upon billions of possible daily keys. If you did not know the exact arrangement of the machine at that precise millisecond, an intercepted radio message looked like an explosion in a typesetter’s shop—meaningless, chaotic noise. The Occultists rested easy, completely wrapped in the warm, arrogant illusion of unbreakable mathematical secrecy.

But they had not accounted for the Estate of Impossible Sums, or the strange man who lived within its grounds.

His name was Mr. Turing. He was a brilliant, awkward, socially nonconforming mathematician who walked with a shambling gait, smelled faintly of bicycle grease, and routinely failed to look people in the eye. He did not fit the pristine, starched-collar mold of the Crumbling Empire's intellectual elite. He colored wildly outside the lines of conventional society—a trait that, in a later chapter, we will see his own ungrateful empire punish him for with unspeakable, clinical cruelty. But here, in the damp heart of the conflict, his refusal to accept rigid human categories was exactly what made him dangerous to a totalitarian regime.

Where others looked at the Riddle Engine and saw a mountain of impossible arithmetic, Mr. Turing looked at it and saw a fundamental vulnerability: it was a machine built by humans, operated by bureaucrats, and fed by a culture of mandatory performance.

You see, Dear Reader, the Riddle Engine had one profound mechanical flaw engineered into its very reflections: a letter could never encrypt to itself. An *E* could become anything in the alphabet, but it could never, under any circumstances, remain an *E*. This was the tiny lever Turing needed. But to prize open the box, he required a place to set his lever—a guessed fragment of plaintext, which the codebreakers called a "crib."

And where did those cribs come from? They came straight from the compulsory loyalty of the Determined Occultists.

Every morning, without fail, a lonely weather observer stationed on a freezing sandbar in the North Sea would log into his radio set. He was cold, bored, and terrified of his superiors. He would input the day's settings into his Riddle Engine, type out the exact same phrase—"Weather report for the morning"—followed by the conditions, and close his transmission with the absolute, legally required salute-phrase to the High Occultist.

Further south, military clerks would end every single encrypted operational order with that exact same verbal monument to their leader's ego. It was an unbending rule. The state demanded that loyalty be displayed consistently, predictably, and publicly.

Mr. Turing sat in his cluttered room at the Estate of Impossible Sums and smiled a dark, brilliant smile. The Occultists' mandatory obedience had handed him his knife. Because they were terrified *not* to salute, the codebreakers knew with absolute mathematical certainty that the final letters of hundreds of intercepted messages would always decrypt to the exact same sequence of characters honoring the High Occultist.

To turn this predictability into victory, the Estate of Impossible Sums did not rely on standard mathematics or human hands. They built a monster of their own: the Thunder-Loom.

Let us be precise, Dear Reader. The Thunder-Loom was not a modern computer. It did not have software, it did not store images of cats, and it could not think. It was a massive, clattering electromechanical search engine made of wood, steel, and miles of copper wire. It looked like a row of giant, vertical weavers’ looms, housing dozens of spinning red and blue drums that replicated the internal wiring of the Occultists' rotors.

The operators—mostly young women from the domestic services who worked long, exhausting shifts in freezing concrete huts—would read the encrypted messages, guess the location of the mandatory salute based on its length and position, and plug the corresponding letters into the back of the Thunder-Loom. Then, they would throw the iron switch.

The room would instantly fill with a deafening, rhythmic roar—a sound like thousands of knitting needles dropping onto a tin roof at once. *Click-clack-clack-clack.* The drums would spin at furious speeds, testing thousands of possible rotor setting combinations every second. The machine was not doing advanced calculation; it was conducting an aggressive, mechanical elimination process. It would test a setting, look for an impossible contradiction—such as a letter encrypting to itself—and, upon finding it, reject that entire branch of mathematics with a sharp electrical snap.

When the spinning drums suddenly stopped with a heavy, metallic thud, a hush would fall over the hut. The machine had found a configuration where no contradictions existed. The women would rush forward, note the positions of the dials, and pass them to the linguists. Within an hour, the Crumbling Empire would be reading the High Occultist’s private orders before his own generals had finished their morning coffee.

It was a miracle of technical infrastructure, but it birthed an absolute nightmare of moral mathematics.

Suppose, Dear Reader, you have cracked the code. You are sitting at the Estate of Impossible Sums, and you read a message fresh off the wires: a pack of iron submarines has just been ordered to intercept a merchant convoy crossing the dark waters of the Atlantic. The convoy is carrying three thousand tons of grain and five hundred young sailors from a tiny republic. You have the power to reroute the ships. You have the power to save every single one of those lives.

But if you move the convoy, what happens?

The Occultists' security officers are not stupid. They will look at their charts, see that the islanders avoided their trap with perfect, predictive accuracy, and they will realize that the Riddle Engine is no longer a secret. They will recall every machine, scrap the codebooks, add a fifth rotor to the chassis, and pull the curtain of absolute darkness back down over the continent.

So, you must sit on your hands. You must watch the map. You must allow the submarines to strike. You must let the ships burn and the boys drown in the freezing salt, because if you spend the currency of your secret to save a few hundred lives today, you will lose the ability to save hundreds of thousands tomorrow when the grand invasion begins.

Think of the absolute, freezing weight of that calculation. How much cargo justifies risking the code? How many regiments do you throw into the teeth of an ambush just to keep the enemy's security officers from guessing that your machines can hear their salutes? Can a human life be weighed like the frequency of a letter in a paragraph? Can ethics truly be reduced to a binary state—a one or a zero?

This terrible choice transformed information from a tactical tool into the primary, industrial infrastructure of modern power. The war did not just change borders; it permanently accelerated the birth of computer science, modern cryptography, and the cold art of statistical decision-making under absolute uncertainty. Secrets were no longer things hidden in velvet drawers; they were systems that had to be managed at a massive, computational scale.

Now, Dear Reader, let us climb back up through the layers of the simulation. Let us return straight back to Gregory, standing under the dim lights of the city lounge, turning that stamped silver coin over and over in his scaled hands.

Do you see the alignment now?

A crown only has power because people agree to bow when it enters the room. A coin only has value because twenty-five districts agree to trade it for bread. A title like *High Magistrate* or *Sovereign* only grants authority because the commoners and the elites perform recognition around it every single day during the assembly phase. The game’s structures are designed to make you look at the roles, the spotlight, and the official positions on the map.

But identity survives the class. True lineage cannot be completely ironed out by an administrative audit, and a secret cannot remain hidden forever when the people protecting it are forced to perform their loyalty according to a static script. Gregory is about to realize that the token in his hand isn't just an asset to be spent or a status badge to be displayed to the audience. It is a crib. It is a predictable fragment of an older, broken truth left behind by the original Architects.

Let us drop back down into the scene. Let us watch Gregory drop his academic posturing, narrow his eyes, and begin to search for the mechanical patterns hiding right behind the law.
