# Interlude: The Doomsday Shelf

Sorry for interrupting you again, Dear Reader.

I know. I know. We were just getting somewhere. A beacon has changed color, which is always the sort of thing that makes practical people want to keep moving, preferably at a brisk pace and with fewer interruptions from the voice in the margins.

Unfortunately, the story has used a word, and when stories use certain words, they become doors. If we are going to walk through the next one without tripping over the threshold, we should probably stop and look at it.

The word is **cataclysm**.

Merriam-Webster gives us a few useful shapes for it: **flood**, **deluge**, **catastrophe**, and, most importantly for our purposes, **“a momentous and violent event marked by overwhelming upheaval and demolition”**; broadly, **“an event that brings great changes.”**

That is a very polite way for a dictionary to say: *the house falls down, and afterward everyone claims they did not know houses could do that.*

So let us talk about the Doomsday Shelf.

Not a real thing, naturally. Perish the thought. We would never discuss a real place where intelligent creatures looked at an unstable structure, named it something melodramatic and accurate, and then continued behaving as if the name were decorative.

No, no. This is a fake shelf. A proverb shelf. A shelf in a totally imaginary room.

Let us say there is a wall, and attached to that wall is a shelf. Beneath the shelf sit many delicate glass things: vases, little bowls, ceremonial jars, family keepsakes, moral excuses, and a few very expensive crystal animals nobody likes but everyone pretends to admire because someone important bought them.

The shelf is called the Doomsday Shelf because if it falls, it will smash everything underneath it.

Now, reasonable people, seeing a shelf called the Doomsday Shelf, might respond with a touch of caution. They might reinforce it. They might prop it up. They might move the glass things out from under it. They might ask whether the brackets are sound, whether the wall is cracked, whether the entire room has been designed by someone who had never heard of weight.

At the very least, one imagines they would avoid hammering beneath it.

One imagines they would not begin tearing open the wall below the shelf.

One imagines that if the wall were made of ice, just as an entirely random example, they would make some small effort not to melt it.

But imagination, Dear Reader, is often more generous than history.

In our fake room, the people looked at the Doomsday Shelf and decided that the true danger was not the shelf falling. The true danger was inconvenience. Repairing the wall would cost money. Moving the glass would take effort. Changing the room would require admitting the room had been built badly in the first place, and that is a very rude thing to say about ancestral architecture.

So they rolled the dice.

And then they rolled them again.

And then their children rolled them, and their grandchildren rolled them, and generation after generation stood under the shelf with little hammers and excellent explanations.

They said the shelf had always wobbled.

They said shelves were complicated.

They said more research was needed.

They said perhaps the shelf was not really leaning; perhaps the floor was simply judgmental.

They said the people warning them about the shelf were being dramatic.

They said glass breaks all the time.

They said it would be economically disruptive to stop hitting the wall.

And because saying things can feel very much like doing things, they congratulated themselves on their seriousness and continued striking the supports.

This is one of the great comforts of inaction: it often arrives dressed as caution. It does not feel like a decision. It feels like waiting. It feels like patience. It feels like maturity. It feels like refusing to overreact.

But a wall does not care whether you call your hammer patience.

Then, eventually, the shelf fell.

Of course it did.

It fell through the glass, which shattered beautifully, because disasters are often visually impressive when you are not the one being cut by them. It tore free from the wall. Then the wall came down too, because, as it turned out, the wall was load-bearing. And because the wall was load-bearing, the roof followed.

That is the part people always forget about cataclysms.

They imagine one clean break. One shelf. One crash. One mess to sweep up.

But a cataclysm is rarely polite enough to destroy only what was directly underneath it. It finds the hidden dependencies. It pulls on the nails you forgot were connected to beams you forgot were connected to ceilings you forgot were keeping the rain out.

And after the roof caved in, the people of this absolutely fictional room stood in the wreckage with dust in their hair and glass in their shoes and said the ancient, sacred words of the surprised negligent:

*How were we supposed to know?*

Which is a fascinating question.

Because they had named it the Doomsday Shelf.

They had watched it sag.

They had been warned, repeatedly, by people who understood shelves, walls, weight, ice, heat, hammers, and the rude habit gravity has of not negotiating.

But warning is only useful if someone is willing to let it become action.

That is the part that matters here.

Not the shelf. Not the glass. Not even the roof.

The action.

Or, more precisely, the refusal to admit that inaction was action.

We have already met this idea before, have we not? The Sun People and the Apathetic Pagans, in that entirely fictional mythological world that was absolutely not real, learned a great deal about the violence hidden inside the phrase *we merely chose not to participate.* They learned that a closed valve can be a weapon. That a locked vault can be an ultimatum. That a hand kept deliberately still may still be holding the knife in place.

The people of the fake Doomsday Shelf learned the same lesson later, and less usefully.

They learned it after the wall.

They learned it after the roof.

They learned it while standing in the ruin of a house they might once have saved if they had understood that refusing to change behavior is still behavior. Continuing the pattern is still a choice. Pretending not to see a consequence does not make the consequence imaginary; it only makes the eventual apology more insulting.

And now we must ask the uncomfortable question, because Dear Reader chapters exist mostly to ask uncomfortable questions while pretending to be whimsical.

What do you do after the roof caves in?

Do you rebuild?

Do you move houses?

Do you search for another room, another wall, another shelf, another set of glass things, and promise that this time you will be more careful?

Perhaps you do, if you have the means.

Perhaps you do, if there is another house to move into.

Perhaps you discover, a bit late, that not every species gets a spare house.

Thank goodness, then, that this is all imaginary. A proverb. A fable. A nonsense little room full of fake glass and fake dust and fake people who made fake choices until the fake roof came down.

Otherwise I might feel bad for them.

Not equally, of course.

I would feel bad for the children who inherited the cracks. For the careful ones who begged the hammerers to stop. For the people who tried to move the glass and were laughed out of the room. For the ones standing outside, watching the collapse spread toward walls they had never been allowed to touch.

As for the people who chose inaction over action, who kept striking the wall and then called the collapse unforeseeable?

Well.

I suppose I would feel something for them too.

But it would not be pity.

Now, let us return to Addie and her companions, who have just learned that a dam can be a weapon, a silence can be a grave, and a story can survive two thousand years by refusing to let the roof fall quietly.
