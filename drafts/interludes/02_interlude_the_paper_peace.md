# Interlude: The Paper Peace

Welcome back, dear reader.

I must apologize for tearing you out of the story once more. I know you are anxious to see what Gregory does next, now that the weight of the spotlight is pressing against his chest and his illusions of cozy academic obscurity are starting to crack. But to understand the true cost of an unanswered threat, we must leave Gregory and his dragonkin texts behind for a brief interlude.

While the Sun People and the Apathetic Pagans were locked in their distant, river-staring turf war across the great waters, another crisis was brewing across a narrower river. We must speak of a different sort of theater—one where the stage was made of borders, and the actors wore the heavy wool of empires and the iron of fanatical certainty.

Let us look upon the Western lands, where two primary forces stood facing one another: the Crumbling Empire and the Determined Occultists.

The Determined Occultists were a people reeling from the humiliation of an earlier great war. They had been beaten, stripped of their finery, and subjected to a harsh, grinding punishment by the victors. I do not tell you this to excuse what they became, dear reader. A wound is not a crown, dear reader, but many kings have worn one as if it were. Instead of healing, their wound festered. Out of that rot crawled a grievance-speaker—their own dark analogue of a Mad King. He was an outcast who discovered he could hold vast crowds in the palm of his hand simply by whispering their own anger back to them. With a clinical, terrifying efficiency, he consolidated power, replaced law with loyalty, and turned a broken nation into a singular, unsleeping machine.

The world watched, but the world did not move. Before they ever gathered in the City of Beer, the occultists had already marched their iron boots across their own borders, swallowing a neighboring country whole in the bright light of day. The Crumbling Empire was not facing an untested threat or an unknown variable. They knew exactly what was marching.

And yet, if we look closely at the table, the Crumbling Empire and the Determined Occultists were more alike than either side cared to admit. They shared ancient roots, old overlapping languages, centuries of intertwined rivalries, and the deep, habitual muscle-memory of old imperial habits. The difference between them was not a matter of blood, but of political condition. The Crumbling Empire still possessed its grand reputation and the heavy, slow-moving restraint of an elder; the occultists possessed only a raw, burning humiliation and a voracious appetite.

But respectability can become a heavy leash. The Crumbling Empire was watched by the world, deeply indebted, profoundly exhausted, and utterly terrified of another generation dying in the mud of another great war. They wanted balance. They wanted comfort. The occultists, meanwhile, had realized a fundamental truth of the world: rules only restrain people who accept consequences. They learned that broken rules are incredibly profitable when nobody moves to enforce them. A rule unenforced against a bully becomes a map.

This brought them all eventually to the City of Beer.

The spineless leader of the Crumbling Empire arrived with his inkwells and his luggage, desperate to buy safety at any cost. Sitting alongside the tired republic, he signed his name to the Paper Peace. The apple was bruised, inspected, and chained to the table. The orange had decided it was a cannonball.

To satisfy the occultists' appetite, a small mountain-border nation was broken apart. The borderlands—the region known as the Sootland—were handed directly to the monsters. Make no mistake, dear reader, this was not a symbolic sacrifice of mere dirt and trees. The Sootland contained the small nation’s entire concrete spine: its fortresses, its natural mountain defenses, its heavy steel industries, and its entire capacity to protect itself from an invasion.

And where were the Check-Mark People while their survival was being bartered away? They were waiting in the hallway. They were treated as an object on the table instead of a people at the table.

When the spineless leader returned home, waving that fragile sheet of paper to cheering crowds, it was hailed as a triumph of wisdom, patience, and high civilization. But let us call it what it truly was. It was called diplomacy because cowardice looks better in formalwear. The spineless leader of the Crumbling Empire did not stop the war; he merely made sure someone smaller paid the first bill.

A bully does not experience appeasement as mercy. A bully experiences appeasement as permission.

The Paper Peace bought nothing but shame. Within less than six months, the occultists broke their polite promises, marched past the empty, defenseless mountains, and swallowed the remainder of the Check-Mark lands without firing a shot. The Crumbling Empire woke up to a cold reality: they had not purchased peace at all. They had simply sold an innocent ally’s armor and bought their executioner six more months to sharpen his axe.

When the occultists finally turned their eyes toward the red line drawn around the plains of Opland, the Crumbling Empire and the tired republic stood up and declared they would fight. They did not draw this line because they had suddenly become noble overnight, dear reader. They drew it because they had finally run out of illusions. There were no more small nations left to throw into the fire to keep themselves warm.

But the empires were not the only ones who thought they could out-calculate the monster.

We must introduce another power sleeping in the East: the Bear. The Bear was not sentimental. The Bear did not believe in paper mercy, parliamentary speeches, or international committees. The Bear believed in winter, prisons, iron grids, maps, bargains, and teeth.

The Crumbling Empire was not the last power to miscalculate what the occultists were capable of. In the late days of August, as the storms gathered, the world was shocked by the signing of the Two-Minister Pact—the Bear’s Bargain. It was a scene of exquisite horror: two servants of separate monsters, sitting in a quiet room, signing polite words over a map that had already been sliced neatly in half.

The Bear did not think he was appeasing the occultists. That was the funny part. He thought he was using them. He believed that by signing the pact, he was de-risking his own borders, letting the Western empires tear each other to pieces while he sat safely on his high ledge. The bargain secretly divided the eastern kingdoms between them, and Opland was chosen as the shared meal.

When September arrived, the occultists slammed into Opland from the west, and the Bear entered quietly from the east to claim his half of the carcass. The Bear’s Bargain had functioned perfectly for the occultists; it ensured they could execute their violence without the immediate threat of the Bear’s teeth sinking into their flank. Predators do not become friends because they ate beside you once. But the Bear’s miscalculation—the belief that the occultists were his friend—belongs to another testament.

No frightened boy ever had to learn that silence can be a kind of applause.

Now, let us return straight back to Gregory’s story. Gregory is not a king. He is not a crumbling empire, and he is certainly not the Bear. He is just a scholar, an entertainer, a young man hiding behind an old posture and unnecessary spectacles because the alternative is letting the room see his teeth. But he understands the profound, heavy temptation to look away. He understands the desire to avoid the confrontation, to hand over one small flowerbed at a time, to let someone else pay the bill, and to call the resulting silence peace.

But as Gregory is about to learn, beauty is not preserved by surrender. It is not saved by making oneself small and quiet in the corner. Sometimes, when the walls are burning and the ink is dry, the entertainer has to put the books away.

Sometimes, dear reader, you have to do the impossible.
