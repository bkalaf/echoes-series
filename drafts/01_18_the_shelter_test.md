## Chapter Function

This chapter should do five things:

1. decompress after the swamp-church / orange checkpoint without letting the tension vanish;
2. show the companions seeing ordinary displaced people as people, not statistics;
3. formalize the companion reading-mode idea through a natural late-night strategy conversation;
4. let Brickett reveal that he can “read Mother” through ordinary signs, not just one note;
5. create the first serious ethical rift between Mae and Yurislav over whether manipulating Brickett is acceptable.

This is a social-pressure chapter, not an action chapter. The danger is moral pressure inside the team.

# Beat-By-Beat Skeleton

## Subchapter 1 — The Last Transit

1. Pick up after the business transfer and orange checkpoint.
2. Tallowmire offers to put the companions up for the night because it is late and Portalport transit is likely closed or nearly closed.
3. The companions could pull rank, but choose not to.
4. Mae insists that emergency authority should be saved for actual emergencies.
5. Core idea: if everything is an emergency, nothing is.
6. This matters because the companions do not know what crisis is coming next.
7. Tallowmire places them in a crowded shelter.
8. It is partly hospitality and partly test: he wants to see how they handle being put among the people rather than above them.

## Subchapter 2 — The Shelter Test

1. The shelter is packed.
2. Families and displaced people fill the good cots / private rooms.
3. The companions are offered better space but insist the families take it.
4. The companions take a carved-out corner of the open shelter.
5. No one sleeps well.
6. The shelter should feel wetland-built: reed screens, raised dry mats, damp wood, low lanterns, communal sleeping, water nearby, and frogfolk comfort with cramped wetland domesticity.

## Subchapter 3 — People Who Want Better

1. Begin in the middle of the night, not the morning.
2. Addie, Gregory, and Mae talk quietly with families whose children are asleep.
3. Include frogfolk, crocodilian folk, and two male peacock/showbird refugees.
4. The conversation centers on what ordinary people hope the Conjunctions will change.
5. The showbirds say they are not looking for grand change. They just want people to treat each other better.
6. A crocodilian woman says the government mostly talks, and talking does not feed children or watch eggs.
7. Frogfolk say they have been let down many times. Maybe this is the time for change, but history says change comes with punishment.
8. The frogfolk hush one another, and the companions understand they are referring to the old Pollyr Fen punishment without openly naming it.
9. Tallandra and Carrio notice the absence of deception: this is one of the most honest conversations they have had.

## Subchapter 4 — Sparks For Toddlers

1. Brickett plays with the shelter children.
2. He makes little sparks / failed fire effects.
3. The toddlers laugh hardest when he burns himself or backfires.
4. Brickett is genuinely having a good time.
5. Addie realizes Brickett lost a childhood he should have had.
6. He is still only fourteen, and he is good with the children because he understands wanting distraction, warmth, and attention.
7. He gives parents a break and never complains.
8. This should deepen Addie's protectiveness toward him before the later manipulation scene.

## Subchapter 5 — Fifteen Minutes

1. Conversations wind down as parents and children begin sleeping.
2. The companions gather in their small corner.
3. Carrio pulls out a small enchanted hourglass.
4. He turns it and creates a pocket of quiet around them.
5. Required Carrio idea:

> "All right. We have fifteen minutes before the enchantment breaks. What? I like my private time. Don't get judgy."

6. Gregory casts or contributes a dimming spell so the bubble does not shed light into the shelter.
7. The group can finally strategize without disturbing anyone.

## Subchapter 6 — Same Strategy

1. The companions realize they have to go back into a beacon / seam tomorrow.
2. They debate whether to use the same strategy: let the Overseer and Horror each choose one witness-participant.
3. Brickett says it makes sense: someone working for them picks one person who knows the story, and someone working against them picks one person who knows the story.
4. Tallandra asks why he insisted on the Overseer choosing last time.
5. Brickett says the Overseer gave them the hint and he suspects something else, but does not have enough evidence yet.
6. The room questions whether the Overseer is truly working with them.

## Subchapter 7 — Reading The Readers

1. The companions start comparing their intuitive reads.
2. Yurislav says the Overseer is deceptive, but not because he means them harm. It feels like the opposite: deception to protect them.
3. The group asks, "From whom?" No one can answer yet.
4. Tallandra says the Overseer is the hardest read she has ever had: some moments are completely truthful; others are completely deceptive.
5. Carrio agrees there is genuine deception but cannot reduce it to malice.
6. Mae asks whether anyone can read the Witnesses.
7. Mae says she did not get an aggressive read; the strange thing is that she got a read at all.
8. Yurislav says he sensed self-protective deception from them.
9. Tallandra says Horror reads as nearly complete deception, almost nothing truthful.
10. Brickett points out that everyone seems to read something different.
11. Gregory suggests this may be worth formalizing.
12. Brickett names examples:
    - Matthieu reads patterns.
    - Gregory reads symbols.
    - Tallandra reads deception.
    - Yurislav reads hidden emotion.
    - Carrio reads people / social emotion and leverage.
13. Brynn and Rhün can joke or grumble that some gifts are more useful than others.
14. Rhün does not yet know what his own comparable read is, if any.

## Subchapter 8 — I Can Read Mother

1. Brickett says he can read Mother.
2. Everyone stops.
3. Matthieu objects that Mother left Brickett one gift and one note.
4. Brickett says no: she leaves him notes every day.
5. He clarifies that Matthieu means written notes, but Brickett means the little things no one else notices.
6. Examples:
   - extra trash guiding him toward a safe place;
   - a candle left out;
   - a light that tells him not to take a path;
   - ordinary objects nudging him away from danger or toward shelter.
7. Brickett says the written note was different because it had very specific instructions to save Addie.
8. Addie jumps on this because it is the first time she has heard Brickett explicitly say Mother told him to save her life.
9. Brickett slaps his hands over his mouth and says through his fingers:

> "Dammit."

## Subchapter 9 — The Brick Named Brickett

1. The group presses Brickett because he has revealed too much to retreat cleanly.
2. Brickett says he cannot tell them everything.
3. The note was clear:
   - become attached to Miss Sun-Tag;
   - be pivotal in something coming up;
   - without him, the group would not succeed;
   - first task: stop his namesake.
4. Brickett says he always thought he was named after a charcoal briquette and his parents could not spell.
5. Now he understands he was named after the brick he was supposed to stop from killing Addie.
6. This should land emotionally for Addie: Brickett's ridiculous entrance was not random.

## Subchapter 10 — Backstabbers

1. Mae tries to coax more information out of Brickett.
2. She does it gently, but manipulatively.
3. She implies he has already told them things he has not actually confirmed.
4. She references the pack, Mother, where he found Addie, and Skyrend Peak before the scrolls lit.
5. Tallandra and Matthieu may start to help the coaxing by affirming the false memory trail.
6. Yurislav sees the group effort and cuts it off.
7. Yurislav says Mother told Brickett not to say anything and they do not know the consequences of violating that.
8. He says they need the information, but manipulating a fourteen-year-old into saying more than he should is not acceptable.
9. This is the first visible line between Yurislav and Mae:
   - Yurislav protects people he sees as unable to defend themselves.
   - Mae is willing to cross lines, bribe, pressure, or manipulate if the outcome matters enough.
10. Yurislav insists he and Brickett will sleep somewhere else, where they do not have to watch for backstabbers.

## Subchapter 11 — The Sound Of Poison

1. Yurislav and Brickett leave the corner.
2. Gregory feels awful about participating or nearly participating.
3. Brynn hums because the emotional air has shifted badly.
4. Addie realizes she feels gross for trying to take advantage of the kid.
5. She privately decides that, as much as she wants to know what Brickett knows, he saved her life and may be the purest of them.
6. Addie quietly vows to herself to be on Yurislav's side in this line.
7. Addie tries to sleep but hears Mae breathing heavily, furious and awake.
8. Closing Addie thought should be something like:

> At least she wasn't sharpening a knife.

9. Then Addie's back room supplies the worse question:

> What does poison sound like when someone applies it to a blade?

10. End on that uneasy line.
