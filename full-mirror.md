The thread slipped from Silence's fingers so gently that, for an instant, Addie thought the little fox-kit intended to catch it again.

She didn't.

The pale strand drifted lazily into the darkness between them, but instead of falling, it simply... continued. It stretched farther than the room should have allowed, remaining impossibly taut as though some unseen hand were drawing it through the fabric of the world itself. Addie found herself leaning forward before she realized she was doing it, trying to judge the distance. There was nothing to judge. The thread simply kept going until, without warning, it unraveled into thousands of luminous fibers that crossed and knotted around one another with effortless precision.

A doorway bloomed from the weaving.

Not constructed.

Remembered.

Beyond it waited another world.

Or so Addie thought.

She took one step after Silence, her boot finding solid stone on the opposite side, and immediately understood that it wasn't the landscape that unsettled her. The floor beneath her feet still felt like polished stone. The air remained cool and unnaturally still. Even the silence carried the same impossible quality it always seemed to around the fox-kit.

It was the sky.

Everything inside her recoiled from it before she consciously understood why.

The constellations refused to resolve into patterns. Familiar instincts—habits built from a lifetime of looking upward—kept trying to impose order upon them, only to collapse a heartbeat later. A pale emerald ribbon arced from one horizon to another before dissolving into stars that shimmered with impossible colors. The heavens themselves seemed closer somehow, pressing downward with the uncomfortable intimacy of a ceiling lowered too near one's head.

It wasn't merely unfamiliar.

It was wrong.

Wrong in the quiet, instinctive way a familiar face becomes unsettling when the eyes sit just slightly too far apart.

"This..." Addie whispered before she realized she'd spoken aloud. "This isn't Eidolon."

Silence followed her gaze for a long moment before answering.

"No."

There was no mystery in the reply.

No playful smile.

No riddle.

Only quiet certainty.

The fox-kit lowered her ears ever so slightly, and for the first time since Addie had met her, the confidence that always seemed to surround her gave way to something softer.

"I owe you an apology."

Addie looked down.

The words were so unexpected they took a moment to settle.

"An apology?"

Silence nodded.

"For what comes next."

She folded her hands together in front of her overalls, her eyes fixed not on Addie but somewhere beyond the impossible sky.

"There are parts of this memory you cannot hear."

She hesitated.

"I could tell you why."

A faint smile crossed her face.

"You wouldn't understand it yet."

Another pause.

"I dislike deceiving you."

The admission carried no self-pity, only quiet honesty.

"I dislike even more that, before this is over, you'll wonder whether what you've heard was complete."

Addie frowned.

"What are you hiding from me?"

Silence's expression became almost painfully gentle.

"Today?"

She gave the smallest shrug.

"Quite a lot."

The answer should have irritated her.

Instead, something about the apology itself made it difficult.

Silence wasn't enjoying this.

She wasn't playing games.

Whatever she was withholding, she seemed to regret the necessity of it.

"I'm asking something unfair of you," Silence continued. "I'm asking you to watch people while knowing someone has covered part of the painting."

She finally looked up at Addie.

"But people are more honest than words."

Her tail curled lazily behind her.

"They reveal themselves in where they hesitate."

"In who they answer."

"In who they refuse to answer."

A small smile returned.

"And in what the room quietly makes space for."

Addie let out a slow breath.

It wasn't an answer.

It was, however, something she knew how to do.

Archives had never been about collecting facts.

Facts were easy.

People were difficult.

People lied.

People omitted.

People chose words carefully.

Sometimes they chose silence even more carefully.

The realization steadied her.

Silence noticed.

"Hmm."

The tiny sound somehow carried approval without becoming praise.

"Good."

She turned toward the chamber.

"Observe first."

"Theories can wait."

Together they stepped fully into the impossible hall.

It was enormous without feeling cavernous, as though its proportions had been designed around ceremony instead of architecture. Polished obsidian reflected the alien sky overhead so perfectly that Addie had the disorienting impression of walking between two heavens, one beneath her feet and one stretching endlessly above. Dark columns climbed into the distance until they disappeared into starlight itself, leaving no visible ceiling to separate the chamber from the impossible cosmos beyond.

At its center rested a great circular table of black stone.

The chairs surrounding it were carved from the same obsidian, each marked by a polished brass nameplate from which a small living flame emerged. No wick fed the fire. It simply burned—white, blue, green, crimson, amber—each flame distinct from the next, as though every name possessed a color entirely its own.

The arrangement possessed a mathematical precision that Addie found immediately comforting.

Until she noticed the empty chair.

Nothing distinguished it from the others.

No cloth draped across its back.

No flowers.

No memorial.

And yet the vacancy carried a presence of its own, subtle but undeniable, as though everyone who entered this room had long ago learned to speak around an absence that would never again be filled.

Her eyes drifted beyond the circle.

A second chair stood apart from the gathering, removed just far enough that no one could mistake it for part of the ring.

Its flame burned amber.

Lower than the others.

Patient.

The brass plate caught the strange starlight.

NOEL SMUKK.

The man seated there did not look excluded.

Nor did he look invited.

He wore the relaxed posture of someone who had chosen precisely where to sit long before anyone else had arrived, and who saw no reason to justify the choice. He watched the gathering with an expression of mild interest, but what struck Addie wasn't that he followed the discussion.

He wasn't watching the conversation.

He was watching the people having it.

Something about that lodged uncomfortably in the back of her mind.

Not fear.

Not suspicion.

Simply a question she couldn't quite dismiss.

Why does the room accept his distance?

Before she could linger on it, a soft crimson light spread across the far wall.

It did not project an image so much as reveal one that had always been woven into the stone itself.

A woman emerged from the crimson latticework with silver-blonde hair and weary eyes that seemed to have forgotten what uninterrupted rest felt like. She carried herself with quiet dignity, but there was a weight to her posture that no effort of will could conceal. It wasn't age. It wasn't exhaustion.

It was responsibility carried for far too long.

She did not look immediately toward the gathering.

Instead, her eyes lifted beyond the chamber, as though some part of her attention remained fixed upon a place unimaginably distant.

Only after several silent heartbeats did she lower her gaze.

"I apologize," she said, her voice warm despite its weariness, "that I cannot join you in person."

For reasons Addie could not explain, something inside her chest tightened.

She had never seen this woman before.

She was certain of that.

And yet the feeling that accompanied her voice—a strange mixture of comfort, grief, patience, and fierce protectiveness—felt so deeply familiar that Addie instinctively searched her memory for a face that refused to appear.

Beside her, Silence didn't watch the woman.

She watched everyone else.

Without looking away from the gathering, she spoke quietly enough that only Addie could hear.

"Watch the room."

Not the words.

"The people."

She let the instruction settle before continuing.

"Watch who answers whom."

"Watch who people wait for."

"Watch who changes the direction of the conversation..."

Her ears flicked once.

"...without ever raising their voice."

Then, after the smallest smile,

"The really good part..."

She looked back toward the table.

"...is about to begin."

No one acknowledged the woman in crimson with surprise.

There were no formal greetings, no ritual expressions of relief that she had managed to join them. Whatever arrangement bound this gathering together, it had existed long enough that her absence—and whatever mechanism allowed her to stand among them despite it—required no explanation.

A man with silver threaded through his dark beard inclined his head first.

"We're grateful you could make the time."

The woman offered an apologetic smile.

"I'm afraid that's all I've been able to make."

A ripple of restrained amusement moved almost imperceptibly around the table. Not laughter exactly. Recognition. The sort of quiet understanding shared among people who had worked beside one another for years.

It vanished almost as quickly as it appeared.

"The reports?" someone asked.

She nodded.

"I've reviewed everything that reached me before the uplink."

Her gaze swept slowly around the circle.

"I wish I could say the picture has improved."

Silence settled over the chamber.

Not uncomfortable.

Expectant.

A broad-shouldered man with weathered hands rested both palms on the obsidian table.

"The repositories in Beijing are secured."

Another voice answered before he had fully finished.

"So are San Diego's."

A woman farther around the circle shook her head.

"Berlin remains unstable."

"We're still moving material."

"It won't finish before—"

The rest disappeared.

The pressure struck behind Addie's eyes so suddenly she flinched.

The room didn't grow quieter.

It grew farther away.

Voices continued moving around the circle, mouths opening and closing in measured cadence, but whatever words followed seemed to sink beneath deep water before they reached her. She could still see concern crossing faces. She could still watch someone lean forward to disagree, another shake his head, another quietly concede a point.

She simply couldn't hear why.

A small hand rested lightly against her sleeve.

Silence.

"I told you."

There was no apology in her voice this time.

Only quiet sympathy.

Addie swallowed her frustration and forced herself to stop listening.

Instead...

...she watched.

One speaker finished.

No one interrupted.

Another picked up the thought exactly where the first had left it, as though they had rehearsed nothing and yet understood each other's reasoning instinctively.

They weren't debating.

They were building.

One concern laid beside another.

One value balancing another.

Not trying to win.

Trying to make room.

The pressure eased.

Sound returned all at once.

"...Longyearbyen remains viable."

The broad-shouldered man spoke with the calm confidence of someone accustomed to solving impossible logistical problems.

"If Beijing, San Diego, and Berlin can all route their preservation samples north, we still have a path."

He looked toward the crimson figure.

"Dianne."

She met his eyes.

"I'll need to know which boat can retrieve them."

For the first time since Addie had arrived, the weary woman seemed almost grateful to be asked something with a straightforward answer.

"Gabriel."

She didn't hesitate.

"He can complete the route."

A tiny smile touched her lips.

"I'll add it to his itinerary."

The man nodded once.

"Good."

No celebration followed.

The answer hadn't solved anything.

It had merely kept one possibility alive.

That, Addie realized, seemed to be how this entire council functioned.

No triumphs.

Only the steady elimination of disasters.

Her attention drifted naturally from the exchange until it settled on the man who had spoken.

There was something reassuring about him. Not because of anything as simple as appearance, but because of the way everyone else unconsciously accepted his practicality. He spoke of routes and storage as though they were moral questions rather than engineering problems.

Across the table another woman leaned forward.

"We cannot begin selecting lives before we've decided what makes a civilization worth preserving."

Several heads turned toward her.

Immediately another answered.

"Law."

A third voice.

"Memory."

"No," someone else said quietly.

"People."

The conversation didn't fracture.

It deepened.

Each answer became another layer rather than another objection.

Silence made the smallest sound beside Addie.

"Hmm."

"Watch."

"I am."

"No."

Silence smiled faintly.

"You're listening again."

Addie blinked.

She was right.

Without realizing it, she'd begun chasing the words instead of the people.

She forced herself to look away from the speaker and toward those who weren't speaking.

One woman folded her hands tightly enough that the knuckles whitened whenever children were mentioned.

Another unconsciously nodded whenever someone defended openness over efficiency.

A man who had scarcely spoken all meeting relaxed only when discussion returned to infrastructure and distribution.

No one reacted equally to every subject.

Every face betrayed a value before its owner ever gave it voice.

Addie felt something in the back of her mind begin quietly arranging itself.

Not conclusions.

Shelves.

Places where observations belonged until enough of them formed a pattern.

She didn't notice Silence glance up at her.

But the fox-kit noticed the almost imperceptible smile that crossed Addie's face.

"Hmm."

The sound carried the slightest warmth.

"Better."

The discussion moved with the unhurried confidence of people who had spent years learning one another's thoughts. No one spoke merely to fill silence. When someone did choose to speak, the others listened with the curious discipline of people who believed the next sentence might genuinely change their own mind.

Addie found herself forgetting who had begun each idea.

The conversation refused to belong to any one person.

A woman with silver woven through dark hair rested her fingertips lightly on the table.

"We cannot begin with quotas."

Her voice was calm, almost conversational, yet the room seemed to tighten around the sentence.

"If our first act is to decide whose life is worth more than another's, then we've already surrendered the civilization we're trying to preserve."

Across from her, a broad-shouldered man inclined his head.

"The numbers don't disappear simply because we dislike them."

"No," she agreed. "They don't."

"But numbers aren't laws."

"They're constraints."

Another voice entered before either could continue.

"Our laws exist for precisely this reason."

The speaker was younger than Addie expected, her posture impossibly straight, every movement deliberate without becoming rigid.

"When this meeting ends, none of us should possess the authority to quietly alter what we've decided."

She looked slowly around the circle.

"If we choose together..."

"...then history deserves to know we chose together."

A murmur of agreement passed around the table.

Not unanimous.

Respectful.

Near the opposite side, another woman shook her head almost imperceptibly.

"We're already speaking about humanity as though it were a collection."

She folded her hands.

"It isn't."

"It is billions of individual people."

"If we begin ranking bloodlines..."

"...measuring ancestry..."

"...or deciding which families are genetically desirable..."

She let the unfinished thought hang between them.

"...we'll create the very sickness we're claiming to cure."

Several people nodded.

Not because the argument had ended.

Because it had become part of the foundation beneath everything else.

A man who had remained quiet until now leaned forward, his expression carrying the patient enthusiasm of someone who had spent a lifetime watching living things refuse to obey expectations.

"We're trying to preserve life."

He smiled faintly.

"I'd rather preserve its ability to surprise us."

A few puzzled expressions answered him.

He continued.

"Healthy ecosystems don't survive because they remain unchanged."

"They survive because they never stop adapting."

His eyes moved around the table.

"If we preserve only what we already understand..."

"...we aren't preserving life."

"We're preserving a museum."

The woman beside him nodded before he had fully finished.

"And museums don't sing."

The sentence caught Addie off guard.

The speaker smiled apologetically.

"I study animals."

"I've watched species disappear one valley at a time."

"They don't care whether we remember their names."

"They care whether forests still exist."

She looked toward the woman in crimson.

"If we're preserving seeds..."

"...they're not optional."

"If we're preserving humanity..."

"...they're still not optional."

For the first time since the meeting had begun, the woman woven into crimson allowed herself the smallest smile.

"I know."

Quietly.

"I know."

Silence stood beside Addie without moving.

"What do you see?"

Addie kept watching the room.

"They're not arguing."

Silence made her familiar thoughtful sound.

"Hmm."

"They disagree..."

"...but they keep building."

"They're protecting something."

"What?"

Addie didn't answer immediately.

She watched one speaker stop halfway through a sentence because someone else had found better words.

Watched another abandon an argument she'd been defending only moments earlier after hearing a perspective she hadn't considered.

No one appeared interested in winning.

Only in making certain nothing essential had been forgotten.

Finally Addie spoke.

"They're protecting each other."

Silence looked up at her.

For just a heartbeat.

Perhaps.

Then she turned back toward the gathering.

"Keep watching."

The conversation flowed onward almost immediately, the next voice picking up not where the previous speaker had stopped, but where the room itself seemed ready to continue.

No one announced whose turn it was.

No one needed to.

Addie had begun noticing something she hadn't seen at first.

Every person around the circle carried a different instinct about what civilization meant.

Law.

Memory.

Stewardship.

Adaptation.

Infrastructure.

Faith.

None of them defended their own idea as the answer.

Each spoke as though terrified it might become the one thing everyone else accidentally forgot.

Without realizing it, Addie smiled.

In the back room of her mind, another shelf quietly slid into place.

Not for conclusions.

For values.

She had the unmistakable feeling she would need them later.

No one seemed interested in controlling the conversation.

That, more than anything else, surprised Addie.

She had expected interruptions, personalities jockeying for influence, perhaps even the quiet territorial behavior she'd seen in every archive committee she'd ever attended. Instead, ideas seemed to pass around the table almost independently of the people who voiced them. Someone would identify a flaw, another would strengthen the argument rather than defend against it, and a third would quietly shift the discussion in an entirely different direction without anyone appearing to resent the interruption.

It was less like a debate than the careful construction of something fragile, each speaker testing whether the next stone could bear weight before placing another upon it.

"We're beginning in the wrong place."

The voice belonged to a woman seated almost opposite them. She hadn't raised it, yet the room settled naturally around her words.

"We keep talking about capacity." Her fingers rested lightly against the polished stone, tracing nothing in particular. "Storage. Numbers. Transport."

She looked slowly around the gathering.

"Those matter."

"They simply aren't the first question."

No one answered immediately.

The silence wasn't resistance.

It was permission.

"Our first question," she continued, "must be whether any of us possesses the right to decide that one human life carries greater worth than another."

Across the table, a broad-shouldered man folded his hands together before speaking.

"The numbers won't disappear because we refuse to acknowledge them."

"No," she agreed without hesitation. "Nor should they."

Her expression softened.

"But mathematics describes constraints."

"It doesn't confer morality."

A younger woman leaned forward before either could continue. There was nothing hesitant about her posture; she spoke with the practiced precision of someone accustomed to weighing every word before allowing it into the world.

"Then whatever process we establish must survive us."

Several heads turned.

"If our successors cannot examine our decisions..."

"If they cannot challenge them..."

"...then we haven't written law."

"We've written permission."

The sentence lingered.

Addie noticed that no one hurried to respond. Around the table, people were no longer watching the speaker. They were watching one another, measuring reactions with the same care that they measured arguments.

A man whose hands still bore the faint calluses of physical work finally broke the silence.

"Transparency won't matter if we've already chosen the wrong principles."

"And choosing the wrong principles becomes very easy," another woman replied quietly, "the moment we begin confusing health with purity."

Her voice carried neither accusation nor anger, only an old weariness.

"I've already heard suggestions that we maximize desirable genetic outcomes."

She allowed herself the faintest shake of her head.

"That language sounds scientific."

"It isn't."

"It's vanity wearing a laboratory coat."

A ripple of uncomfortable recognition passed around the table.

"We are not creating a superior humanity."

"We are preserving humanity."

"All of it."

Addie found herself glancing from face to face rather than following whoever happened to be speaking. Silence had been right. The conversation itself was only half the story.

One man nodded almost imperceptibly at the mention of transparency but frowned at the discussion of genetics. Another seemed visibly relieved that someone else had finally voiced the concern. A third closed his eyes briefly, as though hearing an argument he'd privately rehearsed long before arriving.

No one looked victorious.

No one even looked defensive.

They looked afraid of forgetting something essential.

Beside her, Silence remained perfectly still.

"What do you see?" she asked.

This time Addie didn't answer immediately. She let the question sit while the discussion continued around the table. Another speaker had begun talking about ecological adaptation, but her attention remained on the people rather than the words.

"They aren't trying to defeat each other."

Silence made no sound.

"They're trying to protect each other from themselves."

Only then did Silence glance up at her.

"Hmm."

It was impossible to know whether the tiny sound meant *yes*, *perhaps*, or *keep going*. Addie had begun to suspect that ambiguity wasn't a quirk of Silence's speech.

It was intentional.

Before she could test the thought, the discussion shifted again.

A biologist—she assumed he was one from the direction the conversation had taken—leaned forward with the restrained enthusiasm of someone who genuinely loved the thing he studied.

"We keep speaking about preservation as though life were a painting we intend to hang on a wall."

A faint smile crossed his face.

"It isn't."

"It's a river."

"You don't preserve a river by freezing it."

"You preserve it by making certain it can continue to flow."

The woman beside him laughed softly.

"And rivers don't flow alone."

Every eye turned toward her.

"If we're preserving humanity while allowing forests to vanish..."

"...or insects..."

"...or seeds..."

"...then we're preserving an audience after we've burned down the theater."

The room fell thoughtfully quiet once more.

Addie realized she had stopped trying to remember who was winning.

No one was.

That wasn't what this gathering was doing.

Every person at the table seemed to be standing watch over a different piece of civilization, terrified that if they failed to speak, everyone else might unknowingly leave it behind.

The conversation did not circle.

It spiraled.

Each pass returned to the same central question from a different direction, gathering another layer before moving on again. Addie found herself thinking of scholars annotating the margins of the same manuscript over generations. No single note explained the whole text. Together they slowly revealed what one pair of eyes alone would have missed.

"If we're discussing preservation," a woman observed quietly, "then we're already assuming something survives."

She wasn't asking a question.

She was correcting the foundation beneath one.

"The question isn't merely what survives."

"It's who chooses."

The younger woman who had spoken about law inclined her head.

"And whether the people making those choices are themselves accountable."

"Exactly."

She folded her hands together.

"I have no objection to difficult decisions."

"I object to decisions that can no longer be questioned."

Her gaze drifted slowly around the table.

"History has an unfortunate habit of mistaking secrecy for wisdom."

That sentence lingered longer than most.

Across the circle, a man whose spectacles sat perpetually lower than seemed comfortable adjusted them almost absently before speaking.

"History has another unfortunate habit."

Several people looked toward him.

"It edits."

He spoke without bitterness, only the quiet certainty of someone who had spent a lifetime discovering how incomplete every record eventually became.

"We preserve victories."

"We preserve heroes."

"We preserve the stories that allow descendants to admire their ancestors."

A faint, weary smile crossed his face.

"The truth is rarely so accommodating."

His fingers rested on the edge of the obsidian table.

"If we build archives..."

"...they cannot become monuments."

"They must remain records."

"Especially when those records embarrass us."

Several people nodded.

Not enthusiastically.

Gratefully.

Addie noticed that the woman beside him had closed her eyes while he spoke, her thumb unconsciously brushing the edge of a small notebook lying unopened before her. When she finally looked up, there was a sadness in her expression that made Addie wonder how many histories she had spent her life trying to rescue before they disappeared.

"Every culture believes its own ending is impossible."

The words emerged so quietly that Addie almost missed them.

"It never is."

The woman looked slowly around the gathering.

"If this world ends..."

"...I don't want our descendants inheriting only the stories powerful nations told about themselves."

Her voice remained steady, though something in it had grown heavier.

"I want them to inherit lullabies."

"Recipes."

"Children's games."

"Funeral songs."

"The names grandparents whispered when they thought no one else was listening."

She smiled sadly.

"Civilizations aren't remembered because kings existed."

"They're remembered because ordinary people did."

For the first time since entering the chamber, Addie felt something tighten unexpectedly in her throat.

She hadn't realized she'd been picturing libraries.

Buildings.

Books.

The woman had quietly reminded her that memory lived inside people long before anyone wrote it down.

Silence, without taking her eyes from the council, spoke softly enough that only Addie could hear.

"What changed?"

Addie frowned.

"What?"

"You were thinking about archives."

"I was."

"And now?"

Addie hesitated.

She watched the historian lower her eyes while another member picked up the conversation without missing a beat.

"...Now I'm thinking about families."

Silence made her familiar thoughtful sound.

"Hmm."

Nothing more.

No confirmation.

No correction.

Just enough to let Addie know she'd noticed the shift.

The discussion continued almost seamlessly.

A man whose forearms bore the pale scars of long physical labor rested both hands on the table before speaking. Unlike the historian, he searched for his words carefully, as though accustomed to weighing physical things more often than abstract ones.

"We've spent the better part of an hour talking about what deserves to be saved."

He glanced around the circle.

"I think we're forgetting why objects matter in the first place."

A few puzzled expressions answered him.

He smiled.

"Stone survives."

"Iron survives."

"So do paintings."

He shrugged gently.

"None of them ever loved anyone."

A few people smiled despite themselves.

"We don't preserve a child's toy because wood is valuable."

"We preserve it because someone once refused to sleep without it."

He looked toward the woman who had spoken about lullabies.

"The object isn't the memory."

"It's the handhold."

"The thing that lets another human being reach back across time and briefly touch someone they'll never meet."

Addie hadn't expected that.

Neither, apparently, had several others.

Silence settled over the room once again—not the silence of disagreement, but the silence that follows a thought no one immediately wishes to disturb.

Addie felt another shelf quietly slide into place somewhere in the back room of her mind.

Not labeled.

Not organized.

Simply waiting.

Whatever these people were building together, it wasn't a survival plan.

It was an argument about what being human actually meant.

And, perhaps more surprisingly...

...every person around the table seemed quietly afraid that the thing they had devoted their life to might become the one thing everyone else accidentally left behind.

The conversation continued for several minutes before Addie noticed the man at all.

It wasn't that he had been hidden. Quite the opposite. He had been sitting in plain view from the moment she entered the chamber, listening far more than speaking, his chair turned just enough that only the back of his head and the line of one shoulder had been visible from where she stood beside Silence. Had someone asked her a moment earlier to describe everyone seated around the table, she would probably have mentioned him only in passing.

Then someone farther down the circle addressed him by name.

"Solomon."

It was such an ordinary moment that Addie almost missed it.

The man acknowledged the speaker with a slight inclination of his head before turning in his chair to answer, the movement so natural and unhurried that nothing about it suggested it would become the single most unsettling thing she had witnessed since entering the First Mirror.

At first she saw only his profile.

The angle of his jaw.

The weathered lines at the corner of one eye.

There was a strange familiarity to them that she couldn't immediately place. Her mind reached instinctively into the back room where it stored faces, conversations, fragments of old records and half-forgotten observations, searching through shelves that seemed suddenly to rearrange themselves of their own accord.

Then he turned a little farther.

The search stopped.

Everything stopped.

The face looking calmly across the table belonged to the human Witness she had met scarcely an hour before.

Not someone who resembled him.

Not someone who shared a family likeness.

The same face.

The same thoughtful eyes.

The same quiet kindness that had somehow managed to exist alongside unbearable sorrow.

For one irrational instant Addie became convinced the room itself had made a mistake.

She leaned forward before she realized she had moved, her body already trying to close the distance while the rest of her struggled to assemble an explanation capable of surviving even a single question.

It made no sense.

The Witnesses had stood between stories.

This council existed somewhere else entirely.

Perhaps some ancestor.

Perhaps a descendant.

Perhaps...

She stopped herself.

Those weren't explanations.

They were guesses wearing the clothes of certainty.

Still, the need to see him more clearly became almost overwhelming. She took another step, expecting to draw closer to the table, and frowned when the distance refused to change. The polished obsidian beneath her feet accepted the movement; the room itself simply declined to acknowledge it.

She tried again.

Nothing.

Only then did the impossible nature of the vision assert itself in an entirely new way.

Until this moment she had accepted, almost without thinking, that she was standing inside a memory. Memories, however extraordinary, still obeyed certain instincts. If she wished to move, she moved. If she wished to look more closely, she leaned in. That assumption had survived every impossible thing Silence had shown her.

Now it quietly fell apart.

"I'm..." she began, more to herself than anyone else. "I can't..."

A small hand rested lightly against her sleeve.

"I know," Silence said.

Addie looked down at her, bewilderment replacing urgency for the briefest of moments.

"I need to get closer."

Silence regarded her with patient curiosity rather than concern.

"Do you?"

"That's him."

"Perhaps."

"No," Addie said, surprising herself with the certainty in her own voice. "I'm almost sure."

Silence said nothing.

The quiet itself became an invitation.

Addie opened her mouth to defend the conclusion and discovered, to her irritation, that she couldn't. She had seen the Witness for minutes, not years. She had encountered him in a place whose existence she still couldn't explain after following a fox-kit wearing overalls, a fox mask, and the face of a fox through a doorway stitched together from a strand of flax beneath a sky that could not possibly exist.

The absurdity arrived all at once.

Silence waited until Addie's expression softened with the realization.

"I'm not saying you're wrong," she said gently. "I'm asking why this is where your certainty begins."

Her gaze drifted back toward the gathering.

"...and why this is where your sanity ends."

The words settled without accusation.

They simply existed.

After a moment, Silence tilted her head ever so slightly.

"Why this hill?"

She watched the council rather than Addie.

"And not the one before it?"

Another thoughtful pause.

"...or the one after?"

Addie followed her gaze almost despite herself.

The discussion had continued uninterrupted.

No one had noticed her distress.

Or perhaps they couldn't.

Silence smiled faintly.

"You noticed."

A tiny nod acknowledged the observation without celebrating it.

"Put that in your back room."

The phrase struck Addie strangely. She didn't know why, only that something about it refused to be discarded. Like so many things she couldn't yet explain, she filed it carefully away without attempting to force an answer from it.

Silence's attention never left the gathering.

"It may matter."

Her voice became almost inaudible.

"But you nearly missed the really good part."

Addie forced herself to let Solomon go.

Not from memory—she suspected that face would remain with her for a very long time—but from the center of her attention. Silence had been right about one thing. Whether Solomon mattered or not, the conversation had never stopped while she'd been trying to make sense of him, and whatever these people were building together seemed far too important to sacrifice in pursuit of a single impossible question.

She took a slow breath, settled herself once more beside the little fox-kit, and listened.

"...memory isn't merely storage."

The words reached her just as another speaker yielded the floor, not because he had been interrupted, but because someone else had found a more fundamental way to express the thought. Addie was beginning to recognize that pattern. Around this table, no one seemed possessive of ideas. If another person could strengthen an argument, the argument simply changed hands and continued forward.

A man whose features suggested long years spent behind instruments rather than in public life adjusted a pair of spectacles before speaking again.

"We continue describing memory as though it were an archive."

A faint smile crossed his face.

"It isn't."

"It is identity."

He rested both hands on the obsidian table, looking not at any single member of the gathering but somewhere into the middle of the circle itself, as though addressing the problem rather than the people.

"If I remove enough memories from a person, at what point have I stopped repairing them and started replacing them?"

No one answered immediately.

The question had not been rhetorical.

Across the table, a physician whom Addie had noticed only in passing until now slowly folded her hands together.

"We've become remarkably comfortable speaking about preservation in the abstract."

There was compassion in her voice, but also fatigue.

"We preserve cultures."

"We preserve knowledge."

"We preserve genomes."

She looked down briefly before raising her eyes once more.

"We preserve people."

The final word carried a different weight.

"People are not collections."

"They are not inventories."

"They are not repositories waiting to be catalogued."

Her expression hardened, though only slightly.

"The moment medicine begins speaking about human beings as replaceable components..."

"...medicine has ceased to exist."

Silence settled briefly over the chamber.

Not because anyone disagreed.

Because several people seemed unwilling to let the sentence pass without giving it the consideration it deserved.

A man seated farther along the circle finally broke the pause.

"Nor should security."

He spoke quietly enough that Addie almost missed him.

"I've heard proposals already suggesting compulsory containment."

He glanced around the table.

"They are well-intentioned."

"I don't question that."

"But if the first language of our new civilization is restraint..."

"...we will spend generations teaching our descendants to fear one another before they've had the chance to become neighbors."

Several members exchanged thoughtful glances.

No one hurried to defend the proposal he'd just criticized.

Instead, another voice entered the discussion from an entirely different direction.

"Fear also edits memory."

The historian spoke almost absentmindedly, as though finishing a thought that had begun years earlier.

"We remember danger vividly."

"We remember kindness poorly."

His fingers traced the polished edge of the table.

"If this project succeeds..."

"...our descendants will inherit whatever stories survive with them."

He looked toward the physician.

"If those stories begin with prisons..."

"...they will spend centuries inventing reasons those prisons must have been necessary."

A quiet murmur of agreement moved around the gathering.

Addie watched the faces rather than the words.

Every time someone spoke, she found herself asking a different question now.

Not *Do I agree?*

Instead—

*What part of the world has this person spent their life protecting?*

The realization arrived so naturally that she almost smiled.

The physician wasn't defending medicine.

She was defending dignity.

The historian wasn't defending records.

He was defending honesty.

The man concerned with security wasn't arguing against protection.

He was arguing against fear becoming habit.

Silence hadn't exaggerated.

People really did reveal themselves if you watched long enough.

Beside her, the fox-kit made her familiar thoughtful sound.

"Hmm."

Addie glanced down.

"I know," she whispered, almost embarrassed she'd answered aloud.

"No."

Silence's ears twitched.

"You don't."

Addie blinked.

Silence's expression remained impossibly gentle.

"But..."

She looked back toward the council.

"...you're beginning to ask better questions."

That was all.

No explanation.

No reassurance.

Just enough to remind Addie that understanding and certainty were not the same thing.

The conversation flowed onward before she had time to dwell on it.

A weathered man with broad shoulders and calloused hands leaned forward, waiting until the previous speaker had completely finished before adding his own concern.

"We've spent hours discussing people."

"I'd like to speak briefly for the roads."

A few smiles appeared around the table.

He acknowledged them with the faintest inclination of his head.

"I'm serious."

"If medicine matters..."

"...someone must build the road that carries it."

"If knowledge matters..."

"...someone must keep the lights on in the archive."

"If food matters..."

"...someone must maintain the bridges before the harvest arrives."

He spread his hands across the polished stone.

"Infrastructure isn't separate from compassion."

"It's compassion made visible."

"You can always tell what a civilization loves."

"Follow the roads."

Addie hadn't expected the sentence to affect her.

It did.

Not because it was dramatic.

Because it felt quietly, stubbornly true.

Without warning she found herself wondering how many ordinary things she'd spent her entire life overlooking simply because they had always worked.

Someone had built every road she'd ever walked.

Someone had carried every book she'd ever read.

Someone had repaired every roof she'd ever studied beneath.

The conversation had become strangely dangerous.

Not because it threatened her understanding of the world.

Because every few minutes it quietly persuaded her to see an ordinary part of that world differently.
